Here's the basic idea:

During the course I will guide you through a bunch of readings which discuss some hot topics in ethics: abortion, euthanasia & assisted suicide, capital punishment, and animals' rights. There are lots of other issues that may interest you, though, such as:
Is our nation's prohibition of certain drugs defensible?
What do we owe, if anything, to people who are starving or homeless? 
When is censorship justified? 
Should gay couples be allowed to marry? to bring up children?
Should affirmative action be allowed? If so, when, and for whom?
Was the US invasion of Iraq justified? Is the "War on Terror" productive?
Should cloning of animals be allowed? of people? or how about stem cell research?
You will need to pick one of these issues or another of interest to you, find fellow students with similar interests, form your own groups of of 2-4 people (though I might accept other sizes), get me to approve your topic, then research your topic and do a presentation on it in front of the class.

The deadline for choosing a topic and forming a group is Wednesday of 8th Week (March 9th to be exact), which is when we will officially assign dates. The sooner you clear your topic and group with me, the better the choice of dates you'll have. Once a date is set, it will be subject to the late policy stated in the course syllabus.

Sources

This is a research project, so I will expect you to rely on an absolute minimum of two credible sources. Most good presentations rely on four or more.

For topics and source material I encourage you to draw from the chapters of our textbook that I won’t be covering in lecture. They contain interesting and valuable arguments on these and other issues. 

At the end of each chapter in the book you will also find "Suggested Additional Readings" on the topic of the chapter. Look there for more material.

The Armacost Library has access to the online Philosopher's Index, JSTOR, and other databases which contains a vast trove of articles. Once you've settled on a topic and gotten approval for it, try entering it in these databases to see what pops up.

You may also use the Internet, newspapers, magazines, etc., but you must be careful to find good-quality material. The Internet, for example, is much like a city dump: there are great things to be found there, but to reach them you have to sort through a lot of garbage. See me if you have any questions about what source materials to use.

To search on the Internet start at my Philosophy Research Links Page (click here or on the "Research Links" button on any of the course web pages).

Format

Expect to take 20 minutes or so. Make sure you include something that involves the class in your presentation. Visual aids (writing on the board, creating posters, doing PowerPoint presentations, etc.) are encouraged. Handouts which summarize the main points or arguments are strongly encouraged; if you get them to me by the night before your presentation I can copy them for you.

You might...
Have each person present a different perspective on the issue, or
Stage a debate, or
Lecture the way I do, or
Make a film, or include clips from movies on your topic, or
Do something completely different.
Each group will be expected to
  • present more than one side of an issue
  • be fair to all sides—that is, to present each side as strongly as possible whether you agree with it or not. Philosophy is, after all, a search for truth, not an attempt to merely win a point.
  • create discussion questions or some other way of involving the class
  • use (and cite) at least two sources, though more would be better.

Credit

Each participant in the group will be graded separately for their work, and must submit written evidence of what they contributed and what sources they relied on. This evidence is due in class when the presentation is given. This project is worth 20% of your grade for the course, so I will expect to see evidence of very substantial, new work by everyone in each group. Just downloading a web page and reading it to the class or copying it onto a PowerPoint slide, for example, is not acceptable. Presenting work already submitted for credit in another course is also not acceptable.

The work you do might consist of
Research to find material to present,
Summarizing, re-writing, and/or organizing the material,
Creating visual aids or handouts, and/or
Doing the presenting itself.
Each group should visit with me well before the presentation date to discuss plans and get some coaching.

I will create study questions based on student presentations, and they (like all other study questions) will be fair game for exams. So an extra benefit of doing a presentation is that you will be the class expert on some of the study questions.

 


 
Or else...

The alternative to doing a small-group presentation is to write a 4-6 page essay on one of the two following topics:

The Death Penalty. Should it be retained? If so, under what circumstances? Or should it be abolished entirely? For this essay, you must (a) present your view, (b) defend it with the strongest arguments you can (these arguments can come from any source), (c) present, in the most convincing and forceful way you can, the strongest argument(s) from the course readings that conflict(s) with your position, and (d) give me the strongest reasons you can to take your side in spite of the opposing arguments in part (c).

The Value of Life. Many of the arguments we’ve studied either implicitly or explictly endorse some criteria for determining whether a life has moral value: having certain abilities, having a certain sort of future, having rational capacity, and so on. Is one of these criteria (or sets of criteria) clearly superior to the others? Is there some other one not discussed in the class that is even better? For this essay, you must (a) present your view on what gives life moral value, (b) give me the strongest reasons you can to endorse your favored account of the value of life, by showing how it is superior to at least one competing account given in the course readings, and (c) show what ethical conclusions this account leads us to in one of the issues we’re studying in this course (abortion, euthanasia/PAS, the death penalty, or our treatment of animals).

If neither of these topics interests you, you may propose your own for my approval. But whatever topic you write on, you must take a side on an ethical issue and defend it against strong criticism, and I will strongly prefer that you consider at least some argument(s) from the assigned reading for the course.

Whatever your topic, there will be some firm requirements:

Format. Papers must be typed, double-spaced, with 1-inch margins all around (note that many word processors have a default setting of 1.25"; you may need to adjust yours), in a normal-sized font, and free of typographical and grammatical errors. I primarily evaluate content rather than length or style, so don’t worry if the length isn’t just right or if a couple of misspellings leak through.

Style. Try to express yourself in plain, everyday language, using your own words as much as possible and quoting as little as possible. But regardless of whether you quote someone else's work or not, you must still cite your sources.

References. Be sure to cite your source whenever you use or refer to an idea you got from someone else, whether you quote it or not. If that means you have ten citations within a paragraph, so be it! Failure to cite your sources will be dealt with harshly: penalties range from having to rewrite the paper to failing the course; if it turns out to be a repeat offense you could be expelled from the University. References to articles in Social Ethics should be by the article author’s name and page number (e.g., Stewart et al., p. 121). References to outside sources must include full bibliographic information (author, title, publisher, date, etc., or full URL if the source is a web page). 
If you have the slightest doubt about proper procedures for citing sources and avoiding plagiarism, ask me before you turn your paper in. You might also consult the links regarding plagiarism on my links page.
Papers are due in class on the final class meeting, Monday April 18. See the Syllabus for the policy on late papers. The essay will count for 20% of your grade for the course.
I strongly encourage you to submit a draft for feedback at least four days in advance of the due date. I also encourage you to take advantage of the free assistance with writing provided by Student Services in the Writing Lab on the ground level of the Armacost Library. Writing Lab hours are posted there, or you can call ext. 4079 for information.
