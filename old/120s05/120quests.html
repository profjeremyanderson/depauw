As we go through the course I will create study questions which will cover the important points to remember. The questions on the exams will be a subset of the study questions—in other words, the exams will consist of some of these exact questions. 

The study questions will be posted here for you weekly. You will be notified by e-mail of new postings if you get yourself on the class mailing list. To ensure that you're on it, e-mail me at jeremy at jeremyanderson dot net (substitute '@' for 'at' and '.' for 'dot' and remove any spaces) and ask to be added to it.

I--and the students who took this course before you--strongly recommend that when new questions are posted you

  1. Review the readings and your lecture notes,
  2. Write out the clearest, most thorough answers you can to these questions, and 
  3. Study them periodically. 
I encourage you to work together with others in the class on this.

Sample answers to some of these questions have been posted here on the Sample Answers page.

Remember that the midterm and final exams will be comprehensive, so questions that did not appear on an earlier exam might still show up on the next one.

Once a question appears on an exam given to the whole class, it will not appear again. Questions that have been used will be struck through (like this). ALL other questions still might appear on an exam, even if they appeared on a make-up exam.

Unit I: Introduction to Philosophy in General

1. For each field discussed in lecture (biology, philosophy, and theology), give at least three questions that people within that field try to answer.

2. Within philosophy, what are the main questions associated with epistemology, metaphysics, and ethics? (Be sure to associate each question with the correct philosophical specialty.)

3. For each field discussed in lecture (biology, philosophy, and theology), give at least two of the methods characteristically used within the field to find answers to its questions.

4. When it comes to answering philosophical questions, why can't philosophers run experiments like biologists and other scientists do?

5. Of the three fields discussed in lecture (biology, philosophy, and theology), which do, and which do not, allow the use of private evidence? Explain the difference between private and public evidence, and give an example of each kind.

6. Define the following terms as they are used in philosophy: 'conclusion,' 'premise,' 'argument.'

Unit II: Abortion

7. Under what circumstances, if any, does John Paul II think abortion is permissible?

8. Why is it important for John Paul II to establish that the life of a fetus “is neither that of the father nor the mother; it is rather the life of a new human being” (p. 12)?

9. Why is it important for John Paul II to establish that the human fetus is innocent?

10. Give John Paul II’s argument regarding the permissibility of abortion. (You may use what was written on the board or put it in your own words, but be sure to make his line of reasoning as clear as you can and identify any unstated premises he relies on.)

11. Under what circumstances, if any, does Warren think abortion is permissible?

12. Does Warren think fetuses are persons or not? Explain how she arrives at this opinion, being careful to include her reasons for believing that personhood is not established by being genetically human.

13. How does Warren respond to the claim that the fetus deserves protection because it is a potential person?

14. According to Warren, what is wrong with infanticide (killing babies)?

15. Make an objection against some part of Warren’s argument. That is, identify a conclusion she reaches which you think may be mistaken, and give the strongest reasons you can for me to agree with you that she is mistaken. (You may use one of the objections raised in class if you like.)

16. Explain how Marquis figures out what's wrong with killing you or me. Then explain how he thinks this applies to abortion.

17. Why does Marquis need to show that contraception is not morally wrong? How does he do so?

18. Explain at least one problem with Marquis' argument against abortion. (You may rely on lecture if you like.)

19. Despite their obviously different conclusions about abortion, what important assumption is shared by Pope John Paul II and Warren? What does Thomson think of this assumption?

20. Some anti-abortionists argue that even if a woman's life is threatened by her pregnancy, a third party (like a doctor) may not intervene to help her abort it. According to Thomson, what reason might anti-abortionists give for this?

21. How does Thomson argue that third parties (like doctors) may intervene to help a woman abort a life-threatening pregnancy?

22. Thomson considers two different versions of "what it comes to, to have a right to life" (p. 55f). What are they, and what reasons does she give for rejecting each of them?

23. Under what circumstances does Thomson think abortion is permissible--that is, when would abortion not be an injustice to the fetus? When would abortion be unjust? (I'm not asking when she thinks abortion is decent or indecent.)

24. How does Thomson argue that the use of birth control amounts to denying permission for the fetus to use a woman's body?

25. Identify at least one problem with Thomson's "burglar" and/or "people seeds" analogies (pp. 58-9).

26. How does Thomson argue that it is unfair to require pregnant women to be "good Samaritans" to their fetuses?

27. The state of Texas claimed that abortions should be prohibited (except to save the mother's life) because abortion is dangerous to women. How does Blackmun respond to Texas on this point? What conclusion did he draw from this about when and why states may restrict abortion?

28. The state of Texas claimed that abortions should be prohibited (except to save the mother's life) because fetuses are persons. How does Blackmun respond to Texas on this point? What conclusion did he draw from this about when and why states may restrict abortion?

29. In the case of Planned Parenthood of PA v. Casey, what restrictions did the state of Pennsylvania place on access to abortion? What did O'Connor et al. rule regarding these restrictions?

Unit III: Euthanasia and Physician-Assisted Suicide

30. How was "euthanasia" defined in class? Explain how euthanasia differs from murder and from eugenics.

31. Define the following as they were presented in the course: "killing," "letting die," "active euthanasia," "passive euthanasia."

32. Define the following terms: voluntary euthanasia, non-voluntary euthanasia, involuntary euthanasia. Why are we not debating the morality of involuntary euthanasia in this course?

33. In lecture, three reasons for forbidding voluntary active euthanasia were presented and rejected. Explain each of them, and why each was rejected.

34. In lecture, two better reasons for forbidding voluntary active euthanasia were presented and taken seriously. Explain each of them.

35. In lecture, the two usual reasons in favor of allowing voluntary active euthanasia were presented. Explain each of them, including why each may be weak on its own.

36. What is now the "Standard View" on euthanasia in law and among most doctors?

37. Explain Rachels' argument that, because VAE can be more humane than VPE, the Standard View is mistaken. (You need not present it word for word, but explain his reasoning.)

38. Explain Rachels' argument that killing is not intrinsically worse than letting die. If he's right, why should we conclude that VAE should be permissible? (In other words, explain how Rachels thinks the parallel cases establish that VAE should be permitted.) Finally, explain at least one problem with Rachels' parallel cases.

39. Many doctors claim that VAE is wrong because it causes death, but VPE is okay because it is the patient's condition that kills, not the doctor. Explain Rachels' argument against this.

40. Why does Brock think self-determination is important? What view on VAE does this lead him to support?

41. Why does Brock think that VAE supports patient well-being? How does his view differ from Rachels' in this regard?

42. Gaylin says that legalizing VAE would undermine trust our trust of doctors (p. 78). What is Brock's response? Why might Brock's response be inadequate?

43. Of the various considerations & arguments offered so far in favor of allowing VAE (from Rachels and Brock), which do you find most persuasive? Which do you find least persuasive? Give me the strongest reasons you can to agree with your assessments. 

44. Explain Callahan's "moral" argument about the distinction between killing and letting die.

45. In his "medical" argument, Callahan argued that active euthanasia should not be allowed because it is incompatible with doctors' traditional role. What does Callahan think a doctor's proper role is? When we discussed this in class, what came up for and against Callahan on this point?

46. What impact does Potts think that legalized euthanasia could have one the search for new cures and better medical treatments (his #1) ? How come?

47. What abuses does Potts fear if voluntary active euthanasia is legalized (his #4, 5, & 9)?

48. What form do slippery slope arguments have? (In other words, what two propositions must they persuade us of in order to be sound?)

49. In what two ways does Potts argue that legalizing voluntary active euthanasia could lead us down a slippery slope (his #7 and 8)? What problems with his arguments were discussed in class? (Hint: One good way to do this would be to explain how Potts' arguments try to fulfill the requirments for a slippery slope argument, and what problems they have doing so.)

50. Explain the "Dilemma of Regulation" with regard to voluntary active euthanasia.

51. What three kinds of rights were discussed in class? Define and give an example of each.

52. Watts & Howell identify three different types of assisted suicide (p. 85). What are they? Which ones do Watts & Howell think should be allowed?

53. Why do Watts & Howell believe that legalizing (some forms of) physician-assisted suicide would not undermine doctor-patient trust (pp. 87-8)?

54. Rehnquist believes that legalizing physician-assisted suicide could endanger members of certain "vulnerable groups" (p. 96). How come? And what reasons lead Watts & Howell to disagree (pp. 86-7)?

Unit IV: The Death Penalty, and Presentations

55. How have the methods for executing people and the circumstances of execution changed over the years?

56. Define the following terms: retentionist, abolitionist.

57. In lecture, two common but faulty arguments regarding the death penalty were discussed. Explain each one, and the problems each suffers from.

58. Define the following terms as they were presented in class: retribution, equal punishment, equal harm.

59. In lecture we discussed four goods that utilitarians have thought punishment should seek. Explain what they are, and explain why some are compatible with the death penalty while others are not.

60. In their 1972 ruling in Furman v. Georgia, why did the Supreme Court strike down Georgia's death penalty law?

61. In their 1976 ruling in Gregg v. Georgia, Stewart et al. claim that the death penalty accords with human dignity, i.e., that it is not excessive. What are their reasons for this?

62. In their 1976 ruling in Gregg v. Georgia, Stewart et al. claim that the death penalty is acceptable to the American public. What evidence do they give of this?

Study Questions for Phil 120-02 (meets at 9:30 MW)

02-01. Marshall argues that the death penalty is excessive. Explain his reasons.

02-02. Marshall believes that the death penalty would be rejected by an informed American public. What are his reasons for this?

02-03. In their ruling in Gregg v. Georgia, why do Stewart et al. conclude that Georgia's new (post-1972) law applies the death penalty in a constitutionally acceptable way?

02-04. Kant argues that utilitarianism should be rejected as a rationale for punishment because it could sanction injustice. Explain one of the examples used in class to show this.

02-05. Kant uses several examples to show that his "equal punishment" interpretation of the Lex Talionis is clear and easy to apply. Explain one of them (one that does NOT involve the death penalty). Why is it easier to apply "equal punishment" than it would be to apply Utilitarianism?

02-06. Both Kant and Primoratz believe that, for the crime of murder, nothing less than the death penalty will do. Explain why. (If it helps you to draw diagrams, that's fine; but be sure to explain what they mean.)

02-07. How does Nathanson argue that Kant's "equal punishment" principle fails to guide us?

02-08. Does Kant consistently stick to his principle of "equal punishment"? Explain how. Does American society follow "equal punishment"? Explain how. What implications does this have for retentionism?

02-09. How does Nathanson argue that Primoratz's "equal harm" principle fails to guide us?

02-10. How would we have to modify our methods of punishment if we want to follow Primoratz's "equal harm" principle? If we are unwilling to do so, what implications does this have for retentionism? How come?

02-11. What is Nathanson's "proportionate retribution" proposal? Why does he think we should not include the death penalty on our list of punishments?

02-12. Many people think that the death penalty is clearly a better incapacitator than life in prison, and that it is also a better deterrent. Why are these beliefs both mistaken?

02-13. What is Pojman's "common-sense" argument for the death penalty?

02-14. What anecdotal evidence does Pojman offer to support his common-sense argument? (Note that I am asking about the anecdotes, not about the argument.) Why do these anecdotes fail to help support the argument very strongly?

02-15. The "common-sense" argument for the death penalty assumes that scarier punishments have more deterrent value. How does Reiman show that this is not necessarily the case?

02-16. If you are sentenced to death in the US, how long (on average) will it be before you are executed? Explain what effect this has on the deterrent value of the death penalty.

02-17. If you commit murder in California, approximately how likely are you to be executed for it? How about in Texas? Explain what effect this has on the deterrent value of the death penalty.

02-18. How does van den Haag (via Pojman) argue that retaining the death penalty is our "best bet"?

02-19. From the presentation on affirmative action: Charles Murray believes that preferential treatment places minorities in a situation where they are considered inferior, and thus fosters a new form of racism. How does he argue for this?

02-20. From the presentation on affirmative action: Hettinger admits that his affirmative action proposal judges people on the basis of involuntary characteristics. How does he argue that this objection isn't strong enough to reject his proposal?

02-21. From the presentation on world hunger: Hardin believes that, if a country fails to control its population, giving it food aid will actually make things much worse in the long run because of the "ratchet effect." Explain this effect and why Hardin thinks it will lead to greater disaster.

02-22. From the presentation on world hunger: Sen points out that famine can occur when there is plenty of food. What does this tell us about Malthus's theory of the cause of poverty?

02-23. From the presentation on genocide in Darfur: Fiona presented three main reasons for the US to intervene in Darfur. Explain at least two of them.

02-24. From the presentation on genocide in Darfur: Molly defended a Social Darwinist approach to Darfur. What does Social Darwinism recommend we do about Darfur? How is this different from eugenics?

02-25. Van den Haag (via Pojman) argues that retaining the death penalty is our "best bet" for saving innocent lives. How did Jeremy argue that in fact the opposite is true?

02-26. From the presentation on animal rights: Singer denies that our higher intellect entitles us to eat animals and experiment on them. According to him, what do animals have that obliges us to stop causing animal suffering?

02-27. From the presentation on animal rights: According to Cohen (whose argument was presented by Matthew), why can't animals have rights?

02-28. From the presentation on same-sex marriage: What sorts of problems does Kmiec believe children may have if raised by gay parents?

02-29. From the presentation on same-sex marriage: Identify at least two differences between marriage and civil unions.

This completes the list of study questions for this section of this course. The final exam for Phil 120-02 will be Friday April 22nd from 9-11am in our regular classroom. The questions that will be fair game are:
(a) all questions from 1-62 that have not been struck out (like this), and
(b) questions 02-01 to 02-29
but the final exam will emphasize questions from after the midterm (after 51).

03-27. From the presentation on the death penalty: Why does van den Haag think that the unfair distribution of the death penalty is not a reason to abolish it?

03-28. From the presentation on gun control: Identify at least three factors affecting the crime rate. Why has legalized abortion allegedly lowered the crime rate?

03-29. From the presentation on gun control: In what three ways do current laws restrict minors' access to guns?
