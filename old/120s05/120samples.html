Prior to exams I will post a list of study questions to help you prepare. Here are sample answers to a few of the questions, so you can get a sense of what I'm looking for.



WARNING: the questions I answer here may still appear on an exam.


Some study questions only require simple, straightforward answers.

For example, take Study Question #1, which asks, "For each field discussed in lecture (biology, philosophy, and theology), give at least three questions that people within that field try to answer." It would be okay to just give three questions for each field, but since the question says "at least three" you'll get more credit if you can give more than three for at least one of the fields. So this would be a great answer:  

Biologists ask questions like, "How do living things work?" "What sorts of living things are there?" "How do living things break down?" "How do we fix them when they break down?" and so on.

Philosophers ask questions like "How do we know what (we think) we know?" "What's the universe really like?" "What should I do?" and so on.

Theologians ask questions like, "What is God?" "What does God want us to do?" "How can we tell?" "What is the proper interpretation of Scripture?" What should I do?"
 

Answering other questions will require more care.

For example, here's a sample answer to question #8, which asks, 'Why is it important for John Paul II to establish that the life of a fetus "is neither that of the father nor the mother; it is rather the life of a new human being" (p. 12)?'  
     It is possible to give a very brief answer, like this:
Because some pro-choice people claim the fetus is just a part of the pregnant woman's body.

That would be an accurate and relevant answer, but it doesn't demonstrate much understanding of the issue. An answer like this one would do much better:
Some pro-choice people claim that abortion should be allowed because the fetus is just a part of the pregnant woman's body. If the fetus were just the mother's tissue, then aborting the fetus would be no more controversial than getting your appendix removed, because we are free to dispose of our own bodily tissues if we please.
     By establishing that the fetus's life is a new one, distinct from its parents, the Pope cuts off that pro-choice line of argument.

Note that I wouldn't expect you to get all this word for word! I would, though, expect you to be able to give significantly more than just the first answer above. 
     The more detailed your answer is, the better it demonstrates your understanding and thought, and the more credit you get.
 

Other questions may be still more challenging. In such cases, answering them well will earn you more credit.

For example, let's look at question 12, which asks, "Does Warren think fetuses are persons or not? Explain how she arrives at this opinion, being careful to include her reasons for believing that personhood is not established by being genetically human."
     Note that the question has three distinct parts: (a) saying whether Warren thinks fetuses are persons, (b) explaining how she concludes this, and (c) giving her views on the relevance of genetics to personhood. Your answer should clearly address all three parts of the question, like this one does:  

(a) No, Warren does not think fetuses are persons.

(b) She tries to establish this by asking us to suppose we are space travelers who find an alien species. We must decide whether we should treat the aliens as persons (as having moral rights) or whether we may exploit them for food or firewood or whatever.
     If the aliens showed signs of having religion, art, etc. we might consider them persons, but what if they don't? Warren suggests that we should consider the aliens persons if they demonstrate the following five traits:
1. Consciousness: awareness of internal and/or external objects, esp. the ability to feel pain;
2. Reasoning: ability to solve complex problems;
3. Self-motivated activity: not just instinct or environmental influence;
4. Communication: can "say" indefinitely many things on indefinitely many topics; and
5. Self-awareness: as an individual and/or as a member of a group.
Not all five are needed to be a person. Traits 1+2 could possibly suffice, and 1+2+3 would probably suffice. But Warren believes a being with none of the five is definitely not a person, and fetuses have none of the five--or at least no more of the five than creatures we commonly treat as non-persons, like cows & fish.

(c) If it succeeds, her argument shows that personhood is not genetic because none of 1-5 has to do with genetics. If the alien can be a person despite not being human, then being human is not necessary for being a person. And if a human fetus is not a person despite its being human, then being human is not sufficient for being a person. If being genetically human is neither necessary nor sufficient for being a person, then genetics are irrelevant to personhood. 

Again, I would obviously not expect you to come up with this exact answer. What's important is that this answer does what is asked: it answers all three parts of the question accurately and thoroughly, and demonstrates having done the reading.  You should try to do the same.
