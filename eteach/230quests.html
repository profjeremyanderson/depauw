About the exam questions

All exams will consist of some of these questions.

Questions that appeared on a previous exam will be struck out so they look like this and will not be used again. Any other questions, even those that may have appeared on individual make-up exams, may appear on the next exam.

When new exam questions are posted here (roughly weekly), I will email you via Moodle. If you don't receive notifications, email me and let me know.

Resources for giving good answers

As noted on the syllabus, I offer several kinds of help for you to give good answers:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • The Sample Answers web page has answers to some exam questions and advice on how to write better answers.
  • Some notes and exercises from class will be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups -- in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be review sessions before each exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week. When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.

When you have trouble answering questions, ask me for help.

Academic integrity on exams
  • For exams given in class, no citing or quoting is required. You many not access books or notes of any kind during the exam.

  • For take-home exams (which we will have if class is taught remotely):
    • You may use any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with other people on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions will apply just as in papers and reading responses. Cite all sources you use and put others’ words in quotation marks.


Exam questions about Ethical Theory (our first day)

1. In class we listed many reasons why one should, or should not, hook up with a stranger when their significant other is away. These reasons fell into three main groups. Identify the three groups and, for each one, identify one of the reasons in that group. Name the type of ethical theory which corresponds to each group.



Exam questions about the basis of moral rules

2. Cephalus believes that justice is being truthful and giving back what you have taken from another (Plato, 5). Why does Socrates reject this definition?

3. In class we saw that Cephalus, Socrates, and Polemarchus hold what we called conventional assumptions about justice (Plato, 5-14 passim; Phil 230, 2022-08-26). What are these assumptions?

4. Explain what Thrasymachus means when he says that “justice is nothing other than what is advantageous for the stronger” (Plato, 15; Phil 230, 2022-08-26). How does this challenge conventional assumptions about justice?

5. Thrasymachus defends his moral skepticism by arguing that being just does not benefit you--rather, it is bad for you--and that "justice" is really a sort of scam used to exploit the weak. What evidence does Thrasymachus give for this (Plato, 15-22, especially 21-22)?

6. Define these terms as they are used in this course: “moral skepticism,” “moral subjectivism,” “cultural relativism,” and “ethical objectivism” (Phil 230, 2022-08-29).

7. Benedict describes two main kinds of cultural differences. One kind is where people we have labeled "abnormal" and unfit have a respected (though not necessarily central) place in other societies (Benedict, 1-2). Explain one of Benedict's examples of that kind of difference.

8. Benedict says, "The most spectacular illustrations of the extent to which normality may be culturally defined are those cultures where an abnormality of our culture is the cornerstone of their social structure" (3). First, explain what this sentence means. Then, pick one of the cultures she uses to illustrate this point (3-4), and explain how that culture's practices are supposed to support Benedict's claim.

9. Explain how Benedict uses her examples of differences to support cultural relativism. In class, this was summarized in "Benedict's argument"; state that argument.

10. State the "Cultural Differences Argument" (Rachels, 3). Why does Rachels think it is bad (3-4)?

11. Rachels says, “There are some moral rules that all societies must have in common, because those rules are necessary for society to exist” (7). Explain the examples he uses to support this claim.

12. According to Rachels, there is a culture-neutral standard for telling whether moral rules are justified (Rachels, 9). What is it?

13. King describes how Black Americans were treated under segregation (King, 14). What would a moral skeptic say about whether that treatment was wrong? What would a moral subjectivist say? A cultural relativist (remember this one is tricky)?

14. King says we should obey just laws but disobey unjust laws (15). How does King believe we can we objectively distinguish just laws from unjust laws (King, 16)? (Discussing King's examples from paragraphs 17-19 may be helpful, but it is most important to explain what he says in paragraph 16.)

15. Benedict observes individual cultures and finds differences among them. How does Aquinas differ? (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video will be helpful here.)

16. Aquinas argues for ethical objectivism by saying that human nature -- specifically, our natural inclinations -- provide an objective basis for judging human laws. What are the inclinations Aquinas identifies (Summa, 4-5; Aquinas explainer video, Aquinas chart)? What precepts of natural law does Aquinas derive from these inclinations?

17. What does it mean to say that the precepts of natural law are vague, and that they are authoritative? What are the implications of them being vague, and being authoritative? Give an example to illustrate how the precepts might be used to criticize a society's law. (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video and chart will be helpful here.)

Situation: Adara and Beth disagree. Adara is a conservative Saudi Muslim. Adara criticizes Beth for dressing immodestly because Beth does not cover her face or legs in public. Beth is an American liberal atheist. Beth criticizes Adara for wanting to restrict women’s freedom by dictating how they dress.

18. Explain how Aquinas might try to resolve the dispute between Adara and Beth. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? (What’s most important here is to give a sense of how Aquinas would approach this matter. The more thoroughly you can answer the other Aquinas questions, the better you will be able to explain that.)

Note: Exam questions with highlighted numbers (like this: 18. ) will be worth more points on exams because they call for more extensive, thoughtful answers. Some might also ask you to present and support your own opinion.
