About the exam questions

All exams will consist of some of these questions.

Questions that appear on the mid-term exam will be struck out so they look like this and will not be used again. Any other questions, even those that may have appeared on individual make-up exams, may appear on the next exam.

When new exam questions are posted here (roughly weekly), I will email you via Moodle. If you don't receive notifications, email me and let me know.

Resources for giving good answers

As noted on the syllabus, I offer several kinds of help for you to give good answers:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • The Sample Answers web page has answers to some exam questions and advice on how to write better answers.
  • Some notes and exercises from class will be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups -- in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be review sessions before each exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week. When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.

When (not if, when) you have trouble answering questions, ask me for help.

Academic integrity in exams
  • For exams given in class, you must write your answers solely from memory during the exam. No citing or quoting is required. You many not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam.

  • For take-home exams (which we will have if an exam must be given remotely):
    • You may use any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with other people on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions will apply just as in papers and reading responses. Cite all sources you use and put others’ words in quotation marks.

Exam questions about the basis of moral rules

2. Cephalus says that justice is being truthful and giving back what you have taken from another (Plato, 5). Why does Socrates reject this definition? (See the Sample Answers for advice on answering this and other exam questions.)

3. In class we saw that Cephalus, Socrates, and Polemarchus hold what we called conventional assumptions about justice (Plato, 5-14 passim; Phil 230, 2024-08-23). What are these assumptions?

4. Explain what Thrasymachus means when he says that “justice is nothing other than what is advantageous for the stronger” (Plato, 15).

5. Thrasymachus defends his moral skepticism by arguing that being just does not benefit you--rather, it is bad for you--and that "justice" is really a sort of scam used to exploit the weak. What evidence does Thrasymachus give for this (Plato, 15-22, especially 21-22)?

6. How does Thrasymachus's view of justice challenge each of the conventional assumptions about justice (Phil 230, 2024-08-23)?

7. Define these terms as they are used in this course: “moral skepticism,” “moral subjectivism,” “cultural relativism,” and “ethical objectivism” (Phil 230, 2024-08-26).

8. Benedict describes two main kinds of cultural differences. The first kind is where people we have labeled "abnormal" and unfit have a respected, though not necessarily central, place in other societies (Benedict, 1-2). Explain one of Benedict's examples of that kind of difference.

9. Benedict says, "The most spectacular illustrations of the extent to which normality may be culturally defined are those cultures where an abnormality of our culture is the cornerstone of their social structure" (3). First, explain what this sentence means. Then, pick one of the cultures she uses to illustrate this point (3-4), and explain how that culture's practices are supposed to support Benedict's claim.

10. Explain how Benedict uses her examples of difference to support cultural relativism. In class, this was summarized in "Benedict's argument"; state that 4-step argument.

11. State the "Cultural Differences Argument" (Rachels, 3). Why does Rachels think it is bad (3-4)? Why does his rejection of it nevertheless fail as an attack on Benedict's view (Phil 230, 2024-08-28)?

12. Rachels says, “There are some moral rules that all societies must have in common, because those rules are necessary for society to exist” (7). Explain the examples he uses to support this claim.

13. King describes how Black Americans were treated under segregation (King, 14). What would a moral skeptic say about whether that treatment was wrong? What would a moral subjectivist say? A cultural relativist (remember this one is tricky)?

14. King says (15) that we should obey just laws but disobey unjust laws. How does King believe we can objectively distinguish just laws from unjust laws (King, 16)? (Discussing King's examples from paragraphs 17-19 may be helpful, but it is most important to explain what he says in paragraph 16.)

15. In class we raised several worries about Rachels (specifically, his claim that some rules are justified because society cannot exist without them, 7; Phil 230, 2024-08-28) and about King's claims re how we can objectively distinguish just laws from unjust laws (King, 16; Phil 230, 2024-08-30). Explain one of our worries about Rachels, and one of our worries about King.

16. Aquinas argues for ethical objectivism by saying that human nature -- specifically, our natural "inclinations" -- provide an objective basis for judging human laws. What inclinations does Aquinas identify (Summa, 4-5; Aquinas explainer video; Aquinas chart)? What "precepts of natural law" does Aquinas derive from these inclinations?

17. What does it mean to say that the precepts of natural law are vague, and that they are authoritative? What are the implications of them being vague, and of them being authoritative? Give an example to illustrate how the precepts might be used to criticize a society's law. (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video and chart will be helpful here.)

Situation: Adara and Beth disagree. Adara is a conservative Saudi Muslim. Adara criticizes Beth for dressing immodestly because Beth does not cover her face or legs in public. Beth is an American liberal atheist. Beth criticizes Adara for wanting to restrict women’s freedom by dictating how they dress.

18. Explain how Aquinas might try to resolve the dispute between Adara and Beth. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? (What’s most important here is to give a sense of how Aquinas would approach this matter. The more thoroughly you can answer the other Aquinas questions, the better you will be able to explain that.)

Note: Exam questions with highlighted numbers (like this: 18. ) will be worth more points on exams because they call for more extensive, thoughtful answers. Some might also ask you to present and support your own opinion.

19. Glaucon and Socrates agree there are three ways things can be good (Plato, 36). Explain what each is, and give two examples of each. Which kind of good is justice, according to Socrates?

Note: Material helpful for answering the following Glaucon questions can be found in the Glaucon's Challenge slides PDF, which is stored with class notes.

20. Glaucon describes "what justice is like and what its origins are" (Plato, 37-8). What four claims about human nature does he make there (or, rather, what four claims does he think most people believe)?

21. Suppose Glaucon's four claims about human nature are true. What, then, does Glaucon think we should do (Plato, 37)? What, then, is Glaucon implying about us & society?

22. In class we discussed what people would do if they had a ring that would enable them to do as they please. What were the results -- would people generally behave justly or not? How does that relate to Glaucon's claims about human nature?


 
Exam questions about Consequentialist Ethics

23. What four things must all consequentialist ethical theories do? Why, then, can there be many different consequentialist theories? In your answer, be sure to identify the different possible kinds of accounts of the good, and at least three possible scopes.

24. How does classical utilitarianism (CU) do the four things consequentialist theories do? Why does Bentham think CU's account of the good is correct (Bentham, 1)?

25. State the Principle of Utility, either from class or from Bentham's or Mill’s text (Bentham, 1; Mill calls it the “Greatest Happiness Principle,” 1). What steps should you take to apply the PU properly? (Bentham's description in section V on pp. 3-4 may be helpful here.)

26. Explain why classical utilitarianism has the "accounting problem" we discussed in class. How does Bentham respond to the problem (Bentham, 4, section VI)?

27. How does Bentham think we should rank pleasures? Take two very different activities (maybe, eating Cheetos or watching a film like Parasite) and explain how Bentham's scheme might rank them, using the factors he mentions (Bentham, 3). (Well, you don't have to use all; let's say, use four of them). Which of your two activities would be better, according to Bentham's scheme, and why?

28. What is the "swine problem"? In other words, why has utilitarianism been accused of being "a doctrine worthy only of swine" (Mill, 1)?

29. Discuss Mill's response to utilitarianism's swine problem. In doing so, be sure to answer the following questions. What are the pleasures Mill says that only humans can enjoy (Mill, 1)? How does he try to show that we value them much more highly (Mill, 2)? If Mill were right, how might that solve the swine problem? Finally, in class we noted a problem -- the "snob problem" -- with Mill's response to the swine problem. What was it?

30. Explain Mill's response to utilitarianism's "accounting problem." What does he say most of us may do, most of the time? How does Mill argue that some people have more responsibility than others (Mill, 5)? (In class this was compared to the Spider-Man Rule.) Finally, what concerns were raised in class about Mill's solution?

31. Nozick gives three reasons why, he says, we would not plug in to the Experience Machine (Nozick, 43-4). What are they? If Nozick is right that we would not plug in to the Experience Machine and he is right about why we would not, how would that suggest a serious problem with classical utilitarianism? (The argument from the explainer video or the one given in class 2024-09-18 will be useful here.)

32. Explain why utilitarianism would say that, in Le Guin’s imaginary city of Omelas, it is morally right for the citizens to persecute the child. Why does this make utilitarianism objectionable?

33. What is the "moral conservative" objection to consequentialism (Nielsen, 219)? What is Nielsen's strategy for countering this objection (Phil 230, 2024-09-20)?

34. Moral conservatives have used magistrate-type cases to object to consequentialism, saying consequentialism requires deliberately harming the innocent. How does Nielsen argue that moral conservatives need not object to consequentialism for how it deals with the magistrate case (223-5)? (Be sure to discuss what he says could happen if word gets out, 224.) How satisfactory is Nielsen's argument here? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.

35. Nielsen says consequentialism may clearly require harming the innocent sometimes -- for example, in the case of the fat man stuck in the cave mouth (Nielsen, 222). How does he argue that this is the right thing to do here (228-30)? (Your answer will be better if you discuss how Nielsen responds to concerns about callousness, using the fat man as a means, and/or inhumanity, 228-30.) How satisfactory is his argument regarding this case? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.

Advice: some exam questions, like #36 and 37, ask you to give your opinion and your reasons for it. The best strategy is to:
  • First, before giving your opinion, explain what the author says in as much detail as you can. That will give you things to think critically about. You might agree with part of it but disagree with another part, for example.
  • Second, when giving your opinion, give independent reasons; don't just echo the author's own reasons. For example, saying "I agree with Nielsen in the fat man case because blowing the fat man up is the lesser evil" does not demonstrate any thought on your part. If you can't say much more than that, I suggest answering a different question.
36. Define the following terms as they are used in this course: “act utilitarianism,” “rule utilitarianism” (Hospers, 4, 10; class notes).

37. Hospers presents several cases at the beginning of his article: a student seeking an undeserved grade; a thief seeking to be released; a district attorney considering whether to keep an innocent man in jail; and voting (Hospers, 1-2, 3-4). Pick just one of these cases, and explain why act-utilitarianism would recommend one choice while rule-utilitarianism would recommend a different choice in that case.

38. According to Hospers, what is wrong with having simple rules such as “do not lie” or “do not kill” (Hospers, 8-9, 10)? Why does he think it would also be bad to have rules like this: “do not ____, except where it will do the most good” (11-2)? What sort of rules does Hospers think we should have (9)?

39. What is "negative responsibility" (Williams, 2)? (He calls it "negative responsibility" or the "strong doctrine of negative responsibility.") Suppose Jim decides not to shoot one Indian when Pedro invites him to (cf. Williams, 3-4). According to consequentialism, how responsible is Jim when Pedro orders twenty Indians shot?

40. According to Williams, what does utilitarianism say George should do about that job offer from the weapons lab (Williams, 3-4)? Why does Williams believe utilitarianism is wrong about that (2-3, 4-9)? (Yes this is challenging. Your answer should include the things Williams thinks are important in this case: negative responsibility (2-3), the impact of other people’s decisions on our projects and happiness (4-7), and the impact on our integrity (7-9).)

41. How do we normally think about giving money to help needy people? Why does Singer say we are mistaken (235)?

42. What is Singer's "moderate" principle (Singer, 231)? How does Singer use the example of the child drowning in the pond to illustrate this principle?

43. Singer says that following even his "moderate" principle could affect "consumer society" (241-2). What impact does he say it might have? Why does he think that could be a good thing (in addition to helping the needy)?

44. Among Harwood’s eleven objections to utilitarianism are that it fails to take promises seriously enough (#4; Harwood, 6-7); it overstates our duties to animals (#8; Harwood, 11); and that it panders to bigots and sadists (#9; Harwood, 11-2). Pick one of these objections and explain it. That is, put the objection in your own words and make clear how it is supposed to give us a reason to reject utilitarianism.

45. When we discussed Harwood's objections to utilitarianism, we noted some moral intuitions that many of us share (Phil 230, 2024-10-04). What were they? How might they conflict with each other?

46. Harrison and Harwood (in Harwood, 9-10) object to rule utilitarianism (RU) on several grounds. In class, they were presented as a dilemma: either (a) RU always says to do the same thing as act utilitarianism, or (b) at least sometimes RU says to do something different from AU; but RU is objectionable either way. Explain this dilemma, being sure to make clear why both (a) and (b) are said to imply problems for RU.



Exam questions about Deontological Ethics

47. Lecture discussed three types of deontological ethical theories, which were distinguished by how we are supposed to know what is right and wrong (Phil 230, 2024-10-07). Identify and define these three types. Of these three, which type is Kant's theory?

48. Kant says this about the nature of moral laws and the proper sort of justification for them:

That there must be [a “pure” moral] philosophy is evident from the common idea of duty and of moral laws. Everyone must admit that if a law is to be morally valid, i.e., is to be valid as a ground of obligation, then it must carry with it absolute necessity. He must admit that the command, “Thou shalt not lie,” does not hold only for men, as if other rational beings had no need to abide by it, and so with all the other moral laws properly so called. And he must concede that the ground of obligation here must therefore be sought not in the world in which man is placed, but must be sought...solely in the concepts of pure reason.... (Kant, 1).

Without using Kant's words, explain how Kant argues here that the justification of moral laws must come from pure reason.
 
49. What does Kant mean by "rational being"? Why are rational beings not necessarily human? Why are some human beings not rational beings?

50. Given Kant's commitment to justifying moral laws by pure reason, explain why Kant would reject some other views we have looked at in the course. Pick two of the following: (a) cultural relativism; (b) Aquinas' natural law theory, (c) Glaucon's social contract; or (d) Bentham & Mill's classical utilitarianism. Identify specific things about each one you choose that would lead Kant to reject it.

51. Lecture discussed why our motives are important in ethics (Phil 230, 2024-10-09). Give an example of some action, and explain how having different motives could make that act morally right or morally wrong. (You could use an example from class such as your boss giving you a gift, or make up another example.)

52. According to Kant, what are "inclinations" (Phil 230, 2024-10-09)? Why does Kant say that there may never have been a truly moral action (7)? To answer this, it will help to explain how inclinations are (a) unreliable guides (Kant, 1) and (b) sneaky (that is, unseen by us; 7). Finally, what does Kant think a truly moral action would be?

53. Explain how Kant's categorical imperative (specifically the formula of universal law) logically follows from the idea of a moral law (Phil 230, 2024-10-21). Then, using either Kant's wording or our rewording of it, state Kant's formula of universal law.

54. What is a maxim? (Feldman discusses maxims on pp. 2-4, but the discussion in class 2024-10-21 may be more accessible, and class notes include a definition.)  Give 2 examples of maxims.

55. In class we discussed the maxim, "When I need money I shall rob a bank to get it." Why does this maxim fail? (In other words, explain why it fails the 4-stage test given in class 2024-10-23.)

56. Kant gives an example of someone making a false promise (Kant, 11, #2, from "Another man in need..."). Using the 4-stage test given in class (2024-10-23), explain how to test it. According to Kant, why does this maxim fail?

57. Lecture included Kant's example of refusing to give to the needy (Kant, 11-12, #4, from "A fourth man..."). What is the maxim in this case? According to Kant, why does it fail? How is the failure here different from the failure in the false promise case?

Situation (from Williams, 3): George’s lack of a job is causing trouble at home. He is offered a research job at a chemical and biological weapons lab. Taking the job would be against his conscience because he opposes such weapons. But if George does not take the job, Zed will take it and enthusiastically pursue the research, creating more horrible ways to kill people.

58. Using the four-stage test for applying Kant’s formula of universal law, determine whether George may take the job. To do this well you will have to explain each stage carefully, especially stage 3 and, if you get to it, 4.

59. According to lecture, what would we like to get from any good ethical theory?

60. Feldman criticizes Kant’s formula of universal law for permitting some actions which really are wrong (Feldman, 14-5). Give an example of this--one of Feldman’s, or one from class, or your own--and explain how it demonstrates this problem.

61. Feldman argues that Kant’s formula of universal law is faulty because it forbids some actions which really should be allowed (Feldman, 15). Give an example of this, and explain how it demonstrates this problem. (The example may be one of Feldman’s, or one from class, or your own.)

62. State Kant's categorical imperative, in the formula of humanity (FH) (Kant, 14; O'Neill, 259). Second, explain what Kant means by "humanity" here. Finally, identify the two distinct duties the FH implies we have (cf. O'Neill, 260).

63. According to Kant, what is the difference between treating someone as a means and treating them merely (or "simply") as means (O'Neill, §20, pp. 260-2)? What makes it OK to treat others as means, but not OK to treat them merely as means? (It may be helpful to consider some of O'Neill's examples regarding needy people.)

64. According to Kant, what does it mean to treat humanity in ourselves and others as an end (O'Neill, §21, p. 262)? What sorts of things might this require us to do, or not do?

65. Suppose you are wondering whether you may rob a bank. Would Kant's Formula of Humanity say that you may? Explain how come. Then, suppose you are wondering whether you may withdraw all your money from the bank when the stock market reaches 40,000. Again, would Kant's formula of Humanity permit this? Explain how come.

66. Using the example of choosing whether to keep a lunch date or give aid to an accident victim (Ross, 2), explain what Ross thinks is wrong with Kantianism.

67. Using the example of choosing whether to keep a lunch date or aid an accident victim (Ross, 2), explain what Ross thinks is wrong with utilitarianism. Be sure to explain how Ross thinks that utilitarianism oversimplifies our relationships with others, and how Ross's view is different (2-3).

68. Given the problems he identifies with Kantianism and utilitarianism, what features does Ross think a proper ethical theory should have (Phil 230, 2024-11-01)?

69. According to Ross, what does it mean to say our duties are "prima facie"(Ross, 3, 5; Phil 230, 2024-11-01)? Identify six of our prima facie duties, according to Ross (3-4).

70. How does Ross think we should decide what to do (3; notes from 2024-11-04 will be helpful)?

Situation: See the situation described above for question 60 (George's job offer).

71. Suppose you are George. Adopt Ross’s ethical theory. Explain whether you would take the job or not, and how you would arrive at that decision. To do so, first identify at least three relationships that are salient in this situation, and at least three prima facie duties arising from those relationships which apply in this situation. (By "at least three" I mean that is the minimum for a passable answer.) Given those duties to those people, and considering the situation, which duty (or duties) is overriding in this case? Why?


 
Exam questions about Virtue Ethics

72. In class we discussed reasons why we do bad things (see 2024-11-13 class notes). Identify three of them. Explain why they are failures of character rather than failures of our ethical theories. How does this suggest that virtue theory has some value?

73. Describe the "parts" Plato thinks the soul has (Plato, 129-31). According to Plato, what is the proper division of labor among the soul's parts (131)--in other words, what 'job' should each part do?

74. Describe Plato's ideal soul. What are the virtues it would have, and which part(s) have them?

75. According to Aristotle, why is happiness super important in ethics (Aristotle 1-2)? In your explanation, include why Aristotle thinks happiness is "complete," "self-sufficient," and the "most choiceworthy" good. Why, then, is it important to know what happiness is (Aristotle, 1)?

76. According to Aristotle, what is human happiness (3)? How does Aristotle figure that out? (I.e., explain the function argument, pp. 2-3.)

77. Aristotle explains why "what is true of crafts is not true of virtue" (4). How do they differ? What three criteria does Aristotle say must be met for you to be "in the right state" (4)?

78. Aristotle defines virtue as a state where (a) you decide and your decision (b) consists of a mean; and this mean (c) is relative to you, (d) is defined by reason, and (e) is a mean between two vices -- one of excess and one of deficiency (Aristotle, 6). Pick one of the virtues Aristotle discusses on pp. 7-8. For each point (a) through (e), explain how that virtue would exemplify that point. (You might think of it this way: explain how each of those points is manifested in a person who possesses that virtue.) Finally, explain why someone is likely to be better off behaving along the mean than not.

79. Given Aristotle's conception of virtue, explain why becoming a virtuous person is very challenging. In other words, why does Aristotle say "it is...hard work to be excellent" (9)?
