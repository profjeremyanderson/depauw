About the exam questions

All exams will consist of some of these questions.

Questions that appear on the mid-term exam will be struck out so they look like this and will not be used again. Any other questions, even those that may have appeared on individual make-up exams, may appear on the next exam.

When new exam questions are posted here (roughly weekly), I will email you via Moodle. If you don't receive notifications, email me and let me know.

Resources for giving good answers

As noted on the syllabus, I offer several kinds of help for you to give good answers:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • The Sample Answers web page has answers to some exam questions and advice on how to write better answers.
  • Some notes and exercises from class will be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups -- in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be review sessions before each exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week. When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.

When you have trouble answering questions, ask me for help.

Academic integrity on exams
  • For exams given in class, no citing or quoting is required. You many not access books or notes of any kind during the exam.

  • For take-home exams (which we will have if class is taught remotely):
    • You may use any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with other people on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions will apply just as in papers and reading responses. Cite all sources you use and put others’ words in quotation marks.


Exam questions about Ethical Theory (our first day)

1. In class we listed many reasons why one should, or should not, hook up with a stranger when their significant other is away. These reasons fell into three main groups. Identify the three groups and, for each one, identify one of the reasons in that group. Name the type of ethical theory which corresponds to each group.



Exam questions about the basis of moral rules

2. Cephalus believes that justice is being truthful and giving back what you have taken from another (Plato, 5). Why does Socrates reject this definition?

3. In class we saw that Cephalus, Socrates, and Polemarchus hold what we called conventional assumptions about justice (Plato, 5-14 passim; Phil 230, 2022-08-26). What are these assumptions?

4. Explain what Thrasymachus means when he says that “justice is nothing other than what is advantageous for the stronger” (Plato, 15; Phil 230, 2022-08-26). How does this challenge conventional assumptions about justice?

5. Thrasymachus defends his moral skepticism by arguing that being just does not benefit you--rather, it is bad for you--and that "justice" is really a sort of scam used to exploit the weak. What evidence does Thrasymachus give for this (Plato, 15-22, especially 21-22)?

6. Define these terms as they are used in this course: “moral skepticism,” “moral subjectivism,” “cultural relativism,” and “ethical objectivism” (Phil 230, 2022-08-29).

7. Benedict describes two main kinds of cultural differences. One kind is where people we have labeled "abnormal" and unfit have a respected (though not necessarily central) place in other societies (Benedict, 1-2). Explain one of Benedict's examples of that kind of difference.

8. Benedict says, "The most spectacular illustrations of the extent to which normality may be culturally defined are those cultures where an abnormality of our culture is the cornerstone of their social structure" (3). First, explain what this sentence means. Then, pick one of the cultures she uses to illustrate this point (3-4), and explain how that culture's practices are supposed to support Benedict's claim.

9. Explain how Benedict uses her examples of differences to support cultural relativism. In class, this was summarized in "Benedict's argument"; state that argument.

10. State the "Cultural Differences Argument" (Rachels, 3). Why does Rachels think it is bad (3-4)?

11. Rachels says, “There are some moral rules that all societies must have in common, because those rules are necessary for society to exist” (7). Explain the examples he uses to support this claim.

12. According to Rachels, there is a culture-neutral standard for telling whether moral rules are justified (Rachels, 9). What is it?

13. King describes how Black Americans were treated under segregation (King, 14). What would a moral skeptic say about whether that treatment was wrong? What would a moral subjectivist say? A cultural relativist (remember this one is tricky)?

14. King says we should obey just laws but disobey unjust laws (15). How does King believe we can we objectively distinguish just laws from unjust laws (King, 16)? (Discussing King's examples from paragraphs 17-19 may be helpful, but it is most important to explain what he says in paragraph 16.)

15. Benedict observes individual cultures and finds differences among them. How does Aquinas differ? (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video will be helpful here.)

16. Aquinas argues for ethical objectivism by saying that human nature -- specifically, our natural inclinations -- provide an objective basis for judging human laws. What are the inclinations Aquinas identifies (Summa, 4-5; Aquinas explainer video, Aquinas chart)? What precepts of natural law does Aquinas derive from these inclinations?

17. What does it mean to say that the precepts of natural law are vague, and that they are authoritative? What are the implications of them being vague, and being authoritative? Give an example to illustrate how the precepts might be used to criticize a society's law. (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video and chart will be helpful here.)

Situation: Adara and Beth disagree. Adara is a conservative Saudi Muslim. Adara criticizes Beth for dressing immodestly because Beth does not cover her face or legs in public. Beth is an American liberal atheist. Beth criticizes Adara for wanting to restrict women’s freedom by dictating how they dress.

18. Explain how Aquinas might try to resolve the dispute between Adara and Beth. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? (What’s most important here is to give a sense of how Aquinas would approach this matter. The more thoroughly you can answer the other Aquinas questions, the better you will be able to explain that.)

Note: Exam questions with highlighted numbers (like this: 18. ) will be worth more points on exams because they call for more extensive, thoughtful answers. Some might also ask you to present and support your own opinion.

19. Glaucon and Socrates agree there are three ways things can be good (Plato, 36). Explain what each is, and give two examples of each. (You may use examples from the book or class discussion, but you will get more points if you come up with your own unique examples.) Which kind of good is justice, according to Socrates?

Note: Material helpful for answering the following Glaucon questions can be found in the Glaucon's Challenge slides PDF, which is stored with class notes.

20. Glaucon describes "what justice is like and what its origins are" (Plato, 37-8). What four claims about human nature does he make there (or, rather, what four claims does he think "the many" believe)?

21. Suppose Glaucon's four claims about human nature are true. What, then, does Glaucon think we should do (Plato, 37)? What, then, is Glaucon implying about us & society?

22. In class we discussed what people would do if they had a ring that would enable them to do as they please. What were the results -- would people generally behave justly or not? How does that relate to Glaucon's claims about human nature?

 
Exam questions about Consequentialist Ethics

23. What four things must all consequentialist ethical theories do? Why, then, can there be many different consequentialist theories? In your answer, be sure to identify the different possible kinds of accounts of the good, and at least three possible scopes.

24. How does classical utilitarianism (CU) do the four things consequentialist theories do? Why does Bentham think CU's account of the good is correct?

25. State the Principle of Utility, either from class or from Bentham's or Mill’s text (Bentham, 1; Mill calls it the “Greatest Happiness Principle,” 1). What steps should you take to apply the PU properly? (Bentham's description in section V on pp. 3-4 may be helpful here.)

26. Explain why classical utilitarianism has the "accounting problem" we discussed in class. How does Bentham respond to the problem (Bentham, 4, section VI)?

27. Why does classical utilitarianism need a principled way rank pleasures?

28. How does Bentham think we should rank pleasures? Take two very different activities (maybe, eating Cheetos or watching a film like Parasite) and explain how Bentham's scheme might rank them, using the parameters he mentions (Bentham, 3). (Well, you don't have to use all; let's say, use four of them). Which of your two activities would be better, according to Bentham's scheme, and why?

29. What is the "swine problem"? In other words, why has utilitarianism been accused of being "a doctrine worthy only of swine" (Mill, 1)? (Remember Mother Teresa and the Rolling Stones.)

30. Discuss Mill's response to utilitarianism's swine problem. In doing so, be sure to answer the following questions. What are the pleasures Mill says that only humans can enjoy (Mill, 1)? How does he argue that we value them much more highly (Mill, 2)? If Mill were right, how might that solve the swine problem? Finally, in class we noted some problems with Mill's response to the swine problem. What were they?

31. Explain Mill's response to utilitarianism's "accounting problem." How does Mill argue that some people have more responsibility than others (Mill, 5)? (In class this was compared to the Spider-Man Rule.)

32. Nozick gives three reasons why, he says, we would not plug in to the Experience Machine (Nozick, 43-4). What are they?

33. If Nozick is right that we would not plug in to the Experience Machine and he is right about why we would not, how would that show a serious problem with classical utilitarianism? 

34. Explain why utilitarianism would conclude that, in Le Guin’s imaginary city of Omelas, it is morally right for the citizens to persecute the child. Why does this make utilitarianism objectionable?

35. What is the "moral conservative" objection to consequentialism (Nielsen, 219; class notes)? What is Nielsen's strategy for countering this objection?

36. Moral conservatives have used magistrate-type cases to argue that consequentialism should be rejected because it requires deliberately harming the innocent. How does Nielsen argue that moral conservatives need not reject consequentialism for how it deals with the magistrate case (223-5)? (Be sure to discuss what he says could happen if word gets out, 224.) How satisfactory is Nielsen's argument here? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.

37. Nielsen says consequentialism may clearly require harming the innocent sometimes -- for example, in the case of the fat man stuck in the cave mouth (Nielsen, 222). How does he argue that this is the right thing to do here (228-30)? (Your answer will be better if you include how Nielsen responds to concerns about callousness, using the fat man as a means, and inhumanity, 228-30.) How satisfactory is his argument regarding this case? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.

Advice: some exam questions, like #39 and 40, ask you to give your opinion and your reasons for it. The best strategy is to -- first, before giving your opinion -- explain what the author says in as much detail as you can. That will give you things to think critically about. You might agree with part of it, and disagree with another part, for example. Second, when giving your opinion, try to give independent reasons, don't just echo the author's own reasons. For example, saying "I agree with Nielsen in the fat man case because blowing the fat man up is the lesser evil" does not show much thought on your part. If you can't say much more than that, I recommend answering different question.

38. Define the following terms as they are used in this course: “act utilitarianism,” “rule utilitarianism” (Hospers, 4, 10; class notes).

39. Hospers presents several cases at the beginning of his article: a student seeking an undeserved grade; a thief seeking to be released; a district attorney considering whether to keep an innocent man in jail; and voting (Hospers, 1-2, 3-4). Pick one of these cases, and explain why act-utilitarianism would recommend one choice while rule-utilitarianism would recommend a different choice.

40. What is "negative responsibility" (Williams, 2)? (He calls it "negative responsibility" or the "strong doctrine of negative responsibility.") Suppose Jim decides not to shoot one Indian when Pedro invites him to (cf. Williams, 3-4). According to consequentialism, how responsible is Jim when Pedro orders twenty Indians shot?

41. According to Williams, what does utilitarianism say George should do about that job offer from the weapons lab (Williams, 3-4)? Why does Williams believe utilitarianism is wrong about that (2-3, 4-9)? (Yes this is challenging. Ideally your answer will involve the notions that Williams thinks are important in this case: negative responsibility (2-3), the impact of other people’s decisions on our projects and happiness (4-7), and integrity (7-9).)

42. How do we normally think about giving money to help needy people? Why does Singer say we are mistaken (235)?

43. Singer states a moral principle, but gives two versions of it: a "strong" one and a "moderate" one (Singer, 231). What is the "strong" one? How is it more demanding (requires more sacrifice) than the "moderate" one?

44. What is Singer's "moderate" principle (Singer, 231)? Why does he think it implies we should do much more to aid needy people around the world than we currently do (235-6)?

45. Among Harwood’s eleven objections to utilitarianism are that it fails to take promises seriously enough (#4; Harwood, 6-7); it overstates our duties to animals (#8; Harwood, 11); and that it panders to bigots and sadists (#9; Harwood, 11-2). Pick one of these objections and explain it. That is, put the objection in your own words and make clear how it is supposed to give us a reason to reject utilitarianism.
 
46. Harrison and Harwood (in Harwood, 9-10) object to rule utilitarianism (RU) on several grounds. In class, they were combined into a dilemma: either (a) RU always says to do the same thing as act utilitarianism, or (b) at least sometimes RU does not, but RU is objectionable either way. Explain this dilemma, being sure to make clear why both (a) and (b) are supposed to be problems for RU.



Exam questions about Deontological Ethics

47. Lecture discussed three types of deontological ethical theories, which were distinguished by how we are supposed to know what is right and wrong. Identify and define these three types. Of these three, which type is Kant's theory?

48. Kant says this about the nature of moral laws and the proper sort of justification for them:

That there must be [a “pure” moral] philosophy is evident from the common idea of duty and of moral laws. Everyone must admit that if a law is to be morally valid, i.e., is to be valid as a ground of obligation, then it must carry with it absolute necessity. He must admit that the command, “Thou shalt not lie,” does not hold only for men, as if other rational beings had no need to abide by it, and so with all the other moral laws properly so called. And he must concede that the ground of obligation here must therefore be sought not in the world in which man is placed, but must be sought...solely in the concepts of pure reason.... (Kant, 1).

Without using Kant's words, explain how Kant argues here that the justification of moral laws must come from pure reason.
 
49. What does Kant mean by "rational being"? Why are rational beings not necessarily human? Why are some human beings not rational beings?

50. Given Kant's commitment to justifying moral laws by pure reason, explain why Kant would reject some other views we have looked at in the course. Pick two of the following: (a) cultural relativism; (b) Aquinas' natural law theory, (c) Glaucon's social contract; or (d) Bentham & Mill's classical utilitarianism. Identify specific things about those two that would lead Kant to reject it.
