Here are the steps for the presentation assignment:


1. Pick a topic and an alternate

You may present on any topic within the field of ethics, except for texts I've covered in class.

That leaves a lot of possibilities:
  • A topic from our course that we have not looked at
  • A topic not covered in our course; there are tons more out there.
Pick a topic that interests you! Doing something you're not interested in will bore you, then you'll do a boring presentation and get a distressing grade. Write or come see me to discuss your interests and get ideas about ethical topics that relate to them. There are likely ethical topics related to your major or minor or other things you find important.

Please pick an alternate topic, too, in case somebody else claims your first choice first.


2. Form a group

Groups of 2 or 3 are ideal. (With larger groups it is much harder for each person to earn a high grade.) You can use the Class Open Forum on Moodle to search for partners.


3. Get your topic approved and pick a date

Once you have formed a group and agreed on a topic, send me an e-mail declaring:

1. Who's in your group
2. Your first-choice topic
3. At least one alternate topic

The deadline to email me is 6:00pm Monday October 23 (Monday after Fall Break).

I will call on groups to pick presentation dates in the order you get your topics approved. So the sooner you get your group & topic approved, the better your choice of dates to present.


4. Do your research

Good research is vital to your success. Learn as much as you can about your topic to present it effectively to a group of people who are as smart and critical as you are.

Class materials do not count as research. While you may refer to them in your work--and it can be good to relate what you're doing to what we've already discussed--your work should mostly or entirely come from other sources.

Getting started

First, see me. Your group should meet with me at least twice to discuss your plans.

Identify the names of the people and/or the topics you are presenting about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the info from reference sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and and databases to find scholarly material on your topic. (Here are all the databases, and here are some philosophy-focused ones, plus some online philosophy encyclopedias). Google Scholar is good, too, and has the familiar Google interface. (Generally avoid plain Google; too much garbage there.)

Choose your sources wisely
  • Best: peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields. The more of these you can use, the better.

  • Worst: websites or other writings of unknown origin, sources with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications or no qualifications. Relying on these can seriously hurt your project because they may give you over-simplified, false, or misleading information.

    • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, that's a strong reason not to use that source or, at least ask whether it's OK to use.

    • At least for now, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a very risky source. AI's are unreliable. They use bad sources, are biased, and make false claims. Only only use material from an AI when you can verify it is accurate (which makes AI pretty useless; generally best to avoid it). But if you use it, you of course must disclose it like any other source.

  • In the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are good places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia, since some of its articles are just abysmal and its overall quality is unreliable). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources are good to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but relying much on such sources for your presentation may count against it.
DePauw's Library has many great books and some bad ones. The internet, generally speaking, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, among lots of trash.

See me if you have any questions about what source materials to use.


5. Find some assignment(s) for the class and create at least one reading question for the class to respond to.

The day of your presentation will be a regular class meeting, so we'll need an assignment from you to prepare for it, and reading questions to respond to.

As you do your research, look for things that might be good to assign: some thing, or things, that will get the class thinking about your topic and introduce us to some of the things you'll talk about. You may assign texts, films, clips, music, or other media.

Good reading questions will focus on the parts of your assignment you plan to discuss in your presentation. You might ask students to explain them and maybe also to react to them.

Please get your assignment and reading questions to me by three calendar days before your presentation date so I can make it available to the class.


6. Prepare your presentation

Get help. See me at least twice well before your presentation date to discuss plans and get some coaching. Also, it would be good to schedule an appointment to consult with DePauw's Speaking and Listening Center.

Divide the work roughly evenly among group members. This applies to research, preparation, presenting, and handling discussion.

How long should it be? Plan your group's presentation to take roughly 10-15 minutes per member in a group presentation, about 20-25 minutes in a solo presentation. Good presentations often go longer, but passable ones are seldom shorter. You might...
  • Have each group member present a different aspect or side of your topic, or
  • Stage a debate, with group members opposing each other, or
  • Make a film or include video clips on your topic, or
  • Do something completely different. Creativity is good; just imitating what others have done can get old.
Demonstrate appreciation of alternative views on your topic, based on your research, and be fair. By "fair" I do not mean making them all look equally good. I mean not presenting any view or argument as less (or more) good than it really is. We're searching for truth here, not just trying to score points or do a simplistic "He said, she said" job. You may take a side if you wish, but not by misrepresenting or ignoring opposition.

Visual aids (slides, brief handouts, etc.) are encouraged but not required.

The most important thing is to demonstrate you understand your material. If you know your stuff, you do not need PowerPoint. (Of course you may use it, but you shouldn't need it.) If you do not know your stuff, no technology will make up for it.

Have some questions prepared for the class to get discussion going and keep it going.


Documentation and deadlines

Document your work. Without your documentation, the assignment is incomplete and violates the late policy.

Personal documentation

There are no group grades. Each group member is graded individually, so each of you must turn in written evidence of what you contributed and what sources you relied on.

By 6pm the day before your presentation, send me an e-mail or Word doc or Google Doc like this sample here (or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations) with:
  • A brief -- seriously, a quarter page is ample -- statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was. (If you are presenting all by yourself, there is no need for this part.)

  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, divided into two parts:
    • Sources used in the presentation, and
    • Sources consulted but not used.
Use a standard bibliographic format, and give the URL for any online sources. (When available, use the "stable URL" or "persistent URL" or "Permalink.")
  • A quick guide to citation is here on the Library web page. (I like Turabian.)
Documentation in slides, handouts, etc.

Slides, handouts, etc. need to document sources, too, using a standard bibliographic format.

Turn your slides in, with sources documented, by 6pm on the day you present.

Endnotes are a good way to document sources: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in your slides that comes from some source -- image, claim, quote, whatever -- put the number of the source by it.

DePauw's Academic Integrity Policy applies to this assignment. Your documentation -- personal, and slides etc. -- must include all the sources you relied on in any way whether you quote them or not.

Presenting work done outside this course (for example, work already submitted for credit in this or another course, or done for some extracurricular activity) is not allowed. Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be OK, but discuss it with me beforehand. Any work done outside this course, if you are allowed to use it at all, must also be documented.

Failing to document any source will automatically harm your grade and may be charged as plagiarism, even if it is accidental. Keep track of your sources!

Rubric: how to do well at this assignment.

I want to see evidence of substantial, new work by each person in each group. Just downloading a web page and reading it to the class or copying it onto a PowerPoint slide, for example, is not acceptable. You need to show that you've done significantly more with the information than merely acquiring it.

Alternative assignment for unusual situations: Write Another Paper (one requiring research).

Sometimes it may be impractical or impossible to do a presentation. Then the alternative is to write a 1500-2000 word (5-6 page, not counting notes or bibliography) paper.

Here's what that would look like:

Topic. Negotiate the topic with me. I have veto power. Of course it will have to be something within ethics. If you had been preparing to do a presentation on some topic I already approved, you may do your paper on that topic. You could choose one of the topics we've studied in this course, but, as with presentations, I strongly encourage you to propose topics you're really interested in. As with presentations, let's try to make this an important learning experience for you.

Fairness. As with the presentations, I expect you to present more than one side of an issue, and be fair to all sides. That does not mean making them all look equally good. It means showing a real appreciation of the strengths as well as the weaknesses of those you disagree with.

Requirements for all papers in this course (including academic integrity, format, and style) apply. Please review them here.

Research. Unlike other papers in this course, this paper requires research just like the presentation does. So, as with the presentation, choose your sources wisely. Your job is to learn what other smart people have said about your topic, and write about it in an organized way.

Meet with me. Meet with me early to discuss your topic and find sources to learn about it. Then meet with me later on, but well before the deadline, to discuss how things are going and go over a draft or outline of the paper.

Deadline: 6pm the day of our last class meeting or, if your presentation was scheduled for the last week of class, 6pm of the first day of finals week. After that, the syllabus late policy applies.

I also encourage you to take advantage of the free assistance with writing provided by the Writing Center. Go to their web page to set up an appointment.
