Assignment 14: Dream Doubt and Defective Nature Doubt.
  • Re-read FP 144-6: Descartes, First Meditation; pay special attention to 145-6.
  • Chuang-tzu, “Dreams”:
Dreams
Chuang-tzu

Those who dream of a banquet may wake to lamentation and sorrow. Those who dream of lamentation and sorrow may wake to join a hunt. While they dream, they do not know that they dream. Some will even experience a dream within a dream; and only when they awake do they realize they dreamed of a dream. By and by comes the great awakening, and then we may find out that this life is really an extended dream. Fools think they are awake now, and flatter themselves they know if they are really princes or peasants. Confucius and you are both dreams; and I who say you are dreams—I am but a dream myself. This is a paradox. Tomorrow a wise man may come forward to explain it; but that tomorrow will not be until ten thousand generations have gone by.

Once upon a time, I, Chuang Tzu, dreamt I was a butterfly, fluttering hither and thither, to all intents and purposes a butterfly. I was conscious only of following my fancies as a butterfly, and was unconscious of my individuality as a man. Suddenly I awoke, and there I lay, myself again. Now I do not know whether I was then a man dreaming I was a butterfly, or whether I am now a butterfly dreaming I am a man.
     
(This excerpt is borrowed from Humanistic Texts.)

Reading questions:
A. In the first paragraph of p. 145 Descartes says, "I see plainly that there are never any sure signs by means of which being awake can be distinguished from being asleep." In other words, he is saying there is no absolutely sure way to tell whether you are awake or asleep. This is the conclusion of an argument; what are the premises for it? Try to put the argument in standard form. Is the argument inductive or deductive?
B. If Descartes is right that we cannot tell for sure whether we are dreaming or awake, what would that imply about our knowledge of material things? Why?
C. On p. 145-6, Descartes argues that he may be wrong even about the simplest things, such as that 2+3=5. Can you explain his argument, or put it in standard form? (The argument starts on 145 at "And yet firmly rooted in my mind is the long-standing opinion..." and ends on 146 with "there is not one of my former beliefs about which a doubt may not properly be raised....")

    This video briefly introduces one of Berkeley's central ideas: "esse est percipi" (Latin for "to be is to be perceived") -- that is, that the existence of material things consists solely in their being perceived. (Open Culture has links to other interesting videos, BTW.)

Assignment 30: Ayer's compatibilism.
Read: FP pp. 556-63: Ayer introductory material and "Freedom and Necessity."

Note: in the Ayer introductory material it says Ayer holds that "One is constrained when one's choice over actions is not operative: for example, if one were hypnotized..." (Bailey, 557). Bailey has oversimplified here. Ayer does think that if you are hypnotized, or a kleptomaniac, your choice is not operative--that is, your choice is not what determines your actions--and you are "constrained" in cases like that. But Ayer also holds (561) that I am "constrained" if someone is holding a gun to my head--but even then I can make choices and act on them. For example, I can choose not to give up my money. So Ayer really thinks there are two kinds of constraint: (a) when my choice is not operative, and (b) when I can make a choice, but it is coerced.

Reading questions:

FP p. 557, #1 and 2. (In #1 the word "feminist" is a misprint; it should read, "plausible.")
A. Ayer discuses determinism, and concludes that we cannot be sure whether it is true (558-9). What reasons does he give for this? (Look for what he says about the claim that everything has a cause, 558, and about our ability to predict what people will do, 559.)
B. How does Ayer argue that the only way we can be responsible is if determinism is true (559-60)?
C. How does Ayer distinguish between "constrained" and "free" actions? In other words, when does he think you are constrained, and when are you "free" (561-2)? Notice the different examples of constraint, such as the gun to the head and the kleptomaniac. How are these cases alike, and how are they different?
D. Ayer says you are free, and responsible, when your actions are not constrained, even though such "free" actions are determined. Do you think being unconstrained is a satisfactory basis for holding people responsible?
