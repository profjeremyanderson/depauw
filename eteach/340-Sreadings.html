Readings:


Plato: The Crito.

This is our first reading. It is in the Morgan collection but I'm posting it here for those of you who can't get hold of the textbook right away. If you don't have the textbook, print this out and bring it to class with you.

Links to other works by Plato can be found here at the aptly-named Links to Plato's Works on the Web.

Later we'll be reading from Plato's much more famous work, Republic. In it Plato (in)famously recommends that rulers deceive their citizens by telling them a "noble lie" (Book III, 414b-415d). Just for fun, you might look at a modern example of what could be called a noble lie: Yes, Virginia, there is a Santa Claus.


Aristotle: The Nicomachean Ethics.
I may ask you to read Book X, esp. chapters 5-9 (the numbered sections in Aristotle are chapters).

The Nicomachean Ethics spells out Aristotle's view that we all seek happiness and happiness consists of a virtuous life, which he describes at some length throughout the book. Near the end (in Book X), he presents important arguments that are relevant to this course: (a) that hedonists are mistaken to believe that happiness and pleasure are the same thing; (b) what he thinks happiness really consists of--namely, being virtuous--and (c) that the proper role of government is to use its powers of punishment and reward to make virtuous citizens.

Thomas Hobbes:
"Hobbes on Unity vs. Mere Consent": read this along with Leviathan's chapter 17.
This short piece is to help you understand Hobbes's view of sovereignty.

"Hobbes's Political Theory: Power of the Sovereign" (PDF). This excerpt from Bernard Gert's book Hobbes: Prince of Peace gives Gert's (sympathetic) take on sovereign power, and discusses how Hobbes's notion of sovereignty does and does not (in Gert's view) apply to today's United States.

Look at this: here is a reproduction of the sovereign from Leviathan's famous title page illustration. Notice that the sovereign is made up of the people, and they're all facing the same way. This is actually a pretty important thing to keep in mind for understanding Hobbes: it is a metaphorical representation of an important fact about sovereignty, namely that political power comes from the consent and cooperation of the governed.

We'll be reading several portions of Leviathan. The Morgan collection has all we need of it, but if you want an online version you can go to this site at McMaster University. That site also contains:
  • Two earlier works by Hobbes that are similar to Leviathan: De Cive and The Elements of Law. You may find them useful because they provide similar arguments expressed in different words. 
  • A biography of Hobbes by his acquaintance John Aubrey
  • A severe critique of Leviathan by Hobbes's contemporary, Edward Clarendon
Need help with Hobbes?
A helpful introduction to Hobbes can be found in the version of Leviathan edited by Edwin Curley, which is on reserve in the Roy O. West Library (E. Curley, ed., Leviathan: with selected variants from the Latin edition of 1668). Another general discussion of Hobbes can be found here in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

Robert Filmer:
Read both of these:
Patriarcha. (PDF file; print out and read chapters I and II, pp. 1-18, and bring it to class.)

Anderson, notes on Patriarcha (these will help you figure out what Filmer is up to)
Sir Robert Filmer wrote Patriarcha in 1680 to defend the theory of the Divine Right of Kings. The text is available any number of places; ours is taken from the version posted at the site of the Constitution Society, which maintains a large online library of political and legal works in its Liberty Library of Constitutional Classics.


John Locke:
We'll be reading selections from the Second Treatise of Government. The Morgan collection has it, but you can also find an online version at this site at McMaster University. The site also contains a lot of other works by and about Locke, including:
  • First Letter Concerning Toleration
  • Two Treatises of Government (The First Treatise is Locke's critique of Filmer's Patriarcha, which argued for the divine right of kings, which some had accepted as the justification for monarchy.)
Need help with Locke?
Many students find Locke hard to follow: he, like Hobbes, can be prolix and repetitive. If you'd like help, I offer two very short summaries of Locke's overall political argument in Two Digests of Government. (Sorry I haven't created a similar resource for Hobbes.)

A more sophisticated introduction to Locke and his works can be found in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. 

The Diggers: various tracts and lyrics.
The Diggers and Levellers were 17th-century British communists whose ideas foreshadowed Marx's. This site contains an archive of Digger literature. An archive of Leveller literature can be found via the Constitution Society's Liberty Library.

A brief poetic summary of their ideas can be found in Leon Rosselson's 1975 song, "The World Turned Upside Down." The song lyrics are here; here's a performance by Nancy Kerr.

Henry David Thoreau: Civil Disobedience (PDF; from this text from eServer.org).
The influence of this work is hard to overstate. Thoreau was an inspiration to Mohandas Gandhi and Martin Luther King, Jr. and many others.

Thoreau presents an interesting contrast with some of our other authors. Many of them (Plato, Aquinas, Hobbes, Locke) see government as a good, or at least a necessary evil--with emphasis on "necessary." Thoreau, like Spencer, tends to emphasize the "evil" aspect of government.

But his critique is not only of government. As you read, keep three questions in mind, which will be the foci of our discussion:
1. What does Thoreau believe is wrong with government?
2. What does Thoreau believe is wrong with us?
3. What does Thoreau believe we should do about it?
More of Thoreau's works can be found at The Thoreau Reader.

John Stuart Mill:

Assignment: read chapter 1 of On Liberty with special attention to paragraphs 4-11. The complete text is included in the Morgan collection; an online version is here.

Many of Mill's writings can be found at McMaster University, and even more are available at the aptly-named utilitarian.net. Aside from On Liberty (which is available at both sites), his 1868 speech defending capital punishment may interest you.

If you might want to present (or write about, or just learn) more, there is of course more in the Morgan collection and the Marxists Internet Archive contains a huge trove of Marx's, Marxist, and related works for further reading.

 Michel Foucault: excerpt from Discipline and Punish.
This link takes you to a page at Purdue University.

At the bottom of the page you will notice two strange pictures that seem to bear no relation to the text. These depict the Panopticon prison design, which was created by the British Utilitarian Jeremy Bentham (the stuffed philosopher). Panopticon prisons have the cells arranged in a circle around a central guard tower, so the guards can look into every cell at all times--hence the name Panopticon, from the Greek for "seeing everything." The tower's windows may be designed so that prisoners cannot see the guards and thus can never tell whether they are being watched. The Panopticon design was scrapped until recently. Now it has been found that placing the guards where they can see everything (as opposed to the usual design with cells in rows which the guards must walk along to see into the cells) may make prisons safer, with less fighting and rape.

United States Supreme Court, and other courts.

There are many web sites providing access to legal decisions by the US Supreme Court, Federal Circuit and District Courts, etc. Here are a couple of sites I use a lot:

The Legal Information Institute at Cornell University: This site provides access to many (though not all) decisions by the US Supreme Court, Courts of Appeal (Circuit Courts), state supreme courts, and others. One especially nice feature is its indexes of US Supreme Court decisions by topic, by party, and by author.

A significant downside of the Legal Information Institute is that many of the older decisions are stored in a way that's hard to retrieve. So, once you find the name of a case you want to find out more about, you might want to look for the text of the ruling elsewhere. One good possibility is Findlaw.com's Cases & Codes page.

More law-related links can be found in the law section of my research links page.


Bryan Caplan: Bryan Caplan’s Anarcho-Capitalist Page.
This was written by an economics professor at George Mason University whom I met a while back. In it he describes and defends a particular kind of anarchism.

James Kalb: Sexual Morality FAQ.
Many "liberals" tend to think that, as long as it's between consenting adults, our sexual activity is purely our own business and the law should not regulate it. Over the years, this view has become dominant, and so-called "blue laws" banning sodomy and oral sex have often gone unenforced or been stricken from the books. Kalb disagrees with this line of thinking, and argues that "private" sexual activity is really a matter of public concern.

Nicholas Dixon: Boxing, Paternalism, and Legal Moralism (PDF).
Dixon examines the sport of boxing and argues that the rules should be modified for the safety of the participants. Along the way he examines various kinds of legal paternalism and legal moralism.

Henry Rosemont, Jr.: Confucian Perspectives on Freedom, Human Rights, and Justice.



Paul Teetor: Prelude to a Death.

This article appeared in the May 5, 2002, Los Angeles Times Magazine. It's about a woman who was known to be dangerously mentally ill but whom the law let run free until she killed someone.
 

Eric Rudolph: Statement of Eric Rudolph (PDF; from Wikisource)
This text is transcribed from a handwritten message, so it has quite a few typos. Other versions of it are available, such as this copy at NPR (which omits the postscript). More of Rudolph's writings may easily be found online.

Rudolph is currently serving multiple consecutive life terms at a Supermax prison for setting off a bomb in a park during the 1996 Summer Olympics in Atlanta, bombing two abortion clinics in 1997 and 1998, and bombing a lesbian bar in 1997. His bombs killed two people and injured many others. He argues that the US government has lost its legitimacy, and his allegiance, by allowing legal abortions. FYI, his claims about the number of abortions taking place are accurate; statistics indicate that the rate of abortions in the US is roughly 1 million to 1.5 million per year, which on average is several thousand per day. He further argues that the cause is too urgent to work through the legal avenues available.
