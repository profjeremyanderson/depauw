Discussion instigation

We will all take turns instigating discussion. When it's your turn, do your best to instigate a stimulating and productive discussion of the assigned reading for that day by giving a brief (5-10 minute) talk about some aspect of the reading at the start of class. (You might also develop one of these presentations into a paper or a presentation, too.)

Keep in mind that your job is to set the tone and topic(s) of discussion for the day, and this discussion might turn out to be the class' only examination of a particular text. So be sure to direct us towards what matters most. Ask yourself, "If the class only comes away discussing a couple of things about this text, what should they be?"

There is more than one good way to do this assignment. Some possibilities are:
  • Summarize what you take to be the most important points the author(s) make that are relevant to the course.

    Doing only this may or may not do much to stimulate discussion with your classmates, so I recommend doing something besides, or perhaps instead of, summarizing, such as...

  • Explain why you think the material is important for us.
  • Present some puzzle about the text: some central issue that is unresolved, or resolved in a way we might disagree with.
  • Present a critical evaluation of the text: briefly explain what you think the author does well, and/or point out some important error(s) made. (For some reason, pointing to errors often works better. You might contrast an author's view with a differing view you think is very plausible, or point out some fallacy in the text, or point to some unattractive consequence(s) of the author's view.)
  • Point us to some key passage(s) in the text for us to explore. (A passage may be of interest because it expresses an important point and/or is provocative.)
  • Think of a real-life, or possibly real, example to illustrate, explore, or criticize what an author says.

  • Suggestion: If you'd like to raise a series of questions or points for the class to discuss, raising them all at once might not be best. Instead, you could raise one, have us talk about it, raise another, and so on.
Since your job is to provoke discussion--not teach the text--try to do your spiel in a way that provokes, invites, or at least does not quash discussion. Some pointers for this are:
  • Don't lecture much.
  • Avoid talking as if your view is the only possibly justified one. Your task here is not to convert the class to the One True Reading Of The Text. (You may try to do that during the ensuing discussion.)
  • Raise open-ended questions, as opposed to yes/no questions. Ask questions you think you know the answer to, but also ask questions you are clueless about the answer to.
  • Don't be afraid of silences. Wait for answers to questions you raise. Avoid answering them yourself.
  • When discussion begins, refrain from jumping in even when you have something to say. Let the discussion develop.
Don't feel like you have to prep this on your own. Come see me. Go visit the "S" Center. We're happy to help.

Your instigation will be evaluated based on how well you meet the goals of the assignment, i.e., how well you instigate a productive discussion of the text.
  • It's a discussion when more than one person is talking.
  • Discussion is productive when:
    • More people are talking rather than fewer. Everyone should be in.
    • Discussion centers on the text, though some digression is fine and often unexpectedly enriching.
    • It contributes to our understanding of the text and/or the issues raised by it. (This presupposes having a good grasp of the text.)
    • It gets at what's important in the text. 
You are not alone in this. Just as you are assigned the job of instigating discussion, the rest of us are assigned the job of participating in the discussion and moving it along. To this end, everyone should look at the section on participation below.
 



Class participation

Contribute productively to class discussion. By that, I mean consistently try to make class time an insightful, meaningful conversation about the day's assignment or your classmates' presentations. Listen to what your classmates have to say and ask thoughtful questions during class and the various question and answer periods. 

We are all responsible for furthering discussion in the class.
  • We all should pay attention to how the discussion is going, and contribute to making it go better.
    •  
  • When there is a problem under discussion, help guide the conversation towards solving that problem unless we seem to be getting nowhere. Then you might ask, "Can this issue be resolved? Should we maybe change the subject?"
    •  
  • Differing views should be critically evaluated but nonetheless must be welcomed: each view should be considered, investigated, and appraised but none of us is personally attacked or put down.
    •  
  • We all should notice who is talking and who is not. When only a few are talking, others should be invited. Anyone in the class may call on those who are silent to ask for their input.
    •  
  • There are several different ways of participating in discussion:
      - Raising a question
      - Answering a question
      - Articulating your point
      - Helping others to articulate their points
      - Expressing agreement, perhaps adding additional justification
      - Expressing disagreement
      - Tying together what has been said
      - Moving the discussion in a new direction
    And so on. Don't limit yourself to just one or a couple of ways of participating.

Presentation proposals: submitting and commenting

Your proposal. Write a proposal for your presentation. In it...
  1. State your topic. What would you like to talk about? How does it relate to things we have discussed in the class?
  2. Discuss your motivation. What about this topic interests you? What got you interested in it? Why would be good for the class to hear about it and discuss it?
  3. Say how you think you might go about it. What main parts or sections do you think your presentation might have (i.e., what might the outline look like)? What questions do you think you might try to answer, or need to answer, to explore your topic? For example, what factual information might you need to find? What ethical questions might be raised?
  4. Identify some sources. Identify at least three you think you might use in your presentation. For each, briefly say why you think it's relevant. Use the advice on sources to choose better ones.
Make your proposal 300 - 400 words (no more than that, since you'll all have to read each other's proposals). Post it in the Presentation Proposals forum on Moodle by 6pm Friday April 8.


Your comments on others proposals. Give constructive feedback on each other student's proposal (and, of course, read and consider others' comments on yours!). To do this, consider the presenter's topic and motivation, and think about how the presenter proposes to go about it.

To think of what to say, consider questions like these:
  • Do you have ideas for other questions the presenter might consider, other related areas they might bring in?
  • Do you have suggestions for how they might rearrange the order of their outline?
  • Does anything in the proposal seem especially insightful?
  • Is anything in the proposal unclear? Identify it and say why.
  • Does anything seem perhaps irrelevant or out of place?
  • Can you think of something that seems missing?
  • Can you suggest other sources they might look into?
  • Anything else that might help the presenter refine, deepen, or otherwise improve the presentation?
Make your comments 100 - 200 words (though longer would be OK). Reply to each proposal with your comments Moodle by 6pm Tuesday April 12.




Presentation

Create a presentation on your chosen topic. Your goal is to educate the class about it (What are the relevant facts? What are some important controversies? What have some smart people said about it?) and stimulate discussion on it. You may advocate for one side of a controversy if you like, but whether you do or not you must present alternative views.

You will have an entire class meeting, one hour. Your presentation should take about 30-40 minutes of the hour, leaving the rest for discussion. You might present for 30-40 minutes and hold discussion until afterwards, but often it works well to present a chunk of material, have some discussion on it, present another chunk, have some discussion on that, and so on.

For discussion, you will need to have questions ready, prepare an activity for the class, etc.

You may use aids (slides, short video or music clips, a handout, etc.). Actually, I strongly encourage you to use them.

But you should not need them much. The most important things for success are to do your research and know your material. If you know your stuff, you don't need PowerPoint much; if you don't know your stuff, no technology will make up for it.

Since this will be a class meeting, there must be an assignment to prepare the class to think about your topic. Part of your research will involve finding something(s) to serve as that assignment: a scholarly piece, some relevant news, video, etc.

Research & sources

Good research is vital to your success. Learn as much as you can about your topic, from good sources, to present it effectively to a group of people who are as smart and critical as you are.
Getting started
First, see me. You should meet with me at least twice to discuss your plans.

Identify the names of any people and/or the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the info from reference sources, (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and and databases to find scholarly material on your topic. (Here are all the databases, and here are some philosophy-focused ones, plus some online philosophy encyclopedias). Google Scholar is good, too, and has the familiar Google interface. (Generally avoid plain Google; too much garbage there.)

As you go, look for things that might be good to assign to the class.

Choose your sources wisely
First, don't look in just one place. You may miss some really good stuff. So yes, use JSTOR, but also use the Library's search, Google Scholar, the Philosopher's Index, etc.

Second, be choosy about the quality of your sources: 
    • Best: peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields. The more of these you can use, the better.

    • Worst: websites or other writings of unknown origin, sources with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications or no qualifications. Relying on these can seriously hurt your project.
      • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, that's a strong reason not to use that source.
    • In the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are good places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia, since some of its articles are just abysmal and its overall quality is unreliable). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources are good to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but relying much on such sources for your presentation may count against it.
DePauw's Library has many great books and some poor ones. The internet, generally speaking, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, but lots of trash. See me if you have any questions about what source materials to use.

Documentation

DePauw's Academic Integrity Policy applies to this assignment. Your documentation -- personal, and slides etc. -- must include all the sources you relied on in any way.

Document your work whether you use slides or not

By 6pm the day before your presentation, send me an e-mail or Word doc or Google Doc like this sample here (or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations).

Use a standard bibliographic format. Include the URL for any online sources. (When available, use the "stable URL" or "persistent URL" or "Permalink.") A quick guide to citation is here on the Library web page. (I like Turabian.)

Turn in your slides etc. with sources documented in them

Your slides etc. will be a helpful resource for the class, so turn them in. Slides, handouts, etc. also need to document any and all sources used. Since you might be working on them the day of your presentation, they are not due until 6pm after you present.

Endnotes are one good way to document sources in slides: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in your slides that comes from some source -- image, claim, quote, whatever -- put the number of the source by it.




Feedback on others' presentations

Give constructive, thoughtful feedback on each other's presentations in the Presentation Feedback forum on Moodle. Your feedback is due by two days after each presentation. In other words, if a classmate gives a presentation on Monday your comments are due by Wednesday.

In your feedback, include:
  1. What went well? Why? (Include at least two things.)
  2. What could have been/gone better? Why? (Include at least two things.)
  3. Content of the presentation:
    • What about the presentation was most meaningful, or surprising, or otherwise important?
    • What questions remain for you about this topic?
