1. General instructions for all papers in this course
  • Your paper must be primarily argumentative. That is, it must defend a conclusion which other smart people might well disagree with, and this must be your paper's major focus. There should be no more background material than is necessary to explain and give perspective to the arguments you present.

  • To take a stand effectively, your paper must be critical but fair to any opposition, and your papers' conclusions should not depend on mere expressions of personal opinion or armchair arguments. It is not enough to explain why you think something is so; you must strive to convince a reasonable reader that any right-thinking person should agree with you.

  • Your paper must be submitted as a Word (.docx or .doc) or Rich Text Format (.rtf) file. It must be double spaced, have one-inch margins and numbered pages, use 12-point Times New Roman or similar font, and be free of mechanical errors (grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc.). Your computer's grammar- and spell-checking features are not adequate; you will have to proofread your paper yourself. Include a word count for the body of the paper (that is, not including your name, end- or footnotes, bibliography, etc.) at the top with your identifying information.
     
  • Papers should be clear and organized. I am much more impressed by writers who express ideas clearly using simple language than by those who try to impress readers with big words and long sentences.

  • DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to all assignments in the course.
    • Any time you use any ideas that are not your own, you must cite them. Any time you use another person’s words you must put them in quotation marks and cite them. It does not matter whether the material came from research you did, an AI you consulted, assigned texts, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, a web page, your mom, or whatever: if the idea isn’t yours, cite it; if the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes and cite it.
    • Failing to cite and quote as required will automatically lower the assignment's grade, potentially down to failing. It may also be prosecuted as plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If it is prosecuted successfully, the minimum penalty is usually worse than turning in no work at all. So it is vital you keep track of whose words and ideas you are using. If you have any sources to cite outside our textbook or class discussion, list them on a “Works Cited” page in addition to citing them in the text. If you consult an AI, document your prompt and the AI's response.
      • This is not hard to do. As you work on the paper, make a note of what you read and who you talk to about it, keeping track of what ideas and words came from each source. Then, if you decide to use any ideas or words from these notes, cite the sources.
    • To cite material assigned in the class, give the author's name and the page number in parentheses, like this: (Brentlinger, 121) or (Thucydides, 368). No bibliographic information is needed for assigned readings, but every time you make a claim about what the text says, cite the text.
    • No research outside of assigned texts is needed for the short papers in this course. But if you use any outside sources you must cite them. Citations of outside sources must be given in a standard format. I prefer Chicago or Turabian. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page; click the tab labeled "Turabian (P/RL)" for instructions on how to cite various kinds of sources.
      • For more information about citation, quoting, paraphrasing, etc. consult Charles Lipson's book Doing Honest Work in College. If you want a format suggestion, I happen to like the "author-date" system of the Chicago Style (or the very similar "parenthetical citations/reference list" Turabian style), with one exception: the Chicago manual says that some sources may be omitted from your reference list or bibliography, but in work for me you must include every source.
      • Your list of sources must include:
        • Full bibliographic information for any print source aside from assigned texts.
        • Full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.whitehouse.gov" but the full URL, so that I can paste it into my browser and find the exact material you relied upon.
        • If citing a journal article accessed electronically, give both the bibliographic data plus the full URL. Use the "stable" URL.

  • My policy on using and documenting AI:
I don't forbid using AI's such as ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, Gemini, or whatever. But if you do use them, I have cautions and a policy.

The cautions:
  • AI's are unreliable. When I ask an AI to do something I might ask of you, like answer an exam question, I routinely get answers that would earn failing grades. AI's use both good and bad sources without discriminating between them. They are biased. They "hallucinate" -- that is, they say things that are false. Feel free to experiment with AI's if you like, but for work you turn in for credit I recommend you only use material from an AI when you can verify it is accurate, or as an example you critically evaluate. (Which, in practice, may mean that you don't use it in this course at all.)
  • The more you let AI do for you, the more you undermine your own education. Developing and practicing skills in reading, critical thinking, and writing are among the most important benefits of a liberal arts education. Those skills matter at least as much as whatever facts you learn because they are fundamental to interacting intelligently with the world. Without them, you cannot even assess whether AI is feeding you bullshit.
  • And, of course, the more of the work the AI does, the less credit you earn.
The policy: if you use any AI in any way you must document how you used it by showing your prompts and AI's responses. (Screenshots are good for this.) Any use of AI that you fail to document is a form of plagiarism and may be charged as such.

Here are some possible examples:
  • If you copy any text generated by an AI (whether it's just a turn of phrase or something more), put it in quotation marks and cite the AI.
  • If you use anything (such as an idea, or an example) you got from an AI without copying the words, still cite the AI.
  • If you use an AI to correct your grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc., or to help outline your paper, say so, and cite the AI.
In short, if you use AI to affect the content of your work in any way whatsoever (wording, ideas, examples, arguments, grammar, punctuation, organization, etc.), document it. And, of course, the more work the AI does, the less credit you earn.

I may use a variety of AI detectors to check your work.

This is merely an extension of the plagiarism policy above, and reflects the main goal of academic integrity generally, which is to give credit for any help you got.

As always, if you are unsure what's OK, ask me, preferably before turning work in.


  • You must be judicious about your sources if you are going to do any research beyond assigned texts (as is required for the long research essay and optional for the short essays). This means three things:

    1. First, don't limit yourself to just one kind of source.
      • For example, some students simply do a search on JSTOR and use any relevant-sounding articles near the top of the search results. That is not the worst way to go (the worst is to do that with Google), but it is lazy and, worse, is unlikely to find you the really good stuff. You should also search the Library catalog for relevant books, search other article databases (EBSCO, the Philosopher's Index, Google Scholar), consult a reference librarian, etc. 
2. Second, be careful about the quality of your sources. The internet, for example, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, but to reach them you may have to dig through a lot of trash.
      • Peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields are best. If you are unclear about how to tell which periodicals are scholarly, consult a librarian or me.
      • Among the worst are websites of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications. Reliance on these can seriously hurt your project.
        • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is, and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, you probably should not use that source. When in doubt, either ask me or do not use the source.
      • Around the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are nice places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources may be used to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources for your final paper will count against it.
Some good and interesting sources are available on my Philosophy Research Links page, which has a section devoted specifically to military matters, among other things.

3. Third, you may cite lecture, discussion, reading responses, forum posts, etc. only for material that is not in any text. For anything that is in a text (i.e., any time you make a claim about what an author says), you must cite the text. If you cite material from class discussion, do so like this: (Phil 490 [date]).

 When in doubt about the appropriateness of a given source, consult with me.

Here is a suggestion for getting started in your research. First, identify the names of the people and the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic. The Library has kindly posted helpful research tips. 
  • Although not required, it would be a good idea to seek advice from DePauw's Writing Center prior to each paper's due date.

2. What makes better papers better
  • Meticulous proofreading. Do not trust your college G.P.A. to your computer's spelling & grammar checkers. I recommend reading your paper aloud to spot typos, grammatical errors, awkward word choices, etc.

  • No fluff, no bull, no gratuitous drama. Do not tell me how long humanity has pondered your topic or give me Webster's definition of anything. State your thesis early in the paper, give a quick preview of how you will support it, and then devote the rest of your paper to supporting your thesis, and considering good objections to your view.

  • Presenting even viewpoints you oppose with clarity, care, and charity, as opposed to, say, whacking straw men or presenting opposition so briefly it doesn't get a fair hearing. Athletes say there's no glory in defeating an opponent who is unfairly disadvantaged; you should take the same approach to your papers and grant the side you oppose all the credit it deserves.

  • Relying on superior sources of information when research is employed (e.g., scholarly articles as opposed to encyclopedias, some dude's blog, etc.).

  • Providing relevant empirical data where appropriate, as opposed to armchair arguments.

  • Arriving at conclusions that are clearly implied by the sum total of your arguments and information, as opposed to stating a conclusion that goes beyond the evidence provided, or going off on a tangent. I am more impressed by a careful argument for a "small" conclusion than a grand conclusion that is not well supported.

  • Meticulous and complete citation of sources. 


3. Short-paper instructions

For each of the first two units of the course (War and Love) you will write a short paper on an issue raised in some reading(s) assigned for that unit. By “short” I mean 1200-1700 words, about 4-5 pages.

As with all papers in the course, your short papers should be argumentative: you should take a side on something smart people might disagree about and argue for it. To do this effectively in a short space, you will need to limit the scope of your essay. For example:
  • For War, do not try to take on Realism, or Machiavelli. Instead, you might, say, take one of Machiavelli’s examples and carefully evaluate his use of it, examining how well it supports the conclusion he wants to draw from it. Instead of taking on Walzer, or Orend, for example, you might select one argument that is well articulated in one text and critically appraise it.
  • For Love, do not try to take on Love, or eros, or Plato’s whole theory of Forms. Instead, you might write a critical appraisal of Aristophanes’ central thesis in his speech in the Symposium, for example, or critique an argument in one of our secondary readings. 
The general requirements for your short essays are above. No outlines or drafts are required, though discussing your paper with me before it is due would not be a bad idea, as would discussing your paper with folks at the Writing Center.

You are not required to do any research for a short paper, though of course that's not a bad idea, either. Obviously your argument regarding one assigned text may be helped by points made by the author of another text. In such cases, by all means bring in the other author's arguments. (Just remember that if you do, then all the requirements regarding research apply.)

Short-paper deadlines will be found on the Schedule of Assignments.

 

4. Research-paper instructions
As it says in the syllabus, you will be writing a a substantial research paper: roughly 10-14 pages: 3000-4000 words not counting footnotes, endnotes or bibliography. The point of this assignment is for you to pursue in depth some controversial course-related topic that is important to you.

The research paper assignment is scaffolded--that is, it goes in stages:
  • First, by one week before the deadline for your draft, turn in a 1-2 page proposal including
    • The topic of your paper. Put it as a question, like "When, if ever, is it OK to torture?" or "Must eros be selfish as Plato seems to think?" At this point I don't expect you to have settled on answer.  In fact, settling on an answer to your question before doing your research is a bad idea; you should follow the evidence rather than committing to an answer in advance.
    • A brief -- maybe half a page or so -- description or outline of how think you might figure out the answer to your question or how you think the paper might go, and
    • A tentative annotated bibliography of several sources you think you might use. That is, each source, include at least a brief note of a sentence or two explaining how that source is relevant to your topic. Relax: I do not expect this to be a complete or final list of your sources! Rather, the point of this is to get you started on thinking about and researching your topic.

      It would be helpful to see me to discuss your paper ideas, the sooner the better, before submitting the proposal. (You can come see me after, too.)

  • Second you'll turn in (to your instructor and your commentator), a complete draft, which you will also present to the class. The draft is due one week prior to your formal paper presentation; deadlines will be posted on the schedule of assignments.

  • Finally, based on comments received on that draft, you'll turn in a polished, finished paper.
The proposal is strongly encouraged but not required. The draft will not be graded, but the finished paper's grade will be impacted if the draft is inadequate or missing.

This paper is linked to the "S" requirements for this course in the following ways:
  • You must prepare a formal presentation of your nearly-finished paper, to be given in the last weeks of class. As part of this presentation you will receive formal comments from a classmate. Part of the preparation for this presentation is that you will supply a nearly-finished draft of your paper to the instructor and to your assigned commentator at least one week before your presentation.
    • Failure to supply a complete draft on time will result in your final paper grade being reduced by a full letter grade; if it is so late as to interfere with your commenter's ability to comment effectively, the paper may fail. 
  • You will also serve as the commentator for a classmate's paper. You'll turn in a copy of your comments to your instructor and the paper's author by 6pm the night before you give your comments in class.
  • Dates for all these, and the deadline for the finished paper, will be found on the Schedule of Assignments.
Show clear evidence of good research in your subject. A good way to do this is to use at least six good sources in your paper outside of assigned materials which, taken together, give a balanced and reasonably thorough coverage of your chosen topic. Class materials do not count as research. Demonstrate understanding of those sources in your paper. Six is a suggested minimum (i.e., necessary to get a passing grade); to write a really good paper you will likely need more. Heed the instructions above regarding academic integrity and judiciousness about your sources.

Do feel free to discuss your ideas with me concerning what to write about. But  I shall not assign topics. I want you to pick a subject that matters to you and explore it by researching and writing about it. Appropriate topics include anything within the scope of this course, that is, within the areas we are exploring: War, Love, and Compatibilism. I urge you to think back over what we've covered and ask yourself which topic, problem, puzzle, reading, argument, etc. most excited you, inspired you, or annoyed you. Build a topic based on that.

If you are casting about for possible ways to handle your favorite topic, here are some suggestions.
    • There's always the obvious: Here's position X. Here's an argument for X. Here are some objections to X. Here's why those objections don't defeat the arguments for X. So, X.

      Or, you could get fancier:

    • Show that position X seems plausible but really suffers from significant objection O. (For example: "So-and -so believes that threatening to use nuclear weapons is never justified, and gives appealing reasons for this position. But I'll show that (at least under certain circumstances) it is nevertheless justified.")
    • Show that position X seems to suffer from objection O, but in fact it does not.
    • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that X may be correct but the justification is faulty. (For example, you might like Libertarianism but dislike a particular argument for it, so show that although that argument may be respected/popular/influential it's really not good.)
    • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that although the justification may seem bad it's really not.
    • Show that P is a vexing problem, for which S is a more promising solution than some alternative(s). (For example: "Here are some proposed responses to al Qaeda-type terrorism; of them, S is superior.")
    • Show that P is a vexing problem for which S seems like a promising solution, but really S is bad (and perhaps that we should do T instead).

      And so on. The main point here is that there are many ways you could go about this if you like.
