About the course
What is real? What is our place in the world? Who am I? What’s important in life? Am I free or are my choices foreordained? And how can we learn more about these issues by watching movies? This course will explore some of the deep, heavy questions of philosophy through the (relatively) light, convenient medium of films such as The Truman Show, American Beauty, Being John Malkovich, and others of your choice. Readings will point us to the issues to watch for in each film, which we will discuss after viewing. Assignments will include readings, viewings, and written reactions to them. Students will form groups, and each group will lead discussion of a given film. Students will be evaluated on participation in discussions, consistency and quality of written work, and film-discussion leading.


Course goals
With at least moderate effort on your part, this course should:
  • Acquaint you with several major philosophical questions and problems
  • Enable you to identify those questions and problems arising in popular media
  • Enhance your skill in discussing these issues in a productive way
  • Enhance your ability to take informed, reasoned stands on these issues
  • Sharpen your writing, especially at identifying issues, stating arguments, and responding to them
  • Enhance your critical thinking skills and film appreciation
  • Enhance your presentation skills

Assignment instructions and grading

Here are your four assignments:
1. Respond to daily reading assignments by 7:00pm the night before each film. Reading(s) will be assigned prior to each class meeting. Write a response to each day’s readings, totaling 350 words minimum not including your name etc., and upload it to the class Moodle page. In each response:
  • A. Summarize each assigned reading.
    In other words, identify the main issues discussed in the readings in a way that demonstrates you read everything carefully. If more than one reading is assigned, you must summarize each reading.
  • B. Separately from the summary/ies, critically reflect on at least one of the readings.
    For example, you might explain why you think the reading or some portion of it is especially insightful, interesting, or wrongheaded -- disagreement is encouraged! You might relate it to some other reading(s), film(s), etc.
    You may not simply express your personal opinion. You must not simply say “I think...” or "I feel" or “It seems to me that....” Rather, you must strive to provide good reason(s) for why any reasonable person should agree with you.
  • C. Employ correct grammar, spelling, word usage, etc. Excessive errors are grounds for failing a reading response.

    Want to see what an "excellent" (4) response looks like? Here are two samples (PDF).

  • Submit responses in Microsoft Word or RTF format (.doc or .docx or .rtf; no Pages!), with the file named after the film, like this: TheTrumanShow.docx (or .doc or .rtf). 
  • At the top of your reading response put your name, the title of the film, and a word count for the text of your response (i.e., do not count your name, film title, etc.).
  • Submit your response to the drop box for that film here on Moodle so that they are time-stamped by 7:00pm the night before the class meeting for which the readings are assigned. Responses time-stamped 7:01 or later will be deemed late (see late policies below), so manage your time and get them in sooner than that.
  • If Moodle is unavailable you may e-mail me your response (jeremyanderson@depauw.edu); the deadline is the same.

2. Participate in in-class discussions every day. At the beginning of each meeting we will discuss the day’s readings for a bit and then watch the day’s film. Then after a short break we’ll discuss the film in more depth. You must participate in those discussions, and do so in a way that demonstrates both having done the reading and having paid careful attention to the film. You should take note of things you’d like to talk about as you do the readings and as you watch the films, and keep those notes in front of you. I expect you to participate every day.

3. Participate in after-class online discussions by (a) posting your own view and (b) responding to someone else's post by 7:00pm the day each film is shown. I have set up online discussion forums on the class Moodle site for each film. When posting your own view, remember to give reasons why other reasonable people should agree with you. When responding to someone else's post you may agree or disagree, in whole or in part, but, again, give reasons why others should accept your view. The more you show thoughtfulness and critical reflection on course materials, the better. As in your reading responses, proper grammar, spelling, etc. are required; excessive errors are grounds for failing. Your online postings will be due by 7:00pm after each class meeting. Postings time-stamped 7:01pm or later will be deemed late (see late policies below).

4. Small-group presentation and discussion leading. I will lead the first few class meetings. After that, you will lead. That is, you will form groups of two (maybe three) in the first week of class and each group will pick a film and a date to present it. Then, each pair will view the film in advance of that day, research it, and master the reading(s) assigned for that film. Finally, on the day the film is shown the group will do a presentation on that film prior to our watching it and lead discussion afterwards. Each person in each group will be graded for their individual contribution. Presentations will be evaluated based on:
  • How effectively you identify the relevant philosophical issues in the reading(s) and film and prepare the class for viewing the film, and
  • How effectively you lead discussion after the film is shown.
Presenting your film is a major assignment, worth 25% of your grade for the course -- and to pass the course with an "S" you must earn a score of at least 2 on your presentation. This assignment requires substantial work. Instructions for preparing your presentation are posted to the class web page. I expect everyone to follow them. You will also find a list of philosophical issues with films (most of which are available from Roy O. West Library or Putnam County Public Library two blocks from campus) which explore those issues; you may also propose films not on the list.

Grading

Various terms, ideas, arguments, themes, etc. will come up in readings, lecture, presentations, discussion, and of course in the films we view. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate that you understand, and have thought about, all that material. Your grade will be derived as follows:

25%  Responses to daily reading assignments
25%  Participation in in-class discussions
25%  Participation in after-class online discussions
25%  Small-group presentation and discussion leading

This course is "S/D/U," i.e., the possible course grades are "Satisfactory," "D," or "Unsatisfactory." Grades of "S" or "D" earn credit; "U" does not. Each assignment will be graded 4 (= A), 3 (= B), 2 (= C), 1 (= D), or 0 (= F). No fractional grades (e.g., 2.5) will be given. To get an "S" for the course your average score for all assignments must be at least 1.7. Just to be clear, this means that an average score of, for example, 1.6999 will yield a grade of "D" for the course; I do not round up. An average score of at least 0.7 but less than 1.7 will yield a "D" for the course, and an average score of less than 0.7 (and again, I do not round up) will yield a "U" for the course. One exception: you will not get an "S" for the course without completing the presentation assignment with a score of at least 2 (and if you look at the presentation assignment, you'll see that even getting a 2 on it will require very substantial work).

To summarize:
1.7 average score or greater for all assignments, including at least 2.0 on your presentation = S for the course
0.7 to less than 1.7 = D for the course
Less than 0.7 = U for the course (and no Winter Term credit)
Less than 2 on the presentation = D or U for the course, depending on your other scores.


Course policies
If any of these policies might be a problem for you, see me.

Disagreement is great! But keep it civilized. In philosophy, critical perspectives are not only allowed but prized. But we criticize ideas (and, in this course, characters in movies), not our classmates. For discussion to be productive we need to listen carefully, and critically, to what others have to say; we need to push each other to think more deeply and carefully; and we need to regard disagreement as a chance to learn from others even if ultimately we do not accept what they say. However, personal attacks in class or online are not allowed. For example, it is fine to say, "I really disagree with what you've said; I think that's mistaken because...." and proceed to give some reason(s) for your view. However, it is not OK to say, "Man, how can you believe something so stupid?"

 Be here, on time. Unless you have very strong excuse, be in class by 9:30am ready to rock ‘n’ roll. Strong excuses include severe or contagious illness, death in the family, and so on. Documentation may be required. You will be allowed one unexcused absence. More than one will be grounds for a “U” in the course, and so will excessive tardiness. Falling asleep in class, e.g., during a film, counts as an unexcused absence.

 Minimize distractions in class. Please visit the restroom before class rather than during. Electronic devices should be out of sight during films, and during discussion unless you get specific permission from me. Cell phones, PDA’s, pagers, etc. must be set to vibrate only, and put away, unless you clear it with me beforehand. No laptops etc. unless you clear it with me beforehand and even then all technology use must be directly course-related: no texting, e-mail, Facebook, etc. in class. And so on: be here mentally as well as physically.

Participate in in-class discussions. If you are quiet for a day, you will need to make sure to speak up the other days. If you are prevented from speaking on a given day you may make up for it by posting additional contributions to that day’s after-class online discussions. Insufficient in-class participation will be grounds for a failing score for this portion of the course.

Use proper spelling, grammar, word usage, etc. in all writing for this course. Excessive  errors in a given assignment are grounds for failing it, and consistent inattention to proper mechanics in your writing is grounds for a “U” in the course.

Late reading responses get no credit without a very strong excuse (see "Be here, on time" above--also, "My book hasn't come in yet" is not an excuse). Missing two or more days’ reading responses without a strong excuse will be grounds for failing this assignment regardless of how good your other responses are. If you run into trouble with Moodle, e-mail your response to jeremyanderson@depauw.edu by the deadline. If you have trouble getting the textbook by the first day of class, see me.

Late posts to after-class discussions get no credit without a strong excuse (see above). Missing two or more days' posting without a strong excuse will be grounds for failing this assignment regardless of how good your other contributions are. If you run into trouble with Moodle, e-mail your posting(s) to jeremyanderson@depauw.edu by the deadline.

 DePauw and I are committed to providing reasonable accommodations to students with properly documented disabilities--psychiatric, attentional, learning, vision, hearing, physical, medical, etc. If you believe you may need an accommodation because of a disability or learning challenge, please contact Student Accessibility Services to learn how to receive accommodations and support. You must to contact them; I cannot do it for you. Student Disability Services' phone number is 765-658-6267, their email is studentaccessibility@depauw.edu. Plagiarism and other forms of dishonesty violate DePauw policy on academic integrity. The most common type is when you present another person’s ideas or words as if they were your own, failing to give proper credit to your sources. Often it is unintended, but even then it is a serious offense and I may prosecute you. The minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all, and penalties may include failing the course, suspension, or expulsion. Much plagiarism is due to ignorance of the standards and requirements for documenting sources, so I urge you to familiarize yourself with DePauw's policy and your obligations and rights, which are stated in the Academic Handbook, and use these resources to better understand academic integrity at DePauw. You can avoid many problems if you follow these simple rules: if you use an idea you got from someone or somewhere else, cite it; if you use their words, put them in quotes and cite.
