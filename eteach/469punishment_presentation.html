Here's the idea
During the course I help guide you through a bunch of readings which discuss topics related to punishment: cross-cultural and cross-historical comparisons, the nature of legal punishment, justifications for punishment, problems with those justifications, the yardstick problem, punishing the innocent, rights forfeiture, expressivism, blah, blah, blah. Some of these issues may have been covered only superficially in class, however, and you might want to understand one of those issues in more depth. Or you might want to explore something very different, something not discussed in the reading assignments. Fine: pick something of interest to you, find a fellow student or two with similar interests and form your own groups of 2-3 people (though I might accept other sizes), get me to approve your topic (which is easy), research your topic, and present what you have learned to the class.

If you don't already have a topic in mind, there are many places to seek inspiration. You may draw from our assigned readings, the footnotes at the end of many of the articles, and articles and cases we haven't read. Also, look at your and others' reading responses. What interests you? 

The class will need to do some prep work for presentations as well. Each group of presenters needs to provide at least one reading or other assignment relevant to the presentation topic, which the rest of the class will study prior to the presentation. This preparatory assignment could be a court case or excerpt from one, some news article(s), a scholarly article, some video(s), or something the presenters create. It should help us all to become familiar with the topic and some of its key issues, and thereby enhance discussion. Since these will be reading assignments, responses to them will be required -- and count -- just like all other reading responses in the course.

The deadline for choosing a topic and forming a group is 6pm Tuesday after Spring Break (March 29): you will need to e-mail me to let me know who is in your group, and give me your proposed topic and at least one alternate topic in case your first choice is already taken.

On Thursday after Break (March 31), at the beginning of class, we will officially assign dates for you to present. The sooner you clear your topic and group with me, the better the choice of dates you'll have (not to mention having more time to prep), so I encourage you to form groups and notify me of topics sooner. Once a date is set, it will be subject to the late policy stated in the course syllabus. You may use the class open forum here on Moodle to find partners and air out ideas.


Sources
This is a research project. I will not dictate a number of sources you must have -- so, for example, if you read a couple of books that could count as well as reading a larger number of articles. But as with all research-based work the more, and more credible, sources you have the better. Larger groups will have to use more—otherwise, there won’t be enough work involved for each person to receive much credit. Follow the advice on the Papers page to be judicious about your sources.

How to begin? Here are suggestions. First, see me. Also, identify the names of the people and the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic.


Format
Expect to talk at least 20 minutes. Presentations often go longer, but good ones are seldom shorter. Make sure you include something that involves the class in your presentation. Visual aids (writing on the board, creating posters, doing PowerPoint presentations, etc.) are encouraged. Handouts which summarize the main points or arguments are strongly encouraged. If you need any equipment that isn't already in the room (like a Mac video adapter) you need to bring it. (Media Services has equipment available for checkout.)

You might...
  • Have each person present a different perspective on the issue, or
  • Stage a debate, or
  • Lecture the way I sometimes do, or
  • Make a film, or include clips from movies on your topic, or
  • Do something completely different.
I expect each group to
  • Provide the preparatory reading to me and the rest of the class by the previous class meeting at the very latest; two meetings prior is much preferred. (One good way is to supply me with either links to readings or PDF's I can post. I can help with copying if given advance notice.)
  • Demonstrate strong understanding of the topic. (I expect this of every member of every group, not just of groups as a whole.)
  • Present more than one side of an issue.
  • Be fair to all sides. By "fair" I do not mean making them all look equally good; I mean not presenting any view or argument as less (or more) good than it really is, and demonstrating an appreciation of the strengths as well as the weaknesses even of views you oppose. Philosophy is, after all, a search for truth, not merely an attempt to win a point. This doesn't mean you can't take a side; it just means you can't defend your side by misrepresenting anything.
  • Do something to involve the class in some way: some exercise, activity, discussion questions, etc.
  • Each member of each group must submit the documentation described in the next section, the night before the presentation.
  • Lastly, I recommend reading your classmates' reading responses prior to presenting to have a sense of what's on their minds.

Documentation & Academic Integrity
READ THIS CAREFULLY:

All students will be graded separately for their work; there are no group grades. You (that is, each individual presenter) must submit written evidence of what you contributed and what sources you relied on. This evidence shall consist of:
1. A single e-mail or Word document attachment (like this sample here -- or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations) containing:
  • A brief statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was. (For example: focusing on a certain aspect of the presentation topic; researching it; summarizing, re-writing, and/or organizing material; discussing ideas with your co-presenters; creating slides or handouts; presenting a portion of the material, etc.)
  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, in a standard bibliographic format (preferably Chicago or Turabian--see the guide here on the Library web page. ). Divide it into two parts: those sources used in the presentation and those consulted but not used.
2. Any audio/visual aids you use (PowerPoint, handouts, etc.) with any and all sources used in them documented in them. Endnotes are one good way to document sources in PowerPoints: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in the PowerPoint that comes from some source--image, claim, quote, whatever--put the number of the source by it.

This evidence is due via e-mail by 9:00pm the night before the presentation is given. Without it, the assignment is incomplete (and hence violates the late policy) and you could be prosecuted for plagiarism.

DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to this assignment. I will expect the documentation you submit to include all the sources you relied on in any way whether you quote them or not. The policy also applies to any audio/visual aids you use: for example, PowerPoint presentations must cite (e.g., using endnotes) all sources used for quotes, ideas, graphs, images -- everything.

Presenting work done outside this course (for example, work already submitted for credit in this or another course, or done for some extracurricular activity) is not acceptable. Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be acceptable; consult with me beforehand. Any work done outside this course, if you are allowed to use it at all, must also be documented.

Failure to document any source will automatically harm your grade and may be prosecuted as plagiarism, even if it is accidental.

 
How to do well
 
I will expect to see evidence of substantial, new work by every person in each group. Just downloading a web page and reading it to the class or copying it onto a PowerPoint slide, for example, is not acceptable. You need to show that you've done significantly more with the information than merely acquiring it.
