Sample 1.
Answering some exam questions is relatively easy. But even for easy questions, there are better and worse answers.

One exam question might be, "Cephalus believes that justice is being truthful and giving back what you have taken from another (Plato, 7). Why does Socrates reject this definition?"

Want to get about a "C"? Give an answer like this:

Socrates says it wouldn't be just to give weapons back to a man when he was insane.

Want to get an "A"? Give an answer like this:

Socrates gives an example where following Cephalus' definition of justice would lead us to do something unjust. Suppose someone loaned you weapons while he was sane, but then wants them back when he is insane. To give the weapons back to him, or to be completely truthful with him (maybe by telling him truthfully where they are? Socrates does not say), would result in harm. Socrates says that would be unjust (Plato, 7).

Exam grades depend on how well you demonstrate you understand and have thought about the stuff presented in the course. The second answer does a much better job at that.


Sample 2.

Suppose you are asked to define terms, like "moral skepticism," "moral subjectivism,"  "cultural relativism," and "ethical objectivism." 

Want to get about a "C"? Turn in something like this:
Moral skepticism: there are no moral truths.
Moral subjectivism: morality is a personal decision.
Cultural relativism: morality is based on culture.
Ethical objectivism: some rules apply universally.

Nothing in this answer is false, but it's still not very good. It says a lot less than was discussed in class. It shows minimal understanding; it's about a "C."

Want to earn an "A"? Turn in something like this:

These are different views about the basis of moral rules--that is, about how moral rules get their validity, if any.

Moral skepticism admits that there are moral rules, but it says they have no basis, or none we can know for sure. So, skeptics think there are no real moral obligations. Moral skepticism suggests that ethics are a kind of fraud.

Unlike moral skepticism, moral subjectivism says that moral rules can be justified, and they are justified by your opinion. In other words, subjectivism holds that an act is right only if you think it is; otherwise it is not.

Cultural relativism also says that moral rules can be justified. Unlike subjectivism, cultural relativism says the basis of moral rules is culture: an act is right only if your culture approves of it; otherwise not.

Ethical objectivism says that at least some (but not necessarily all) moral rules have a basis independent of personal opinion or culture, such that they apply to everyone regardless of what their individual judgment or society says.

This shows a clear understanding that these are views about whether and how moral principles can be justified, and clearly states each view's take on the matter.


Sample 3.
Some questions are harder. Questions with highlighted numbers (like this: 6. ) will be worth more points on exams because they call for more extensive, thoughtful answers, sometimes requiring you to present and support your own opinion.

Here's an example: imagine a situation where a conservative Saudi Muslim, Azim, condemns Beth's immodest clothing and Beth--a liberal American atheist--condemns Azim's attempt to control her behavior. I might ask, "Explain how Aquinas might resolve the dispute between Azim and Beth. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? What’s most important here is to say how Aquinas would approach this matter."

Here you are asked to take ideas from Aquinas and apply them to a possible situation. This is a dispute where Azim and Beth dislike each other's behavior and this dislike is connected with moral rules of their respective cultures.

Want to get an "F"? Try turning in something like this:
Since Aquinas is a Christian, he thinks that both Azim (a Muslim) and Beth (an atheist) ought to convert to Christianity and obey its rules. Beth could then show her face in public and Azim would respect this behavior, and both of them would avoid going to Hell.


Ahem. This answer is not factually wrong, so I wouldn't give it a zero. But you could give this answer based merely on knowing Aquinas was Christian, without ever doing the reading or paying attention in class. So this is a bad answer.

Want an "A"? Try something like this:
Based on the reading we have, it’s hard to tell exactly how Aquinas might resolve Azim & Beth’s dispute. Aquinas gives us a universal moral standard of natural law that might be used to settle disputes between people from different cultures. But it’s not clear that this standard (at least what we have of it) tells us what to do to settle this case.

On one hand, Aquinas definitely thinks there are some universal moral truths. There are some precepts we can figure out by observing human nature (Aquinas, 4-5), and anyone’s behavior may be judged by those precepts. Moreover, any particular rule in a given society may be evaluated by reference to those precepts. Because of this, it seems that for Aquinas there may be some way of resolving this dispute--that is, some way we can get beyond the unproductive head-butting of subjectivism (which leaves us with “he said vs. she said”) or cultural relativism (which leaves us with “his culture says vs. her culture says”). 

More specifically, Aquinas says that we all seek the good and that we are naturally inclined towards some particular goods, including social living (Summa, 5; cf. De Regno, 1). He says that "whatever pertains to this inclination belongs to the natural law; for instance...to avoid offending those among whom one has to live" (Summa, 5). This means that natural law tells us to do what will help us live socially, and that includes avoiding offending people. Also, he emphatically says that law always tells us to seek the common good, not just our own private good, because as members of society we are like parts of a whole (Summa, 2). Azim and Beth are offending each other, so we could at least conclude that their dispute should not continue, especially if it could lead to any disruption of society.

On the other hand, Aquinas allows that a given moral rule--not one of the precepts of the law of nature, but a human-made moral rule derived from the precepts--may not apply in all particular cases. For example, he says it is generally right to give back what you have borrowed, but not if they would be used "to fight against one's country" (Summa, 5). Aquinas says the precepts--which he sometimes calls "general principles" (Summa, 5) apply to everyone. But to apply the precepts we derive "secondary principles" (Summa, 6)--the particular rules of our society--which might not always apply. He identifies two ways in which a given rule may fail to apply universally. First, it may apply in some societies but not others. A modern example of this is the rule of driving on the right side of the road. Second, like the rule about giving back what is "held in trust" (Summa, 5), there may be circumstances where justice requires an exception to a rule that is generally legitimate.

So Aquinas gives us a general approach to discovering the universal moral law: identifying first principles, then deriving secondary principles from them which we can then apply to particular situations.

But given what is said in the readings and the prompt, it is not clear how this might resolve Azim and Beth's disagreement. Is Azim's rule about women's public attire more clearly justified by the precepts than Beth's, or vice versa? Can we deduce from the precepts some rule about how to adjudicate their dispute? The answers to these questions are not obvious. Perhaps it would be helpful if we knew more. For example, if the dispute were taking place in Saudi Arabia--which takes rules about women's attire much more seriously than the United States--Aquinas might say that the potential for social disruption and giving offense to others is lower if Beth wears a veil, and that therefore she ought to do so.

Wow, right? That's impressive. This would get an 'A.'

This answer does not say "Aquinas would say that Azim and/or Beth should do X." But that's fine, because the answer gives reasons, grounded in the Aquinas reading, for why it's hard to come to such a neat conclusion. That's what to do here: show how Aquinas thinks about a situation.

However, if your answer shows a way that Aquinas could resolve their dispute which is consistent with his general principles, that'd be great, too.

Of course I wouldn't expect you to give this exact answer! Nor would I expect you to quote Aquinas or give page numbers [since you will have access to sources on the final exam, you must cite all sources]. I put them here to help you find the material in the text if you want.
