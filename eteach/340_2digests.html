Two Digests of Government

Jeremy Anderson
Here are two "digests" of Locke's Second Treatise of Government--that is, brief summaries of it. Of course these are not substitutes for reading Locke. They are meant to accompany the reading. Also, I don't mean for what I say here to override your own interpretation of what Locke has to say. Feel free to disagree with me.

The first digest is a tribute to George Boolos, who wrote "Goedel's Second Incompleteness Theorem Explained in Words of One Syllable." The second is set out in more or less logical, stepwise fashion, with references. Both of them share the same goal: to show that underneath his prolixity and repetition, Locke has a fairly tightly-argued kernel to his political theory. Necessarily, much that is interesting in Locke has been omitted.

My reading of Locke is obviously Hobbesian, but what's wrong with that? Actually my reading is Kavka-esque, since it's informed by the conceptual framework the late Prof. Greg Kavka gave me for understanding social contract theories. They generally have three parts: (1) they start with a "decision situation" or state of nature, (2) they end up with some form of government that is claimed to be legitimate (a set of "output principles"), and (3) they provide an argument to get you from the former to the latter.

Reference numbers refer to sections (paragraph numbers) in the Second Treatise.



 
First Digest of Government
Locke's Second Treatise of Government Explained
in Words of One Syllable
  
When we're all out on our own, we have some nice rights God gave us, and we are all free. Since God gave us those rights, they're ours to keep. No one can take them from us, but we can give them up if we want to. Or we can lose them if we do bad things.

We can't just do as we please, though. We have a law we should all stick to, and we all can tell what it is. But the thing is, when we're all out on our own we don't all stick to it. Or we don't all see eye to eye on how to stick to it. So we don't have much fun when we keep all those rights. We fight, and life sucks.

Locke says that if we just get three things, life will cease to suck--well, at least it will cease to suck in some big ways. What we need is one clear set of laws we all stick to, a fair judge to tell us how to stick to those laws, and cops to make us stick to them when we don't feel like it. To get these things, each of us has to say, in a way, "I give up my right to do as I please. From now on, I'll stick to this law and do what the judge says. And if some guys don't stick to the law, I'll help make them." Then we are one, and we can all be free and have fun.

We can set up a queen or king, or else a group to rule us. But we all have to keep in mind that the law and the judge and the cops can go just so far. The whole point was to have them keep us safe and work for our good. They have to look out for all of us. If they don't, we have to think, "Why did we give up the rights God gave us?"

But once in a while the law, the judge, or the cops screw up. They may work for their own good and not for ours. Or they may think they don't have to do what the law says. Or some judge or cop may think he gets to be in charge of his own case. If they do that, we don't have to take it. We gave them their jobs, so we can fire them.

But that's such a big deal that we don't do it on a whim, or for each time a judge, cop, or law errs. We save it for the big stuff, what Locke calls "a long train" of bad things that make it clear to us that the folks who rule us are not on our side.

Some might think that what Locke says will make us want to fire the folks in charge all the time, which would be a bad thing to do, a big waste and a big risk to our lives. But Locke says no. He thinks that if all of us (and that means the judge and the cop and those who pass the laws, too) know that we can fire the ones in charge if they screw up too much, they won't screw up so much. And that would be good. It would mean that they work to keep us safe and sound.
 
 
 
 
Second Digest of Government
The Academic-Sounding Version
 
1. In the State of Nature (SN), we naturally have the liberty to be judges and executors of the Law of Nature in our own cases (4, 6-8), and sometimes in others' too. This liberty is a natural right, given to us by God--so it can't be taken away unless we consent to give it up or forfeit it through misbehavior.

2. There are certain "inconveniences" present in the SN, namely various invasions by other people which prevent us from enjoying our liberty (123).

3. These inconveniences exist because the SN lacks certain things:
    (a) a law or "legislative" we all submit to (124)
    (b) an impartial judge who decides disputes under that law (a judiciary, 125), and
    (c) the power to enforce the judge's decisions (an executive, 126).

4. These deficiencies arise out of the exercise of our natural liberties. Thus, if we want to escape them, we must give up our natural liberty and unite into a "civil society" (87) and accept the rule of law (129-31). Consenting to legitimate legal restraints--somewhat paradoxically--assures our freedom (57).

5. The goal for which we left the SN establishes an important limit on government: since we only gave up our natural liberty in order to escape the SN's deficiencies, the sole purpose of government is to remedy those deficiencies by working for the "Peace, Safety, and publick good of the People" (131; cf. Chapter XI).

6. Thus, while government may take various forms (132), it must always conform to certain fundamental laws: the positive law of establishing the legislative power (without which none of the SN's inconveniences can be remedied (note that premise 3(a) above is a necessary precondition of 3(b) and 3(c)), and the natural law of preserving society--which is the protector of each of us (134).

7. If government fails to conform to these limits and laws, it fails to do what we created it to do: protect us from those inconveniences mentioned above, no more no less.

8. Such failures may take different forms. Among these are tyranny, in which rule is exercised only for the rulers' good (199). This violates the limit established in premise 5 above, as well as (arguably) the fundamental natural law in premise 6. Another form of failure is absolute government, in which rulers are not accountable to anyone and are allowed to judge their own cases. Absolute government is really no government at all (90-1). That's because it is an instance of two of the inconveniences of the SN: it is an instance of 3(a) because the ruler is above the law, and it is an instance of 3(b) because absolute rulers are not impartial, at least not when judging their own cases.

9. If government fails to do what we created it to do, the people (i.e., we who gave up our natural liberty in order to be safe) have the right to alter or abolish it (149).

10. And since the government is appointed by the people--it is their "deputy" (240)--the people are to judge when this is appropriate (240-2).

11. Such action is not appropriate for every mismanagement, since revolution is dangerous after all. But it is to be expected after a "long train of abuses" (225).

12. Rather than encouraging rebellion, Locke thinks his doctrine is the best defense against it (226). This is because it puts those who are most likely to subvert the law--namely, those in power--on notice that they may justly face violent opposition if they do so.
