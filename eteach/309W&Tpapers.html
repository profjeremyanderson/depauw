1. General requirements for all papers in this course
  • Take a side. Your paper should be argumentative rather than expository. It should defend a conclusion which other smart people might well disagree with. This should be your paper's major focus; there should be no more background material than is necessary to explain and give perspective to the arguments you present.
          To take a stand effectively your paper must be fair while being critical.
           Your paper's conclusions should not depend on mere expressions of personal opinion or armchair arguments. It is not enough to explain why you think something is so; you must strive to convince a reasonable reader that any right-thinking person should agree with you.

  • Format. Your paper must be submitted as a Word (.docx or .doc) or Rich Text Format (.rtf) file, double spaced, one-inch margins, numbered pages, 12-point Times New Roman or similar font, with a a word count for the body of the paper (that is, not including your name, end- or footnotes, bibliography, etc.) at the top with your identifying information.
          It should be free of mechanical errors (grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc.). Your computer's grammar- and spell-checking features are not adequate; you will have to proofread your paper yourself.
          Do not turn in your first draft, no matter how great a writer you are. Complete the assignment and then let it sit for at least a few hours and then come back and re-write it to make it even clearer and more complete.
     
  • Papers should be logically organized and clear. Here are some suggestions:
          First, give a brief introduction which includes a thesis and road map. Your thesis is the overall conclusion your paper will argue for; state it up front and clearly. Also give a road map to your paper--i.e., briefly summarize the the main steps you will take to support your thesis, in the order you will make them. Resist the temptation to do much more than this in your introduction. The intro for a short paper should be no more than half a page; the intro for a long paper no more than a page.
          Organize your paper as an argument: a series of statements which, taken together, logically imply a conclusion. Your thesis is the overall conclusion, and each paragraph should make a point which contributes to the argument for your thesis. The sentences in each paragraph, then, should explain and/or support that paragraph's point. I will expect the paper to proceed according to the road map given in the introduction.
          Strive for simplicity. As far as style goes, I am much more impressed by writers who express ideas clearly using simple language than by those who try to impress me with big words and long sentences.
  • DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to all assignments in the course.
    • Any time you refer to or use any ideas that are not your own, you must cite them. Any time you use another person’s words you must put them in quotation marks and cite them. It does not matter whether the material came from assigned texts, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, a web page, your mother, or whatever: if the idea isn’t yours, cite it; if the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes and cite it. DePauw provides lots of info on understanding academic integrity and how to avoid plagiarism on this helpful page.
    • Failure to cite as required will automatically lower the paper's grade, potentially down to failing. It may also be prosecuted as plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If it is prosecuted successfully, the minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all. So it is vital that you keep track of whose words and ideas you are using. If you have any sources to cite outside our textbook or class discussion, list them on a “Works Cited” page in addition to citing them in the text.
      • This is easy to do. As you work on the paper, make a note of everything you read and anyone you talk to about it, keeping track of what ideas and words came from each source. Then, if you decide to use any ideas or words from these notes, cite the sources.
    • No research outside of assigned texts is needed for the short papers in this course. But if you use any outside sources you must cite them. Citations of outside sources should be given in Chicago or Turabian format, using the parenthetical citations/reference list style. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page.
      • For more information about citation, quoting, paraphrasing, etc. consult Charles Lipson's book Doing Honest Work in College. If you want a format suggestion, I happen to like the "author-date" system of the Chicago Style (or the very similar "parenthetical citations/reference list" Turabian style), with one exception: the Chicago manual says that some sources may be omitted from your reference list or bibliography, but in papers for me you must include EVERY source in your list of references.
      • Your list of sources must include:
        • Full bibliographic information for any print source aside from assigned texts.
        • Full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.whitehouse.gov" but the full URL, so that I can paste it into my browser and find the exact material you relied upon. 
        • If citing a journal article accessed electronically, give both the bibliographic data plus the full URL. Use the "stable" URL. 

  • Be judicious about your sources for any research beyond assigned texts (as is required for the research paper and optional for the short essays). This means three things:
    1. First, don't limit yourself to just one kind of source.
      • For example, some students simply do a search on JSTOR and use any relevant-sounding articles near the top of the search results. That is not the worst way to go (the worst is to do that with Google), but it is lazy and, worse, is unlikely to find you the really good stuff. You should also search the Library catalog for relevant books, search other article databases (EBSCO, the Philosopher's Index), consult a reference librarian, etc. Some relatively good and interesting sources are available on my Philosophy Research Links page, which has a section devoted specifically to military matters.
    2. Second, be careful about the quality of your sources. The internet, for example, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, but to reach them you have to dig through a lot of garbage. 
      • Peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields are best. If you are unclear about how to tell which periodicals are scholarly, take 3 minutes and watch this video.
      • Among the worst are websites of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications. Reliance on these can seriously hurt your project.
      • Around the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are nice places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia). You may use encyclopedia-type sources to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources for the content of your final paper will count against it.
    3. Third, you may cite lecture, discussion, reading responses, forum posts, etc. only for material that is not in any text. For anything that is in a text (i.e., any time you make a claim about what an author says), you must cite the text. If you cite material from class discussion, do so like this: (Phil 309B class discussion 2/28/2012).

      When in doubt about the appropriateness of a given source, consult with me.
    • Here is a suggestion for getting started in your research. First, identify the names of the people and the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic.
  • Although not required, it would be a good idea to seek advice from DePauw's Writing Center prior to each paper's due date.


2. What makes better papers better
  • Meticulous proofreading, and carefully following the format requirements given above.
  • Careful, logical organization of the paper as a whole and of individual paragraphs.
  • Presenting even viewpoints you oppose with clarity, care, and charity (as opposed to, say, whacking straw men or presenting opposition so briefly it doesn't get a fair hearing).
  • Relying on superior sources of information when research is employed (e.g., scholarly articles as opposed to Wikipedia etc.).
  • Providing arguments and information that are clearly relevant to your thesis.
  • Providing relevant empirical data where appropriate, as opposed to armchair arguments.
  • Arriving at conclusions that are clearly implied by the sum total of your arguments and information, as opposed to going off on a tangent at the end, or stating a conclusion that goes beyond the evidence provided.
  • When providing original arguments, demonstrating careful, sophisticated thought.
  • No filler, no bull. For example, do not tell me how long humankind has pondered your topic or give me Webster's definition of anything.


3. Short paper-specific requirements
During the semester you will write two short essays. Each will be 1000-1500 words (that is, roughly3-5 pages) long. In each, you will need to take a position regarding some of the material we have studied and support it.

To do this effectively in a short space you will need to limit the scope of your essay. Take on a small question rather than a large one. For example, do not try to show that realism is preferable to pacifism, or that humanitarian intervention is always wrong. (These would be challenging to do even in your long paper.) It might also be too much to take on the argument of an entire text. For example, Reader's "Making Pacifism Plausible" presents arguments regarding war-ism, having a war-machine, the justifiability of killing in war, and hit squads. That is too much to take on in a short paper. Instead, I recommend choosing some particular argument in a text. For example, is Reader's argument that self-defense cannot justify killing in war sound? How effective is Jones' rebuttal to Bush's argument for invading Iraq? Or you might set aside the question of whether an author's conclusion is correct and focus more on the quality of an argument, critically evaluating it.
One place to look for potential topics is in your own reading responses: what portion of the readings has most intrigued you? Is there a particular argument or issue you want to write about? Consider the cases we have read about in Walzer. Do you think Walzer came to the right conclusion regarding a given case? Consider some case we have read about in news articles: fleeing Iraqi soldiers, or shooting the boys who picked up a weapon, or designing ordnance to wound, or another. Do the actions depicted accord with the rules of just war? What does that tell us? Feel free to discuss possible topics with me.

4. Research paper - specific requirements
You'll be writing a research paper of about 3000-4000 words (not counting footnotes, endnotes or bibliography; i.e., about 10-14 pages) or so. The point of this assignment is for you to pursue in depth some controversial course-related topic that is important to you.

Philosophy majors: this paper can contribute to satisfying the Writing In the Major requirement if your finished paper is revised in light of feedback on a draft and it earns at least a C (73% or higher).

Of course I have some ideas for what you could write about, and have provided some general topic ideas and possible paper strategies below. If one of the ideas below appeals to you, great. But I prefer you pick a subject that matters to you and explore it by researching and writing about it. If not, come see me and we'll create a topic for you—but don't write a paper about something without checking with me first.

Your paper should demonstrate understanding of those sources. This is a suggested minimum (i.e., necessary to get a passing grade); to write a really good paper you may well need more. Regardless of how many sources you rely on, you must be judicious about them as described above.

5. Possible research paper (and presentation) topics
Appropriate topics include anything within the scope of this course. I urge you to think back over what we've covered and ask yourself which topic, reading, argument, etc. most excited you, inspired you, or annoyed you. Build a topic based on that. Or look back through your reading responses for ideas. Or go through the tables of contents and indexes of our texts, including what we have not covered, and see what sparks your interest. Or come and talk with me about your interests.

Some possible strategies you might use in your papers are (these are just suggestions; your paper need not do any of these):
  • Show that position X seems plausible but really suffers from significant objection O. (For example: "So-and -so believes that threatening to use nuclear weapons is never justified, and gives appealing reasons for this position. But I'll show that (at least under certain circumstances) it is nevertheless justified.")
  • Show that position X seems to suffer from objection O, but in fact it does not.
  • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that X may be correct but the justification is faulty. (For example, you might like Realism but dislike a particular argument for it, so show that although that argument may be popular or influential it's really not good.)
  • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that although the justification may seem bad it's really not.
  • Show that P is a vexing problem, for which S is a more promising solution than some alternative(s). (For example: "Here are some proposed responses to al Qaeda-type terrorism; of them, S is superior.")
  • Show that P is a vexing problem for which S seems like a promising solution, but really S is bad (and, perhaps, that we should do T instead).
You might think about...
  • Realism vs. Pacifism vs. Just-War Theory:
    • Is there a particular problem with one of them that makes it unacceptable?
    • Or, is there some particular advantage one has that puts it ahead of the others?
    • Is there a particular argument or passage you want to explore?
    • Is there an interesting argument for or against any of them we have not looked at?
  • Jus ad Bellum:
    • Are the traditional criteria appropriate/justifiable? Should they be different?
    • Is it possible to fulfill them? (If not, what then?)
    • Would a war that fulfilled them truly be just?
    • Is there one criterion you find especially interesting/problematic?
    • Look at one particular war or intervention and critically evaluate its justice. (Sadly, the possible examples are legion.)
    • Is there some war or intervention that seems possible or imminent? (Again, examples are legion.) Critically evaluate its potential justice.
  • Jus in Bello:
    • Are the traditional criteria appropriate/justifiable? Should they be different?
    • Is it possible to fulfill them?
    • Would a campaign or engagement that fulfilled them truly be just?
    • Is there one criterion you find especially interesting/problematic?
    • Look at one particular campaign or engagement or reprisal etc. and critically evaluate its justice.
    • Terrorism, counterinsurgency, and other asymmetrical warfare:
      • Do the rules change? How? Why?
      • Consider some controversial tactic(s) such as detention, torture, attacks on civilians ("civilians"?), etc. When, if ever, are they excusable, or justified?
...and so on. These are all very general; in your paper you will almost certainly want to narrow the topic to something more specific, perhaps considering some particular case or type of case. In class we have identified many interesting and significant issues, so your recollections, notes, and the class' reading responses are a rich source of topics.
