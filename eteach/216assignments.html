2. Baruch Spinoza
We will read Part I of The Ethics (pp. 144-64) and spend about 3-4 meetings on it.

The Ethics is famously difficult. Spinoza lays out his general philosophy with extreme care, in the manner of Euclidean geometry. Here is my recommendation for how to approach it:
  • First, read the very first paragraph of the Appendix on p. 160. Here he summarizes the main points of Part I, so this gives you an idea of where his overall argument is headed. Read it several times.
  • Then, read through Part I, paying most attention to:
    • The Definitions (p. 144), especially D1-D6
    • The Axioms (pp. 144-5), especially A1, A4, and A5
    • Propositions P5, P6, P7, P11, P14, P16, P17, P29, P31, P32, and P33.
    • The remainder of the Appendix (pp. 160-4).


3. Gottfried Leibniz (pronounced GOT-freed LYE-bnits)
Assignment 7:
The Principles of Philosophy, or the Monadology (pp. 275-83)
The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology (PDF)

Note: The Monadology systematically and briefly lays out the main ideas of Leibniz' metaphysics and epistemology. It is a pretty challenging read. But here is the upside: if you can understand what he's saying in the first couple dozen sections (numbered paragraphs) you will have a pretty good handle on Leibniz' whole system of thought, and a basis for understanding the rest. So we will begin by paying very careful attention to those opening sections. I hope the handout ("The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology") will be helpful for this.

Assignment 9: Primary Truths and A New System of Nature (pp. 265-74)
Note: Primary Truths has a structure: Leibniz begins on p. 265 with a definition of what "primary truths" are, and discusses that definition; part of it is that when a statement is true its "predicate...is always [contained] in the subject." Philosophers today call such statements "analytic" (as in, "it's analytic that 'bachelors are male'"). Leibniz then goes on to articulate, and explain, a series of claims which he thinks are true in this way. Those are the statements in italics. One of these is that "There is no atom" (267). This may be confusing, since in the Monadology he declared that there are monads, and that monads are the "true atoms of nature" (M2). In PT, the "atoms" he's talking about are not monads but rather the sort proposed by philosophers like Epicurus, which were tiny bits of matter, which (according to Epicurus and others) cannot be divided into parts, are surrounded by empty space, and which are passive--that is, they can be bumped around by other atoms. Leibniz argues that there are no such things as these Epicurean atoms.

4. John Locke
An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (called the Essay for short) is divided into books, which are divided into chapters, which are divided into numbered paragraphs called sections. The standard reference scheme for the Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = is the book, Y = the chapter, and Z = the section. So II.III.1 refers to Book II, Chapter III, section 1 (p. 329).
