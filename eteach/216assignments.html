Assignment 10:
  • First Truths (PDF here on our Google drive)
  • A New System (PDF here on our Google drive)
First Truths goes like this: Leibniz begins on p. 1 with a definition of what "first truths" are, and discusses that definition. Part of it is that when a statement is true its "predicate...is always [contained] in the subject" (2). Philosophers today call such statements "analytic" -- as in, "it's analytic that 'bachelors are male'." Leibniz then goes on to state, and explain, a series of claims which he thinks are true in this way. Those are the statements in boldface.
       Some of those claims may seem pretty lame because they go against what modern science tells us. An example of this is "There is no vacuum" (5). Modern physics tells us that material things consist almost entirely of empty space. Nevertheless, look at how Leibniz argues for these claims.
       One claim may be pretty confusing: that "There is no atom" (5). This may be confusing because in the Monadology Leibniz declared that there are monads, and that monads are the "true atoms of Nature" (Monadology, sec. 3). In First Truths, the "atoms" he's talking about are not monads but rather the sort of atoms proposed by philosophers like Epicurus, which were tiny bits of matter -- which, according to Epicurus and others, cannot be divided into parts (yet, oddly, have varying shapes!), are surrounded by empty space, and which can be bumped around by other atoms. Leibniz is arguing that there are no such things as these Epicurean atoms.

3. Baruch Spinoza (1632 - 1677)
We will read Part I of Ethics Demonstrated in Geometric Order (PDF here on our Google drive), usually just called the Ethics, and spend about 4-5 meetings on it.

The Ethics is famously hard. Expect to find it puzzling.

Spinoza lays out his general philosophy with extreme care, emulating Euclidean geometry much more closely and explicitly than Descartes or Leibniz. If you remember your high school geometry (postulates, definitions, axioms, and how they are used to prove theorems), that will be helpful. But Spinoza is still tough to follow.

Here is how we will approach it:
  • First, read the very first paragraph of the Appendix (left column on p. 18, especially the bullet points). Here Spinoza summarizes the main points of Part I, so this gives you an idea of where his overall argument is headed. Read it several times.
  • Then, read through Part I. Spinoza starts with definitions and axioms and then uses them to prove the propositions. So, understanding the axioms and definitions will help you understand all that follows. But some parts are more important for us than others! Pay most attention to:
    • Definitions D1-D6
    • Axioms A1, A4, and A5
    • Propositions:
      • P5 (no two substances can have the same attribute in common)
      • P6 (substances cannot produce other substances)
      • P7 (it pertains to the nature of a substance to exist)
      • P11 (God necessarily exists)
      • P14 (God is the only substance)
      • P16 (God causes everything)
      • P17 (God alone determines God's actions)
      • P29 (nothing is contingent; everything has to be as it is)
      • P32 (there is no free will, even for God)
      • P33 (God could not have produced things in any other way)
    • The remainder of the Appendix (pp. 18 - 22).

  • To help understand what Spinoza is saying, try following along with my lecture notes on Spinoza (PDF here on our Google drive). 

    • If you'd like some more help with Spinoza, the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Spinoza has quite a nice explanation of the main points of the Ethics. See sections 2 and 2.1 (which covers our portion, Part I) here.

4. John Locke (1632 - 1704)
We'll read pieces of Locke's An Essay Concerning Human Understanding, called the Essay for short.

The Essay is divided into books, which are divided into chapters, which are divided into numbered paragraphs called sections.

The standard reference scheme for the Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = the book, Y = the chapter, and Z = the section. So II.iii.1 refers to Book II, Chapter iii, section 1 (p. 24).

I have assembled all the Locke readings into a single PDF titled "4 Locke All-in-One PDF," which is here on our Google drive). Page numbers in the assignments refer to the page numbers at the bottom center of each page in the PDF.


Assignment 12 (page numbers in these assignments refer to the page numbers at the bottom center of each page in the PDF):
  • In Book I:
    • I.i to I.ii (pp. 1-8 in the all-in-one PDF of Locke here on our Google drive): introduction; against innate ideas
  • In Book II:
    • II.i.1-9 (pp. 18-20 in the all-in-one Locke PDF); ideas in general, and their origin
    • II.ii to II.xii (pp. 23-45 in the all-in-one Locke PDF); simple ideas; perception; retention; discerning; complex ideas; II.viii on primary & secondary qualities is especially noteworthy.
      • In II.viii, Locke explains what primary and secondary qualities are in several different ways. Some are very confusing. I recommend:
        • Section 8: definition of "quality"
        • In sections 9 and 10, Locke lists the simple ideas caused by primary qualities and those caused by secondary qualities
        • Sections 14-15 and 23 (pp. 31, 33) have the most succinct and straightforward explanations of what primary vs. secondary qualities are.
