2. Baruch Spinoza (1632 - 1677)
We will read Part I of Ethics Demonstrated in Geometric Order (PDF here on our Google drive), usually just called the Ethics, and spend about 3-4 meetings on it.

The Ethics is famously hard, so don't feel bad if you find it puzzling. Most of us do! Spinoza lays out his general philosophy with extreme care, in the manner of Euclidean geometry, but it's still tough to follow. Here is my recommendation for how to approach it:
  • First, read the very first paragraph of the Appendix (left column on p. 18, especially the bullet points). Here Spinoza summarizes the main points of Part I, so this gives you an idea of where his overall argument is headed. Read it several times.
  • Then, read through Part I, paying most attention to:
    • The Definitions (p. 1), especially D1-D6
    • The Axioms (pp. 1 - 2), especially A1, A4, and A5
    • Propositions P5, P6, P7, P11, P14, P16, P17, P29, P31, P32, and P33.
    • The remainder of the Appendix (pp. 18 - 22).

3. Gottfried Leibniz (pronounced GOT-freed LYE-bnits; 1646 - 1716)
Assignment 7:
  • The Principles of Philosophy known as Monadology -- usually just called the Monadology (PDF here on our Google drive)
  • The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology (PDF here on our Google drive)
The Monadology systematically and briefly lays out the main ideas of Leibniz' metaphysics and epistemology. It is a pretty challenging read. (So, don't feel bad if you find it confusing.) But here is the upside: if you can understand what he's saying in the first couple dozen sections (numbered paragraphs) you will have a pretty good handle on Leibniz' whole system of thought, and a basis for understanding the rest. So we will begin by paying very careful attention to those opening sections. I hope the handout ("The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology") will be helpful for this.

Assignment 8:
  • Discourse on Metaphysics (PDF here on our Google drive)
  • Road Map to Leibniz (PDF here on our Google drive)

Assignment 9:
  • First Truths (PDF here on our Google drive)
  • A New System (PDF here on our Google drive)
First Truths has a structure: Leibniz begins on p. 1 with a definition of what "first truths" are, and discusses that definition; part of it is that when a statement is true its "predicate...is always [contained] in the subject" (2). Philosophers today call such statements "analytic" (as in, "it's analytic that 'bachelors are male'"). Leibniz then goes on to articulate, and explain, a series of claims which he thinks are true in this way. Those are the statements in boldface.
       One of these is that "There is no atom" (5). This may be confusing, since in the Monadology he declared that there are monads, and that monads are the "true atoms of Nature" (Monadology, sec. 3). In First Truths, the "atoms" he's talking about are not monads but rather the sort of atoms proposed by philosophers like Epicurus, which were tiny bits of matter -- which (according to Epicurus and others) cannot be divided into parts, are surrounded by empty space, and which can be bumped around by other atoms. Leibniz argues that there are no such things as these Epicurean atoms.

4. John Locke (1632 - 1704)

An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (called the Essay for short) is divided into books, which are divided into chapters, which are divided into numbered paragraphs called sections. The standard reference scheme for the Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = is the book, Y = the chapter, and Z = the section. So II.III.1 refers to Book II, Chapter III, section 1 (p. 329).
