Assignment description

Here's what the syllabus says

The presentation will entail you forming small groups of 2 or 3 if the class is large, or working alone or in pairs if the class is small. ...Each individual/group will take responsibility for researching a topic within the philosophy of law and presenting it to the class. Your presentations are part of the course (their contents will be fair game for the final exam, for example), so they need to be done well, and I will help you do well. I can suggest presentation topics, but it's best if you choose what interests you.

Here's some more detail:

During the course I help guide you through a bunch of topics in the philosophy of law: criminal law, tort law, constitutional law, excuses, punishment, Shimp, David Cash, interpretation, legal moralism, executing dogs, blah, blah, blah. Most of these issues have been covered only superficially in class, however, and you might want to understand one of them in more depth. Or you might want to explore something very different. Fine! You will need to pick something of interest to you, find a fellow student or two with similar interests and form your own groups of 2-3 people, get me to approve your topic (which is easy), research your topic, and present what you have learned to the class.

If you don't already have a topic in mind, there are many places to seek inspiration. Consider what brought you into the class: is there some topic or problem in law or public policy you wanted to explore? Some real-life event you want to examine and discuss? Think about your major: how in what ways does it connect with philosophy of law? In our textbook, look at the study questions for each section, the footnotes at the end of many of the articles, and articles and cases we haven't read. Look at the topics listed at the bottom of the Papers page, and your and others' reading responses. Come see me to discuss your interests.

Most important: pick a topic that interests you.

The class will need to do some prep work for presentations as well. You'll create an assignment to prepare the class for your presentation. The assignment could be a court case or excerpt from one, some news article(s), a scholarly article, and/or video or other media. It should help us all become familiar with the topic and some of its key issues, and thereby enhance discussion. Since these will be assignments, responses to them will be required -- and count -- just like all other reading responses in the course.

The deadline for choosing a topic and forming a group is 6pm Monday after Fall Break (October 24): you will need to e-mail me to let me know who is in your group, and give me your proposed topic and at least one alternate topic in case your first choice is already taken.

The sooner you clear your topic and group with me, the better the choice of dates you'll have (not to mention having more time to prep), so I encourage you to form groups (if the class is large) and notify me of topics sooner.

By Wednesday after Fall Break (October 26) at the beginning of class we will officially assign dates for you to present. Once a date is set, it will be subject to the late policy stated in the course syllabus. Use the class open forum here on Moodle to find partners and air out ideas.

Research & sources

Good research is vital to your success. Learn as much as you can about your topic to present it effectively to a group of people who are as smart and critical as you are.

Materials assigned in this course don't count as research. While you may refer to them in your work--and it can be good to relate what you're doing to what we've already discussed--your work should mostly or entirely come from other sources.

Getting started

First, see me. Your group should meet with me at least twice to discuss your plans.

Identify the names of any people and/or the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the info from reference sources, (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and and databases to find scholarly material on your topic. (Here are all the databases, and here are some philosophy-focused ones, plus some online philosophy encyclopedias). Google Scholar is good, too, and has the familiar Google interface. (Generally avoid plain Google; too much garbage there.)

As you go, look for things that might be good to assign to the class.

Choose your sources wisely

First, don't look in just one place. You may miss some really good stuff. So yes, use JSTOR, but also use the Library's search, Google Scholar, the Philosopher's Index, etc.

Second, be choosy about the quality of your sources: 
    • Best: peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields. The more of these you can use, the better.

    • Worst: websites or other writings of unknown origin, sources with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications or no qualifications. Relying on these can seriously hurt your project.

      • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, that's a strong reason not to use that source.

    • In the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are good places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia, since some of its articles are just abysmal and its overall quality is unreliable). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources are good to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but relying much on such sources for your presentation may count against it.
DePauw's Library has many great books and some poor ones. The internet, generally speaking, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, among lots of trash. See me if you have any questions about what source materials to use.


What it should look like

Plan to take roughly 10-15 minutes per member in a group presentation, about 25-30 minutes in a solo presentation. Good presentations often go longer, but passable ones are seldom shorter. You might...
  • Have each person present a different perspective on the issue, or
  • Stage a debate, or
  • Lecture the way I sometimes do, or
  • Make a film, or include clips from movies on your topic, or
  • Do something completely different. Imitating what others have done can get old.
Include something that involves the class in your presentation: an exercise, activity, etc. Have questions prepared for the class to get discussion going and keep it going.

Visual aids (writing on the board, creating posters, doing PowerPoint presentations, etc.) are encouraged. Handouts which summarize the main points or arguments are strongly encouraged.

Note to Macintosh users: if you prepare a PowerPoint using your Mac it might not look right when played using a PC such as the computers in our tech classrooms, or a partner's laptop. So you should either prepare PowerPoints using a PC or make sure you have the video adapter necessary to hook up your Mac laptop to the video cable in our classroom.

Present more than one side of an issue, and be fair about it. By "fair" I do not mean making all sides look equally good; I mean not presenting any view or argument as less (or more) good than it really is. Philosophy is, after all, a search for truth, not merely an attempt to win a point. You may take a side if you wish, but not by misrepresenting or ignoring opposition.

The most important thing is to demonstrate you understand your material. If you know your stuff, you do not need PowerPoint. (Of course you may use it, but you shouldn't need it.) If you do not know your stuff, no technology will make up for it.


Documentation & deadlines

Document your work
Without your documentation, the assignment is incomplete (and qualifies as late).

Personal documentation

There are no group grades. Each group member is graded individually, so each of you must turn in written evidence of what you contributed and what sources you relied on.

By 6pm the day before your presentation, send me an e-mail or Word doc or Google Doc like this sample here (or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations) with:
  • A brief -- seriously, 1/4 page is ample -- statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was. (If you are presenting all by yourself, this part is not needed.)

  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, divided into two parts:
    • Sources used in the presentation, and
    • Sources consulted but not used.
Use a standard bibliographic format, and give the URL for any online sources. (When available, use the "stable URL" or "persistent URL" or "Permalink.")
    • A quick guide to citation is here on the Library web page. (I like Turabian.)
Documentation in slides, handouts, etc.

Slides, handouts, etc. should document sources, too. Turn them in, with sources documented, by 6pm on the day you present.

Endnotes are one good way to document sources: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in your slides that comes from some source -- image, claim, quote, whatever -- put the number of the source by it.

As with your personal documentation, use some standard bibliographic format.

DePauw's Academic Integrity Policy applies to this assignment. Your documentation -- personal, and slides etc. -- must include all the sources you relied on in any way whether you quote them or not.

Presenting work done outside this course (for example, work already submitted for credit in this or another course, or done for some extracurricular activity) is not allowed. Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be OK, but discuss it with me beforehand. Any work done outside this course, if you are allowed to use it at all, must also be documented.

Failing to document any source will automatically harm your grade and may be charged as plagiarism, even if it is accidental. Keep track of your sources!
