For this “S” course you'll do these speaking/listening activities:
A. Instigating discussion
B. Participating in class discussion and question & answer periods
C. Speech on the value of Philosophy
D. Formal paper presentation
E. Paper comments

To earn the “S” for this course you must perform each of these five speaking/listening activities passably. Earning a passing grade in the course does not guarantee that you will receive your “S” nor does earning the “S” guarantee you will pass the course. Further details will be posted as needed as the course progresses.
 


A. Instigating discussion

I will begin. After that, you will take turns. When it's your turn, during class meetings you will do your best to instigate a stimulating and productive discussion of the assigned reading for that day by giving a brief (4-7 minute) talk about some aspect of the reading at the start of class. (If you'd like to develop this presentation into one of your papers, that’s fine.)

There is more than one good way to do this assignment. Some possibilities are:
  • Briefly summarize the most important points the author(s) make. Doing only this will likely not do much to stimulate discussion with your classmates, so do something else besides, or maybe instead of, summarizing, such as one or more of the following...
  • Explain why you think the material is important for us.
  • Present some puzzle about the text--for example, some central issue that seems unresolved, or something that seems very intriguing but unclear.
  • Criticize the text: briefly explain what you think the author does well, and/or point out some important error(s) made. (For some reason, pointing to errors often works better. You might contrast an author's view with a differing view you think is very plausible, or point out some fallacy in the text, or point to some unattractive consequence(s) of the author's view.)
  • Point us to some key passage(s) in the text for us to explore. (A passage may be of interest because it expresses an important point and/or is provocative, like Machiavelli's story of Cesare Borgia and Remiro d'Orco.)
  • Think of a real-life, or possibly real, example to illustrate, explore, or criticize what an author says. (Since we're talking a lot about things we all have some experience of--war, love, moral judgment--it's fair to consider how things might work out in practice.)
Keep in mind:
  • If you'd like to discuss a series of passages, or raise a series of questions or points for the class to discuss, that's great! But don't raise them all at once. Instead, raise one, have us talk about it, raise another, and so on.
  • The discussion you lead might turn out to be the class' only examination of a particular text. So be sure to direct us towards what matters most. Ask yourself, "If the class only comes away discussing a couple of things about this text, what should they be?"
Some further pointers I hope will be helpful:
  • Use the blackboard or slide(s) to set the day's agenda. (I always have chalk if you need it.)
  • Skim your classmates' reading responses to see what's on their minds; this can help you decide what to focus on.
  • Don't lecture much.
  • Avoid talking as if your view is the only possibly justified one. Your job here is not to convert the class to the One True Reading Of The Text. (You may try to do that during the ensuing discussion.)
  • Raise open-ended questions, as opposed to yes/no questions. Ask questions you think you know the answer to, but also ask questions you are clueless about the answer to, if they seem important.
  • Wait for answers to questions you raise. Avoid answering them yourself. Don't be afraid of silences.
  • When discussion begins, refrain from jumping in even when you have something to say. Let the discussion develop.
Don't feel like you have to prep this on your own. Come see me. Go visit the "S" Center. We're happy to help.
     Your performance at this assignment will be evaluated based on how well you meet the goals of the assignment, i.e., how well you instigate a productive discussion of the text:
  • It's a discussion when more than one person is talking.
  • Discussion is productive when:
    • More people are talking rather than fewer. Everyone should be in.
    • Discussion centers mostly on the text, though some digression is fine and often unexpectedly enriching.
    • It contributes to our understanding of the text and/or the issues raised by it.
Lastly: you are not alone in this. Just as you are assigned the job of instigating discussion, the rest of us are assigned the job of participating in the discussion and moving it along. To this end, everyone should consult item (b) below.
 



B. Participating in class discussion and question & answer periods

You are expected to contribute to class discussion. You are also expected to listen to what your classmates have to say and ask thoughtful questions during class and the various question and answer periods. 

We are all responsible for furthering discussion in the class: 
  • We all should pay attention to how the discussion is going, and contribute to making it go better.
    •  
  • When there is a problem under discussion, help guide the conversation towards solving that problem unless we seem to be getting nowhere. Then you might ask, "Can this issue be resolved? Should we maybe change the subject?"
    •  
  • Differing views should be critically evaluated but nonetheless must be welcomed: each view should be considered, investigated, and appraised but none of us is personally attacked or put down.
    •  
  • We all should notice who is talking and who is not. When only a few are talking, others should be invited. Anyone in the class may call on those who are silent to ask for their input.
    •  
  • There are several different ways of participating in discussion:
      - Raising a question
      - Answering a question
      - Articulating your point
      - Helping others to articulate their points
      - Expressing agreement, perhaps adding additional justification
      - Expressing disagreement
      - Tying together what has been said
      - Moving the discussion in a new direction
     And so on. Don't limit yourself to just one or a couple of ways of participating.



C. Speech on the value of Philosophy

You will give a three-minute (max) presentation on the value of the philosophy major at the philosophy major recruitment event. The event will be just before or after spring break. Details to follow, but we will take class time to prepare for this. We will do some preparatory reading. You will get time to reflect on and discuss your experience as a philosophy major. We will develop, rehearse and refine these presentations.


 

D. Formal paper presentation

The basic idea

At semester's end, we will mimic a professional philosophy conference. You will present your paper (10-15 minutes), a classmate will comment on the paper (5 minutes max), and then we will open the floor to questions from the audience (~10-15 minutes; 20-30 minutes total per paper). The goals here include: To practice presenting and discussing complex philosophical issues orally and to receive feedback from classmates on a complete draft of your final paper before it is due so that your final paper will be even better than it would otherwise have been.
Mission and responsibilities of paper presenters

Your goal is to explain the thesis and central argument(s) of your paper to the audience in a clear, well-organized, easy-to-follow way. You may use whatever props you think are helpful; a good handout can go a long way, particularly if you will refer repeatedly to quotations, principles, or arguments. You may use notes. However, you may not simply read from your paper or anything else. Your presentation should be at least 10 but no more than 15 minutes long. This is challenging. To do it well will require substantial preparation beyond simply writing a good paper. Practicing in advance is strongly recommended. You may also want to make use of the “S” center. Presenting complex philosophical ideas orally is quite different from presenting them in writing; sentences that make sense on the written page can be difficult to grasp when spoken aloud. So the way you present your ideas in the talk may differ from how you it do in the paper.

You must provide your commentator and instructor with a complete draft of your paper at least one week prior to your presentation. Failure to do so will result in your final paper grade being reduced. If it is late by even one day it will be reduced by a full letter grade; if it is so late as to interfere with your commenter's ability to comment effectively, the paper may fail.

This is not your final draft, but it should be a complete draft. The ideas and arguments in your presentation should correspond to the ideas and arguments in the draft that you give to your commentator so that your commentator’s comments will be relevant to your talk.


 

E. Paper comments

Mission and responsibilities of commentators

Each of you will present your paper, and each of you will present prepared comments on a classmate's paper. When you serve as commentator, your central mission is to provide a brief (5 minutes max) commentary on the speaker’s paper that stimulates both the speaker and the audience to think more deeply about the speaker’s ideas. One good way to do this is to provide constructive criticism of the speaker’s arguments.

Just as the speaker should provide you with a draft of his or her paper in a timely fashion, you should read and think about that paper in a timely fashion. Depending on the nature of the paper in question, you may want to do additional reading in preparing your comments. Reading the paper the morning before the presentation and spewing out a few half-assed questions is neither appropriate nor a way to pass this assignment. Instead, provide the speaker with some idea of the direction of your comments the evening prior to the talk. The goal here is not to spring a devastating objection upon your hapless speaker but rather to stimulate them to write a better paper.

It is likely you will have comments on "big" issues, such as whether the paper's main points are clear and whether its arguments are sound, but also "small" issues like matters of style. When you present your comments in front of the class, focus on the big issues, and e-mail the smaller ones to the paper's author and the instructor.

R.E.S.P.E.C.T.

Questioning and criticizing the views and arguments of your classmates and professor is appropriate and encouraged, but should always be done in a courteous and respectful way. Courtesy and respect range from the way speaker and commentator interact prior to the presentation (e.g., providing the speaker with a sense of your main criticisms in advance rather than springing them on them with no warning) to the way in which the criticisms are presented (e.g., “One possible problem with Anderson's argument is that P” rather than “Anderson has obviously overlooked P, which decisively refutes his position; it’s hard to believe that anyone could make such an embarrassing blunder; but Anderson has did”).
