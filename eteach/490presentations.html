Because this is an “S” course you'll do each of the following speaking/listening activities:
A. Discussion instigation.
B. Informal paper topic explanation. 
C. Formal paper presentation.
D. Paper comments.
E. Participation during class discussion and question and answer periods.

To earn the “S” for this course you must perform each of these five speaking/listening activities passably. Earning a passing grade in the course does not guarantee that you will receive your “S” nor does earning the “S” guarantee you will pass the course. Further details will be posted as needed as the course progresses.
 

A. Discussion instigation

When it's your turn, during class meetings you will do your best to instigate a stimulating and productive discussion of the assigned reading for that day by giving a brief (5-10 minute) talk about some aspect of the reading at the start of class. (If you'd like to develop this presentation into one of your papers, that’s fine.)

There is more than one good way to do this assignment. Some possibilities are:
  • Summarize what you take to be the most important points the author(s) make that are relevant to the course. Doing only this may or may not do much to stimulate discussion with your classmates, so I recommend doing something besides, or perhaps instead of, summarizing, such as...
  • Explain why you think the material is important for us.
  • Present some puzzle about the text: some central issue that is unresolved, or resolved in a way we might disagree with.
  • Present a critical evaluation of the text: briefly explain what you think the author does well, and/or point out some important error(s) made. (For some reason, pointing to errors often works better. You might contrast an author's view with a differing view you think is very plausible, or point out some fallacy in the text, or point to some unattractive consequence(s) of the author's view.)
  • Point us to some key passage(s) in the text for us to explore. (A passage may be of interest because it expresses an important point and/or is provocative, like Machiavelli's story of Cesare Borgia and Remiro d'Orco.)
  • Think of a real-life, or possibly real, example to illustrate, explore, or criticize what an author says. (Since we're talking a lot about things we all have some experience of--war, love, moral judgment--it's fair to consider how things might work out in practice.)

  • Suggestion: If you'd like to raise a series of questions or points for the class to discuss, raising them all at once might not be best. Instead, you could raise one, have us talk about it, raise another, and so on
As you prepare, keep in mind that your job is to set the tone and topic(s) of discussion for the day, and this discussion might turn out to be the class' only examination of a particular text. So be sure to direct us towards what matters most. Ask yourself, "If the class only comes away discussing a couple of things about this text, what should they be?"
     And since your job is to provoke discussion--not teach the text--try to do your spiel in a way that provokes, invites, or at least does not quash discussion. Some pointers for this are:
  • Don't lecture much.
  • Avoid talking as if your view is the only possibly justified one. Your task here is not to convert the class to the One True Reading Of The Text. (You may try to do that during the ensuing discussion.)
  • Raise open-ended questions, as opposed to yes/no questions. Ask questions you think you know the answer to, but also ask questions you are clueless about the answer to.
  • Don't be afraid of silences. Wait for answers to questions you raise. Avoid answering them yourself.
  • When discussion begins, refrain from jumping in even when you have something to say. Let the discussion develop.
Don't feel like you have to prep this on your own. Come see me. Go visit the "S" Center. We're happy to help.
     Your performance at this assignment will be evaluated based on how well you meet the goals of the assignment, i.e., how well you instigate a productive discussion of the text.
  • It's a discussion when more than one person is talking.
  • Discussion is productive when:
    • More people are talking rather than fewer. Everyone should be in.
    • Discussion centers on the text, though some digression is fine and often unexpectedly enriching.
    • It contributes to our understanding of the text and/or the issues raised by it. (This presupposes having a good grasp of the text.)
Of course you are not alone in this. Just as you are assigned the job of instigating discussion, the rest of us are assigned the job of participating in the discussion and moving it along. To this end, everyone should consult item (e) below.
 


B. Informal paper topic explanation

The 3 goals of this assignment

You'll present your ideas for your research paper to the class. This assignment is meant to accomplish three things.  First, to get you thinking about your research paper long before it is due. Second, to give you early feedback about your topic.  Third, for you to practice explaining philosophical ideas to a non-philosophical audience.  In line with the third goal, when preparing your presentation you should have in mind an audience of people who are intelligent, intellectually curious, but unfamiliar with philosophy and somewhat skeptical about its worth. 

Here’s one way you might think of it: Suppose you were hanging out with the parents of your significant other and had about fifteen minutes to kill, and one of them casually asked you, “So, what are you writing your big philosophy paper on?”  (For this to work, assume that the parents in question are not complete idiots/jerks.  If necessary, please adjust your thought experiment accordingly).

The particulars

You will be on the spot for 15-20 minutes. Roughly the first five minutes of your time should consist of you explaining the topic of your paper in general terms and providing some specifics about at least one of the main issues, questions, puzzles, arguments, etc. that you plan to discuss in your paper.  At this early stage you do not need to have the entire paper planned out or know what your ultimate conclusion will be, but you should have a good grip on at least one of the central issues you will take up in the paper.  Your mission is to lay out one of these issues in a compelling way; show us why this issue is worth thinking about.  As when instigating discussion of a reading, you may use notes or an outline, but do not simply read to the class.  You are free to use props (e.g. handouts, PowerPoint) this time around if you like, but props are not at all necessary.  Please use them only if they will help you better get your ideas across. 

After your initial 5-minute presentation, you will field questions from your fellow students and professor for about 10-15 minutes.  The best way to prepare for this brief question-and-answer period is to have a clear grasp of your topic and its importance.

Staying on schedule

Because of the number of students we have (at least on Wednesday April 8), it is important that these presentation/discussions be limited to about 20 minutes and occur one right after the other.  This means that you should use your time well. You should plan and rehearse what you want to say.

Any set-up time counts as part of your allotted time.  This is something to keep in mind when making decisions about using props; if you have to spend five minutes fussing with some piece of equipment before you begin, this will have a negative impact on your grade.  You must rule technology; don’t let technology rule you.


 

C. Formal paper presentation

The basic idea

We will follow the typical format of a professional philosophy conference: a speaker will present his or her paper (15 minutes), a second speaker will comment on the paper (5 minutes), and then we will open the floor to questions from the audience (10 minutes). The goals here include: To practice presenting and discussing complex philosophical issues orally and to receive feedback from classmates on a complete draft of your final paper before it is due so that your final paper will be even better than it would otherwise have been.
Mission and responsibilities of paper presenters

As speaker, your central mission is to explain the thesis and central argument(s) of your paper to the audience in a clear, well-organized, easy-to-follow fashion. You may use whatever props you think are helpful in this endeavor; a good handout can go a long way, particularly if you will refer repeatedly to quotations, principles, or arguments. You may use notes. However, you shall not under any circumstances simply read from your paper or anything else. Your presentation should be roughly fifteen minutes in length. This is a challenging task. To do it well will require substantial preparation (beyond simply writing a good paper). Practicing in advance is strongly recommended. You may also want to make use of the “S” center. Presenting complex philosophical ideas orally is quite different from presenting them in writing; sentences that make sense on the written page can be difficult to grasp when spoken aloud. So the way in which you present your ideas in the talk may differ from the way you do in the paper.

You must provide your commentator and instructor with a complete draft of your paper at least one week prior to your presentation. Failure to do so will result in your final paper grade being reduced. If it is late by even one day it will be reduced by a full letter grade; if it is so late as to interfere with your commenter's ability to comment effectively, the paper may fail.

This is not your final draft, but it should be a complete draft. The ideas and arguments in your presentation should correspond to the ideas and arguments in the draft that you give to your commentator so that your commentator’s comments will be relevant to your talk.


 

D. Paper comments

Mission and responsibilities of commentators

Each of you will present your paper, and each of you will present prepared comments on a classmate's paper. When you serve as commentator, your central mission is to provide a brief (five minute) commentary on the speaker’s paper that stimulates both the speaker and the audience to think more deeply about the speaker’s ideas. One good way to do this is to provide constructive criticism of the speaker’s arguments.

Just as the speaker should provide you with a draft of his or her paper in a timely fashion, you should read and think about that paper in a timely fashion. Depending on the nature of the paper in question, you may want to do additional reading in preparing your comments. Reading the paper the morning of the presentation and spewing out a few half-assed questions is neither appropriate nor a way to pass this assignment. Instead, you'll provide the speaker with some idea of the direction of your comments the evening prior to the talk. The goal here is not to spring a devastating objection upon your hapless speaker but rather to stimulate him or her to write a better paper.

It is likely you will have comments on "big" issues, such as whether the paper's main points are clear and whether its arguments are sound, but also "small" issues such as matters of style. When you present your comments in front of the class, focus most on the big issues, either leaving the small ones for last if there is time, or e-mailing them to the paper's author.

R.E.S.P.E.C.T.

Questioning and criticizing the views and arguments of your classmates and professor is appropriate and encouraged, but should always be done in a courteous and respectful way. Courtesy and respect range from the way speaker and commentator interact prior to the presentation (e.g., providing the speaker with a sense of your main criticisms in advance rather than springing them on him or her with no warning) to the way in which the criticisms are presented (e.g., “One possible problem with Anderson's argument is that P” rather than “Anderson has obviously overlooked P, which decisively refutes his position; it’s hard to believe that anyone could make such an eye-popping blunder; but Anderson has done it”).

 

E. Participation during class discussion and question and answer periods

You are expected to contribute to class discussion. You are also expected to listen to what your classmates have to say and ask thoughtful questions during class and the various question and answer periods. 

We are all responsible for furthering discussion in the class: 
  • We all should pay attention to how the discussion is going, and contribute to making it go better.
    •  
  • When there is a problem under discussion, help guide the conversation towards solving that problem unless we seem to be getting nowhere. Then you might ask, "Can this issue be resolved? Should we maybe change the subject?"
    •  
  • Differing views should be critically evaluated but nonetheless must be welcomed: each view should be considered, investigated, and appraised but none of us is personally attacked or put down.
    •  
  • We all should notice who is talking and who is not. When only a few are talking, others should be invited. Anyone in the class may call on those who are silent to ask for their input.
    •  
  • There are several different ways of participating in discussion:
      - Raising a question
      - Answering a question
      - Articulating your point
      - Helping others to articulate their points
      - Expressing agreement, perhaps adding additional justification
      - Expressing disagreement
      - Tying together what has been said
      - Moving the discussion in a new direction
    And so on. Don't limit yourself to just one or a couple of ways of participating.
