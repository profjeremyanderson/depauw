My expectations--including some sample answers
  • For a term, can you give its definition + say why it matters in this course?
    • For example, for "metaphysics, you should be able to
      (a) define it, like "Metaphysics is the area of philosophy that mainly asks, 'What exists?' It also asks related questions like 'Does God exist?' 'Are numbers real?' or 'What is the difference between a person and their body?'" and also
      (b) say why it matters in the course. You could say, "Metaphysics matters in this course because it is one of the three main areas of philosophy. We also studied free will, and whether we have free will is a metaphysical question."
    • Or, for "valid," you should be able to
      (a) define it, like "Validity is something deductive arguments can have. An argument is valid when its conclusion would have to be true if its premises were true," and also
      (b) explain why the term matters in the course. You could say, "Validity matters in this course because we often evaluate arguments, and seeing whether an argument is valid helps us figure out whether its conclusion is well supported."

    • To say why a term matters, can you say something specific about that term? Vague answers like, "Term X matters in philosophy because philosophers discuss X" or "because we studied X in the course" do not show understanding.
    • Instead, identify where the term played a role in the course, and what that role was.
      • For example, "libertarianism." It came up in our discussion of free will; you could say, "Libertarianism matters in this course because it is one of the three main solutions philosophers have to the problem of free will."
      • Or "strong." It comes up in basic logic, where discussed arguments and how to evaluate them. So, for "strong," you could say "It matters in this course because it is relevant to how we evaluate inductive arguments; this is important because arguments are the most basic tool of philosophy."
      • Or "monism." You could say, "Monism is related to the mind/body problem. It is one way of solving the problem: if there is only one substance in the universe, then there is no need to explain how two different substances could interact. Berkeley is a monist."

  • For a name, can you identify the main points that person made in the assigned reading?
    • For example, for "Paul Rée," can you say more than that he was a hard determinist? Identify the main points he made in the assigned reading, like "Rée believed there is no free will because everything in the universe is determined by a sufficient cause. He argued for this by comparing a stone, a donkey, and a person. Because we are determined, he believed we cannot be responsible for our actions and that our belief we are free is mistaken."
    • Or, for "St. Anselm of Canterbury," can you say more than just that he gave an argument for God? Identify the main points he made in the assigned readings, like this: "Anselm argued that God exists. To do that, he gave a deductive, ontological argument. He also replied to Gaunilo's objections to his argument."
    • Or for "Bertrand Russell," can you do more than just describe his argument about the cat? Identify the main points he made in the assigned portion of The Problems of Philosophy, like this: "Russell was writing about our knowledge of the external world, and he argued that although (a) appearances often differ from (what we take to be) reality, and (b) we cannot prove that life isn't just a continuous dream or illusion, nevertheless (c) we can still be confident that things like cats and people do exist outside us."

  • For an argument, can you...
    • State the argument? You might state it in standard form (numbered premises and conclusion) and/or write it out in a paragraph, or both -- whatever best demonstrates your understanding. But however you state it, you should both identify its premises and, for each conclusion, explain how that conclusion is supposed to be supported by the premises. Even if you put it in standard form, you should explain.
    • Critically evaluate the argument, if this was discussed in class? For example, for Descartes's argument for corporeal objects, be able to explain its weaknesses. You may not be able to explain them all -- in fact, trying to do that would be too much -- but even in an exam you should be able to identify at least one substantial criticism of an argument if asked to, if those were discussed in class.

Academic integrity on exams

For exams given in class, you must write your answers solely from memory during the exam. No citing or quoting is required. You may not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam.

For take-home exams (which we will have if an exam must be given remotely):
  • You may use any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
  • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with other people on the exam.
  • The academic integrity instructions for papers and reading responses will apply to take-home/remote exams. Cite all sources you use and put others’ words in quotation marks.


How to use this guide & do well on exams

To do well on exams (and papers):
  • Memorize as much of this material as you can.
  • There will be many citations to texts, notes, etc. on the study guide. Use them; they point to stuff that's important.
  • Use the handouts and exercises in the Google Drive folders linked to our Moodle page.
  • This may seem obvious, but try to keep track of who said what about what. We will learn about many authors' views on several topics, so this can be challenging.
I will notify you when I add things to the study guide. Emails will come from Moodle's Announcements forum to your DePauw e-mail, so you should check them, or visit this page, regularly.

Re-load this page each time to ensure you see the latest version.

When new items appear:
  1. Review your study materials (readings and e-readings on our Google drive,  notes from class, class recordings, etc.)--especially anything cited in the study guide,
  2. Write out responses to each item, making your definitions, explanations, critiques, etc. clear, complete, and thoughtful, and
  3. Study these materials and your responses periodically to prepare for exams and class discussions.
Need help with anything here? Ask me for help.

Items which have been struck out (so they look like this) will not be used on future exams.

I encourage you to study with others. However, on papers and exams you need to do your own work. No collaborating with others.


Study items for: What is Philosophy?

Terms (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about terms):
  1. Metaphysics (Bailey, xiv)
  2. Epistemology (Bailey, xiv)
  3. Value Theory (aka Ethics) (Bailey, xiv-xv)
You should be able to:
  1. Identify whether a question or problem pertains to metaphysics, epistemology, or value theory--or more than one of them--and explain why (Bailey, xiv-xv).
  2. Explain the similarities and differences between philosophy and science (for example, biology) with regard to what questions they try to answer and their methods for seeking answers (Bailey, xiii-xiv; discussion 2025-08-22).
  3. Explain why philosophy’s methods differ from those used in empirical sciences like biology. (Bailey, xiii-xiv may be helpful, but the most thorough answer comes from our discussion in class on 2025-08-22.)



Study items for: Do we have free will?

Terms:
  1. Free will (use the definition given in class)
  2. Determinism (class notes)
  3. The problem of free will (the definition from class is very different from the one in Bailey, 413, so use class notes)
  4. Hard determinism (class notes; beware that Bailey treats "determinism" as a synonym for "hard determinism" (413), but in class we do not)
  5. Libertarianism (class notes)
  6. Compatibilism (class notes)
Names:
  1. Sarah Koenig (host of the "Blackjack" podcast segment)
  2. Paul Rée (pronounced "ray")
  3. David Eagleman
  4. C.A. Campbell
  5. A.J. Ayer
You should be able to:
  1. From the podcast on the gambling addict, Angie Bachmann: Identify at least three pieces of evidence the podcast uses to suggest that Bachmann's losses were the casino's fault, not hers.
  2. Explain how Rée uses his comparison of the stone, the donkey, and us to argue that our choices are determined (417-9).
  3. Explain how Rée and Eagleman account for our belief that we have free will (Rée's section 4, pp. 422-5; Eagleman, 3-5). (It may be helpful to discuss Eagleman's "stowaway" or "newspaper" comparisons, 3-4, 4-5.)
  4. How does Rée argue that we are not responsible for what we do (Rée, 425)?
  5. According to Campbell, three conditions must be met for you to be morally responsible. What are they (Campbell, section 2, 545-7)?
  6. Which of our choices does Campbell say are free (and for which we may be responsible) (550, last paragraph of section 5)? Give an example of such a free choice, and explain why it's an example.
  7. Campbell says that introspection is good evidence of free will (Campbell, 550). Why might we doubt that introspection is good evidence (Eagleman, 3-5; class notes)?
  8. How might a determinist argue that our predictability shows that we do not have free will (Campbell, section 9, 552; class notes have a simpler version you may use). Then, explain one of Campbell's replies to this argument (552-3).
  9. Explain why Ayer is unsure whether determinism is true (439-40). If determinism is false and our choices are undetermined, why does Ayer think we couldn't be responsible for our choices (440)?
  10. How does Ayer try to show we can be responsible even if we are determined? Specifically, when does Ayer think we are "free" and responsible, and when does he think we are not? (To do this well, you will need to explain Ayer's definition of freedom, his criterion for responsibility, and what he thinks constraints are, 441-2.)



Study items for: Basic logic

Terms (all the logic terms are defined in the Logic Terms handout on our Google Drive):
  1. Ethos (Aristotle, 7)
  2. Pathos (Aristotle, 7)
  3. Logos (Aristotle, 7)
  4. Argument
  5. Premises
  6. Conclusion
  7. Inductive argument (Anderson, sec. 2)
  8. Strong
  9. Weak
  10. Deductive argument
  11. Valid (Anderson, sec. 5; also see the validity exercise from class, with notes on Moodle)
  12. Invalid
  13. Sound
  14. Unsound
Names (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about names):
  1. Aristotle
You should be able to:
  1. Give examples of ethos (both kinds!), pathos, and logos, and explain why each is an example.
  2. Identify an argument, its premise(s), and its conclusion(s).
  3. Identify, or at least make an educated guess, whether a given argument is inductive or deductive, and explain why. (You cannot tell by just the wording of the conclusion! Use the definitions of "inductive" and "deductive" and try to figure out which best fits.)
  4. When discussing premises, conclusions, deductive arguments, and inductive arguments, properly apply the terms "true," "false," "valid," "invalid," "strong," and "weak"--in other words, show you know which terms apply to what things. (The end of the Logic Terms handout gives some help here.) This applies to all assignments: reading responses, exams, and papers.


Study items for: Does God exist?

Terms:
  1. Ontological argument (Bailey, 2 may be helpful but notes on Moodle will be better)
  2. Cosmological argument (Bailey, 2 may be helpful but see class notes)
  3. Design (or teleological) argument (Bailey, 2 may be helpful but see class notes)
Names:
  1. St. Anselm of Canterbury
  2. Gaunilo of Marmoutiers (pronounced "gow-nillow of mar-moo-tee-ay")
  3. St. Thomas Aquinas (pronounced "uh-KWAI-nuss")
Arguments (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about arguments; class notes and notes on Moodle) will often be helpful):
  1. Anselm's argument that God exists (Anselm, 7) (see the standard-form version explained in class with notes on Moodle.)
  2. Aquinas's "First Way" (Aquinas, 26; also see the Aquinas notes on Moodle)
You should be able to (again, notes on Moodle may be helpful so check them when available):
  1. Explain why "Does God exist?" is an important question.
  2. Explain at least one of Gaunilo's objections to Anselm's ontological argument (for example, Gaunilo's "Lost Island" objection in paragraph 6, p. 10; see notes on Moodle). (A well-formed objection to an argument is itself an argument, so explain your objections accordingly: make an argument concluding, "So, Anselm's argument is unsound.")
  3. Explain at least two objections to Aquinas' "First Way." (Notes on Moodle will be helpful here; they give three objections.)
