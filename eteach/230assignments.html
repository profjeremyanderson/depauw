Assignment 2: Ruth Benedict suggests we are we so conditioned by culture that we cannot be objective. If so, cultural relativism may be right about how moral rules are justified.
  • Read (both are on our Google drive)
    • Ruth Benedict, "Anthropology and the Abnormal" (abridged)
      • The term "abnormal" is very important in Benedict. "Abnormal" doesn't just mean "not normal." When we say someone is "abnormal" we often also mean there is something wrong with them, and that their abnormality makes them less fit for society. Benedict uses examples from anthropology to argue that our judgments of what is normal/abnormal, fit/unfit for society -- and moral/immoral -- are products of our culture.
    • the first paragraph of the 2002 National Security Strategy of the United States (this PDF is just the intro to a much longer document)
Reading questions (again, always come to class prepared to talk about the reading questions for each assignment. Also, turn in a written response to at least one reading question per week in your reading responses. See the reading response instructions here, and submit your reading responses here):
  1. Why would Benedict object to the claims in the National Security Strategy's opening paragraph? (It may help to consider her opening paragraph, 1.)
  2. Benedict presents several examples of cultural practices very different from our own. These examples fall into two categories: first, those which show "the ease with which our abnormals function in other cultures" (Benedict, 1; the examples continue to the top of p. 3); second, "those cultures where an abnormality of our culture is the cornerstone of their social structure" (3; the examples continue to p. 5). What does she mean by this distinction? What does she want us to conclude from the examples (5-6)?
  3. What does Benedict mean when she says, "The very eyes with which we see the problem are conditioned by the long traditional habits of our own society" (Benedict, 5)?
  4. On page 6 Benedict says there is a "range of human behavior that is found wherever a sufficiently large series of individuals is observed. But the proportion in which behavior types stand to one another in different societies is not universal. The vast majority of individuals in any group are shaped to the fashion of that culture. In other words, most individuals are plastic to the moulding force of the society into which they are born." What does Benedict mean by this?

By "practical reason" he means "figuring out what to do," such as forming moral rules. Try to explain this analogy.   1. In Leviathan ch. 13, Hobbes is describing what he calls the "natural condition" of humanity, often called a "state of nature." What he means by this is, "How humans would be if there were no government": no police, firefighters, written laws, etc. Within a couple of pages he concludes that, in such a state, we'd be in a "war, where every man is enemy to every man," and our lives would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" (Hobbes, 71). Assignment 18: Intro to Deontology, and why Kant hates most of we've looked at so far
  • Immanuel Kant, excerpts from Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals (on our Google drive)
    • You should read the whole Kant piece--you'll need to for future assignments anyway--but in our next meeting we will focus on Kant's Preface, p. 1, and mostly on the paragraph quoted here in the reading questions. (Yes, it's that important.)
    • Kant uses odd words. Some explanation of his terms is available here in this glossary. Many terms are interrelated so you may have to skip around a bit, but it's helpful.
Reading questions:

Kant says this about the nature of moral laws and the proper sort of justification for them:

That there must be [a “pure” moral] philosophy is evident from the common idea of duty and of moral laws. Everyone must admit that if a law is to be morally valid, i.e., is to be valid as a ground of obligation, then it must carry with it absolute necessity. He must admit that the command, “Thou shalt not lie,” does not hold only for men, as if other rational beings had no need to abide by it, and so with all the other moral laws properly so called. And he must concede that the ground [i.e., justification] of obligation here must therefore be sought not in the world in which man is placed, but must be sought a priori solely in the concepts of pure reason.... (Kant, 1)

  1. What does this mean? Try explaining it slowly, sentence by sentence.
  2. When he says the justification of obligation "must...be  sought not in the world in which man is placed, but must be sought...solely in the concepts of pure reason," what implications might that have for, say, utilitarianism? How about for cultural relativism, or Aquinas' or Hobbes's natural law theories, or Glaucon's way of justifying rules?

Assignment 23: Kant's Formula of Humanity and how it implies we should help the needy
  • (Re-)read Kant's discussion of the "formula of humanity," starting from the note at the end of p. 12 to the end of the reading, p. 15.
    • The "formula of humanity" (or "formula of the end in itself") version of the categorical imperative appears at the beginning of p. 14. The preceding text is an argument for it, and the text after applies it to the same four examples used for the FUL.
  • Onora O'Neill: "Kantian Ethics and World Hunger"
    • O'Neill is explaining how to apply the formula of humanity to a real-world problem.
Reading questions:

Kant says

All the objects of inclinations have only a conditioned value; for if there were not these inclinations and the needs founded on them, then their object would be without value. But the inclinations themselves, being sources of needs, are so far from having an absolute value such as to render them desirable for their own sake that the universal wish of every rational being must be, rather to be wholly free from them. Accordingly the value of any object obtainable by our action is always conditioned.  (Kant, 13)
  1. What does Kant mean by this? (It may be helpful to think of an example of something you're "inclined" to want, like money or a diploma or a nice house.)
  2. Kant distinguishes between "subjective ends" and "objective ends" (13--in both the left and right columns). What does he mean by this distinction? What does he mean by these terms?
  3. O'Neill says Kant's "formula of the end in itself" (also called the "formula of humanity") implies we have two distinct duties: (a) "avoid acting on maxims that use others as mere means" and (b) "act on maxims that treat others as ends in themselves" (423). How would one use another as a mere means (423-4)? What must we do to treat others as ends (424)--why may we not just leave others alone?
