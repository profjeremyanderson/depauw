  • Assignment 25: About 9/11/2001
    The US' "Global War on Terror," the invasion and occupation of Afghanistan and Iraq, the ongoing campaign of covert warfare in many countries (including drone warfare and various special operations)--all were to some extent reactions to the events of 9/11/2001. But what were those events? What were the attacks about? And what might that tell us about what the proper response might be?
    • Review the Osama bin Laden readings above, especially the 1996 piece.
    • Speeches by President George W. Bush--notice the dates:
      • "Statement by the President in His Address to the Nation" (September 11, 2001, 8:30pm)
        • What reasons does Bush give for why the US was attacked? At that point, were the attackers known?
      • "Address to a Joint Session of Congress and the American People" (September 20, 2001; this speech declared the "Global War on Terror")
        • Again, what reasons does Bush give for why the US was attacked? What does he say al Qaeda hoped to achieve? How, according to Bush, will this war differ from previous wars?
          Notice contrasts:
          • between Bush and bin Laden on al Qaeda's motives
          • between how Bush characterizes Americans vs. how Churchill does (if you look at the Churchill)
    • Michael Scott Doran, "Somebody Else's Civil War," originally published in Foreign Affairs, January/February 2002. (In an episode from The West Wing, the character Sam Seaborn claims terrorism has "a 100% failure rate." Doran argues otherwise.)
      • Note: Doran's piece gets into a lot of details of groups and people. There's a bewildering number of names you probably won't recognize. Don't worry about keeping up with that. The main thing is to get a sense of how he argues that the United States was basically suckered into intervening in somebody else's civil war.
    • Optional:

  • Assignment 27: Torture (content warning)

We will discuss torture mostly in relation to recent examples committed by the government of the United States. However, you should know that (a) the USA's history of torture and other atrocities is very long (notorious examples include US treatment of Filipinos in the wake of the Spanish-American War--see here and this cartoon of US soldiers waterboarding a Filipino); but at the same time, (b) the USA is not unusual in torturing people.

    • Background to skim:
      • Read the opening summary and browse the photos in "Abu Ghraib torture and prisoner abuse" (Wikipedia)
      • Skim Wikipedia's list of methods of torture. The main point is to see how many there are.
    • Read these two short articles on the ethics of torture:
      • Sam Harris, "In Defense of Torture" (PDF). Harris presents a version of the "ticking bomb" argument, which is by far the most popular argument for torture. Original posted here.
      • Catherine McDonald, "Defusing the ‘ticking bomb’: why the argument for torture fails" (PDF), Online Opinion, April 12, 2007. Original posted here.
    • Optional:
      • Matthew Alexander, "I'm Still Tortured by What I Saw in Iraq," Washington Post, November 30, 2008. Alexander, an American interrogator in Iraq, claims "Torture and abuse cost American lives."
      • Pages 1-28 of "ICRC Report on the Treatment of Fourteen 'High-Value Detainees' in CIA Custody," International Committee of the Red Cross, February 14, 2007. This report details the torture of people held by the CIA in its "black sites."
      • Read the remainder of the ICRC report, which includes detainee testimonials.
      • Ben Taub, "Guantánamo’s Darkest Secret," The New Yorker, April 15, 2019. This article is quite long, but is maybe the most informative single article on the United States' programs of extraordinary rendition and torture I have seen. If you can't read Taub's whole article, scroll down to the sections titled "ENEMY COMBATANTS" and "SPECIAL PROJECTS," which focus on the treatment of detainees at Guantánamo Bay.
      • From Wikipedia's page on Torture, in the section, "Laws Against Torture," read "United Nations Convention Against Torture" and scroll down to and read "Geneva Convention IV exemptions."
      • Read the remainder of the Wikipedia page on Abu Ghraib
      • Documentaries:
        • "The Torture Question" (2005; PBS Frontline; watch online.)
