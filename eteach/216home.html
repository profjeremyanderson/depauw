Course goals & outcomes
Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, Locke, Berkeley, Hume, and Kant attempted some of the more ambitious projects in Western philosophical history, trying to identify the extent of human knowledge and the true nature of the universe. This course is a chance to understand and critically engage with their projects and begin to see the universe in radically different ways through the eyes of these brilliant people. We will survey key texts from each of them, getting a sense of their overall arc and zooming in on especially interesting arguments. We will note how their ideas respond to perennial philosophical concerns and contribute to ongoing debates. In your writing you will practice articulating complex and difficult ideas and critically evaluating them.

Student Outcomes

To put the above another way, students will be able to:
  • Understand and summarize important works by Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, Locke, Berkeley, Hume, and Kant.
  • Explain how these thinkers argue for their views.
  • Identify areas of agreement and disagreement between them. 
  • Critically evaluate the arguments of those thinkers and others (e.g., me, other students) give.
  • Identify their contributions to perennial philosophical debates.
  • Demonstrate your grasp of the above in reading responses, exams, and discussion.
Our work in this course furthers several of DePauw's learning goals (listed here under "The Purpose and Aims of DePauw"), specifically #2, 3, 6, 8, 9, and, I dearly hope, #1.

Participation is demonstrating you are thinking about the course. It is not hard. You can earn up to one point per class meeting (i.e., three points per normal week). The best form is participating in class discussions, but it could also consist of discussing course-related things with me outside of class in person or by e-mail, contributions to the class open forum, etc. Mere attendance, looking thoughtful, taking notes, etc. do not count. Writing

Reading responses are to get you thinking about the readings before class, and also to practice writing and academic integrity.
Each reading assignment will give you a lot to think about. It's good to write your thoughts down as you read, before class. The reading responses assignment gives you credit for doing some of that. Asking you to turn in a written response to every assignment would be too much, though, so I only ask that you do so at least once per week, and submit it in the forums set up on Moodle. (If Moodle is down, email it to me.)

Each reading response will be scored credit/no credit. To get credit, turn in at least 200 words where you...
  • First, summarize the assigned reading (or, if more than one is assigned, at least one of them) in just a few sentences. Your goals here are to practice briefly explaining the big picture and to show you have done the reading.
    • For example, if assigned Descartes's First Meditation, you might say, "Descartes is troubled by the false beliefs he was raised with. He resolves to start over in hopes of attaining more reliable beliefs. He starts over by rejecting any beliefs that are doubtful (M1, 1). After arguing that he might be dreaming and might be radically deceived by God (M1, 1-2), he finds that all his old beliefs must be rejected (M1, 2-3)." (Yes, you must cite even assigned readings, and put any borrowed words in quotation marks; see integrity instructions below.)
  • Second, in a separate paragraph or more, discuss at least one interesting thing in the reading. Something might be interesting because it seems especially smart, or useful, or puzzling, or stupid, or...you tell me! Make this discussion detailed enough for readers to understand why the thing (or things) is interesting.
  • Write at least 200 words, decently and with integrity. I don't expect polished poetry here. But fix your grammar, spelling, and punctuation. Cite any sources you use, whenever you use them, and put others' words in quotation marks per the academic integrity requirements. Responses that are too sloppy won't get credit, and integrity lapses could be charged as dishonesty.
  • Be on time. Responses should be in the right Moodle forum (they are here) and time-stamped by three hours before class starts. So, for example, if you write a response to the reading assigned for Friday, your response is due by 9:30am Friday. (Suggestion: turn them in the evening before class, because rushing at the last minute could result in poor work which would not get credit.)
To earn a B (85%), do one passing reading response per week, for a total of 14 passing responses. To earn an A+ (100%), do at least one response each week (passing or not), but ensure you write a total of 16 passing responses during the semester.

Academic integrity

The general idea

We learn a lot from others. I expect ideas and words from various sources -- readings, classmates, me, articles, books, AI, etc. -- to show up in your work. But we must acknowledge everyone who helped us, every time we use their help. Whenever others' words or ideas appear in your work, you must cite them whether you quote them or not.

I take academic integrity very seriously and I expect you to. Often, cheating is unintended, but even then it is a serious offense and I may charge you with misconduct. The minimum penalty is usually worse than turning in no work at all, and penalties can include failing the course, suspension, or expulsion. Much plagiarism is due to ignorance of the standards for documenting sources, so I urge you to:
  • Ask me any questions you have about academic integrity, preferably before you turn in your work.
  • Consult (and follow) the academic integrity instructions for assignments in this course (just below).
  • Get to know DePauw's policy and your obligations and rights, which are in the Academic Handbook.
  • Use these resources to better understand academic integrity at DePauw.
You can avoid many integrity problems if you follow these simple rules: if you use an idea you got from someone or somewhere else, cite it; if you use their words, put them in quotes and cite.


Academic integrity instructions for assignments in this course

Academic integrity on exams:
  • For exams given in class, you must write your answers from memory during the exam. You many not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam. That's taxing enough, so no citing or quoting is required.

  • Take-home exams (which we will have if an exam must be given remotely) will be open book, open notes, so you must cite any sources you use. But:
    • You may use any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with other people on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions for reading responses (just below) will apply. Cite all sources you use and put others’ words in quotation marks.
Academic integrity in reading responses:

No research is required for reading responses. But you'll often use or refer to assigned texts and possibly discussion, and if you do that without always acknowledging every source you use, that's plagiarism. Using other sources without properly acknowledging them is also plagiarism.

So let me help you avoid that.

This section will not discuss all kinds of academic integrity. It will focus on the problems that occur most commonly. You are still responsible for following DePauw's academic integrity policy.

The most common integrity problems are:
A. Plagiarism--that is, presenting others' work as if it were your own, intentionally or not -- or
B. Citing others' work, but badly.

To avoid both, follow these guidelines.

A. Avoiding plagiarism

Plagiarism is presenting another's work as if it were your own. "Another's work" includes anything you didn't make up yourself: the work of other people, material produced by AI, etc. It includes their ideas and the words they used to express their ideas. Therefore, to avoid plagiarizing:
  • Any time you use something you get from another, cite it, whether you quote it or not.

    • Whatever the source was -- assigned texts, a video, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, an academic journal, a web page, your mother, space aliens, AI, or whatever -- cite it.
  • If you use another's words in your work, put them in quotation marks and cite.

    • Even if you change some of the wording you might still be plagiarizing. Either put the material in quotation marks or completely rephrase it. See this exercise for help.

  • Keep track of your sources.

    • Citing properly will be easier if you make a note of what you read and who you talk to about your work. Keep track of what words and ideas come from each source. Then, every time you use any words or ideas from your sources, cite them, and put any of their words in quotation marks.
  • Unsure what to do? Ask me before you turn in the work.


B. Citing properly
 
Always give enough information for readers to easily find what you're citing. (That's true for all citing of any sources whatsoever, in any course, regardless of citation format.)

In this course, insert parenthetical citations whenever you use something from a source. That is, cite whenever you include anything you got from any source -- anything you didn't make up yourself -- including assigned texts. Obvious examples would be any time you say, "Descartes says..." or "Locke thinks..." etc.

For citing assigned texts and class discussion, the parenthetical citation is enough. For anything outside those, you need to give enough information to track the exact source down. See "Citing anything else," below.

Citing assigned texts
How to cite assigned texts adequately
 
A simple, straightforward way to cite our assigned texts is to give the author, abbreviated name of the work, and the page number from the EarlyModernTexts.com PDF I've assigned. Like: (Descartes, Discourse, 9) or (Descartes, Preliminaries, 5), (Hume, Enquiry, 12), and so on.

When it's really obvious who the author is, you may omit the name. For example, if you're doing a reading response and it's obvious you're only talking about Leibniz, just give the work and page number, like (Discourse, 4).

When it's also really obvious what the work is, you may omit that, too. For example, if you're writing a paragraph about Descartes's argument for God in the Third Meditation and citing different parts of it, your first citation could be (Descartes, M3, 14) but after that just give the page number, like (16).

Better: LEVEL UP YOUR CITING GAME!
 
Here's how folks in the Philosophy Biz often do it. Page numbers are okay, but authors sometimes give us paragraph/section numbers or other indicators that are more precise, so we often use them. You can, too!

Scholars have some fancy ways of doing that. There are standard schemes of reference for each of the authors we're reading, and once you learn them, they're a big help. But I prefer not to over-complicate things in a 200-level course.

To level up your citing game, add the section/paragraph etc. number to your citation, briefly indicating that you're doing so. Examples:
  • Instead of (Leibniz, Monadology, 2) you could put (Leibniz, Monadology, sec. 9, 2).
  • Instead of (Spinoza, Ethics, 8) you could put (Spinoza, Ethics, Prop. 15 Note II, 8).
  • Instead of (Berkeley, Principles, 13) you could put (Berkeley, Principles, sec. 11, 13).

  • OR: when we're only reading one work by a given author (as with Spinoza, Locke, Berkeley, and Hume), you can omit the title of the work:
    • (Spinoza, Prop. 15 Note II, 8)
    • (Berkeley, sec. 11, 13)
And so on. Again, whenever it's really obvious who the author is, or the work, you may omit them.


Citing class discussion

Give the class and the date of the discussion, e.g., (Phil 216, 9/1/2023). (So, keep track of dates in your notes, OK?)

 
Citing anything else
When you use any sources outside of class materials, include a list of Works Cited for all of them. If you only use class materials (which is fine), a Works Cited is not needed in this course.

Remember, "any sources" means anything at all that you didn't make up. If you got an idea from a friend or relative, cite it. If you borrowed some wording from an AI, cite it and put the words in quotation marks. This is important.

For your Works Cited, you may use any standard citation format. I happen to like Chicago and  Turabian, and the Library website has guides to different formats including a quick guide to Turabian here; click the Turabian (P/RL) tab for instructions on citing various sources.

How to succeed in this course
Read, and read carefully. Not just once, not quickly like it’s a news article, and not with distractions around. Turn off your TV, phone, radio, MP3; when reading online, close all other windows, set Skype to offline, turn of chats, and release yourself from Facebook etc. Philosophical texts reward focused, careful, and repeated reading.
     Consider this observation about reading:
Reading is an act of contemplation, perhaps the only act in which we allow ourselves to merge with the consciousness of another human being. We possess the books we read, animating the waiting stillness of their language, but they possess us also, filling us with thoughts and observations, asking us to make them part of ourselves.... In order for this to work, however, we need a certain type of silence, an ability to filter out the noise.
David L. Ulin, “The Lost Art of Reading,” Los Angeles Times, 8/9/2009

Make time, in a quiet space, to read.
     One strategy for reading that has proven helpful is this: Before class, read through the assignment carefully at least once, trying to make as much sense of it as you can. Then come to class and take good notes and participate (see below). Then, after class, read the assignment again. You should find it more comprehensible then. (If you don’t, come see me right away.)

Come to class, on time, prepared. Bring the current reading and your notes with you. Be prepared to answer questions about the main points of the reading without having to consult your notes or open the book. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, early in the semester, to share notes.

Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take a moment after class to fill them out.

Participate a lot. Some students assume their questions aren't worth asking or their comments not worth making, but often others wanted to say the same thing. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you are not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.

If you miss class, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, at the beginning of the semester, to share notes. If class is taught remotely, watch the recordings.

Keep up with the study guide. When new items are added, sit down with your books and notes (and classmates, if you like) and write out the clearest, most complete and thoughtful responses you can. Study your answers regularly to prepare for class discussion and for exams. This will pay off.

Seek help when you need it, or when you think you might need it, or just because. You do not need an appointment to drop in, though we can make one if you like. I do not bite, I promise.
