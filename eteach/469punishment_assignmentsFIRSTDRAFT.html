Assignments
 		Phil 469: Punishment
Prof. Jeremy Anderson
. .

Dates for assignments will be announced in class. Details and timing are subject to change; other readings may be added or substituted. We will probably not read everything here. Our pace will be dictated partly by our collective interests in the material but also by the need to cover certain areas.


Topic 1: The problem of punishment, and why it’s a big problem.

Topic 2: Other places and times, other practices.

Topic 3: Consequentialist (aka Utilitarian) justification of punishment.

Topic 4. Retributive justification of punishment.

Topic 5: Other justifications of punishment.

Topic 6: Abolition?




Topic 1: The problem of punishment, and why it’s a big problem. Readings:
1.1. Theoretical problems:
1.1.1.  Alan H. Goldman, “The Paradox of Punishment” (Philosophy & Public Affairs 9:1, October 1979)

1.1.2.  Boonin, The Problem of Punishment, chapter 1

1.2. Practical problems (especially the US predicament):
1.2.1.  Daniel P. Mears and Michael D. Reisig, “The Theory and Practice of Supermax Prisons” (Punishment and Society 8:33, 2006)

1.2.2.  Michael Tonry, “Causes and Cures of Racial Disparities in Sentencing” (YouTube; lecture given at IU Maurer School of Law, 3/7/2014)

1.2.3.  Chris Arnade, “The wealthy ‘make mistakes,’ the poor go to jail” (Guardian, 9/8/2013)

1.2.4.  David Kaiser and Lovisa Stannow, “The Shame of Our Prisons: New Evidence” (New York Review of Books, 10/24/2013)

1.2.5.  Jane Gross, “Born of Grief, 'Three Strikes' Laws Are Being Rethought” (Los Angeles Times, 12/2/2013)

1.2.6.  Henry C. Jackson, “Push for sentencing reform underway in Congress” (Associated Press, 1/4/2014)

1.2.7  Associated Press, “Arizona inmate dies 2 hours after execution began” (7/24/2014)

1.2.8.  Transcript of the execution of Joseph Randolph Wood III (7/23/3014)


Topic 2: Other places and times, other practices. Readings:

2.1.  Walter Woodburn Hyde, “The Homicide Courts of Ancient Athens” (U of Pennsylvania Law Revew and American Law Register 66:7/8, June 1, 1918)

2.2.  Olivia F. Robinson, “Crime and punishment and human rights in Ancient Rome” (Le monde antique et les droits de l'homme, 1998)

2.3.  William Christie McLeod, “Aspects of the Earlier Development of Law and Punishment” (Journal of Criminal Law and Criminology 23:2, 1932)

2.4.  unknown author, “Whipping and Castration as Punishments for Crime” (The Yale Law Journal 8:9, June 1899)

2.5.  Amelia Gentleman, “Inside Halden, the most humane prison in the world” (Guardian, 5/18/2012)

2.6.  Richard Orange, “Sweden closes four prisons as number of inmates plummets” (Guardian, 11/11/2013)

2.7.  Carol J. Williams, “First fatal shooting by police mars Iceland's serene history” (Los Angeles Times, 12/2/2013)

2.8.  Saeed Kamali Dehghan, “Iranian killer's execution halted at last minute by victim's parents” (Guardian, 4/16/2014)

2.9.  Iconic Photos, “Last Public Execution by Guillotine” (1/31/2014; video of the event—which is surprisingly brief but graphic—can be found online)

2.10.  Heinrich et al., “Costly Punishments Across Human Societies” (Science 312:5781, June 23, 2006)


Topic 3: Consequentialist (aka Utilitarian) justification of punishment. Readings:

3.1. The consequentialist rationale:
3.1.1.  Plato, excerpts from Gorgias

3.1.2.  Jeremy Bentham, excerpts from An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation

3.1.3.  Julian P. Alexander, “The Philosophy of Punishment” (Journal of the American Institute of Criminal Law and Criminology 13:2, August 1, 1922)

3.1.4.  Anthony Ellis, “A Deterrence Theory of Punishment” (The Philosophical Quarterly 53:212, July 2003)

3.1.5.  David Wood, “Punishment: Consequentialism.” (Philosophy Compass 5:6 June 2010)

3.2. Objections to the consequentialist rationale:
3.2.1.  J. D. Mabbott, “Punishment.” (Mind, New Series 48:190, April 1939)

3.2.2.  Igor Primorac, “Punishment and Utilitarianism” (International Philosophical Quarterly 22, December 1982)

3.2.3.  Boonin, The Problem of Punishment, chapter 2

3.2.4.  Paul H. Robinson, and John M. Darley, “Does Criminal Law Deter? A Behavioural Science Investigation” (Oxford Journal of Legal Studies 24:2, July 2004)

3.3. Objections to consequentialism, cont: does consequentialism sanction punishing the innocent?
3.3.1.  M. R. Glover, “Mr. Mabbott on Punishment” (Mind, New Series 48:192, October 1939; see esp. pp. 500ff)

3.3.1.  John Rawls, “Two Concepts of Rules” (The Philosophical Review 64:1, January 1955)

3.3.3.  George Schedler, “On Telishing the Guilty” (Ethics 86:3, April 1976)

3.3.4.  Igor Primorac, “Utilitarianism and Punishment of the Innocent.” (Rivista Internazionale Di Filosofia Del Diritto 57, October 1980)

3.3.5.  Guyora Binder and Nicholas J. Smith, “Framed: Utilitarianism and Punishment of the Innocent” (Rutgers Law Journal, Fall 2000)

3.4 Another problem for consequentialists: addressing the causes of crime:
3.4.1.  Thomas J. Bernard, Vold’s Theoretical Criminology, chapters 1 and 2


Topic 4. Retributive justification of punishment. Readings:
4.1. The retributivist rationale:
4.1.1.  Immanuel Kant, excerpts from The Metaphysics of Morals

4.1.2.  J. Angelo Corlett, “Making Sense of Retributivism” (Philosophy 76:295,  January 2001)

4.1.3.  J. Angelo Corlett, “Making More Sense of Retributivism: Desert as Responsibility and Proportionality” (Philosophy 78:304, April 2003)

4.1.4.  Hegel, excerpts from Philosophy of Right

4.1.5.  Thom Brooks, “Is Hegel a Retributivist?” (Bulletin of the Hegel Society of Great Britain 49–50,  January 2004)

4.1.6.  Herbert Morris, “Persons and Punishment” (The Monist 52:4 October 1968)

4.2. Objections to retributivism:
4.2.1.  Boonin, The Problem of Punishment, chapter 3

4.2.2.  K. G. Armstrong, “The Retributivist Hits Back” (Mind 70:280, October 1961)

4.2.3.  David Dolinko, “Some Thoughts About Retributivism” (Ethics 101:3, April 1991)

4.3. Objections to retributivism, cont: the right to be punished:
4.3.1.  David A. Hoekema, “The Right to Punish and the Right to Be Punished” (from John Rawls’ Theory of Social Justice, 1980)

4.3.2.  Igor Primorac, “Punishment as the Criminal’s Right” (Hegel-Studien XV, 1980)

4.3.3.  John Deigh, “On the Right to Be Punished: Some Doubts” (Ethics 94:2 January 1984)

4.3.4.  Markus Dirk Dubber, “The Right to Be Punished: Autonomy and Its Demise in Modern Penal Thought.” (Law and History Review 16:1, April 1998)

4.4. Objections to retributivism, cont: rights forfeiture
4.4.1.  John Locke, excerpts from Second Treatise of Government

4.4.2.  B J. Smart, “Understanding and Justifying Self-Defence.” (International Journal of Moral and Social Studies 4, September 1989)

4.4.3.  Richard L. Lippke, “Criminal Offenders and Right Forfeiture.” (Journal of Social Philosophy 32:1, March 2001)

4.4.4.  Stephen Kershnar, “The Forfeiture Theory of Punishment: Surviving Boonin’s Objections” (Public Affairs Quarterly 24:4 October 2010)

4.4.5.  Christopher Heath Wellman, “The Rights Forfeiture Theory of Punishment” (Ethics 122:2, January 2012)

4.5. Objections to retributivism, cont.: the yardstick problem
4.5.1.  Claudia Card, “Retributive Penal Liability” (American Philosophical Quarterly 7, January 1973; see esp. the problem she presents on p. 19)

4.5.2.  Michael Clark, “The Moral Gradation of Punishment” (The Philosophical Quarterly 21:83, April 1971)

4.5.3.  Tziporah Kasachkoff, “Hegel’s Retributivist Position on Punishment” (Philosophical Studies (Ireland) 25, January 1977)

4.5.4.  Michael Davis, “How to Make the Punishment Fit the Crime” (Ethics 93:4 July 1983)

4.5.5.  Roman David and Susanne Y. P. Choi, “Getting Even or Getting Equal? Retributive Desires and Transitional Justice” (Political Psychology 30:2 April 2009)


Topic 5: Other justifications of punishment. Readings:
5.1.  Alan H. Goldman, “Toward a New Theory of Punishment” (Law and Philosophy 1:1 April 1982)

5.2.  Jean Hampton, “Correcting Harms versus Righting Wrongs: The Goal of Retribution” (UCLA Law Review 39, 1992)

5.3.  Matthew D. Adler, “Expressive Theories of Law: A Skeptical Overview” (University of Pennsylvania Law Review 148:5, May 2000)

5.4.  Nathan Hanna, “Say What? A Critique of Expressive Retributivism” (Law and Philosophy 27:2, March 2008)

5.5.  Boonin, The Problem of Punishment, chapter 4


Topic 6: Abolition? Readings:
6.1.  Boonin, The Problem of Punishment, chapter 5

6.2.  Nathan Hanna, “Liberalism and the General Justifiability of Punishment” (Philosophical Studies 145:3, September 2009)

6.3.  Greg Roebuck and David Wood, “A Retributive Argument against Punishment” (Criminal Law and Philosophy 5:1, January 2011)
