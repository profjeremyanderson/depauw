Questions for the early exam (Friday February 25).


The point of this exam is to encourage you to reflect on the material we have covered so far, and consider which elements of it are most important: for jus ad bellum, what the criteria are and what they mean; for realism, pacifism, and just-war theory, what main conclusions different thinkers arrive at and their main reasons for doing so. This matters because these form the background for much of what we will discuss for the rest of the course.

I will ask you to explain views from the areas we have studied. By "explain" I mean: don't attempt to provide every possibly relevant detail. There is not time for that. Instead, your task is to identify and articulate what is most important and distinctive about the views you explain.

On the other hand, it would not be good to try and boil each view down a mere few sentences; these views are too sophisticated to be summarized that briefly. You will have the entire class period, and should use it to demonstrate how well you grasp what we've covered. I recommend ensuring you cover the core of each view you discuss, and then using any remaining time to go back and add detail.

You may use your laptop to write your answers, provided you follow some simple security measures I will impose during the exam. The exam itself will be closed book, closed notes: you must write out your answers from memory during the class period.


You must answer question #1. Then answer two – and only two -- of the other questions (#2, 3, or 4).


1. Criteria for jus ad bellum. Identify the eight criteria for jus ad bellum in the Cook reading. For each one, briefly (a) explain how it is supposed to constrain us from resorting to war and (b) discuss how it might be problematic.   


2. Realism. Here is a definition of realism: “Political leaders may, and will, do whatever they believe will maintain or increase their power. Morality plays no role in this, so rulers will ignore it. So, morality has no place in international relations, including in war.” Pick either Machiavelli or Hobbes; not both. For the one you choose, answer these questions:
  • To what extent do they (Machiavelli or Hobbes) fit this definition of realism?
  • To what extent to they (Machiavelli or Hobbes) depart from it?
Give examples from the text to support your answers (since the exam is in class you need not cite).


3. Pacifism. We have examined two defenses of anti-war pacifism: Lackey’s, and Reader’s. Choose either Lackey or Reader (not both) and explain how he or she supports the view that it is morally wrong to go to war. If you discuss Lackey, focus on section 5 (Lackey, 31-35); you may ignore the rest. If you discuss Reader, focus on sections 2 and 3 (Reader, 171-8).


4. Criticism of pacifism or realism. We have read Anscombe’s critique of pacifism and Walzer’s critique of realism. Pick just one of them. If you choose Anscombe, (a) explain her reasons for rejecting pacifism and (b) discuss how effective her reasons are against either Lackey or Reader (not both). If you choose Walzer, explain his reasons for rejecting realism.
