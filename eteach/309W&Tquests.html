Questions for the early exam (Monday February 25).

The exam questions will be posted below. You will have the whole class period, 90 minutes, to answer the questions. You must answer three (and only three), so you will have about half an hour per question.

The point of this exam is to encourage you to reflect on the material we have covered so far, and consider which elements of it are most important: for realism, pacifism, and just-war theory, what main conclusions different thinkers arrive at and their main reasons for doing so; for jus ad bellum, what the criteria are and what they mean. This matters because these form the background for much of what we will discuss for the rest of the course.

To accomplish these goals, I will ask you to explain views from the areas we have studied. By "explain" I mean: don't attempt to provide every possibly relevant detail; there is not time for that. Instead, your task is to identify and articulate what is most important and distinctive about the views you explain. On the other hand, it would not be good to try and boil each view down a mere few sentences; these views are too sophisticated to be summarized that briefly.

You will have the entire class period, and should use it to demonstrate how well you grasp what we've covered. I recommend ensuring you cover the core of each view you discuss, and then using any spare time to go back and add detail.

You may use your laptop to write your answers, provided you follow some simple security measures I will impose during the exam. The exam itself will be closed book, closed notes: you must write out your answers during the class period.

For the exam you will need to answer #4 and at least two of the other questions given here. (You can get an 'A+' with great answers to three questions.) I will pass out copies of the questions at the exam.

1. Realism. We have examined the views Machiavelli's views. He makes empirical claims about how things are and, based on those claims, makes recommendations to political leaders regarding war and relations with other people. Explain (a) Machiavelli's view of how things are, (b) his recommendations to political leaders, and (c) how his recommendations arise from his empirical claims. Of course Machiavelli has lots of recommendations about lots of things, like colonizing and so on. So focus on what he says about war, and what he says about relating to other people (e.g., whether to be ethical) and why.

2. Pacifism. We have examined two defenses of anti-war pacifism: Lackey's, and Reader's. Choose either Lackey or Reader (not both) and explain how he or she supports the view that it is morally wrong to go to war. If you discuss Lackey, be sure to discuss section 5 (pp. 16-20), including his discussion of World War II at the end. If you discuss Reader, be sure to include section 1 (170-1) and section 3 (pp. 174-8); it would also be good to include her critique of the presumption of war-ism (pp. 171-2). (Since this is a question about the wrongness of going to war, you need not include Reader's argument against having a war-machine.)

3. Just-war theory. We have read three arguments for just-war theory as an alternative to realism and/or pacifism: Kilner's, Anscombe's, and Walzer's. Choose one of them (you need not discuss more than one) and (a) explain the author's critique of pacifism (or, in Walzer's case, realism) and (b) explain the author's account of how war can be morally justified.

4. Criteria for jus ad bellum. Identify the eight criteria for jus ad bellum in the Cook reading. For each one, (a) explain how it is supposed to constrain us from resorting to war and (b) discuss how it might be problematic.
