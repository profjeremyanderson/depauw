Questions for the early exam.

There are four questions below. You will have the whole class period, 60 minutes, for the exam. Your task will be to answer question #1 plus two (and only two) of the others.

The goals of this are to
  1. Get you to memorize the criteria for jus ad bellum, and
  2. Encourage you to reflect on realism, pacifism, and just-war theory; specifically, what main conclusions different thinkers arrive at and their main reasons for doing so. This is important because these ideas form the background for much of what we will discuss for the rest of the course.
For #2, I will ask you to explain views from the areas we have studied. By "explain" I mean: don't try to provide every possibly relevant detail. There is not time for that. Instead, identify and articulate what is most important and distinctive about the views you explain. On the other hand, it would not be good to try and boil each view down a mere few sentences; these views are too sophisticated to be summarized that briefly.

I suggest you:
  • Answer #1 briefly. The other questions require more thought and explanation.
  • For the other two you answer, ensure you cover the core of each view you discuss, and then use any spare time to go back and add detail.
You may use your laptop to write your answers, provided you follow some simple security measures I will impose during the exam. The exam itself will be closed book, closed notes: you must write out your answers during the class period.


For the exam you will need to answer #1 and two (and only two) of the other questions. I will pass out copies of these questions at the exam.

1. Criteria for jus ad bellum. Identify the eight criteria for jus ad bellum in the Cook reading. For each one, (a) briefly explain how it is supposed to constrain us from resorting to war and (b) briefly discuss how it might be problematic.   

2. Realism. We have examined the views Machiavelli's views. He makes claims about how things are and, based on those claims, makes recommendations to political leaders regarding war and relations with other people. Explain (a) Machiavelli's view of how things are, (b) his recommendations to political leaders, and (c) how his recommendations arise from his empirical claims. Of course Machiavelli has lots of recommendations about lots of things, like colonizing and so on. So focus on what he says about war, and what he says about relating to other people (especially whether to be ethical) and why.

3. Pacifism. We have examined two defenses of anti-war pacifism: Lackey's and Reader's. Choose either Lackey or Reader (not both) and explain how he or she supports the view that it is morally wrong to go to war. If you discuss Lackey, be sure to discuss section 5 (pp. 16-20), including his discussion of World War II at the end. If you discuss Reader, be sure to include section 1 (170-1) and section 3 (pp. 174-8); it would also be good to include her critique of the presumption of war-ism (pp. 171-2). (Since this is a question about the wrongness of going to war, you need not include Reader's argument against having a war-machine.)

4. Just-war theory. We have read three arguments for just-war theory as an alternative to realism and/or pacifism: Kilner's, Anscombe's, and Walzer's. Choose just one of them and (a) explain the author's critique of pacifism (or, in Walzer's case, his critique of realism) and (b) explain the author's account of how war can be morally justified.
