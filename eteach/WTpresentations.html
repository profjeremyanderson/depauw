For your presentation, I expect you to to identify the philosophical issues in your film and the assigned readings, prepare the class to identify and think about those issues as they watch the film, and lead a discussion exploring them as deeply as possible after the film. You must demonstrate that you’ve done relevant research and become familiar with the film and thought about it all. Doing this well requires substantial preparation. More specifically:


Basic requirements (i.e., how to do at least a passable job):
Presenting your film is a major assignment, worth 25% of your grade for the course. To pass the course you will need to earn a score of at least 2 on your presentation. To do that you must do at least an adequate job at each of the following. If you do not, you will not pass this assignment, and will get a D or F in the course.

A. Meet with me. At least one meeting is mandatory (and helpful for you!).

B. Master the assigned reading. Obviously you should read it especially carefully, several times, and well beforehand. You’ll need to be able to refer back to it during class discussions. If you have trouble understanding it, consult with me, sooner rather than later. If you have ideas about what the class should read, let me know ASAP so I can consider it.

You will still need to submit a reading response, and you’ll still have to post to the online discussion after class, by the way. But considering your preparations, those should be easy. 

C. Research your film and the philosophical issues in it. This is essential even if you think you know your film very well. To do this:
1. Learn about your film and reactions to it. Go to the Internet Movie Database, find your film, and click on the links to learn more about it. Check the critics' reviews (called "external reviews," accessible by clicking "MORE" on the menus just above the film title): under the names of the director and writers, there are links to reviews from many publications. Read a bunch to find out how others have reacted to the film. If Wikipedia has a page on your film, check it out (but don’t believe all you find!). It may have leads to interesting articles and books about the issues in your film.

2. Learn more about the philosophical issues in your film. In the assigned reading(s) for your film, various terms and issues will arise. (For example, in the reading for The Truman Show we came across “the Problem of the External World.”) Find out more about those terms and issues. Some discussion of them may be found in the material you find via IMDB, Wikipedia, etc.; more may be found by consulting a dictionary of philosophy (for terms, and brief bios of philosophers) and encyclopedias of philosophy (for issues). A trove of philosophy links is available via my links homepage, http://jeremyanderson.net (or here's a direct link to the links). Really good freely available encyclopedias include:
  • The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy
  • The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy
Other sources for discussion of philosophy in films:
  • Google Scholar. Enter the title of your film, or the title plus the word "movie", or the title plus the director's name, or any of those and the word "philosophy," and interesting writing about your film may well come up.
  • Wikipedia may have a page devoted to your film. If you're lucky it may have some discussion of philosophical issues in the film and/or references to such discussions which you can then look up. Wikipedia will also likely provide links to reviews which may be philosophically interesting.
  • Philosophical Films (list of films with philosophical ideas, includes some discussion of each and often some interesting discussion questions you might use)
  • Filmosophy (kinda like Philosophical Films, but seems to have some more in-depth discussion)
  • Film-Philosophy ("an international, fully open access academic journal dedicated to the engagement between film studies and philosophy").

D. Come see me to talk about what you’re planning (or to ‘fess up that you can’t think of anything). I’ll help you. One meeting is mandatory. More is better. See me sooner rather than later. If my office hours don’t work for you, let’s make an appointment.

E. Turn in some things to me by 2:00pm the day before your film:
  • Document your work, and submit your documentation.  Instructions are below. 
  • Along with your documentation, submit at least two questions for me to post to the after-class discussion forum for your film.

F. Do something before the movie to set up the class for viewing it. You could:
  • Give any background that might be helpful for understanding the film
  • Discuss how the readings connect with the film
  • Identify the issues you think are most important for people to think about
  • Identify some aspect(s) of the film, or scenes in it, that you want people to pay special attention to
  • Present questions you want us to think about during the film so we can discuss them afterwards
  • Etc.
Whatever you do should help set up whatever you want to do after the film.

G. After the film, you’ve got some options.
1. You could present, and argue for, your take on the film. You may agree with the assigned readings or not, but you should make some connection between your take and the readings’. The challenge here is being able to explain persuasively why the class should agree with you.

2. Alternatively, you could lead discussion (which is what I tend to do). This is challenging, too, but in a different way: you’ll need to be prepared to lead discussion for a while. Think about all the issues in the film, and then decide which are most important. Develop questions to ask the class, and think about what follow-up questions you might ask to get people to think more deeply. Try not to “lead” the class into giving your answers. Push class members to think more carefully. 

3. You could do some of both of these. Or you could come up with something different. It’s up to you.

And of course you can come talk with me about any of this stuff.


How to do an excellent job
The above are things needed to do a passable job on this assignment. You could make it even better. For example, you could isolate a clip or two from the film to show afterwards, so that we can re-examine it and discuss it. (This might have been good for the couch scene between Lester and Angela near the end of American Beauty, for example, since it highlights important differences between them.) Or you could bring in a quick clip from a different film for comparison. If you think it’d help, you could use PowerPoint to enhance your presentation. It is often very helpful to create a 1-2 page handout briefly summarizing any points you want to make.

What makes a presentation excellent, though, isn't just doing more things. It's doing things which (a) demonstrate that you have done a good deal of careful research and thought and (b) raise the intellectual level of discussion in the class. 


Documentation and academic integrity: READ THIS CAREFULLY
Each student in each group must submit written evidence of what she or he individually contributed to the presentation. This is not hard, but it is important.

Document your work by submitting, by 2:00pm the day before you present:

1. A single Word document attachment containing (here's a sample of what I'm after):
  • A brief statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was.
  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, divided into two parts: those sources used in the presentation and those consulted but not used.
    • Your list of sources must include: 
      • Full bibliographic information for any print source, including assigned texts
      • Full bibliographic information, plus the full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.jstor.org" but the full, exact URL so that I can click on your e-mailed source list and find the exact material you relied upon. Use the "stable" or "persistent" URL whenever possible.
      • If citing a journal article accessed electronically, give BOTH the bibliographic data plus the full URL
    • You must cite your sources in Chicago or Turabian format. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page.
2. Any audio/visual aids you use (PowerPoint, handouts, etc.) with any and all sources used in them documented in them. Endnotes are one good way to document sources in PowerPoints: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in the PowerPoint that comes from some source--image, claim, quote, whatever--put the number of the source by it.

This documentation is due by e-mail by 2:00pm the day before your presentation. Timely documentation is part of the assignment, so your presentation is not considered completed without it. Lateness without a strong reason will result in a grade penalty, and the later it is the worse the penalty. This is potentially very serious: since (according to the syllabus) you must get at least a 2.0 on this assignment to pass the course, failure to submit your documentation on time could prevent you from passing the course.

DePauw's policy on Academic Integrity applies to this assignment (and all work for the course, actually). It is not acceptable to present work done for another course; that's getting credit twice for the same work. Nor may you present work done for some extracurricular activity, because then you're not doing new work for this course. (Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be acceptable; consult with me beforehand.) You must cite all the sources you relied on for any part of your presentation--the ideas, arguments, images, clips, everything, whether you quoted them or paraphrased them or just borrowed the inspiration. These citations must be included in the required documentation of your work for the presentation, and if you do a PowerPoint show, all sources used in it must also be listed at the end of the slides, and you must send me a copy of the PowerPoint. Failure to meet these requirements will automatically and substantially hurt your assignment grade, and might be prosecuted for academic dishonesty. The minimum penalty for dishonesty is worse than not doing the assignment at all, and will almost guarantee a failing grade for the course.
