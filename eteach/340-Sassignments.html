Justifications of governmental authority:

Plato in the Crito justifies obedience to government based on gratitude, consent, and self-interest. In Republic Plato develops his political theory as an analogue of human virtue; he says living in the right sort of political order serves both individual and collective goods.
1.  Plato: Crito (CMPT pp. 64-71 or online; read this before the first class meeting if notified to)
2.  Plato: Republic, Book I (CMPT pp. 75-93)
3.  Plato: Republic, Book II (CMPT pp. 93-110)
4.  Plato: Republic, Book III (CMPT pp. 110-30)
5.  Plato: Republic, Book IV (CMPT pp. 130-47)


Aquinas argues that humans are naturally social, and develops a theistic natural law theory.

Hobbes, like Plato and Aquinas, argues that we need government due to our nature, though his view of human nature differs from either of theirs. Like Aquinas, Hobbes is a natural law theorist--but a secular one, and Hobbes founds society on a hypothetical social contract.

The limits of authority and the right to disobey:

Paine and Jefferson argue that revolutionary violence is justified in response to certain injustices. Note the clear echoes of Locke in the Declaration of Independence.

34.  Paine: Common Sense (online)
35.  Jefferson (et al.): The Declaration of Independence (online)


Thoreau critiques government generally, and argues for nonviolent resistance to injustices.

36.  Thoreau: Civil Disobedience (online; notice the three questions in the Thoreau blurb on the Online Readings page)


Spencer argues that we may simply ignore government.

37.  Spencer: The Right to Ignore the State (online)


Marx argues for revolution, on very different grounds from Paine or Jefferson.
38.  Marx: Alienated Labor (CMPT pp. 1160-6 [1086-92])
39.  Marx: Manifesto of the Communist Party, to the end of Section II, plus the last paragraph of Section IV (CMPT pp. 1183-1202 [1109-28])


Hughes points out American racism with poetry.

40.  Hughes: five poems (online)


King borrows from Aquinas and Thoreau to argue for a nonviolent approach to gaining equality. His ideal is a just and integrated America.
41.  King: Letter from Birmingham Jail (online)


Malcolm X also wants equality for Black people, but seeks separation, not integration.
42.  Malcolm X: The Ballot or the Bullet (online)


Eric Rudolph argues that violence (in his case, terrorist bombing) is justifiable.
43.  Eric Rudolph: Allocution (online)


Legal paternalism and legal moralism:
Aristotle (Plato's most famous student) argues that the purpose of government is to make us happy by making us better people.

44.  Aristotle: Nicomachean Ethics, Book X (CMPT pp. 349-360), especially chapters 5-9; Politics Book I chapters 1-2 (CMPT pp. 361-2 [same])


Mill argues that government may only interfere with your liberty to prevent you from harming others.

45.  Mill: On Liberty, chapter 1 (CMPT pp. 1010-17 [936-42])


Teetor tells about a mentally disturbed woman. At what point may, or should, government intervene?

46.  Teetor: Prelude to a Death (online)


Kalb argues that your private business isn't really just your business, but also society's.

47.  Kalb: Sexual Morality FAQ (online)
