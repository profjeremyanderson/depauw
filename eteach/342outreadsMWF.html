Assignment 5: The insanity defense.
  • Background: review the portions of B. Justification and Excuse, pp. 491-9 [4th ed.:  406-14] which discuss insanity.

    • Norval Morris, The Abolition of the Insanity Defense, pp. 518-22 [4th ed.: 433-7]
      • Morris's "ghetto argument" is often misunderstood. Readers think Morris wants social adversity to be an excuse, but he does not. Here is the ghetto argument in standard form:
        1. Insanity causes some crimes.
        2. But “gross social adversity” causes even more crimes.
        3. So (by 1, 2), if insanity is an excuse, then gross social adversity should also be an excuse.
        4. But gross social adversity should NOT be an excuse.
        5. So (by 3, 4), insanity should not be an excuse, either.
    • Stephen J. Morse, Excusing the Crazy: The Insanity Defense Reconsidered, pp. 522-6 [4th ed.: 437-40]

Assignment 9: Justifying punishment, cont.
  • Background: portions of several previous readings are relevant to the justification of punishment one way or another, and so are worth another look.
    • Review the portions of "C. Punishment and Responsibility," pp. 528-31 [4th ed.: 442-6] that discuss the justification of punishment.
    • Review the portions of Kadish ("The Criminal Law and the Luck of the Draw") which discuss different kinds of retributivism--specifically, the last five paragraphs of the long section titled, "The Rational Indefensibility of the Harm Doctrine." This text gives you a sense of how some different forms of retributivism work, albeit from a critic's perspective.
    • Review the portion of Rosen ("The Battered Woman's Defense") which discusses "forfeiture theory"--the four paragraphs from "Essentially, Robinson's theory is a forfeiture theory" onward. Rosen discusses forfeiture theory specifically regarding self-defense, but forfeiture's basic idea is that by committing a crime you "forfeit" some rights, and that idea is very common in people's thinking about punishment.
  • Main readings:
    • Richard B. Brandt, The Utilitarian Theory of Criminal Punishment, pp. 553-7 [not in 4th edition; find it on our Google drive or on Library e-reserves]
      • The title here is misleading. Brandt is a rule-utilitarian; as he explains starting at the very end of 553, he believes society should have rules, and the criterion for a rule is, "Would it maximize expected utility if everyone followed this rule, or not?" Contrast this with act-utilitarianism, which holds that we should judge every individual action by whether it maximizes utility.
    • Michael Moore, The Argument for Retributivism, pp. 558-62 [4th ed.: 456-61]
    • Kant on Retribution and the Death Penalty (excerpts from The Metaphysics of Morals)
      • Moore argues for retributivism mainly by rejecting the consequentialist rationale for punishment. He refrains from arguing much for retributivism, and doesn't say much about retributivism. Kant tells us more about how at least his 's particular "eye for an eye" version of retributivism is supposed to work.
  • Optional: Jeremy Bentham, A Utilitarian Theory of Punishment [4th edition pp.  452-6; not in 5th edition; ; find it on our Google drive or on Library e-reserves] (When discussing Bentham, Brandt alludes to this piece.)

      • Let me help you with this challenging read. Dworkin discusses three kinds of originalism: semantic originalism, which he distinguishes into (a) dated semantic originalism and (b) principled semantic originalism; and something else he calls (c) expectation originalism. In other words, Dworkin thinks Scalia is wrong about what he says he's doing, and what Scalia says is the right approach is actually wrong anyway.

Assignment 32: Classical legal theories 1: positions, and positivism.

Our main goal in this assignment is to understand Legal Positivism (LP). But LP is weird, which makes it hard to understand at first. However, it will be more understandable if we see it as a response to something more familiar: Natural Law (NL).
       So, in this assignment, use the background reading to get a general sense of NL. Then use Nino to get a sense of how NL could work in practice. The opinion of the first justice, Sempronius, is an extreme form of NL. St. Augustine once wrote, "An unjust law is no law at all" (and natural law fans Aquinas and Martin Luther King, Jr. quote him). Sempronius takes that literally. (The third justice, Ticius, represents a more moderate form of NL: that a country's laws deserve a presumption of respect, but that presumption may be overridden by gross violations of fundamental moral principles.) Sempronius and Ticius may remind you a bit of Robert Jackson.
       The second judge, Caius, criticizes NL sharply. Look for Caius's reasons for rejecting Sempronius's view. Caius more or less represents LP.
       You'll see similar criticism of NL in Austin, the champion of LP. Austin criticizes NL as "stark nonsense" and "mischievous" (Austin, 68 [4th ed.: 53]--last two paragraphs of Lecture 5). Why does Austin say that about NL? How does Austin try to avoid those problems?
  • Background: C. Classical Theories of Law, pp. 58-64 [4th ed.: 40-6]
  • Main readings:
    • Carlos Nino, Legality and Justice: A Fictional Case [4th ed.: 46-9; not in 5th edition; find it in on our Google drive or on Library e-reserves]
    • John Austin, Legal Positivism, pp. 64-9 [4th ed.:  49-54]
