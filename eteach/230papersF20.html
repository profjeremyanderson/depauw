Requirements for all papers.

Every paper should:
  • Meet the general requirements here for format, style, and integrity
  • Respond to the prompt
  • Participate in the peer review process: turn in a complete draft on time and comment on others' drafts.
    • Your draft will not be graded. But the grade for your finished paper may be lowered if the peer-review draft is missing, incomplete, not well proofread, or doesn't meet the academic integrity requirements.
  • To be complete, the draft:
    • Needs an introduction with thesis and road map (see advice for writing a good paper, below)
    • Needs at least a couple of pages of discussion of your topic
    • Like reading responses, it must be carefully proofread and meet the academic integrity requirements

Format:
  • Word (.docx or .doc) or Rich Text Format (.rtf) only. No Pages files.

  • Double spaced, 1" margins, numbered pages, 12-point Times New Roman or similar font.

  • Your name and an accurate word count (for the text of the paper, not counting any footnotes, endnotes, or works cited) at the top.

  • Free of errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc. Do not trust your college GPA to auto-correct or to grammar- or spell-checkers; proofread your paper yourself. Read it aloud!

  • Length: 1100-1500 words (3-5 pages). However, I primarily grade content, not length. I will not reduce your grade just because your paper is a little below or a little over the suggested word count. Instead, think of it this way: if you write your paper and end up significantly below the suggested length, you probably need to say more. If you end up well over the suggested length, you probably need to omit some material or be more concise.
Style:
  • Strive for simplicity. Use simple, clear language as much as possible. For more specific advice:
    • Here are quick guides to writing concisely, rephrasing to eliminate extra words, and avoiding some common kinds of wordiness.
    • Here is a method for simplifying your writing.
    • Here is a guide to how NOT to write.
  • Avoid cliches. Please don't tell me how long humankind has pondered your topic ("For centuries, humanity has pondered whether God exists..."), or give me Webster’s definition of anything.

  • You may write in the first person in this course if you prefer.
     
  • Revise and rewrite. Do not turn in your first draft, no matter how great a writer you are. Complete the paper and then let it sit for at least a few hours. Then come back and revise the whole thing to make it even clearer and more logical. Then repeat that process. I also recommend you get assistance from DePauw's Writing Center. Of course all this means that it is a bad idea to start the paper right before it is due, but you knew that.
Academic integrity, and how to cite:
  • Citing and quoting properly is vital. Failing to cite and quote as required will automatically lower your grade, possibly down to failing. I may also prosecute you for plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If the prosecution is successful, the minimum penalty is usually worse than turning in no work at all. So you must keep track of, and properly document, whose words and ideas you are using. DePauw provides lots of info on understanding academic integrity and how to avoid plagiarism on this helpful page. But here are some guidelines that cover most cases:
  • Use these guidelines:

    • If the idea isn’t yours, cite it even if you don't quote it. 
      If the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes       and cite it.
      • Whatever the source was--assigned texts, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, a web page, your mother, space aliens, or whatever--cite it.
    • Citing properly is easy. As you work on the paper, make a note of what you read and who you talk to about it, keeping track of what words and ideas come from each source. Then, if you decide to use any words or ideas from these notes, cite the sources and put their words in quotation marks.

    • Examples of citing:

      When you mention an idea from the text without quoting it, cite like this:

              We would not want to live our lives in an experience machine
              because, in it, we would have the experience of doing things
              but never actually do them (Nozick, 43).

              (Notice the period comes after the citation.)

      When quoting your source, cite like this:

               Although Williams grants that "Utilitarianism is in more than one
                way an important subject," he rejects it and predicts that
                "The day cannot be too far off in which we hear no more of it" (9).

               (Notice the citation is outside the quotation marks, and the period
               comes after the citation. That's because the quotation marks
               contain only what the author said, and the citation is part of your
               sentence.)

    • For:
      • Readings with page numbers: Give the author's name and the page number in parentheses, like this: (Plato, 15) or (Nielsen, 229). (Only cite Pojman for things he wrote, like the introductory material or his article on moral objectivism.) Exception: if the author's name is mentioned in your sentence, just the page number is enough.

      • Readings with paragraph numbers: give the author's name and the paragraph number, like this: (King, 18).
         
      • Readings without page or paragraph numbers: If the assigned text is, for example, a web page with no page numbers, just cite the author, like this: (Garrett).

      • Citing class discussion: first, please don't cite class discussion for material that is in assigned texts. That's lazy. It's also misleading; you should cite the origin of an idea whenever you can, not just where you last heard it. For any material that is in an assigned text or web page or video or whatever, use that assignment as your source and cite it. But if you use material from class--for example, objections that aren't in any text--cite it like this: (Phil 230, 9/2/2020).

      • If you get something from a recorded class meeting, also cite the time: (Phil 230, 10/16/2020, 15:35).

      • Citing outside sources: Cite them in parentheses--like (Miyazaki, 247)--wherever you use them in the paper, and provide a bibliography. You must include the URL for all sources you access via the internet.

        Use Chicago or Turabian format, using the parenthetical citations/reference list style. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page; click the tab labeled "Turabian (P/RL)" for instructions on how to cite various kinds of sources. Use the "Parenthetical Citations/Reference List Style," where you put the parenthetical reference in your text--notice it's in the same (author, page #) format to use for citing our texts--and at the end of the paper give the bibliographic information in a reference list.
Research:

There are many sources you might consult, but they are not all equally good. You must be judicious about what sources you rely on in papers and presentations. Short paper 3 on Kant.

Deadlines are here on Moodle.

Prompt:

Write an argumentative essay on Kantian ethics. Topics could include, but are not limited to:
  • As mentioned in class (2020-11-02), there are several interesting situations we've discussed this semester. Your paper could
    (1) explain what Kant's Formula of Universal Law would say to do in one of these situations and
    (2) argue for whether the FUL gets it right.
    Situations include: 
    • Giving back weapons to someone who’s gone crazy (Plato, 3—and a similar case in Aquinas, 30)
    • King disobeying segregation laws (King, 15)
    • Plugging in to the Experience Machine (Nozick)
    • Persecuting the child in Omelas (Le Guin, 3-5)
    • The fat man stuck in the cave mouth (Nielsen, 222-3, 228-30)
    • The magistrate and the threatening mob (Nielsen, 223-5)
    • Giving a student an undeserved grade (Hospers, 1, or his other examples—the burglar, the district attorney, or voting)
    • Jim and the Indians (Williams, 3-4)
    • Saving people from famine (Singer)
    • Lots of examples in Harwood
Advice for this topic: to do (1) well, there are two challenges to keep in mind. The first is stating the maxim so it  fits the situation. The second is to remember Kant is not a consequentialist. A maxim doesn't pass the 4-step test by having good results but by being logically possible to universalize (step 3), and logically consistent to will (step 4).
  • Kant argues that the that the moral law can only be justified a priori (by pure reason), and not by anything related to human nature or circumstances (Kant 1). Several authors we've read would disagree, including Rachels, Aquinas, Glaucon, Bentham, and Mill. You could present Kant's argument as charitably as possible, present an opposing view from one of the others, and argue for who makes the better case about how morality can be justified.

  • Feldman argues that Kant's Formula of Universal Law is faulty because it (a) permits some actions which really are wrong and (b) forbids some actions which really should be allowed OK (Feldman, 226-7). You could present one of those criticisms -- (a) or (b) --  and then critically evaluate whether Feldman's criticism of Kant is fair.

    Advice for this one: avoid writing an "Amen!" paper--one where you present Feldman's criticism and basically say, "Yeah, Feldman's right." That is too easy! It's not much more than writing up notes from class. You need to subject Feldman to serious scrutiny. For example, you might grant that Feldman is right that the FUL has problems, but consider whether those problems are any worse than, say, some problem we noted with some consequentialist theory. Or you might argue that Feldman is mistaken--that he has been unfair to Kant in some way.
If you would like to write about something else, contact me and let's negotiate a topic.

As before, your paper should be about 1100-1500 words (3-5 pages) long.

Short Paper 1 on the basis of moral rules.

Deadlines are here on Moodle.
Prompt:

Our texts have promoted competing ideas about the basis of morality: that there is no basis (Thrasymachus); that morality's sole basis is culture (Benedict); and that there is some universal, objective basis (Rachels, King, and Aquinas). They cannot all be right.

So where do you stand? Well, that's too big a question for a short paper. So let's make it more specific: write a paper in which you take a stand on one of our readings, or (since some of them said a whole lot), some significant part of one of our readings.

For example, here are some things you might write about. These are just suggestions:
About Thrasymachus

He claims that "justice is the advantage of the stronger" (Plato, 15), implying that morality is just a scam orchestrated by the strong against the weak. What are his reasons for this (Plato, 16-22, especially 21-2)? How compelling are they? You might consider them in light of some argument for objectivism we've read. Or, you might consider whether some objection(s) from Socrates (Plato, 22-34) raises later in the Plato reading, such as the one about the band of robbers (30-1). What conclusions, if any, can we draw about Thrasymachus' view on the value or nature of justice?

About Benedict

Benedict argues that society shapes most of its members to fit within its norms (6). She suggests this "moulding" is so profound that when we judge others we can do it only via the norms of our society, which vary so radically it is doubtful we can have any objective basis for morality (5). For a paper on Benedict, you might discuss: To what extent, if any, is she right about this? You might consider Benedict in light of Rachels's argument that there is less moral disagreement than it seems (Rachels, 5-6), or Rachels's argument that all cultures must have some values in common (Rachels, 7), or some other argument for moral objectivism from Rachels, King, or Aquinas. Who's more persuasive, and why?

About Rachels

His article is divided into nine sections. You could focus your paper on any one of section 3, 4, 5, 6, or 7. For example, Rachels rejects the "Cultural Differences" argument in section 3; your paper could consider whether his rejection applies to Benedict. Rachels points to consequences of taking cultural relativism seriously in section 4; your paper could discuss how big a problem they are (or not). Is he right that there is less disagreement than it seems (section 5)? How plausible is his argument that all cultures have some rules in common (section 6)? In section 7, does Rachels manage to show that there is a culture-neutral standard of right and wrong (section 7)? For any of these, you might consider how someone else, like Thrasymachus or Benedict, might differ from Rachels.

About King
King argues that we can tell just from unjust laws (par. 16-19), and that we should obey just laws but disobey unjust laws (par. 10). How persuasively does he argue for this? You might consider whether King refutes Thrasymachus' arguments, or Benedict's. (If you're going to write about King, be sure to read paragraphs 1-14 as well as the assigned part.)
 
About Aquinas
Aquinas argues that there are some objective facts about human nature, and that some moral rules are objectively justified because they fit with our nature. More specifically, he says our natural inclinations are the basis for precepts of natural law, which in turn are the basis for making and evaluating human laws (Aquinas, 4-7). To what extent is any of this plausible? In what way(s) is it problematic? You might discuss how much Benedict's observations about culture create a problem for Aquinas' view.

Have another idea for a paper about these texts? Come see me soon to discuss it.

Your paper should be about 1100-1500 words (3-5 pages) long.


Advice for writing a good paper:

A good paper will show accurate comprehension of whoever you're writing about, and your careful, critical thinking about them. Here is a guide to how you might do that well. I am not saying this is the only way, but this works.
  • Give a brief (no more than half a page) introduction which includes a clear thesis and road map.
    • Your thesis is the overall conclusion that you are going to argue for; the main thing you want to persuade readers of. Something like, "Although Thrasymachus' claim that 'injustice is always stronger than justice' is open to some counterexamples, it is nevertheless more plausible than Socrates thinks," or "Benedict exaggerates how deeply our values are 'moulded' by culture; Pojman shows how we can identify some universally-valid moral rules."
    • After the thesis, give a road map--a short preview of the main steps you will take to argue for your thesis. For example, briefly state some objection(s) you will consider, and how you intend to rebut it/them.
    • Resist the urge to do much more than this in your intro.

  • Explain an author's view, using the text, in a page or two.
    • Pick something substantial from one of our texts which you want to support or criticize.
    • State the strongest reasons your chosen author provides for it, not all of them. Whether you agree with that argument or not, try to present it charitably: what is the most persuasive way the author's view can be stated?
    • Do not just write out your notes from class. Go to the text and find the argument there. You may find that it differs from the impression you got in class.
      • One good way to do this is quote-explain-quote-explain: to reconstruct an author's view, quote the author and explain what they mean, quote some more, explain some more, and so on.
    • Give yourself a page or two to do this well.

  • Critically evaluate that view.
    • One way to critically evaluate a view is to challenge it with objections. Given the disagreements between our authors, there are plenty of objections available.
    • It's more effective to give one or two strong points against the author's view, and state them well, than to raise many objections.
    • You may provide objections of your own or borrow from the text or class discussion. (Plenty of objections can likely be found in the text, especially from other authors, as the topic ideas above suggest.)
    • Again, give yourself a page or two to do this well.

  • In the end, draw a reasonable conclusion from the evidence you're provided.
    • You may support the author's view or reject it, in whole or in part, but whatever you conclude needs to be clearly supported by the evidence you've given.
    • Do not say what you think. Instead, explain why any reasonable reader should agree with your conclusion, giving clear reasons why. Philosophy is less about personal expression than it is about rational persuasion.
    • By the way: evaluating the author's view doesn't have to mean showing it is bad. Maybe it turns out the view is good--or at least that some objection(s) fail to show it's bad. There are lots of ways a paper can go. Here are some examples:
      • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Here's why the objection shows the author is wrong. The end. 
      • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. The objection, though, actually fails to show the author is wrong. So, the author's view is immune to that objection. 
      • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Here's why this objection fails. But wait, here's another objection, and it succeeds.
      • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Oh, and here's another. But here's why they both fail to show the argument is bad.
      • ...and so on.

  • Rewrite your paper at least once, and check your work.
          Clean up any misspellings, bad grammar, awkward phrasing, and so on. Make sure you have followed all the general requirements for papers given above.     Make sure you've met the academic integrity requirements.
