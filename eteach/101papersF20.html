Requirements for all papers

Every paper should:
  • Meet the general requirements here for format, style, and integrity
  • Respond to the prompt
  • Participate in the peer review process: turn in a complete draft on time and comment on others' drafts.
    • Your draft will not be graded. But the grade for your finished paper may be lowered if the peer-review draft is missing, incomplete, not well proofread, or doesn't meet the academic integrity requirements.
  • To be complete, the draft:
    • Needs an introduction with thesis and road map (see advice for writing a good paper, below)
    • Needs to present some argument from the course and critically evaluate it
    • Like reading responses, it must be carefully proofread and meet the academic integrity requirements

Format:
  • Word (.docx or .doc) or Rich Text Format (.rtf) only. Double spaced, 1" margins, numbered pages, 12-point Times New Roman or similar font.

  • Your name and the word count (for the text of the paper, not counting any footnotes, endnotes, or bibliography) at the top. 

  • Free of errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc. Do not trust your college GPA to auto-correct or to grammar- or spell-checkers! Proofread your paper yourself. Read it aloud!

  • About length: I grade content, not length. I will not lower your grade merely because your paper is not within the stated word count. Instead, think of it this way: if you write your paper and end up at only 800 words, you probably need to say more. If you end up at 1500+ words, you maybe should cut some material out or be more concise.
Style:
  • Strive for clarity. Use simple, clear language as much as possible. Please don't tell me how long humankind has pondered your topic ("For centuries, humanity has debated whether God exists..."), or give me Webster’s definition of anything. For more specific advice:
    • Here are quick guides to writing concisely, rephrasing to eliminate extra words, and avoiding some common kinds of wordiness.
    • Here is a method for simplifying your writing. 
    • Here is a guide to how NOT to write.

  • You may write in the first person in this course if you prefer.

  • Revise and rewrite. Complete a draft and then let it sit for at least a few hours. Then come back and revise the whole thing to make it even clearer and more logical, and to proofread it. I also recommend you get assistance from DePauw's Writing center. Of course all this means you should get started well before the deadline.
Academic integrity in writing (papers, reading responses, and exams):
  • Citing and quoting properly is vital. Failing to cite and quote as required will automatically lower your grade, potentially down to failing. I may also prosecute you for plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If the prosecution is successful, the minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all. So you must keep track of, and properly document, whose words and ideas you are using. DePauw provides lots of info on understanding academic integrity and how to avoid plagiarism on this helpful page.

  • Use these guidelines:

    • If the idea isn’t yours, cite it even if you don't quote it. 
      If the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes       and cite it.
      • Whatever the source was--assigned texts, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, a web page, your mother, space aliens, or whatever--cite it.

    • Citing properly is easy. As you work on the paper, make a note of what you read and who you talk to about it, keeping track of what words and ideas come from each source. Then, if you decide to use any ideas from these notes, cite the sources. If you use others' words, put them (or your close paraphrase) in quotation marks.

    • Examples of citing:

      When you mention an idea from the text without quoting it, cite like this:

              We should not completely trust our senses because they can deceive us; for example,
              at any moment we might be dreaming (Descartes, 145).

               (Notice the period comes after the citation.)

      When quoting your source, cite like this:

               The Third Meditation argument for God is essentially that "the idea of God
               which is in us must have God himself as its cause" (Descartes, 143).

               (Notice the citation is outside the quotation marks, and the period comes after
               the citation.       That's because the quotation marks contain only what the author said,
               and the citation is part of your sentence.)

      When you mention the author's name in the sentence, you don't need to include it in the citation (though including it is never wrong):

              Descartes says we should not completely trust our senses because they can
              deceive us; for example, at any moment we might be dreaming (145).

      For:
      • Readings with page numbers: Give the author's name and the page number in parentheses, like this: (Descartes, 145) or (Locke, 187). Only cite Bailey for things Bailey wrote, like introductory material, the logic reading, etc.

      • Readings with paragraph numbers: give the author's name and the paragraph number, like this: (Behe, 21).

      • Readings without page or paragraph numbers: If the assigned text is, for example, a web page with no page numbers, just cite the author.

      • Assigned videos: Give the video's title and the time at which the cited material begins, like this: ("Evidence for Intelligent Design," 4:13). After you give the full title the first time you cite it, you may abbreviate the title:

                First time cited: ("Evidence for Intelligent Design," 4:13)
                Second and later citations: ("Evidence," 2:03) etc.

      • Citing class discussion: first, please, do not cite class discussion for material that is in assigned texts or videos. That's lazy. It's also misleading; cite the origin of an idea whenever you can, not just where you last heard it. For any material that is in an assigned text or video or whatever, use that assignment as your source and cite it. But if you use material from class--for example, objections that aren't in any text--cite it like this: (Phil 101, 10/16/2020).

      • If you get something from a recorded class meeting, also cite the time: (Phil 101, 10/16/2020, 15:35).

      • Citing outside sources. No research outside of assigned texts is needed for papers in this course, and if you don't cite anything besides class and assigned texts or videos, I don't require a "works cited" page. But if you use any outside sources:
        • Choose them carefully. Here's a handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is, and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, you probably should not use that source. When in doubt, either ask me or do not use the source.
        • Cite outside sources in Chicago or Turabian format, using the parenthetical citations/reference list style. In other words, cite an outside source in parentheses--like (Miyazaki, 247)--wherever you use it in your paper, and provide a bibliography. You must include the URL for all sources you access via the internet. A quick guide to Turabian format is here on the Library web page; click the tab labeled "Turabian (P/RL)" for instructions on how to cite various kinds of sources.
  • DePauw provides lots of info on understanding academic integrity and how to avoid plagiarism on this helpful page.   

Paper Assignment #2

 Deadlines for your draft & finished paper are here on Moodle.

Prompt: explain, and evaluate, one of the arguments from our unit on matter--one you did not write about in the first exam's last question.

They are:
  • Descartes's argument for defective nature doubt (145-6)
  • Descartes's argument that God exists (M3; see the reading for assignment 16 on our Google drive)
  • Descartes's argument that God is no deceiver (158; cf. 157 and the reading for assignment 16 on our Google drive)
  • Descartes's argument that mind and body are "really distinct" (i.e., separable; 167)
  • Descartes's argument that material things exist (167-8)
  • Locke's argument that material things exist (185-7, esp. §§3-8)
  • Berkeley's argument that we cannot know matter exists (§§18-19)
  • Berkeley's argument that God exists (§§29-32; he restates it in §§145-149)
If you would like to write about something else in the course from after James, see me!

Explain your chosen argument carefully and completely. Then critically evaluate it. For this paper, the critical evaluation is just as important as getting the exposition right.

As before, your paper should be about 1000-1300 words (about 3-4 pages).


Here is my advice for how to do this well:
  • First, provide a brief (1/2 page or less) introduction which includes
    • A clear thesis (why you think the argument is good or bad) and
    • A road map (a brief summary of how you will support your thesis). Again, resist the temptation to do much more than this in your introduction.
    • Make your thesis and road map specific, not generic:

    • Generic, less informative maps:
      		 Better, more specific map:
      "First I will present the argument. Then I will object to it and conclude the argument is unsound."


      "I will first present the argument. Then I will present an objection to it and show why the objection fails."

      		"After explaining Descartes' argument that God exists, I will defend it against an objection. The objection tries to show Descartes' argument is unsound because people do not all share the same idea of God. I will argue, however, that this objection does not show that any of Descartes' premises are false or that his argument is invalid, so the objection fails."
  • Second, explain your chosen argument, in 1-2 pages. Use the text; don't just write up your notes. 
    • My advice for this part is the same as for the first paper. Again, presenting the argument by just writing up your notes is not as good as a paper where you consult the text and explain, in your own words, what it says.

  • Third, evaluate that argument. Argue for why some objection(s) shows, or fails to show, it is unsound or weak, giving the best reasons you can for why any reasonable person should agree with your assessment.
    • As noted above, this part is just as important as the exposition in this paper.
    • My advice for this part is the same as for the first paper, plus this:
      • If you present 1-2 good objections and explain how each tries to show the argument is weak or unsound, that will be good.
      • If you do that, but also consider what rebuttal(s) a supporter of the argument might offer, and discuss how effective the rebuttal is, that's even better.
    • By the way: evaluating the argument doesn't have to mean showing the argument is bad. Maybe it turns out the argument is good--or at least that some objection(s) fail to show it's bad. There are many ways a paper can go. Here are some examples:
      • Here's an argument. Here's an objection. Here's why the objection shows the argument is bad. The end. 
      • Here's an argument. Here's an objection. The objection, though, fails to show the argument is bad. So, the argument is immune to that objection. 
      • Here's an argument. Here's an objection. Here's why this objection fails. But wait, here's another objection, and it succeeds. So the argument is bad.
      • Here's an argument. Here's an objection. Oh, and here's another. But here's why they both fail to show the argument is bad. So the argument is immune to both those objections.

  • Before you turn in your draft:
    • Make sure you have followed the general requirements for papers given above, including the academic integrity requirements. 
    • Ask yourself whether your arguments would persuade someone who did not already agree with you.
    • Ask yourself whether there is a clearer or more persuasive, logical way to say what you mean.

  • Before submitting the final version, consider the feedback you received and use it to improve the paper still more.




 
Paper Assignment #1

 Deadlines for your draft & finished paper are here on Moodle.

Prompt: explain, and evaluate, one of the arguments about God in our assigned texts--one you did NOT write about in the first exam's last question.

They are:
  • Anselm's argument that God exists (Anselm, 22)
  • Aquinas's "First Way" (or his Second or Third--your choice) (Aquinas, 41-2)
  • Kenyon & Dembski's argument (see the video)
  • Carl Sagan's argument for a naturalistic account of DNA (see both the Sagan videos)
  • Michael Behe's argument
  • Pascal's Wager (in James, 112), or James's defense of it
Explain your chosen argument carefully and completely. This is the most important part of this paper. Then critically evaluate the argument--that is, explain why some objection(s) shows, or fails to show, it is unsound or weak; for this paper, this is less important than explaining the argument well.

Your paper should be about 1000-1300 words (about 3-4 pages).


Here is my advice on how to do this well:
  • First, provide a brief (1/2 page or less) introduction which includes a clear thesis and a road map.
    • Your thesis is the main conclusion that you are going to argue for in the paper: why you think the argument you're writing about is sound or unsound, strong or weak, whether a given objection against it works or not, or whatever. If think of your paper as an argument--and you should--your thesis is your paper's conclusion.
    • After the thesis, provide a road map. That is, briefly state the main points you will make to argue for your thesis. (Road maps say things like, "First, I will.... Then I will argue that...." and so on.) Think of your map as a short sketch of your paper's argument.
    • Resist the temptation to do much more than this in your introduction.

  • Second, explain your chosen argument (one of the options above) in about 1-2 pages. Use the text; don't just write up your notes.
    • This is the most important part of this paper assignment. (In the second paper, the critical evaluation is equally important.)
    • As in your reading responses, you may quote (putting quoted parts in quotation marks), but you should not assume the quotes are self-explanatory. Explain what they mean--and do so in your own words, in your own voice. This is not easy, but it is an important exercise for understanding the text and communicating your understanding of it to others.

    • Some more advice. 
      • To explain the argument, you might imagine you are writing for a peer who is unfamiliar with the philosopher you're writing about, and explain the argument, including every step, so that your peer will understand it well.
      • Or you might think of this as doing, in prose in a paper, what I do in class when we look at an argument from the text: I refer to the text, show what the premises of the argument are, and explain how they are supposed to support the conclusion.
      • Be fair to the author, being careful not to make their arguments appear weaker than they are. In fact, you should strive to be charitable: try to come up with the strongest, most persuasive version of the argument that is consistent with what the author says in the text.
      • Identify all the premises. Explain how each conclusion is supposed to be supported. When you look carefully at the text you may find that it differs from what you recall from class. Your assignment is not to write out your class notes; it's better to work from the text.
      • As you begin to draft this portion of your paper, it may be helpful to write the argument out in standard form (premise 1, premise 2, ...conclusion) as I often do on the blackboard; here is a guide to help you do that if you like. This can help you understand the argument better, so that you can then explain it in the paper.

  • Third, evaluate that argument. That is, argue for why some objection(s) work, or don't work. Only discuss one or two objections. Give the best reasons for why any reasonable person should agree with you. You may use ideas from any source as long as you cite every idea that is not your own per the academic integrity requirements above.
    This part is less important than the exposition in this paper, but still should show some care. This part should take at least a full page.

    • Still more advice. 
      • To evaluate the argument, present the strongest one or two objections to the argument that you can. In class, we discussed objections to these arguments. Those objections were not all equally good. Whether you choose an objection from class or make up one of your own, present the objection(s) that, in your honest judgment, presents the greatest challenge to the argument.
      • Then, explain whether this objection actually shows the argument is weak or unsound, and why. You need not conclude the argument is bad; you might find that the argument is only somewhat weakened by the objection, or that the argument withstands the objection just fine.
      • I suggest that you—in your pre-writing phase—sketch out all the objections to the argument that you can find or think of. (Don’t forget to keep track of where you got them). Then consider how the argument may be defended against those objections. Then look over what you’ve got and decide which considerations are most persuasive, most important for evaluating the argument, and write this part of your paper accordingly.
      • The key point here is to argue for the conclusion which is, in your judgment, best supported by the evidence. If you find you cannot support your original thesis very well, you will need to either find better evidence or change your thesis to match your evidence.

  • Finally, revise and rewrite your paper at least once. 
    • Before you turn in the first draft for peer review, ask yourself: 
      • Have you followed the format instructions?
      • Is the paper free of errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, and so on?
      • Does the draft follow the academic integrity instructions?
      • Is there a clearer or more logical, persuasive way to say what you mean?
      • Would your arguments would persuade someone who did not already agree with you?

    • Before submitting the final version, consider any feedback you received and use it to improve the paper still more.
