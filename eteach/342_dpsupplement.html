Extra Readings on the Death Penalty
Here are optional readings regarding the death penalty. Some argue for it, others against it, others provide general information about it.
General interest:
Michael H. Reggio, A History of the Death Penalty.
This is provided by Frontline, a documentary series on PBS. This reading briefly covers the history of the DP in ancient and medieval times, and devotes a lot of attention to the history of the DP in America from colonial times to the present.

Excerpt from Thurgood Marshall's concurring Opinion in Furman v. Georgia (1972).
This includes the results of Marshall's research into data relevant to the death penalty.

Scott Gold, Two Portraits of Grandmother's Killer Emerge (Los Angeles Times, January 27, 2002)

Adam Liptak and Ralph Blumenthal, New Doubt Cast on Crime Testing in Houston Cases (New York Times, August 5, 2004)
Rone Tempest, Death Row Often Means a Long Life (Los Angeles Times, March 6, 2005)

Methods of execution:
Methods of Execution in the United States. This page lists which execution methods are used in each state, and then describes each method in detail. It is on the web site of the the Clark County, Indiana Prosecutor, who is a retentionist. (By the way, this web site has a very extensive collection of information about the DP plus "1,000+ Death Penalty Links" to other sites with both retentionist and abolitionist arguments. Those sites include some crap of course, but overall it's still a useful resource.)

Methods of Execution Worldwide, borrowed from Amnesty International, an (among other things) abolitionist organization. This page has two lists: first, a list of nations with the methods of execution they use, and then a list of methods with the number of nations using each. (Did you know stoning and crucifixion were still practiced as of 1999?)

For the curious:

The Arizona Department of Corrections has a web page on the history of that state's executions. It includes the date, time, and race of those executed and for many of them also includes their last meal requests.

The Texas Department of Criminal Justice maintains one of the country's busiest execution schedules. They also maintain a death row information web site presenting a good deal of information on those currently on death row and those already executed, which includes their last words.
