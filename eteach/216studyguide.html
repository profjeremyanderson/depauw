Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good. I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Questions for exam 1, on Descartes
1. First Meditation (M1). State the two rules of Descartes's Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Explain how he applies those rules in the rest of the Meditation--in other words, explain how he shows that all his old beliefs are doubtful by means of Dream Doubt (1-2) and Demon Doubt (2-3--i.e., the "If God exists...or if God doesn't exist..." one).

2. Second Meditation (M2). In M2 Descartes claims to prove that he exists (4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). How does he argue for each? How does he use the wax to help show that mind is better known than body (6-8)?

3. Third Meditation (M3). 9. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (12-14)? (Your explanation will be better if you can include how he uses "representative reality" to form his argument.)

4. Fourth Meditation (M4). How does Descartes argue, in the Fourth Meditation, that God would not deceive him (18)? He says this presents "one difficulty" for him; what is it (18)? Explain how he avoids this difficulty--that is, how he thinks he can be sure that what he "vividly and clearly perceives" is true (19-22, esp. 21-2).

5. Fifth Meditation (M5). In the Fifth Meditation, Descartes examines his ideas of geometric figures like triangles (23-4). What does he conclude from this (24)? How does he use that to argue that God exists (24-5)?

6. Sixth Meditation (M6). How does Descartes argue that "I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it" (29)? What does he mean by "really distinct"? What is the "mind/body problem"? Briefly explain two solutions to the mind/body problem besides Descartes's. (The Mind/Body Problem Chart or Handout will be helpful.)

7. Sixth Meditation (M6). Explain how Descartes argues that bodies exist (30). (Bonus, since we did not discuss it: Identify at least two problems with his argument.)



Questions for exam 2, on Spinoza and Leibniz (date TBA)
Leibniz questions.

Note: All topics here are on the "Road Map to Leibniz." Look there for references to the relevant texts. The handout on "The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology" may also be helpful. Both documents here on our Google drive).

8. Explain why Leibniz thinks monads have no size or shape, and cannot be naturally created or destroyed. Why does he think a monad cannot be altered by anything outside itself (except God)?
 
9. If the universe is made of monads, each of which cannot be changed by any other monads, what accounts for the diversity and change that we see in the universe, according to Leibniz? (You might give an example of something like walking to lunch, or throwing a ball, to illustrate.)
 
10. What is "pre-established harmony" (or "concomitance")? How does Leibniz's theory of pre-established harmony account for the fact that things appear to interact but don't actually interact? Why does Leibniz say that every monad is a mirror of the entire universe?

11. Explain Leibniz's reasons for saying that ours is the best possible world (see esp. Discourse on Metaphysics secs. 1-5, pp. 1-3). Explain how he uses the example of Judas to argue that the universe is better with some evil in it (DM sec. 30, p. 21).

12. Suppose you are standing outside looking at a tree. Explain how Leibniz's account of your sensing the tree differs from Descartes's account. Explain why Leibniz would say Descartes's account must be wrong. What does Leibniz's account imply about which of our ideas are innate (that is, implanted in us by God)?

13. How does Leibniz argue that, even though God knows what we will do, we are still free and may be blamed for our sins (Dialogue on Human Freedom and the Origin of Evil, 1-2; cf. Discourse on Metaphysics, secs. 13, 30)?

Spinoza questions.

Note: When asked to give Spinoza's argument for a proposition, I do not expect you to give Spinoza's exact argument from the text! If you like, you may do that, or you may give any alternative line of reasoning presented in class or the posted notes on Spinoza. However you do it, try to articulate, as best you can, Spinoza's reasoning.

14. We discussed how Spinoza's views differ radically from others' (Spinoza notes; cf. Spinoza, Ethics, Appendix, 18). Discuss how Spinoza differs regarding (a) whether God is separate from the world, (b) whether God is "personal," (c) how many substances there are, and (d) whether we are free.

15. Give Spinoza's definitions of these terms: "substance" (D3), "attribute" (D4), "mode" (D5), and "God" (D6). It's OK to paraphrase them if you don't recall Spinoza's exact words, but strive to express the same meaning.

16. Explain Spinoza's argument for why there cannot be two or more substances with the same attribute (P5).

17. Explain Spinoza's argument that God exists (P11). Then explain Spinoza's argument that God is the only substance (i.e., why P14 is true). 

18. Discuss the implications of God being the only substance (Spinoza notes). Specifically, how does this view imply that traditional theistic beliefs are mistaken? How does it provide a different sort of solution to the mind/body problem than Descartes or Leibniz does?

19. Spinoza says "Things could not have been produced by God in any way or in any order other than that in which they have been produced" (P33, p. 15). How does Spinoza argue for this claim?

20. In the Appendix, Spinoza wants to dispose of prejudices that keep us from grasping his views, and says those prejudices all come from one false but "common supposition" (p. 18). What is that supposition? Where does he say it comes from (pp. 18-19)? In class, we identified some prejudices and superstitions Spinoza wants to free us from; what were they?

Exam 3, on Locke and Berkeley (date TBA)
Locke questions.

Note: The reference scheme for Locke's Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = book number, Y = chapter number, and Z = the section number. So II.iii.1 refers to Book II, Chapter iii, section 1 (p. 24).

21. How does Locke argue that there are no innate ideas (Essay I.ii)? According to Locke, what are the two ways we get our ideas (II.i.2-4)?

22. According to Locke, what is a quality (II.viii.7-8)? What is the difference between primary and secondary qualities (II.viii.9-23, but especially II.viii.15 and 23)?

23. According to Locke, why are general terms important (III.iii.1-4)? How do general terms get their meaning (III.iii.6)? Using an example from our Locke text or the portions of Locke quoted by Berkeley, explain how Locke thinks we form general ideas.




Berkeley questions.
The reference scheme for Berkeley will follow Bennett's in the PDF:
    • For sections in the Introduction: X intro, where X = section number. For example, "8 intro" refers to section 8 of the Introduction, on p. 2 of the PDF file
    • For sections in The Principles of Human Knowledge (Principles for short): just the section number. For example, 147 refers to section 147 of the Principles, on p. 52 of the PDF
24. How does Berkeley argue that, even if matter existed, we could never know it (18, 19)? In other words, how does he argue that (a) sensation alone does not show matter exists, (b) there is no good deductive argument that matter causes sensations, and (c) there is no good inductive argument that matter causes sensations?

25. If matter does not cause our sensations, what does? In other words, how does Berkeley argue that God exists (29-30; 146)? Explain in as much detail as you can what features of our sensations point to God's being their cause, according to Berkeley. 


Hume, Kant, and comprehensive questions for the final exam
Hume questions.

26. Explain how Hume distinguishes "impressions" from "ideas" (Enquiry, section 2, pp. 7-8). How does he argue that "all our ideas...are copies of our impressions" (section 2, pp. 8-9)? Explain why he thinks understanding the distinction between impressions and ideas (and that the latter come from the former) can be helpful for resolving philosophical disputes (section 2, p. 9).

27. Hume says, "All the objects of human reason or enquiry fall naturally into two kinds" (Enquiry, section 4, p. 11). What are the two kinds, and what features does Hume say each has (p. 11)? Give an example of each kind, and explain why it is an example.

28. According to Hume, what are "all reasonings about matters of fact" based on, besides those we currently sense or remember from the past (Enquiry, section 4, p. 12)? Give an example to illustrate what he means. How does he argue that there is no basis in reason for this, but only in experience? What sort of experience, specifically (p. 12)?

29. What assumption must we make, according to Hume, to get from "B has always followed A in the past" to "B will follow A in the future" (Enquiry, section 4, p. 16)? Why does he say this assumption cannot be justified by reason (16)?

Comprehensive questions.

C-01. A metaphysical game of catch. Descartes throws a ball to Spinoza, who catches it and throws it to Leibniz, who catches it and then throws it to Berkeley. Explain how each of these philosophers would account for what's really going on, given his views on what sorts of substances there are and how those substances affect others (or don't). Be sure to account for (a) each thrower's decision to throw the ball, (b) how that decision is related to the motion of the ball, and (c) each player's perception of the ball.

C-02. What's God doing here? Most of the philosophers we've studied have discussed God in some prominent way. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and for each one, discuss the role(s) God play(s) in his system.

C-03. How does he know you're out there? Many of the philosophers we've studied have grappled with our knowledge of ordinary objects such as tables and chairs. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and discuss how, and how well (e.g., how accurately, with what degree of certainty, etc.), each of them believes we know about such things. Do not present their arguments; I'm not interested in seeing, for example, Descartes's M6 argument that material things exist. Instead, explain how each accounts for our knowing about such things, explain how accurate he thinks our knowledge of them is, and why.

C-04. You tell me. Suppose I insist that my refrigerator teleported to Mars and back last night. Starting from the premise that "I exist," give a deductively valid, geometric-style proof that this could not have happened, using only premises that are vividly and clearly perceived to be true. --Just kidding. The real question is, What are the two most important things you have learned in this course? Why are they important to you? (Answers will be evaluated on the same criteria as others: show that you have understood, and thought about, some material in the course.)
