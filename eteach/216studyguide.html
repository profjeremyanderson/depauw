Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good. I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Questions for exam 1, on Descartes (Monday September 18)
1. What is Descartes's explanation for why our opinions differ (Discourse, 1)? Why does that imply, to Descartes, that the right method (i.e., following the right path, going in the correct order) is very important? (It's helpful to consider what Descartes says about opinions that are disputed, 4, and how many truths there are about any matter, 10.)

2. How does Descartes argue that it is important, after learning about others' opinions, to work on your own by yourself (Discourse, 5-6)?

3. Descartes describes following his method as being "like a man walking alone in the dark" (Discourse, 8). How is it like that? Still, how much knowledge does Descartes believe he can find by proceeding his way, in small steps, slowly and carefully (8, 9)?

4. State the two rules of Descartes's Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Explain how he thinks it will help him achieve his goal of finding knowledge that is "stable and likely to last" (M1, 1).

5. State Descartes's Dream Doubt argument (M1, 1-2) and his Defective Nature Doubt argument (M1, 2). Why does Descartes think he needs to "suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon" is deceiving him (M1, 3), despite the fact that he has shown all his old beliefs are doubtful "on the basis of powerful and well thought-out reasons" (M1, 2)?

6. In M2 Descartes claims to prove that he exists (4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). How does he argue for each? What does he mean by a "thinking thing" (M2, 5-6)?

7. Why does Descartes believe he must prove God exists (M3, 9-10)?

8. When Descartes says that some ideas "contain within themselves more representative reality" than others (M3, 11), what does he mean by an idea's "representative reality"? Why do ideas of substances have more representative reality than ideas of modes (or qualities)? (Class notes and my September 4 Announcement email may be helpful here.)

9. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (M3, 12-14; Phil 216,  9/6/2023)?

10. How does Descartes argue, in the Fourth Meditation, that God would not deceive him (M4, 18)? How does this present "one difficulty" for Descartes (M4, 18)?

11. Descartes claims that his intellect is limited but his will is unlimited (M4, 19). How does he use this to show that any errors are his own fault, and that he can avoid errors (M4, 20-1)?

12. In the Fifth Meditation, Descartes examines his ideas of geometric figures like triangles (M5, 23-4). What does he conclude from this (24)? How does he use that to argue that God exists (M5, 24-5)?

13. In the Sixth Meditation, Descartes argues that mind and body are "really distinct" (M6, 29). What does he mean by "really distinct"? How does he argue that mind and body are really distinct (29; Phil 216, 9/13/2023)?

14. What is the "mind/body problem"? How does this problem arise from Descartes's argument that mind and body are really distinct (cf. M6, 29)? Identify two solutions to the mind/body problem besides Descartes's. (Phil 216, 9/15/23; The Mind/Body Problem Chart or Handout will be helpful.)

15. Explain how Descartes argues that bodies exist (M6, 30; Phil 216, 9/15/2023).


Questions for exam 2, on Spinoza and Leibniz (Friday October 27)
Leibniz questions.

Note: All topics here are on the Road Map to Leibniz. Look there for references to the relevant texts. The handout on The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology may also be helpful. Both documents here on our Google drive).

16. Explain why Leibniz thinks monads have no size or shape, and cannot be naturally created or destroyed. Why does he think a monad cannot be altered by anything outside itself (except God)?
 
17. If the universe is made of monads, each of which cannot be changed by any other monads, what accounts for the diversity and change that we see in the universe, according to Leibniz? (You might give an example of something like walking to lunch, or throwing a ball, to illustrate.)
 
18. What is "pre-established harmony" (or "concomitance")? How does Leibniz's theory of pre-established harmony account for the fact that things appear to interact? Why does Leibniz say that every monad is a mirror of the entire universe?

19. Explain Leibniz' reasons for saying that ours is the best possible world (see esp. Discourse on Metaphysics secs. 1-5, pp. 1-3). Explain how he uses the example of Judas to argue that the universe is better with some evil in it (DM sec. 30, 21).

20. Suppose you are standing outside looking at a tree. Explain how Leibniz's account of your sensing the tree differs from Descartes's account. Explain why Leibniz would say Descartes's account must be wrong. What does Leibniz's account imply about which of our ideas are innate?

21. Explain what the problem of free will is. How does God's foreknowledge seem to imply we are not free (Phil 216, 10/2/2023)? How does Leibniz argue that, even though God knows what we will do, we are still free (Dialogue on Human Freedom and the Origin of Evil, 1-2; cf. Discourse on Metaphysics, secs. 13, 30)?


 Spinoza questions.

Note: When asked to give Spinoza's argument for a proposition, I do not expect you to give Spinoza's exact argument from the text! If you like, you may do that, or you may give any alternative line of reasoning presented in class or the posted Spinoza notes.

22. Lecture discussed the influence of Euclidean geometry on Early Modern Philosophers (Phil 216, 10/6/2023). How does Euclid-style reasoning show up in Descartes (Discourse, 9; Meditations)and Leibniz (esp. Monadology)? How does Spinoza differ, especially from Descartes?

23. Lecture discussed how Spinoza's views differ radically from others' (Phil 216, 10/6/2023; cf. Spinoza, Ethics, Appendix, 18). Discuss how Spinoza differs regarding (a) whether God is separate from the world, (b) whether God is "personal", (c) how many substances there are, and (d) whether we are free.

24. Give Spinoza's definitions of these terms: "substance" (D3), "attribute" (D4), "mode" (D5), and "God" (D6). It's OK to paraphrase them if you don't recall Spinoza's exact words, but strive to express the same meaning.

25. Explain Spinoza's argument for why there cannot be two or more substances with the same attribute (P5).

26. Lecture discussed the three traditional types of arguments that God exists (Phil 216, 10/11/2023). Define each of the three. Then, explain Spinoza's argument that God exists (P11).
