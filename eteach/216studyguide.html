Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good: give accurate answers to all parts of each question and demonstrate your understanding of the material by explaining things thoroughly. To come up with good answers, I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the classschedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Academic integrity on exams
  • For exams given in class, you must write your answers from memory during the exam. You many not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam. That's taxing enough, so no citing or quoting are required.

  • Take-home exams (which we will have if an exam must be given remotely) will be open book, open notes, so you must properly cite any sources you use. But:
    • You may use, and must cite, any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with others on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions for reading responses will apply, except that no sources from outside class are allowed. Cite all sources you use, and put others’ words in quotation marks. 



Questions for exam 1, on Descartes (2/18; after we cover assignment 7)
1. What is Descartes's explanation for why our opinions differ (Discourse, 1)? Why does that imply, to Descartes, that the right method (i.e., following the right path, going in the correct order) is vital? (It's helpful to consider what Descartes says about opinions that are disputed, 4, and how many truths there are about any matter, 10.)

2. In the Discourse,  Descartes likens his method to the "long chains of very simple and easy inferences that geometers customarily use" (Discourse, 9), and also to "a man walking alone in the dark" (8). First, how does he say it is like walking alone in the dark? Second, how much knowledge does Descartes believe he can find by proceeding his way, in small steps, slowly and carefully (8, 9)?

3. State the two rules of Descartes' Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Then, explain how he thinks it will help him achieve his goal of finding knowledge that is "stable and likely to last" (M1, 1). To do this well, explain why each of the two rules is important. It may be helpful to discuss how he thinks knowledge is like a building.

4. Explain Descartes's Dream Doubt (M1, 1-2) and his Defective Nature Doubt (M1, 2). What does each of them cast doubt on and which of his old beliefs, if any, are left undoubted by each? Finally, why does Descartes think he needs to "suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon" is deceiving him (M1, 3), despite the fact that he has shown all his old beliefs are doubtful "on the basis of powerful and well thought-out reasons" (M1, 2)?

5. In the Second Meditation, Descartes claims to prove that he exists (M2, 4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). Explain how he argues for each of those. Second, what does Descartes mean by a "thinking thing" (M2, 5-6)?

6. Descartes says that some ideas "contain within themselves more representative reality" than others (M3, 11). What does he mean by an idea's "representative reality"? Why do ideas of substances have more representative reality than ideas of modes (or qualities)? (Class notes and my February 5 Announcement email will be helpful here.)

7. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (M3, 12-14)? In class we discussed several weaknesses in his argument; explain any two of them.

8. In the Fourth Meditation, Descartes considers several ways to reconcile the fact that God would not deceive with the fact that he (Descartes) still makes mistakes. The first three are (a) that "I am somewhere in between God and nothingness" (M4, 18); (b) that God works in mysterious ways (M4, 19); and (c) that "we should look at the universe as a whole" (M4, 19). Explain how each of them is supposed to show how he can make mistakes despite being made by a non-deceiving God.

9. Descartes says God "didn’t give me a faculty of judgment that would lead me into error while I was using it correctly" (M4, 18). First, explain how Descartes believes errors occur (M4, 19-20), and how he believes he can avoid them.

10. In the Fifth Meditation, Descartes examines his ideas of geometric figures like triangles (M5, 23-4). What does he conclude from this (24)? How does he use that to argue that God must exist (M5, 24-5)? 

11. In the Sixth Meditation, how does Descartes argues that mind and body are "really distinct" (M6, 29; class notes)? What is the "mind/body problem," and how does this problem arise from Descartes's argument that mind and body are really distinct? (Class notes and the Mind/Body Problem Chart or Handout will be helpful.)

Questions for exam 2, on Spinoza and Leibniz (3/18)
Leibniz questions.

All topics here are on the "Road Map to Leibniz." Look there for references to the relevant texts. The handout on "The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology" may also be helpful. Both documents are here on our Google drive).

12. Explain why Leibniz thinks monads have no size or shape, and cannot be naturally created or destroyed. Why does he think a monad cannot be altered by anything outside itself (except God)?
 
13. If the universe is made of monads, each of which cannot be changed by any other monads, what accounts for the diversity and change that we see in the universe, according to Leibniz? (You might give an example of something like waving your hand, or throwing a ball, to illustrate.)
 
14. What is "pre-established harmony" (or "concomitance")? How does Leibniz's theory of pre-established harmony account for the fact that things appear to interact? Why does Leibniz think that every monad is a mirror of the entire universe?

15. In class (2026-02-25) we discussed an ancient philosophical problem, the Euthyphro dilemma. First,  explain what that problem is. Second, identify how Leibniz responds to the Euthyphro dilemma. Finally, explain how his response contributes to his view that ours is the best possible world.

16. Suppose you are standing outside looking at a tree. Explain how Leibniz's account of your sensing the tree differs from Descartes's account. Explain why Leibniz would say Descartes's account must be wrong.

17. Explain what the problem of free will is (Phil 216, 2026-03-02). In the Dialogue on Human Freedom and the Origin of Evil (p. 1), how does "A" argue that God's foreknowledge implies we are not free? How does Leibniz argue that, even though God knows what we will do, we are still free (Dialogue, 1-2; cf. Discourse on Metaphysics, secs. 13, 30)?


 Spinoza questions.

Note: When I  ask you to give Spinoza's argument for a proposition, I do not expect you to give Spinoza's exact argument from the text! If you like, you may do that, or you may give any alternative line of reasoning presented in class or the posted Lecture Notes on Spinoza. However you do it, try to fully articulate, as best you can, Spinoza's reasoning.

Also, my Lecture notes on Spinoza in the Course Readings folder are not a substitute for reading Spinoza but should help you understand it.

18. Lecture discussed the influence of Euclidean geometry on Early Modern philosophers (Phil 216, 2026-03-04). How does Euclid-style reasoning show up in Descartes (e.g., Discourse, 9; Meditations)? How about in Leibniz (esp. Monadology)? How does Spinoza differ, especially from Descartes?

19. Lecture discussed how Spinoza's views differ radically from others' (Phil 216, 2026-03-04; cf. Spinoza, Ethics, Appendix, 18). Discuss how Spinoza differs regarding (a) whether God is separate from the world, (b) whether God is "personal," (c) how many substances there are, and (d) whether we are free. 

20. Give Spinoza's definitions of these terms: "substance" (D3), "attribute" (D4), "mode" (D5), and "God" (D6). It's OK to paraphrase them if you don't recall Spinoza's exact words, but strive to express the same meaning.

21. Explain Spinoza's arguments for why there cannot be two or more substances with the same attribute (P5), and for why it pertains to the nature of a substance to exist (P7).

22. Suppose Spinoza's proof that God exists is sound. Explain why, then, God would have to be the only substance (i.e., why P14 is true). Then, discuss the implications of God being the only substance (Phil 216, 2026-03-13). In your discussion, include how this (a) solves the mind/body problem, and (b) implies that some traditional Christian theistic beliefs--such as, that we can please or anger God--are mistaken.

23. In the Appendix, Spinoza wants to release us from our prejudices. He says those prejudices all come from one false but "common supposition" (p. 18). What is that supposition? Why does he think we make this mistaken supposition (pp. 18-19)? Why does Spinoza think it's absurd to suppose God acts for a goal (Propositions 17, 29; pp. 19-20)? Why does he think we're mistaken to suppose things really are good, bad, ugly, beautiful, and so on (pp. 20-22)?

Questions for exam 3, on Locke and Berkeley (4/22)
Locke questions.

Note: The reference scheme for Locke's Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = book number, Y = chapter number, and Z = the section number. So II.iii.1 refers to Book II, Chapter iii, section 1 (p. 24).

24. Lecture discussed Rationalism and Empiricism (2026-03-30). What are the main features of each of them?

25. First, explain how Locke argues that no ideas are innate (Essay I.ii). Then, explain how Locke thinks we get all of our ideas (II.i.2). Explain both kinds of experience (II.i.2-4), give an example of each kind, and briefly explain why it is an example.

 26. Explain what the following are, according to Locke: perception (II.ix.1-2); retention (both kinds) (II.x.1-2); discerning (II.xi.1); comparing (II.xi.4); composition (II.xi.6); and abstraction (II.xi.9; a clearer explanation, with an example, is in III.iii.6-7). Finally, pick any two of them and explain why Locke thinks each of them is important.

27. What "common opinion" is Locke trying to correct with his primary/secondary quality distinction (II.viii.7)? According to Locke, which of the simple ideas we get from an object (say, an apple) match how the object really is? Which ideas do not match how the object really is? (See II.viii.9-23, but especially II.viii.15 and 23.) Give two examples showing that the common opinion is mistaken.

28. According to Locke, why are general terms important (III.iii.1-4)? How do general terms get their meaning (III.iii.6)? Using an example from our Locke text or the portions of Locke quoted by Berkeley, explain how Locke thinks we form a general idea of something.

29. Locke says knowledge is a kind of perception; a perception of what (IV.1.2)? What are the four different sorts of knowledge (IV.I.3-7)? For each kind, give an example. What are the three degrees of knowledge (IV.II.1-2, 14)? Again, for each kind, give an example.

30. How does Locke demonstrate that God exists (IV.x)? Explain his argument, being sure to include how Locke argues that an eternal being exists and that being is also "most powerful" and "most knowing." Then, explain one problem with his argument (several were discussed in lecture).

Berkeley questions.
The reference scheme for Berkeley will follow Bennett's in the PDF:
    • For sections in the Introduction: X intro, where X = section number. For example, "8 intro" refers to section 8 of the Introduction (on p. 2 of the PDF file)
    • For sections in The Principles of Human Knowledge (Principles for short): just the section number. For example, 147 refers to section 147 of the Principles (on p. 52 of the PDF)
31. Berkeley says previous philosophers "have first raised a dust," and then complained they can't see (3 Intro). How did they go wrong? Specifically, explain why Berkeley thinks we cannot have the sort of abstract ideas Locke says we need, using one of Berkeley's examples (9 Intro, 10 Intro, or 13 Intro). What is Berkeley's account of how general terms get their meaning (11 Intro)?

32. How does Berkeley seem to argue, in sections 1-4, that matter is something we cannot know about? What, then, does he think ordinary objects like apples are (1)? What does he mean by, "For unthinking things, to exist is to be perceived" (3), and what view is he arguing against with that claim?

33. How does Berkeley argue that, even if matter existed, we could never know it (18, 19)? In other words, how does he argue that (a) sensation alone does not show matter exists, (b) there is no good deductive argument that matter causes sensations, and (c) there is no good inductive argument that matter causes sensations?

34. If matter does not cause our sensations, what does? In other words, how does Berkeley argue that God exists (29-30; 146)? Explain in as much detail as you can what features of our sensations point to God's being their cause, according to Berkeley. Lastly, identify one problem with Berkeley's argument for God.


Hume, Kant, and comprehensive questions for the final exam
Hume questions.

35. Explain how Hume distinguishes "impressions" from "ideas" (Enquiry, section 2, 7-8). How does he argue that "all our ideas...are copies of our impressions" (section 2, 8-9)? Explain why he thinks understanding the distinction between impressions and ideas (and that the latter come from the former) can be helpful for resolving philosophical disputes (section 2, 9).

36. Explain what Hume means when he says our "ideas are obviously inter-connected" (Enquiry, section 3, 10). Hume claims there are "only three factors connecting ideas with one another" (10); identify each factor. For each, give two examples, and carefully explain why each is an example of that factor.

37. Hume says, "All the objects of human reason or enquiry fall naturally into two kinds" (Enquiry, section 4, 11). What are the two kinds, and what features does Hume say each has (11)? Give an example of each kind, and carefully explain why it is an example.

38. Hume says "all reasonings about matters of fact," besides those we currently sense or remember from the past, are based on cause and effect (Enquiry, section 4, 12). First, give an example to illustrate what he means. (You may use one of his or another; just be sure you make clear how it is an example of cause and effect.) Second, explain how Hume argues that there is no basis in reason for this, but only in experience (or, as explained in class, habit). Finally, what assumption must we make, according to Hume, to get from "B has always followed A in the past" to "B will follow A in the future" (Enquiry, section 4, 16)? Why does he say this assumption cannot be justified (16)?

39. First: What is the problem of free will? (This was explained in class.) Second: What is Hume's solution to it? --Well, that's a bit much to ask! Instead I'll ask a narrower question: How does Hume argue that we know that human actions are necessitated? --Well, even that is too much to ask, so let me narrow it further: Notice that Hume explains (a) how we know about human nature (41-2); (b) how our actions depend on our expectations of others (44); (c) how subjects like history, politics, morals, etc. presuppose we're necessitated (44-5); and (d) how we can "form a single chain of argument involving" human actions and material things (45). Carefully explain any one of these examples and how Hume thinks it shows that human actions are necessitated. Third and finally: If they are necessitated, how does that "solve" the problem of free will?

40. What worry is Hume responding to in part 2 of section 8 (48-9)? (This was explained in class.) Hume uses several examples to show that necessity is really "essential to morality." Carefully explain how Hume argues that our practices of rewards and punishments support his claim that necessity is essential to morality (49; class notes 2026-05-01). If you prefer, you may carefully explain using his example of hatred and vengeance (49) or his example of blame (49-50).

Comprehensive questions.

Comprehensive questions will be worth more points on the final exam.

C-01. A metaphysical game of catch. Descartes throws a ball to Spinoza, who catches it and throws it to Leibniz, who catches it and then throws it to Berkeley. Explain how each of these philosophers would account for what's really going on, given his views on what sorts of substances there are and how those substances affect others (or don't). Be sure to account for at least (a) each thrower's decision to throw the ball, (b) how that decision is related to the motion of the ball, and (c) each player's perception of the ball.

C-02. What's God doing here? Most of the philosophers we've studied have discussed God in some prominent way. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and for each one, discuss the role(s) God play(s) in his system.

C-03. How does he know what's out there? Many of the philosophers we've studied have grappled with our knowledge of ordinary objects such as tables and chairs. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and discuss how, and how well (e.g., how accurately, with what degree of certainty, etc.), each of them believes we know about such things. Do not present their arguments; I'm not interested in seeing, for example, Descartes's M6 argument that material things exist. Instead, explain how each accounts for our knowing about such things, explain how accurate he thinks our knowledge of them is, and why.

C-04. The argument contest! Think back on all the arguments we've looked at in this course. Pick the one you think is the very worst, present it (accurately and completely--no straw men here!) and make your best case for why it is so faulty. Then, pick the one you think is the very best, present it, and make your best case for why it is so good. (In case you are tempted to pick really short arguments, remember that your goal in answering this question--as with all other questions--is to show off as much as possible how very much you've learned in the course.)

C-05. You tell me. Suppose I insist that my refrigerator teleported to Mars and back last night. Starting from the premise that "I exist," give a deductively valid, geometric-style proof that this could not have happened, using only premises that are vividly and clearly perceived to be true. --Just kidding. The real question is, What are the two most important things you have learned in this course? Why are they important to you? (Answers will be evaluated on the same criteria as others: show that you have understood, and thought about, some material in the course.)

This completes the list of exam questions for this course.
