Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good. I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Questions for exam 1, on Descartes (Monday September 18)
1. What is Descartes's explanation for why our opinions differ (Discourse, 1)? Why does that imply, to Descartes, that the right method (i.e., following the right path, going in the correct order) is very important? (It's helpful to consider what Descartes says about opinions that are disputed, 4, and how many truths there are about any matter, 10.)

2. How does Descartes argue that it is important, after learning about others' opinions, to work on your own by yourself (Discourse, 5-6)?

3. Descartes describes following his method as being "like a man walking alone in the dark" (Discourse, 8). How is it like that? Still, how much knowledge does Descartes believe he can find by proceeding his way, in small steps, slowly and carefully (8, 9)?

4. State the two rules of Descartes's Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Explain how he thinks it will help him achieve his goal of finding knowledge that is "stable and likely to last" (M1, 1).

5. State Descartes's Dream Doubt argument (M1, 1-2) and his Defective Nature Doubt argument (M1, 2). Why does Descartes think he needs to "suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon" is deceiving him (M1, 3), despite the fact that he has shown all his old beliefs are doubtful "on the basis of powerful and well thought-out reasons" (M1, 2)?

6. In M2 Descartes claims to prove that he exists (4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). How does he argue for each? What does he mean by a "thinking thing" (M2, 5-6)?

7. Why does Descartes believe he must prove God exists (M3, 9-10)?

8. When Descartes says that some ideas "contain within themselves more representative reality" than others (M3, 11), what does he mean by an idea's "representative reality"? Why do ideas of substances have more representative reality than ideas of modes (or qualities)? (Class notes and my September 4 Announcement email may be helpful here.)

9. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (M3, 12-14; Phil 216,  9/6/2023)?

10. How does Descartes argue, in the Fourth Meditation, that God would not deceive him (M4, 18)? How does this present "one difficulty" for Descartes (M4, 18)?

11. Descartes claims that his intellect is limited but his will is unlimited (M4, 19). How does he use this to show that any errors are his own fault, and that he can avoid errors (M4, 20-1)?

12. In the Fifth Meditation, Descartes examines his ideas of geometric figures like triangles (M5, 23-4). What does he conclude from this (24)? How does he use that to argue that God exists (M5, 24-5)?

13. In the Sixth Meditation, Descartes argues that mind and body are "really distinct" (M6, 29). What does he mean by "really distinct"? How does he argue that mind and body are really distinct (29; Phil 216, 9/13/2023)?

14. What is the "mind/body problem"? How does this problem arise from Descartes's argument that mind and body are really distinct (cf. M6, 29)? Identify two solutions to the mind/body problem besides Descartes's. (Phil 216, 9/15/23; The Mind/Body Problem Chart or Handout will be helpful.)

15. Explain how Descartes argues that bodies exist (M6, 30; Phil 216, 9/15/2023).


Questions for exam 2, on Spinoza and Leibniz (Friday October 27)
Leibniz questions.

Note: All topics here are on the Road Map to Leibniz. Look there for references to the relevant texts. The handout on The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology may also be helpful. Both documents here on our Google drive).

16. Explain why Leibniz thinks monads have no size or shape, and cannot be naturally created or destroyed. Why does he think a monad cannot be altered by anything outside itself (except God)?
 
17. If the universe is made of monads, each of which cannot be changed by any other monads, what accounts for the diversity and change that we see in the universe, according to Leibniz? (You might give an example of something like walking to lunch, or throwing a ball, to illustrate.)
 
18. What is "pre-established harmony" (or "concomitance")? How does Leibniz's theory of pre-established harmony account for the fact that things appear to interact? Why does Leibniz say that every monad is a mirror of the entire universe?

19. Explain Leibniz's reasons for saying that ours is the best possible world (see esp. Discourse on Metaphysics secs. 1-5, pp. 1-3). Explain how he uses the example of Judas to argue that the universe is better with some evil in it (DM sec. 30, 21).

20. Suppose you are standing outside looking at a tree. Explain how Leibniz's account of your sensing the tree differs from Descartes's account. Explain why Leibniz would say Descartes's account must be wrong. What does Leibniz's account imply about which of our ideas are innate?

21. Explain what the problem of free will is. How does God's foreknowledge seem to imply we are not free (Phil 216, 10/2/2023)? How does Leibniz argue that, even though God knows what we will do, we are still free (Dialogue on Human Freedom and the Origin of Evil, 1-2; cf. Discourse on Metaphysics, secs. 13, 30)?


 Spinoza questions.

Note: When asked to give Spinoza's argument for a proposition, I do not expect you to give Spinoza's exact argument from the text! If you like, you may do that, or you may give any alternative line of reasoning presented in class or the posted notes on Spinoza.

22. Lecture discussed the influence of Euclidean geometry on Early Modern Philosophers (Phil 216, 10/6/2023). How does Euclid-style reasoning show up in Descartes (Discourse, 9; Meditations) and Leibniz (esp. Monadology)? How does Spinoza differ, especially from Descartes?

23. Lecture discussed how Spinoza's views differ radically from others' (Phil 216, 10/6/2023; cf. Spinoza, Ethics, Appendix, 18). Discuss how Spinoza differs regarding (a) whether God is separate from the world, (b) whether God is "personal," (c) how many substances there are, and (d) whether we are free.

24. Give Spinoza's definitions of these terms: "substance" (D3), "attribute" (D4), "mode" (D5), and "God" (D6). It's OK to paraphrase them if you don't recall Spinoza's exact words, but strive to express the same meaning.

25. Explain Spinoza's argument for why there cannot be two or more substances with the same attribute (P5).

26. Lecture discussed the three traditional types of arguments that God exists (Phil 216, 10/11/2023). Define each of the three. Then, explain Spinoza's argument that God exists (P11).

27. Suppose Spinoza's proof that God exists is sound. Explain why, then, God would have to be the only substance (i.e., why P14 is true). 

28. Discuss the implications of God being the only substance (Phil 216, 10/23/2023; Spinoza Notes). Specifically, how does this view imply that traditional theistic beliefs are mistaken? How does it provide a different sort of solution to the mind/body problem than Descartes or Leibniz does?

29. Spinoza says "Things could not have been produced by God in any way or in any order other than that in which they have been produced" (P33, p. 15). How does Spinoza argue for this claim?

30. In the Appendix, Spinoza wants to dispose of prejudices that keep us from grasping his views, and says those prejudices all come from one false but "common supposition" (p. 18). What is that supposition? Where does he say it comes from (pp. 18-19)? In class, we identified some prejudices and superstitions Spinoza wants to free us from; what were they (Phil 216, 10/25/2023)?

Exam 3, on Locke and Berkeley (date TBA)
Locke questions.

Note: The reference scheme for Locke's Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = book number, Y = chapter number, and Z = the section number. So II.iii.1 refers to Book II, Chapter iii, section 1 (p. 24).

31. Lecture discussed Rationalism and Empiricism (10/30/2023). What are the main features of each of them? In what ways does Locke exemplify Empiricism?

32. Locke argues that there are no innate ideas (Essay I.ii). Explain his argument for this claim. Then, explain at least one problem with Locke's argument against innate ideas. (For example, why might Descartes object to Locke's argument?)

33. Locke says we get our ideas from experience (II.i.2). What are the two kinds of experience (II.i.2-4)? According to Locke, what are the four ways simple ideas can get into our minds (II.iii.1)? Give an example for each way (II.iii - II.vii).

34. According to Locke, what is a quality (II.viii.7-8)? What is the difference between primary and secondary qualities (II.viii.9-23, but especially II.viii.15 and 23)? What "common opinion" is Locke trying to correct with his primary/secondary quality distinction (II.viii.7)? Give two examples showing that the common opinion is partly wrong.

35. Explain what the following are, according to Locke: perception (II.ix.1-2); retention (both kinds) (II.x.1-2); discerning (II.xi.1); comparing (II.xi.4); composition (II.xi.6); and abstraction (II.xi.9). Pick any one of them and explain why Locke thinks it is important.

36. How does Locke define "will" and "free" (II.xxi.5, 7)? Given his definitions of those, explain why Locke thinks "Is man's will free or not?" is an absurd question (II.xxi, especially II.xxi.14).

37. How does Locke say we get the idea of a substance (II.xxiii.1-2)? Why does this imply that we can never sense a substance? In class it was pointed out that Locke's discussion of substance seems to conflict with his accounts of how we know things and his account of language. How so?

38. Locke says knowledge is a kind of perception; a perception of what (IV.1.2)? What are the four different sorts of knowledge (IV.I.3-7)? For each kind, give an example. What are the three degrees of knowledge (IV.II.1-2, 14)? Again, for each kind, give an example.

39. How does Locke demonstrate that God exists (IV.x)? Explain his argument, and identify at least one problem with it. (Since Locke's argument for an eternal being existing is essentially Aquinas's Third Way, it's fine to use Aquinas for that. But be sure to include how Locke argues that God is also "most powerful" and "most knowing," IV.x.4-5.)

40. According to Locke, why are general terms important (III.iii.1-4)? How do general terms get their meaning (III.iii.6)? Using an example from our Locke text or the portions of Locke quoted by Berkeley, explain how Locke thinks we form general ideas.


Berkeley questions.
The reference scheme for Berkeley will follow Bennett's in the PDF:
    • For sections in the Introduction: X intro, where X = section number. For example, "8 intro" refers to section 8 of the Introduction, on p. 2 of the PDF file
    • For sections in The Principles of Human Knowledge (Principles for short): just the section number. For example, 147 refers to section 147 of the Principles, on p. 52 of the PDF
41. Berkeley says previous philosophers "have first raised a dust," and then complained they can't see (3 Intro). How did they go wrong? Specifically, explain why Berkeley thinks we cannot have the sort of abstract ideas Locke says we need, using one of his examples (9 Intro, 10 Intro, 13 Intro). What is Berkeley's account of how general terms get their meaning (11 Intro)?

42. Berkeley's first sentence in section 1 is, "Anyone who surveys the objects of human knowledge will easily see that they are all ideas," and he denies that we can have an idea of mind (1, 27, etc.). How, then, do we know that mind exists (26)? How does Berkeley describe this knowledge, if it's not of an idea (27, 142)? Finally, identify a problem with Berkeley's argument for mind.

43. How does Berkeley argue that, even if matter existed, we could never know it (18, 19)? In other words, how does he argue that (a) sensation alone does not show matter exists, (b) there is no good deductive argument that matter causes sensations, and (c) there is no good inductive argument that matter causes sensations?

44. If matter does not cause our sensations, what does? In other words, how does Berkeley argue that God exists (29-30; 146)? Explain in as much detail as you can what features of our sensations point to God's being their cause, according to Berkeley. Lastly, identify one problem with Berkeley's argument for God.

45. How do substance dualists like Descartes and Locke think we know about other minds (that is, other people's minds)? How does Berkeley think we do (145-7)? Discuss how Ockham's Razor might favor Berkeley's explanation -- and how it might favor the dualists' explanation. Which do you favor, and why?


Hume, Kant, and comprehensive questions for the final exam
Hume questions.

46. Explain why Hume thinks that the second, "accurate and abstruse" kind of philosophy is "the inevitable source of uncertainty and error" (Enquiry, section 2, 4). Then, explain some of why Hume believes it is valuable nevertheless. To do that, explain any of these: (a) why Hume says it is a benefit for "every art or profession" (4); (b) how it helps free us from "superstition and bad philosophy" (5); or (c) why Hume says there is value in understanding our "mental geography" (5).

47. Explain how Hume distinguishes "impressions" from "ideas" (Enquiry, section 2, 7-8). How does he argue that "all our ideas...are copies of our impressions" (section 2, 8-9)? Explain why he thinks understanding the distinction between impressions and ideas (and that the latter come from the former) can be helpful for resolving philosophical disputes (section 2, 9)?

48. Explain what Hume means when he says our "ideas are obviously inter-connected" (Enquiry, section 3, 10). Hume claims there are "only three factors connecting ideas with one another" (10); identify each factor. For each, give an example and explain why it is an example of that factor.

49. Hume says, "All the objects of human reason or enquiry fall naturally into two kinds" (Enquiry, section 4, 11). What are the two kinds, and what features does Hume say each has (11)? Give an example of each kind, and explain why it is an example.

50. According to Hume, what are "all reasonings about matters of fact" based on, besides those we currently sense or remember from the past (Enquiry, section 4, 12)? Give an example to illustrate what he means. How does he argue that there is no basis in reason for this, but only in experience? What sort of experience, specifically (12)?

51. What assumption must we make, according to Hume, to get from "B has always followed A in the past" to "B will follow A in the future" (Enquiry, section 4, 16)? Why does he say this assumption cannot be justified by reason (16)?

52. In Hume's discussion of miracles, what definition of "miracle" is he assuming? (This was discussed in class; see also Enquiry, section 10, pages 58 and 66-7.) Explain how Hume argues that "case against a miracle is—just because it is a miracle—as complete as any argument from experience can possibly be imagined to be" (58-9).

53. In part 2 of section 10, Hume gives four (sets of) reasons why he thinks testimony of miracles is never strong enough to overcome the presumption against them (59-62). Pick one of them and explain it.

Comprehensive questions.

C-01. A metaphysical game of catch. Descartes throws a ball to Spinoza, who catches it and throws it to Leibniz, who catches it and then throws it to Berkeley. Explain how each of these philosophers would account for what's really going on, given his views on what sorts of substances there are and how those substances affect others (or don't). Be sure to account for (a) each thrower's decision to throw the ball, (b) how that decision is related to the motion of the ball, and (c) each catcher's perception of the ball.

C-02. What's God doing here? Most of the philosophers we've studied have discussed God in some prominent way. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and for each one, discuss the role(s) God play(s) in his system.

C-03. How does he know you're out there? Many of the philosophers we've studied have grappled with our knowledge of ordinary objects such as tables and chairs. Pick three of our philosophers--your choice--and discuss how, and how well (e.g., how accurately, with what degree of certainty, etc.), each of them believes we know about such things. Do not present their arguments; I'm not interested in seeing, for example, Descartes's M6 argument that material things exist. Instead, explain how each accounts for our knowing about such things, explain how accurate he thinks our knowledge of them is, and why.

C-04. The argument contest! Think back on all the arguments we've looked at in this course. Pick the one you think is the very worst, present it (accurately and completely--no straw men here!) and make your best case for why it is so faulty. Then, pick the one you think is the very best, present it, and make your best case for why it is so good. (In case you are tempted to pick really short arguments: remember that your goal in answering this question--as with all other questions--is to show off as much as possible how very much you've learned in the course.)

C-05. You tell me. Suppose I insist that my refrigerator teleported to Mars and back last night. Starting from the premise that "I exist," give a deductively valid, geometric-style proof that this could not have happened, using only premises that are vividly and clearly perceived to be true. --Just kidding. The real question is, What are the two most important things you have learned in this course? Why are they important to you? (Answers will be evaluated on the same criteria as others: show that you have understood, and thought about, some material in the course.)
