Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good: give accurate answers to all parts of each question and demonstrate your understanding of the material by explaining things thoroughly. To come up with good answers, I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Questions for exam 1, on Descartes
1. What is Descartes's explanation for why our opinions differ (Discourse, 1)? Why does that imply, to Descartes, that the right method (i.e., following the right path, going in the correct order) is very important? (It's helpful to consider what Descartes says about opinions that are disputed, 4, and how many truths there are about any matter, 10.)

2. Notice that, in the Discourse,  Descartes likens his method to the "long chains of very simple and easy inferences that geometers customarily use" (Discourse, 9), and also to "a man walking alone in the dark" (8). First, how does he say it is like walking alone in the dark? Second, how much knowledge does Descartes believe he can find by proceeding his way, in small steps, slowly and carefully (8, 9)?

3. State the two rules of Descartes's Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Then, explain how he thinks it will help him achieve his goal of finding knowledge that is "stable and likely to last" (M1, 1). To do this well, explain why each of the two rules is important; it may be helpful to discuss how he thinks knowledge is like a building.

4. State Descartes's Dream Doubt argument (M1, 1-2) and his Defective Nature Doubt argument (M1, 2). Why does Descartes think he needs to "suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon" is deceiving him (M1, 3), despite the fact that he has shown all his old beliefs are doubtful "on the basis of powerful and well thought-out reasons" (M1, 2)?

5. In the Second Meditation, Descartes claims to prove that he exists (M2, 4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). Explain how he argues for each of those. What does he mean by a "thinking thing" (M2, 5-6)? Finally, how does Descartes use the wax to show that, if he knows anything about body, it is through his intellect rather than his senses (6-7)?

6. Descartes says that some ideas "contain within themselves more representative reality" than others (M3, 11). What does he mean by an idea's "representative reality"? Why do ideas of substances have more representative reality than ideas of modes (or qualities)? (Class notes and my February 4 Announcement email will be helpful here.)

7. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (M3, 12-14; Phil 216,  2/7/2025)? Briefly explain any two of the weaknesses we identified with his argument.
