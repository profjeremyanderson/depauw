Resources for giving good answers

Because you will have all possible exam questions in advance, I will expect your answers to be very good: give accurate answers to all parts of each question and demonstrate your understanding of the material by explaining things thoroughly. To come up with good answers, I offer several kinds of help in addition to lecture and discussion:
  • Citations in exam questions point you to material needed for giving good answers. Use them.
  • Some notes and exercises from class may be posted on Moodle. These will be helpful for answering some exam questions. Also, if class is taught remotely, sessions will be recorded and the recordings will be there, too.
  • You may email me your answers to up to three exam questions per week and I will help you improve them.
  • We can discuss your answers during office hours or by appointment. You may also form study groups--in fact, I encourage you to.
  • There will be a review session before the final exam. See the class schedule Google doc.
The best way to prepare is to keep up each week -- or, if you can't do it every week, try at least to avoid just cramming before exams.

When new questions are added, use the texts and notes (and classmates -- like I said, group study is helpful) to remind yourself of the relevant material, write out the best answers you can, and study them periodically.
If you have trouble coming up with answers, contact me for help. You may discuss your answers with me to improve them.


Academic integrity on exams
  • For exams given in class, you must write your answers from memory during the exam. You many not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam. That's taxing enough, so no citing or quoting are required.

  • Take-home exams (which we will have if an exam must be given remotely) will be open book, open notes, so you must properly cite any sources you use. But:
    • You may use, and must cite, any class materials (assigned texts; notes & other materials posted on Moodle or the linked Google Drive folders) and your own notes from class.
    • No other sources allowed. No collaboration with others on the exam.
    • The academic integrity instructions for reading responses will apply, except that no sources from outside class are allowed. Cite all sources you use, and put others’ words in quotation marks. 



Questions for exam 1, on Descartes
1. What is Descartes's explanation for why our opinions differ (Discourse, 1)? Why does that imply, to Descartes, that the right method (i.e., following the right path, going in the correct order) is very important? (It's helpful to consider what Descartes says about opinions that are disputed, 4, and how many truths there are about any matter, 10.)

2. Notice that, in the Discourse,  Descartes likens his method to the "long chains of very simple and easy inferences that geometers customarily use" (Discourse, 9), and also to "a man walking alone in the dark" (8). First, how does he say it is like walking alone in the dark? Second, how much knowledge does Descartes believe he can find by proceeding his way, in small steps, slowly and carefully (8, 9)?

3. State the two rules of Descartes's Method of Doubt (M1, 1). Then, explain how he thinks it will help him achieve his goal of finding knowledge that is "stable and likely to last" (M1, 1). To do this well, explain why each of the two rules is important; it may be helpful to discuss how he thinks knowledge is like a building.

4. State Descartes's Dream Doubt argument (M1, 1-2) and his Defective Nature Doubt argument (M1, 2). Why does Descartes think he needs to "suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon" is deceiving him (M1, 3), despite the fact that he has shown all his old beliefs are doubtful "on the basis of powerful and well thought-out reasons" (M1, 2)?

5. In the Second Meditation, Descartes claims to prove that he exists (M2, 4), and that he is a thinking thing (4-6). Explain how he argues for each of those. What does he mean by a "thinking thing" (M2, 5-6)? Finally, how does Descartes use the wax to show that, if he knows anything about body, it is through his intellect rather than his senses (6-7)?

6. Descartes says that some ideas "contain within themselves more representative reality" than others (M3, 11). What does he mean by an idea's "representative reality"? Why do ideas of substances have more representative reality than ideas of modes (or qualities)? (Class notes and my February 4 Announcement email will be helpful here.)

7. How does Descartes argue, in the Third Meditation, that God exists (M3, 12-14; Phil 216,  2/7/2025)? Briefly explain any two of the weaknesses we identified with his argument.

8. In the Fourth Meditation, Descartes considers several ways to reconcile the fact that God would not deceive with the fact that he (Descartes) still makes mistakes. The first three are (a) that "I am somewhere in between God and nothingness" (M4, 18); (b) that God works in mysterious ways (M4, 19); and (c) that "we should look at the universe as a whole" (M4, 19). Explain how each of them is supposed to show how he can make mistakes despite being made by a non-deceiving God.

9. In the Fifth Meditation, Descartes examines his ideas of geometric figures like triangles (M5, 23-4). What does he conclude from this (24)? How does he use that to argue that God must exist (M5, 24-5)? How does that, in turn, help solve his problem of limited attention (M5, 26)?

10. Explain how Descartes argues that bodies exist (M6, 30; Phil 216, 2/14/2025). We noted problems with two parts of this argument: (a) the first section, where he argues that the cause of his perceptions of bodies must be outside him, and (b) his claim that God "strongly inclined" him to believe that bodies cause his perceptions of them. Explain one of these problems.


Questions for exam 2, on Spinoza and Leibniz (Friday March 28)
Leibniz questions.

All topics here are on the "Road Map to Leibniz." Look there for references to the relevant texts. The handout on "The Opening Arguments in Leibniz' Monadology" might also be helpful. Both documents are here on our Google drive).

11. Explain why Leibniz thinks monads have no size or shape, and cannot be naturally created or destroyed. Why does he think a monad cannot be altered by anything outside itself (except God)?
 
12. If the universe is made of monads, each of which cannot be changed by any other monads, what accounts for the diversity and change that we see in the universe, according to Leibniz? (You might give an example of something like waving your hand, or throwing a ball, to illustrate.)
 
13. What is "pre-established harmony" (or "concomitance")? How does Leibniz's theory of pre-established harmony account for the fact that things appear to interact? Why does Leibniz think that every monad is a mirror of the entire universe?

14. In class (2025-02-26) we discussed two ancient philosophical problems: the Euthyphro dilemma, and the Problem of Evil. First, briefly explain what each of those problems is. Second, identify how Leibniz responds to the Euthyphro dilemma. Finally, explain how his response contributes to his view that ours is the best possible world.

15. Suppose you are standing outside looking at a tree. Explain how Leibniz's account of your sensing the tree differs from Descartes's account. Explain why Leibniz would say Descartes's account must be wrong. Why does Leibniz's account raise a worry about solipsism?

16. Explain what the problem of free will is (Phil 216, 2025-03-03). In the Dialogue on Human Freedom and the Origin of Evil (p. 1), how does "A" argue that God's foreknowledge implies we are not free? How does Leibniz argue that, even though God knows what we will do, we are still free (Dialogue, 1-2; cf. Discourse on Metaphysics, secs. 13, 30)?


 Spinoza questions.

Note: When I  ask you to give Spinoza's argument for a proposition, I do not expect you to give Spinoza's exact argument from the text! If you like, you may do that, or you may give any alternative line of reasoning presented in class or the posted notes on Spinoza. However you do it, try to fully articulate, as best you can, Spinoza's reasoning.

Also, my "Lecture notes on Spinoza" in the Course Readings folder are not a substitute for reading Spinoza but may help you understand it.

17. Lecture discussed the influence of Euclidean geometry on Early Modern philosophers (Phil 216, 2025-03-07). How does Euclid-style reasoning show up in Descartes (Discourse, 9; Meditations)? How about in Leibniz (esp. Monadology)? How does Spinoza differ, especially from Descartes?

18. Lecture discussed how Spinoza's views differ radically from others' (Phil 216, 2025-03-07; cf. Spinoza, Ethics, Appendix, 18). Discuss how Spinoza differs regarding (a) whether God is separate from the world, (b) whether God is "personal," (c) how many substances there are, and (d) whether we are free.

19. Give Spinoza's definitions of these terms: "substance" (D3), "attribute" (D4), "mode" (D5), and "God" (D6). It's OK to paraphrase them if you don't recall Spinoza's exact words, but strive to express the same meaning.

20. Explain Spinoza's arguments for why there cannot be two or more substances with the same attribute (P5), and for why it pertains to the nature of a substance to exist (P7).

21. Suppose Spinoza's proof that God exists is sound. Explain why, then, God would have to be the only substance (i.e., why P14 is true). Finally, discuss the implications of God being the only substance (Phil 216, 2025-03-24). Specifically, how does this imply that some traditional Christian theistic beliefs are mistaken?

22. In the Appendix, Spinoza wants to dispose of prejudices that keep us from grasping his views, and says those prejudices all come from one false but "common supposition" (p. 18). What is that supposition? Why does he think we make this mistaken supposition (pp. 18-19)? Why does Spinoza think it's absurd to suppose God acts for a goal? Why does he think we're mistaken to suppose things really are good, bad, ugly, beautiful, and so on (pp. 20-22)?

Exam 3, on Locke and Berkeley (date TBA)
Locke questions.

Note: The reference scheme for Locke's Essay is of the form X.Y.Z, where X = book number, Y = chapter number, and Z = the section number. So II.iii.1 refers to Book II, Chapter iii, section 1 (p. 24).

23. First, explain how Locke argues that no ideas are innate (Essay I.ii). Then, explain how Locke thinks we get all of our ideas (II.i.2). Explain both kinds of experience (II.i.2-4), and give an example of each kind.

24. According to Locke, what is a quality (II.viii.7-8)? What is the difference between primary and secondary qualities (II.viii.9-23, but especially II.viii.15 and 23)? What "common opinion" is Locke trying to correct with his primary/secondary quality distinction (II.viii.7)? Give two examples showing that the common opinion is mistaken.

25. Explain what the following are, according to Locke: perception (II.ix.1-2); retention (both kinds) (II.x.1-2); discerning (II.xi.1); comparing (II.xi.4); composition (II.xi.6); and abstraction (II.xi.9). Finally, pick any one of them and explain why Locke thinks it is important.

26. How does Locke say we get the idea of a substance (II.xxiii.1-2)? Why does this imply that we can never sense a substance? In class it was pointed out that Locke's discussion of substance seems to conflict with his accounts of how we know things and his account of language. How so?

27. According to Locke, why are general terms important (III.iii.1-4)? How do general terms get their meaning (III.iii.6)? Using an example from our Locke text or the portions of Locke quoted by Berkeley, explain how Locke thinks we form general ideas.

28. How does Locke differ from Descartes about what counts as knowledge? What are Locke's three degrees of knowledge (IV.II.1-2, 14)? For each kind, give an example.

29. How does Locke demonstrate that God exists (IV.x)? Explain his argument, and identify at least one problem with it. (Since Locke's argument for an eternal being existing is essentially Aquinas's Third Way, it's fine to use Aquinas for that.)
