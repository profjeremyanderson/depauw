The answers to some exam questions are relatively easy and straightforward.
Even for easy questions, there are better and worse answers.

Example: one exam question might be, "Cephalus believes that 'justice...is simply to speak the truth and to pay back any debt one may have contracted' (Plato, 3). Why does Socrates reject this definition?"

Here is one possible answer:
Socrates says it wouldn't be just to give weapons back to a man when he was insane.

The answer above isn't wrong, but it's very sparse. This next one gives a more thorough explanation of Socrates' reasoning:
Socrates gives an example where following Cephalus' definition of justice would lead us to do something we don't think is just. Suppose a man loaned you his weapons while he was sane, but then wants them back when he is insane. To give the weapons back to him, or to be completely truthful with him (maybe by telling him truthfully where they are), would result in harm. Socrates says that would be unjust.

Per the syllabus, your grade depends mostly on how well you demonstrate that you understand and have thought about the stuff presented in the course. The second answer above does a much better job at that than the first. It would get full credit while the first answer would not.


Suppose you are asked to define terms, like "moral skepticism," "subjectivism," and "conventionalism." Once again, it's possible to write an accurate but superficial answer:
Moral skepticism: there are no moral truths.
Subjectivism: morality is a personal decision.
Conventionalism: morality is based on culture.

Nothing in this answer is false, but it's still not very good. It contains less information than is in the book (Pojman, 19), and much less than was on the board and discussed in class. It shows very little understanding.

This answer is far better:

Moral skepticism, subjectivism, and conventionalism are different views about the basis of moral rules--that is, about how moral rules get their validity, if any.

Moral skepticism admits that there are moral rules, but it holds they have no basis, or none we can know for sure. So, skepticism does not consider any moral principles to be binding (or "valid" as the book says). Moral skepticism implies that ethics is a kind of sham to the extent that it tries to convince us that rules are justified.

Unlike moral skepticism, subjectivism holds that moral rules can be justified, and they are justified by your personal decision or belief. In other words, subjectivism holds that an act is right only if you think it is; otherwise it is not.

Conventionalism also holds that moral rules can be justified. Unlike subjectivism, conventionalism holds that rules are justified by culture: an act is right only if your culture approves of it; otherwise not.

This shows a clear understanding that both moral skepticism and subjectivism are views about whether and how moral principles can be justified, and clearly states each view's position on the matter.


Answering some questions is more challenging.

Here's a challenging question: imagine a situation where a conservative Saudi Muslim, Azzam, condemns Becky's immodest attire and Becky--a liberal American atheist--condemns Azzam's attempt to control her behavior. I might ask, "Explain how Aquinas might resolve the dispute between Azzam and Becky. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? (What’s most important here is to give me a sense of how Aquinas would approach this matter.)"

Here you are asked to take a sophisticated set of ideas from Aquinas and apply them in a hypothetical but possible situation. This is a dispute where Azzam and Becky dislike each other's behavior and this dislike is connected with moral rules of their respective cultures.

Here is one possible answer:
Since Aquinas is a Christian, he thinks that both Azzam (a Muslim) and Becky (an atheist) ought to convert to Christianity and obey its rules. Becky could then show her face in public and Azzam would respect this behavior, and both of them would avoid going to Hell.


Ahem. This answer is not factually wrong, so I wouldn't give it a zero. But you could give at this answer based merely on knowing Aquinas was Christian, without ever doing the reading or paying attention in class. So the answer above is very poor.

This one is far better:
Based on the reading we have, it’s hard to tell exactly how Aquinas might resolve Azzam & Becky’s dispute. Aquinas gives us a universal moral standard of natural law that might be used to settle disputes between people from different cultures. But it’s not clear that this standard (at least what we have of it) tells us what to do to settle this case.

On one hand, Aquinas definitely thinks there are some universal moral truths. There are some precepts we can figure out by reason, and anyone’s behavior may be judged by those precepts. Moreover, any particular rule in a given society may be evaluated by reference to those precepts. Because of this, it appears that for Aquinas there may be some way of resolving this dispute--that is, some way we can get beyond the unproductive head-butting of subjectivism (which leaves us with “he said vs. she said”) or conventionalism (which leaves us with “his culture says vs. her culture says”). 

More specifically, Aquinas says that we all seek the good and that we are naturally inclined towards some particular goods, including social living (29). He says that "whatever pertains to this inclination belongs to the natural law: e.g., ...to avoid offending those among whom one has to live" (29). This means that natural law tells us to do what will help us live socially, and that this includes avoiding offense. Moreover, he emphatically says that law always tells us to seek the common good, not just our own private good, because as members of society we are like parts of a whole (23). Azzam and Becky are offending each other, so we could at least conclude that their dispute should not continue, especially if it could lead to any sort of social disruption.

On the other hand, Aquinas allows that a given moral rule--not one of the precepts of the law of nature, but a man-made moral rule derived from the precepts--may not apply in all particular cases, as in his example of giving weapons back (30). He says that the "first principles" of morality are "altogether unchangeable," but to apply them we derive "secondary principles"--the particular rules of our society--which might not always apply (p. 30). He identifies two ways in which a given rule may fail to apply universally. First, it may apply in some societies but not others. A modern example of this is the rule of driving on the right side of the road. Second, like the rule about giving back what is held in trust, there may be circumstances in which an exception must be made to a rule that otherwise is generally legitimate.

So Aquinas gives us a general approach to discovering the universal moral law: identifying first principles, then deriving secondary principles from them which we can then apply to particular situations.

But given what is said in the readings and the prompt, it is not clear how this might resolve Azzam and Becky's disagreement. Is Azzam's rule about women's public attire more clearly justified by the precepts than Becky's, or vice versa? Can we deduce from the precepts some rule about how to adjudicate their dispute? The answers to these questions are not obvious. Perhaps it would be helpful if we knew more. For example, if the dispute were taking place in Saudi Arabia, Aquinas might say that the potential for social disruption and giving offence to others is lower if Becky wears a veil, and that therefore she ought to do so.

This answer does not say "Aquinas would say that Azzam and/or Becky should do X." But that's fine, because the answer gives reasons why it's hard to come to such a neat conclusion, and those reasons are grounded in the Aquinas reading. That's what the question is after: showing how Aquinas thinks about a situation. Remember Aquinas, like Aristotle, believes that ethics is an imprecise subject (Aquinas, 30).

However, if your answer shows a way that Aquinas could resolve their dispute which is consistent with his general principles, that'd be great, too.

Of course I wouldn't expect you to give this exact answer, nor would I expect you to quote Aquinas or give page numbers! I put them here to help you find the material in the text if you want.
