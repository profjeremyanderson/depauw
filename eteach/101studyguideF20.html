 My expectations--including some sample answers
  • For a term, can you give its definition + say why it matters in this course?
    • For example, for "valid," you should be able to
      (a) give a definition, such as "Validity is something deductive arguments can have. An argument is valid when its conclusion would have to be true if its premises were true," and also
      (b)       explain why the term matters in the course. You could say, "Validity matters in this course because we often evaluate arguments, and seeing whether an argument is valid helps us figure out whether its conclusion is well supported."
      • To say why a term matters, can you say something specific about that term? Vague answers like, "Term X matters in philosophy because philosophers discuss X" or "because we studied X in the course" do not show much understanding.
      • Instead, to find something specific to say, identify where the term played a role in the course, and what that role was. 
        • For example, "strong." It came up in basic logic, where discussed arguments and how to evaluate them. So, for "strong," you could say "It matters in this course because it is relevant to how we evaluate inductive arguments; this is important because arguments are the most basic tool of philosophy."
        • Or "primary quality." You could say, "Locke uses the term; he  distinguishes primary qualities from secondary qualities so he can say how some of our sensations actually resemble things around us while some sensations don't. Locke thinks this helps us tell how accurate our sensations are."
    • (You might notice some terms here in the study guide are listed in the Philosophical Lexicon at the back of FP (pp. 931-59). Some of the lexicon's definitions differ from those given in class. When they do, you should know the definitions given in class.)
  • For a name, can you identify the main points that person made in the assigned reading?
    • For example, for "St. Anselm of Canterbury," can you say more than just that he gave an argument for God? Identify the main points he made in the assigned readings, like this: "Anselm argued that God exists. To do that, he gave a deductive, ontological argument. He also replied to Gaunilo's objections to his argument."
    • Or for for "Bertrand Russell," can you do more than just describe his argument about the cat? Identify the main points he made in the assigned portion of The Problems of Philosophy, like this: "Russell was writing about our knowledge of the external world, and he argued that although (a) appearances often differ from (what we take to be) reality, and (b) we cannot prove that life isn't just a continuous dream or illusion, nevertheless (c) we can still be confident that things like cats and people do exist outside us."
  • For an argument, can you...
    • State the argument? You might state it in standard form (numbered premises and conclusion) and/or write it out in a paragraph. But however you state it, you need to both identify its premises and, for each conclusion, explain how that conclusion is supposed to be supported by the premises. Even if you put it in standard form, you still need to explain.
    • Critically evaluate the argument, if this was discussed in class? For example, for Descartes's argument for corporeal objects, be able to explain its weaknesses. You may not be able to explain them all--in fact, trying to do that would be too much--but even in an exam you should be able to identify at least one substantial criticism of an argument if asked to, if those were discussed in class.
  • Items that have been struck out (so they look like this) have appeared on a previous exam and will not appear on any future exam. Any items that have not been struck out may still appear on a future exam, even if they were on a previous exam.


How to use this guide
I will notify you when I add things to the study guide. Emails will come from Moodle's Announcements forum to your DePauw e-mail, so you should check them, or visit this page, regularly.

Re-load this page each time to ensure you see the latest version.

When new items appear:
  1. Review your study materials (readings and e-readings, notes from class, class recordings, etc.)--especially anything cited in the study guide,
  2. Write out responses to each item, making your definitions, explanations, critiques, etc. clear, complete, and thoughtful, and
  3. Study these materials and your responses periodically to prepare for exams and class discussions.
Need help with anything here? Ask me for help.

Items which have been struck out (so they look like this) will not be used on future exams.

I encourage you to study with others. However, on papers and exams you need to do your own work; no collaborating with others.

Study items for: What is Philosophy?

Terms (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about terms):
    1. Metaphysics (Bailey, 2, 949)
    2. Epistemology (Bailey, 2, 943)
    3. Value Theory (aka Ethics) (Bailey, 2-3, 943)
You should be able to:
  1. Identify whether a question or problem pertains to metaphysics, epistemology, or value theory--or more than one of them--and explain why (Bailey, 2-3).
  2. Explain the similarities and differences between philosophy and science (for example, biology) with regard to what questions they try to answer and their methods for seeking answers (Bailey, 1-3).
  3. Explain why philosophy’s methods differ from those used in empirical sciences (for example, biology) (Bailey, 2 may be helpful, but the most thorough answer comes from our discussion on 2020-09-02).

Study items for: Basic Logic

Terms (all the logic terms are defined in the Logic Terms handout on our Google Drive):
    1. Ethos (Aristotle, 7)
    2. Pathos (Aristotle, 7)
    3. Logos (Aristotle, 7)
    4. Argument (Bailey, 5, 933; Anderson, section 1)
    5. Premises (Bailey, 5; Anderson, sec. 1)
    6. Conclusion (Bailey, 5; Anderson, sec. 1)
    7. Inductive argument (Anderson, sec. 2)
    8. Strong (Bailey, 7)
    9. Weak (Bailey, 7)
    10. Deductive argument (Anderson, sec. 4)
    11. Valid (Bailey, 7-8; Anderson, sec. 5; also see the validity exercise from class, in the Drive with our class notes)
    12. Invalid (Bailey, 6)
    13. Sound (Bailey, 6; Anderson, sec. 5)
    14. Unsound (Bailey, 6)
Names (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about names):
  1. Aristotle
You should be able to:
  1. Give examples of ethos (both kinds!), pathos, and logos, and explain why each is an example.
  2. Identify an argument,  its premise(s), and its conclusion(s).
  3. Identify, or at least make an educated guess, whether a given argument is inductive or deductive, and explain why.
  4. When discussing premises, conclusions, deductive arguments, and inductive arguments, properly apply the terms "true," "false," "valid," "invalid," "strong," and "weak"--in other words, show you know which terms apply to what things. (This is at the end of the Logic Terms handout.)

Study items for: Metaphysics/Does God Exist?

Terms:
  1. Ontological argument (Bailey, 15 is helpful)
  2. Cosmological argument (Bailey, 15 is helpful)
  3. Design (or teleological) argument (Bailey, 16 is helpful, as is Bailey's discussion of teleological arguments on p. 957)
  4. Irreducible complexity (Behe, 21)
Names:
  1. St. Anselm of Canterbury
  2. Gaunilo of Marmoutiers (pronounced "gow-nillow of mar-moo-tee-ay")
  3. St. Thomas Aquinas (pronounced "uh-KWAI-nuss")
  4. Dean Kenyon & Michael Dembski
  5. Carl Sagan
  6. Michael Behe
  7. William James
Arguments (see "my expectations" above for what you need to know about arguments):
  1. Anselm's argument that God exists (Anselm, 22)
  2. Aquinas's "First Way" (Aquinas, 41-2; the version from class is the third one on the Aquinas class notes)
  3. Kenyon & Dembski's argument (see the video)
  4. Carl Sagan's argument for a naturalistic account of DNA (see both the Sagan videos, but especially the first)
  5. Michael Behe's argument
  6. Pascal's Wager (in James, 112)
You should be able to:
  1. Explain at least two objections to Anselm's ontological argument (for example, Gaunilo's "Lost Island" objection in paragraph 6, pp. 25-6). (Remember that a well-formed objection to an argument is also an argument.)
  2. Explain at least two objections to Aquinas' "First Way." (Class notes will be helpful here.)
  3. Explain (a) how Sagan's argument challenges Kenyon & Dembski's design argument, plus (b) at least one other objection to Kenyon & Dembski's argument. (Class notes will be helpful here.)
  4. Explain at least one objection to Sagan's naturalistic argument for DNA. (Class notes will be helpful here.)
  5. Explain (a) at least two objections to Michael Behe's design argument and (b) how Behe could reply to at least one of those objections. (Class notes will be helpful here.)
  6. Identify whether an argument is ontological, cosmological, or teleological, and explain why.
  7. Explain Clifford's view (which is shared by Clough and Huxley) (James, 113, 117).
  8. In class (2020-09-23) we looked at some reasons why Pascal's Wager seems wrong. Explain at least one of them.
  9. In class (2020-09-23) we looked at how James defends Pascal's Wager. Why does James think it is hopeless to wait for proof (116-7)? Why does James think we should not suspend belief indefinitely?

Study items for: Epistemology & Metaphysics/Can we Know if Matter Exists?

Terms:
  1. Method of doubt (state the two rules) (Descartes, 144)
  2. Dream doubt (just what the doubt is, not the argument for it; the argument is below) (Descartes, 145)
  3. Defective nature doubt (again, just what the doubt is, not the argument for it) (Descartes, 145-6)
  4. Mind/body problem (see the Mind/Body Problem Handout or Chart)
  5. Interactionist dualism (see the Mind/Body Problem Handout or Chart)
  6. Non-interactionist dualism (see the Mind/Body Problem Handout or Chart)
  7. Monism (see the Mind/Body Problem Handout or Chart)
  8. Immaterialist monism (see the Mind/Body Problem Handout or Chart)
  9. Intuitive knowledge (defined in class 2020/10/21; see the class notes)
  10. Demonstrative knowledge (defined in class 2020/10/21; see the class notes)
  11. Sensitive knowledge (defined in class 2020/10/21; see the class notes)

Names:
  1. René Descartes (pronounced "reh-nay day-cart")
  2. John Locke
  3. George Berkeley (pronounced "bark-lee")
  4. Bertrand Russell
Arguments:
  1. Descartes's argument for dream doubt (145)
  2. Descartes's argument for defective nature doubt (145-6)
  3. Descartes's argument that God exists (157; also see "Descartes's Arguments that God Exists and Would Not Deceive," esp. lines 31-89)
  4. Descartes's argument that God would not deceive (158; also see "Descartes's Arguments that God Exists and Would Not Deceive")
  5. Descartes's argument that mind and body are "really distinct" (i.e., separable; 167)
  6. Descartes's argument that material things exist (167-8; see class notes for October 16 and 19)
  7. Locke's arguments that material things exist (185-7, §§3-8; notes for October 23 & 26)
  8. Berkeley's (bad) argument that we cannot know about matter because we have no idea of it (§§ 1-5) (see notes for October 28)
  9. Berkeley's argument that mind (spirit, soul) exists (§26) (see notes for October 30)
  10. Berkeley's arguments that:
    (a) sensations alone don't show that material things exist (§18)
    (b) there is no sound deductive argument for material things (§18)
    (c) there is no strong inductive argument for     material things (§19) (see notes from October 30)
  11. Russell's argument that material things exist (242).
You should be able to:
  1. Identify the five features of our beliefs we identified and discussed in class. What were the "foundations" we identified?
  2. Explain Descartes' overall project: his goal, the main obstacle he faces, and how he proposes to overcome his doubts (144).
  3. Explain how Descartes' standard for what counts as "knowledge" differs from ours. What historical reasons help explain why it is this way (cf. Bailey, 137)? 
  4. Explain why Defective Nature Doubt is more comprehensive than Dream Doubt--that is, why DND casts doubt on everything DD does, plus more of Descartes' old beliefs (Descartes, 145).
  5. Explain why Descartes is certain he exists (147).
  6. Explain why Descartes believes he is (i.e., why in M2 he is only sure of being) a "thinking thing" (147-8). What does Descartes say a thinking thing is (148)?
  7. Explain why proving God exists and is no deceiver would be an appropriate strategy for dispelling defective nature doubt. In other words, why would it work (if it could be done)? Why would it be better than the other strategy we discussed?
  8. Explain at least one objection to Descartes's argument that God exists ("Descartes's Arguments that God Exists and Would Not Deceive" discusses several objections; "Weaponizing the Method of Doubt" may help).
  9. What if matter were all that exists? In class, we identified some concerns about this; what were they? Explain Descartes's reasons for wanting to show that mind and body are "really distinct" (i.e., separable).
  10. Identify what Descartes thinks are the main properties of mind (also called soul, spirit, or mental substance) and the main properties of matter (also called body, corporeal substance, or material substance) (167, 170; class notes).
  11. Explain at least two objections to Descartes' argument that material things exist. (The notes from 2020-10-16 & -19 will help.)
  12. Explain how Descartes' standard for what counts as knowledge differs from Locke's (cf. Descartes, 144 and Locke, 185-6; also see the notes from 2020-10-21).
  13. Explain Locke's view (which Descartes shares, by the way) on the proper role of the senses. In other words, explain what they both think the senses are good for, and what they are not good for (Descartes, 169; Locke, 187 §8, and see the class notes from 2020-10-21).
  14. Identify at least two objections to Locke's arguments that material things exist.
  15. Russell believes sensations often do not tell us how things really are. Explain why Russell thinks this is so regarding color, texture, and shape (237).
  16. Explain at least one objection to Russell's argument that material things exist. (For example, lecture explained how Berkeley's view could explain our sense-data as well as Russell's view.)

Study items for: More Metaphysics/Do We Have Free Will?

Terms:
  1. Free will (class notes from 2020-11-09; Bailey, 944 may be helpful)
  2. Determinism (class notes 2020-11-09; cf. Bailey, 941)
  3. The problem of free will (class notes 2020-11-09)
  4. Hard determinism (class notes 2020-11-09; cf. Bailey, 526--note that Bailey treats "determinism" as a synonym for "hard determinism," but in class we do not)
  5. Libertarianism (class notes from 2020-11-09; cf. Bailey, 526; Bailey 948 (only defn. 1, not 2))
  6. Compatibilism (class notes from 2020-11-09; Bailey, 937, may be helpful) 
Names:
  1. Paul Rée (pronounced "ray")
  2. David Eagleman
  3. C.A. Campbell
  4. A.J. Ayer
Arguments:
  1. Rée's "sufficient cause" argument that our choices are all determined (Rée, 530).
You should be able to:
  1. Explain how Rée argues that our choices are determined by his comparison of the stone, the donkey, and us (531-4).
  2. Explain how Rée and Eagleman account for our belief that we have free will (536-8; Eagleman). (It will be helpful to discuss Eagleman's "stowaway" and "newspaper" comparisons.)
  3. According to Campbell, when are we morally responsible (in other words, what are the three criteria for being responsible) (Campbell, 545-6)? What choices does he say are free (and for which we may be responsible) (550)?
  4. Explain how a determinist could argue that our predictability shows that we do not have free will (cf. Campbell, 552). Then, explain how Campbell argues against this (552-3).
  5. Explain why Ayer is unsure determinism is true (558-9). If determinism is false, and our choices are undetermined, does Ayer think we would then be responsible for our choices? How come (559-60)?
  6. How does Ayer try to show we can be responsible even if we are determined? Specifically, when does Ayer think we are "free" and responsible, and when does he think we are not? (To do this well, you will need to explain Ayer's definition of freedom, his criterion for responsibility, and what he thinks constraints are, 561-2.)

Study items for: Ethics/How Should We Live?

Terms:
  1. Tao (discussed in class 11/30/2020)
Names:
  1. Chuang-tzu
  2. Richard Taylor
  3. Leo Tolstoy
  4. Glaucon (in the Plato reading)
You should be able to:
  1. Explain how "The Tale of the Butcher" uses the butcher and the bull to teach a lesson about how to live. Explain what that lesson is.
  2. Explain the lesson in "The Tale of the Dying Men."
  3. Explain the lesson in "The Tale of the Horses."
  4. Explain at least one problem with Chuang-Tzu's view.
  5. Explain what Taylor thinks is a meaningless life, clearly identifying what he thinks makes it meaningless (1-3, 4). (It would be good to illustrate it, as he does, with the myth of Sisyphus or something similar.)
  6. Explain why Taylor thinks the glow worms' lives are meaningless (4-5), and why he thinks ours are meaningless, too, despite our differences from the worms (5-6, 7).
  7. Taylor considers two possibilities for what might count as a meaningful life. Identify each one, and explain why he rejects one and accepts the other (7-9).
  8. Explain at least one problem with Taylor's view.
  9. Explain what Tolstoy thinks fails to give life meaning (Tolstoy, 1-7). It may be helpful to discuss the "Eastern story about the traveler" (3): what does it imply about Tolstoy's attitude towards death, his work, his family? 
  10. Explain what Tolstoy thinks does make life meaningful (7-8). Why does he think it does this?
  11. Compare Tolstoy's proposal for a good/meaningful life with (a) Chuang-tzu's and (b) Taylor's. How are they similar? How do they differ?
  12. Identify Glaucon's four claims about human nature (Plato, 611-2; also on the slides). Explain what conclusions he draws from them--i.e., how he says they justify government, and what sort of good "the many" say government and justice are.
  13. Explain how Glaucon's views on government and human nature differ from Chuang-tzu's.
  14. Explain how Glaucon's views on morality differ from Tolstoy's.
  15. Describe the results of our survey of what people would do with the Ring of Gyges. How do they help to confirm Glaucon's view?
