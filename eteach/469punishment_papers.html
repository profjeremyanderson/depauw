1. Short essay requirements
During the semester you will write two short essays. Each will be 1000-1500 words (that is, roughly 3-5 pages) long. In each, critically evaluate some of the material we have studied.

To do this effectively in a short space you will need to limit the scope of your essay. Take on a small question rather than a large one. For example, do not try to show that retributivism is superior to consequentialism, or determine whether consequentialism indeed does require harming the innocent sometimes. (These would be tough to do even in your longer research essay.) It might also be too much to take on the argument of an entire text.

For example, Igor Primorac's article "Utilitarianism and the Punishment of the Innocent" provides several different arguments. Discussing all of them would be too much to take on in a short paper. Instead, choose some particular argument in a text. For example, within "Utilitarianism and the Punishment of the Innocent," you could discuss one particular argument Primorac makes, or discuss whether Rawls's position (which Primorac is criticizing) is really vulnerable to Primorac's critique. Or you might set aside the question of whether an author's conclusion is correct and focus more on the quality of an argument, critically evaluating it.

One place to look for potential topics is in your own reading responses: what portion of the readings has most intrigued you? Is there a particular argument or issue you want to write about? Feel free to discuss possible topics with me.

The general requirements for all papers are below. No outlines or drafts are required, though discussing your paper with me before it is due would be helpful, as would discussing your paper with folks at the Writing Center.

You are not required to do any research for a short essay, though of course it can enhance the paper. Obviously your argument regarding one assigned text may be helped by points made by the author of another text. In such cases, by all means bring in other authors' arguments. Just remember that if you use any sources outside of assigned texts (and your own thinking), then all the requirements regarding research apply. In any case, always cite your sources (including assigned texts), and if you go outside of assigned readings be judicious about your sources.

2. Long research essay requirements
You'll be writing an argumentative paper, making strong use of research, of about 3000-4000 words (not counting footnotes, endnotes or bibliography; i.e., 10-14 pages) or so. The point of this assignment is for you to use research to better understand some controversial course-related topic that is important to you and take a stand on it in writing.

Philosophy majors: this paper can contribute to satisfying the Writing In the Major requirement if your finished paper is revised in light of feedback on a draft and it earns at least a C (73% or higher).

Of course I have some ideas for what you might write about, and would be happy to discuss them with you. But I will not assign topics. Rather, I want you to pick a subject that matters to you and, first, explore it by researching it and, second, write about it. If one of the ideas below appeals to you, fine. If not, come see me and we'll create a topic for you—but don't write a paper about something without checking with me first.

Regardless of your topic, there are some things you need to do to succeed in this assignment, in addition to the general requirements for all papers listed below:
  • In the draft and especially the finished paper you must show clear evidence of researching your subject. A good way to do this is to include at least six high-quality sources in your bibliography which are not assigned readings and which, taken together, give a balanced and reasonably thorough coverage of your chosen topic. You must demonstrate understanding of those sources in your paper. This is a  suggested minimum (i.e., necessary to get a passing grade); to write a really good paper you will likely need more. Regardless of how many sources you rely on, you must be judicious about them as described below in the general paper requirements.

  • Submit a 1-2 page proposal by 6pm Friday April 15 -- but get it to me earlier if you can. It must include:
    • The thesis you intend to defend in your paper, 
    • A brief outline or abstract of how you plan to present and defend it, and
    • A bibliography of sources you are thinking of relying on. For each source, include at least a brief note -- a sentence or two -- explaining how that source is relevant to your thesis. Relax: I do not expect this to be a complete and final list of your sources! Rather, the point of this is for you to demonstrate that you've begun researching and thinking about your topic.

      These are the minimum requirements for the proposal. It would be to your advantage to come see me to discuss your paper ideas, the sooner the better.
  • Submit a draft of your paper by 6pm Wednesday April 27, and schedule a time to meet with me to discuss the draft after you turn it in. Again, doing this sooner is better if you can.

    Neither the proposal nor the draft+conference will be graded, but the grade for the final paper will be penalized if any of them is missing.

3. Requirements for all papers in this course
  • Your paper must be primarily argumentative. That is, it must defend a conclusion which other smart people might well disagree with, and this must be your paper's major focus. There should be no more background material than is necessary to explain and give perspective to the arguments you present.
  • To take a stand effectively, your paper must critical but fair to your opposition, and your paper's conclusions should not depend on mere expressions of personal opinion or armchair arguments. It is not enough to explain why you think something is so; you must strive to convince a reasonable reader that any right-thinking person should, if they take all the evidence into account, agree with you.

  • Your paper must be submitted as a Word (.docx or .doc) or Rich Text Format (.rtf) file. It must be double spaced, have one-inch margins and numbered pages, use 12-point Times New Roman or similar font, and be free of mechanical errors (grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc.). Your computer's grammar- and spell-checking features are not adequate; you will have to proofread your paper yourself. Include a word count for the body of the paper (that is, not including your name, end- or footnotes, bibliography, etc.) at the top with your identifying information.
     
  • Papers should be logically organized and clear. Here are some guidelines:
          First, give a brief introduction with your thesis and a road map. In your introduction, state your thesis -- the overall conclusion that you are going to argue for. Then briefly summarize the outline of your paper -- that is, the main steps you will take to argue for your thesis, in the order you will make them. Think of this as a road map of your paper. Resist the temptation to do much more than this in your introduction. The intro for a short paper should be no more than half a page; the intro for the long paper no more than a page.
          Organize your paper as an argument: a series of statements which, taken together, logically imply a conclusion. Your thesis is the overall conclusion, and each paragraph should make a point which clearly contributes to the argument for your thesis. The sentences in each paragraph, then, should explain and/or support that paragraph's point. I will expect the paper to proceed according to the road map given in the introduction.
          Strive for simplicity. As far as style goes, I am much more impressed by writers who express ideas clearly using simple language than by those who use big words and long sentences. To give an example, it is better to adopt a writing style like Michael Moore's ("The Argument for Retributivism" in assignment 7) and less like John Stuart Mill's (Note that I am only commending Moore's style, not his logic!).
          Do not turn in your first draft, no matter how great a writer you are. Complete the assignment and then let it sit for at least a few hours and then come back and re-write it to make it even clearer and more complete. Give yourself enough lead time to seek help from the W center and from me.

  • DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to all assignments in the course.
    • Any time you use any ideas that are not your own, you must cite them. Any time you use another person’s words you must put them in quotation marks and cite them. Whether the material came from assigned texts, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, a web page, your mother, space aliens, or whatever: if the idea isn’t yours, cite it; if the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes and cite it.
    • Failure to cite as required will automatically lower the paper's grade, potentially down to failing. It may also be prosecuted as plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If it is prosecuted successfully, the minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all. So it is vital that you keep track of whose words and ideas you are using. If you have any sources to cite outside our textbook or class discussion, list them on a “Works Cited” page in addition to citing them in the text.
      • This is not hard to do. As you work on the paper, make a note of everything you read and anyone you talk to about it, noting which ideas and words came from each source. Then, if you decide to use any ideas or words from these notes, cite the sources and quote as needed.
    • To cite material assigned in the class (e.g., from our textbook), give the author's name and the page number in parentheses, like this: (Wyzanski, 51) or (Kadish, 403). No bibliographic information is needed for the textbook or other assigned readings, but every time you use something from the text, cite it.
    • No research outside of assigned texts is needed for the short papers in this course. But if you use any outside sources you must cite them. Citations of outside sources must be given in Chicago or Turabian format. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page; use the pull-down menu to see how to cite various types of source.
      • For more information about citation, quoting, paraphrasing, etc. consult Charles Lipson's book Doing Honest Work in College. If you want a format suggestion, I happen to like the "author-date" system of the Chicago Style (or the very similar "parenthetical citations/reference list" Turabian style), with one exception: the Chicago manual says that some sources may be omitted from your reference list or bibliography, but in work for me you must include EVERY source outside of assigned texts.
      • Your list of sources must include:
        • Full bibliographic information for any print source aside from assigned texts.
        • Full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.whitehouse.gov" but the full URL, so that I can paste it into my browser and find the exact material you relied upon.
        • If citing a journal article accessed electronically, give both the bibliographic data plus the full URL. Use the "stable" URL.

  • You must be judicious about your sources if you are going to do any research beyond assigned texts (as is required for the long research essay and optional for the short essays). This means three things:

    1. First, don't limit yourself to just one kind of source.
      • For example, some students simply do a search on JSTOR and use any relevant-sounding articles near the top of the search results. That is not the worst way to go (the worst is to do that with Google), but it is lazy and, worse, is unlikely to find you the really good stuff. You should also search the Library catalog for relevant books, search other article databases (EBSCO, the Philosopher's Index), consult a reference librarian, etc. 
2. Second, be careful about the quality of your sources. The internet, for example, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, but to reach them you have to dig through a lot of garbage.
      • Peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields are best. If you are unclear about how to tell which periodicals are scholarly, take 3 minutes and watch this video. 
      • Among the worst are websites of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications. Reliance on these can seriously hurt your project.
      • Around the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are nice places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources may be used to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources for the content of your final paper will count against it.
Some relatively good and interesting sources are available on my Philosophy Research Links page, which has a section devoted specifically to the law.

3. Third, you may cite lecture, discussion, reading responses, forum posts, etc. only for material that is not in any text. For anything that is in a text (i.e., any time you make a claim about what an author says), you must cite the text. If you cite material from class discussion, do so like this: (Phil 469 class discussion date).
When in doubt about the appropriateness of a given source, consult with me.

Here is a suggestion for getting started in your research. First, identify the names of the people and the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic. The Library has kindly posted helpful research tips. 
  • Although not required, it would be a good idea to seek advice from DePauw's Writing Center prior to each paper's due date.
 

4. What makes better papers better
Think of the general requirements above as minimum requirements. Here is what you should strive for:
  • Meticulous proofreading, and carefully following the format requirements given above.
  • Careful, logical organization of the paper as a whole and of individual paragraphs.
  • Presenting even viewpoints you oppose with clarity, care, and charity (as opposed to, say, employing fallacies or presenting opposition so briefly it doesn't get a fair hearing).
  • Relying on superior sources of information when research is employed (e.g., scholarly articles as opposed to Wikipedia or some dude's blog, etc.).
  • Providing arguments and information that are clearly relevant to your thesis.
  • Providing relevant empirical data where appropriate, as opposed to armchair arguments.
  • Arriving at conclusions that are clearly implied by the sum total of your arguments and information, as opposed to going off on a tangent at the end, or stating a conclusion that goes beyond the evidence provided.
  • When providing original arguments, demonstrating careful, sophisticated thought.
  • No filler, no bull. For example, do not tell me how long humankind has pondered your topic or give me Webster's definition of anything.
