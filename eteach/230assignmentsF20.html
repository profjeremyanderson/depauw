  4. Aquinas discusses our "natural inclinations" and several "precepts of natural law" (Summa, 4-5). What inclinations does Aquinas identify? How do the precepts relate to those inclinations?

By "practical reason" he means "figuring out what to do," such as forming moral rules. Try to explain this analogy. (This analogy helps explain how Aquinas thinks "precepts of natural law" are more authoritative than human-made laws.)

Reading questions:
  1. In this chapter (Leviathan ch. 13), Hobbes is describing what he calls the "natural condition" of humanity, often called a "state of nature." What he means by this is, "How humans would be if there were no government": no police, firefighters, written laws, etc. Within a couple of pages he concludes that, in such a state, we'd be in a "war, where every man is enemy to every man," and our lives would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" (Hobbes, 71). Carefully examine those pages to see how he draws this conclusion. How does he think we're equal? What comes from our equality? Why can't we just get along peacefully with each other?
  2. On pp. 72, 73, and 75, Hobbes states his first, second, and third "laws of nature"--look for the italics. Identify these laws. How do the three of them arise from Hobbes's account of our "natural condition"? In other words, if Hobbes is right about our being miserable in a state of nature, why would the laws make sense?
  3. In Chapter 17 Hobbes explains why nothing short of an absolute ruler ("sovereign") over a large group ("multitude") of people can keep us safe. Why does he think the alternatives are unacceptable?

That there must be [a “pure” moral] philosophy is evident from the common idea of duty and of moral laws. All the objects of inclinations have only a conditioned value; for if there were not these inclinations and the needs founded on them, then their object would be without value.
