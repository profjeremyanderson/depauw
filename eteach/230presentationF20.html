Your small-group presentation will be a major assignment for this course, requiring substantial preparation.

Here is how you do it:
1. Pick a topic and an alternate
2. Form a group
3. Get your topic approved
4. Do your research
5. Find some assignment(s) for the class & create reading questions
6. Plan and prepare your presentation
7. Document your work/academic integrity
Rubric: how to do well at this assignment

(If you don't to a presentation, do a paper--one that requires research--instead. Here's that assignment.)
Here are the steps for the presentation assignment:


1. Pick a topic and an alternate
You may present on any topic within the field of ethics, except for texts I've covered in class. 

That leaves a lot of possibilities:
  • A topic from our course that we have not looked at
  • A topic not covered in our course; there are tons more out there. 
Pick a topic that interests you! Doing something you're not interested in will bore you, then you'll do a boring presentation and get a distressing grade. Write or come see me to discuss your interests and get ideas about ethical topics that relate to them. There are likely ethical topics related to your major or minor, or other things you find important.

Please pick an alternate topic, too, in case somebody else claims your first choice first. 


2. Form a group
Groups of 2 or 3 are ideal. (With larger groups it is much harder for each person to earn a high grade.) Use the Class Open Forum on Moodle to search for partners.


3. Get your topic approved
Once you have formed a group and agreed on a topic, send me an e-mail declaring:
1. Who's in your group
2. Your first-choice topic
3. At least one alternate topic

This e-mail is due by 6:00pm Monday October 26. 


4. Do your research
Good research is vital to your success. You need to learn as much as you can about your topic to present it effectively to a group of people who are as smart and critical as you are.

The more medium- and high-quality sources you rely on, the better. If you cannot tell that an author is well qualified to write about your topic, you should probably not use that source!

How to begin? Here are suggestions. First, see me. We should meet at least twice to discuss your plans.

Also, identify the names of the people and/or the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic.

Choose your sources wisely. Here is how I rank them:
  • Best: peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields.

  • Among the worst are websites or other writings of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications or no qualifications. Relying on these can seriously hurt your project.

    • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, that's a strong reason not to use that source.

  • In the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are good places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia, since some of its articles are just abysmal and its overall quality is unreliable). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources may be used to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources for the content of your presentation will count against it.
The Library has many great books and some poor ones. The internet is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, among lots of trash. See me if you have any questions about what source materials to use. To search on the Internet you could start at my Philosophy Research Links Page; a good place to start researching your topic is with the online philosophy encyclopedias. 



5. Find some assignment(s) for the class and create at least one reading question for the class to respond to.
Your group will give the class and me an assignment, to be completed prior to your presentation, to prepare us for it, and make up reading questions for the class (and you, if you like) to respond to.

Good reading questions focus on the parts of your assignment you plan to discuss in your presentation.

 You might use the Library to find a reading you would like to assign. You may  assign texts, films, clips, music, or other media.

 Please get your assignment to me by three days before your presentation date so I can make it available to the class. 



6. Plan and prepare your presentation
General advice:
Get help. See me at least twice well before your presentation date to discuss plans and get some coaching. Also, it would be good to schedule an appointment to consult with DePauw's Speaking and Listening Center (yes, they're doing online tutorials).

Plan your group's presentation to take roughly 10-15 minutes per member in a group presentation, about 20 minutes in a solo presentation. Good presentations often go longer, but passable ones are seldom shorter. You might...
    • Have each group member present a different aspect or side of your topic, or
    • Stage a debate, with group members opposing each other, or
    • Make a film or include video clips on your topic, or
    • Do something completely different. Creativity is fine. Imitating what others have done can get old.
Decide: in person, or flipped? You may do your presentation "live" in class or, if you prefer, you may create something that can be posted online for the class to view & hear on their own, and class time will be used for discussion and activities or whatever else you like. (If the class becomes asynchronous, which I hope does not happen, all presentations will need to be posted online.). You could...
  • Narrate a PowerPoint slide show, or
  • Record your group presenting in Google Meet, or
  • Create a YouTube video, or
  • Something else.
Visual aids (slides, brief handouts, etc.) are encouraged but not required.

Divide the work roughly evenly for preparation, presenting, and handling discussion.
The most important thing is to demonstrate you understand your material. If you know your stuff, you do not need PowerPoint. (Of course you may use it, but you shouldn't need it.) If you do not know your stuff, no technology will make up for it.

Present more than one viewpoint on your topic, based on your research, and be fair. By "fair" I do not mean making them all look equally good; I mean not presenting any view or argument as less (or more) good than it really is, and demonstrating an appreciation of the strengths as well as the weaknesses even of views you oppose. We're searching for truth here.
    
At least a week before your presentation date:
  • Meet with me.
  • As mentioned above, make sure your reading assignment for the class is available to them by at least two class meetings before you present.
  • Go over the rubric below and make sure you're doing what's needed to do well.
Three days before your presentation:
  • As mentioned above, get your assignment for the class to me.
  • Prepare some reading questions for the class to respond to. Good reading questions focus on the parts of the assignment you plan to discuss, and ask students to explain them and maybe also to react to them.
By 6pm the day before your presentation:
  • Each group member should assemble and submit her or his own required documentation by e-mail, per the instructions below, by 6:00pm.
By 6pm the day of your presentation:
  • Turn in any audio/visual aids you used (slides, handouts, etc.), per the instructions below.
  • Make up at least two exam questions based on your presentation, and submit them to me. You can do this individually or as a group, and you may submit them along with the required documentation.
    • To come up with appropriate exam questions, ask yourself, "What are some main points from the presentation which anyone who paid attention should remember?" Identify those points, and create questions which ask about them.  Try to make at least one "easy" one (for example, some key definitions relevant to your presentation) and at least one challenging question.


7. Document your work
You will be graded individually for your work. There are no group grades. Each individual presenter needs to submit written evidence of what they contributed and what sources you relied on.
By the deadline, send an e-mail or Word document attachment (like this sample here--or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations) containing:
  • A brief (seriously--a quarter page is ample) statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was.

  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, divided into two parts: those sources used in the presentation and those consulted but not used. Use a standard bibliographic format, and give the URL for any online sources. (When available, use the "stable URL or "persistent URL" or "Permalink.")
  • A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page.
Also, document the sources used in your slides or other visual aids. Endnotes are one good way to document sources in PowerPoints: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in the PowerPoint that comes from some source--image, claim, quote, whatever--put the number of the source by it.

Without your documentation, the assignment is incomplete (and hence violates the late policy) and you could be prosecuted for plagiarism.

DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to this assignment. The documentation you submit must include all the sources you relied on in any way whether you quote them or not. The policy also applies to any audio/visual aids you use. 

Presenting work done outside this course (for example, work already submitted for credit in this or another course, or done for some extracurricular activity) is not allowed. Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be OK, but discuss it with me beforehand. Any work done outside this course, if you are allowed to use it at all, must also be documented.

Failing to document any source will automatically harm your grade and may be prosecuted as plagiarism, even if it is accidental.
How to do well at this assignment.
I want to see evidence of substantial, new work by each person in each group. Just downloading a web page and reading it to the class or copying it onto a PowerPoint slide, for example, is not acceptable. You need to show that you've done significantly more with the information than merely acquiring it.

Use this as a guide to what makes better presentations better. Your work will be evaluated along the following five parameters. Alternative assignment for unusual situations: Write Another Paper (one requiring research). 

Sometimes it may be impractical or impossible to do a presentation. Then the alternative is to write a 1500-2000 word (5-6 page, not counting notes or bibliography) paper.

Here's what that would look like:

Topic. Negotiate the topic with me. I have veto power. Of course it will have to be something within ethics. If you had been preparing to do a presentation on some topic I already approved, you may do your paper on that topic. You could choose one of the topics we've studied in this course, but, as with presentations, I strongly encourage you to propose topics you're really interested in. As with presentations, let's try to make this an important learning experience for you.

Fairness. As with the presentations, I expect you to present more than one side of an issue, and be fair to all sides. That does not mean making them all look equally good. It means showing a real appreciation of the strengths as well as the weaknesses of those you disagree with.

Requirements for all papers in this course (including format, style, and academic integrity) apply. Please review them here.

Research. Unlike other papers in this course, this paper requires research just like the presentation does. So, as with the presentation, choose your sources wisely. Your job is to learn what other smart people have said about your topic, and write about it in an organized way.

Meet with me. Meet with me early to discuss your topic and find sources to learn about it. Then meet with me later on, but well before the deadline, to discuss how things are going and go over a draft or outline of the paper. 

Reading question(s): create one or two reading questions about your paper, and email them to me by the deadline.

Deadline: 6pm the day of our last class meeting or, if your presentation was scheduled for the last week of class, 6pm of the first day of finals week. After that, the syllabus late policy applies.
I also encourage you to take advantage of the free assistance with writing provided by the Writing Center in Asbury Hall; call x4037 or go to their web page for an appointment (yes, they are operating Spring 2020, too).
