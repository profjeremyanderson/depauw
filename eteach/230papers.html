Academic integrity, and how to cite

Reading responses, papers, presentations, and remote/take-home exams (if any) must meet these requirements.

For exams given in class, you must write your answers solely from memory during the exam. No citing or quoting is required. You many not access books or notes of any kind, or collaborate with others, during an in-class exam.

C. How to cite properly in this course


A. When you include others' words or ideas in your work without making clear they aren't yours, that is plagiarism. "Others' words or ideas" includes anything you didn't make up yourself: the labor or creativity other people, material produced by AI, etc. So, to avoid plagiarizing:
  • Any time you use anything you got from another, cite it, whether you quote it or not.

    • Whatever the source was -- assigned texts, AI, a video, class discussion, a conversation with a friend, an academic journal, a web page, your mother, space aliens, or whatever -- cite it.

  • If you use another's words in your work, put them in quotation marks and cite.

    • Even if you change some of the wording you might still be plagiarizing. Either put the material in quotation marks or completely rephrase it. See this exercise for help.

  • Keep track of your sources.

    • Citing properly will be easy if you make a note of what you read and who you talk to about your work. Keep track of what words and ideas come from each source. Then, every time you use any words or ideas from your sources, cite them, and put any of their words in quotation marks.

  • Unsure what to do? Ask me before you turn in the work.

Citing and quoting properly are vital. Failing to cite and quote as required will automatically lower your grade, potentially down to failing. I may also charge you with violating DePauw’s Academic Integrity Policy. If the charge is upheld, the minimum penalty is usually worse than turning in no work at all. You must keep track of, and properly document, whose ideas and words you use. DePauw provides lots of info on understanding academic integrity and how to avoid plagiarism on this helpful page. You may also always come to me with whatever questions you have about integrity. 



B. My policy on using and documenting AI

I don't forbid using AI's such as ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, Gemini, or whatever. But if you do use them, I have two cautions and a policy.

The cautions:
  • AI's are unreliable. When I ask an AI to do something I might ask of you, like answer an exam question, I routinely get answers that would earn failing grades. AI's use both good and bad sources without discriminating between them. They are biased. They "hallucinate" -- that is, they say things that are false. Feel free to experiment with AI's if you like, but for work you turn in for credit I recommend you only use material from an AI when you can verify it is accurate, or as an example you critically evaluate. (Which, in practice, may mean that you don't use it in this course at all.)
  • The more you let AI do for you, the more you undermine your own education. Developing and practicing skills in reading, critical thinking, and writing are among the most important benefits of a liberal arts education. Those skills matter at least as much as whatever facts you learn because they are fundamental to interacting intelligently with the world. Without them, you cannot even assess whether AI is feeding you bullshit.
The policy: if you use any AI in any way you must document how you used it by showing your prompts and AI's responses. (Screenshots are good for this.) Any use of AI that you fail to document is a form of plagiarism and may be charged as such.

Here are some possible examples:
  • If you copy any text generated by an AI (whether it's just a turn of phrase or something more), put it in quotation marks and cite the AI.
  • If you use anything (such as an idea, or an example) you got from an AI without copying the words, still cite the AI.
  • If you use an AI to correct your grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc., or to help outline your paper, say so, and cite the AI.
In short, if you use AI to affect the content of your work in any way whatsoever (wording, ideas, examples, arguments, grammar, punctuation, organization, etc.), document it. And, of course, the more work the AI does, the less credit you earn.

I may use a variety of AI detectors to check your work.

This is merely an extension of the plagiarism policy above, and reflects the main goal of academic integrity generally, which is to give credit for any help you got.

As always, if you are unsure what's OK, ask me, preferably before turning work in.

Like the rest of these academic integrity instructions, this policy applies to reading responses, drafts, papers, and take-home exams (if any). Citing and quoting are not required for in-class exams because for them you must write solely from memory.


C. Citing properly

Other instructors and other disciplines may have different standards. (Sorry about that.) Learn and follow the guidelines in each course you take. Follow these instructions in this course. As always, if you are unsure what's OK, ask me, preferably before turning work in.

Works Cited/Bibliography/References: If you only use class materials, a Works Cited is optional in this course. If you use any sources outside of class materials, you must include a Works Cited for them.

How to cite various sources:
  • Readings with page numbers: Give the author's name and the page number in parentheses, like this: (Plato, 15) or (Nielsen, 229).

  • Readings with paragraph numbers: give the author's name and the paragraph number, like this: (King, 18).

  • Readings without page or paragraph numbers: If the assigned text is, for example, a web page with no page numbers, just cite the author, like this: (Garrett).

  • Videos: Give the video's title and the time at which the cited material begins, like this: (Nozick Explainer, 4:13).

  • Citing class discussion or lecture:
    • Caution: don't rely only on class notes for material that is in assigned texts or videos, because the original source is often richer. Also, since we're fallible, notes may be inaccurate. Use the notes to help yourself understand the original source and quote/cite the original instead. But if you use words or ideas from notes, you must quote and/or cite them.
    • Class notes: cite the class and date, like (Phil 230, 2021-02-05) or (2021-02-05 Phil 230 notes)
    • Class discussion: same as for class notes.
    • Recorded class meeting (for classes taught remotely): include the date and, like with any video, the time where the cited material begins: (Phil 230, 2021-02-05, 15:35).

  • Citing outside sources: You may always augment your papers with research outside of assigned readings, but you are only required to if the assignment says so. If you use any outside sources:

    • Choose them carefully. There are many sources you might consult, but they are not all equally good. Follow the advice I've put in the presentation assignment.

    • Cite outside sources in a standard bibliographic format. I will accept any standard format, though I prefer Chicago or Turabian format, using the parenthetical citations/reference list style, which has you cite an outside source in parentheses -- like (Miyazaki, 247) -- wherever you use it in your work, and provide a bibliography (Works Cited) at the end. You must include the URL for all sources you access via the internet. A quick guide to Turabian format is here on the Library web page; click the  Turabian (P/RL)  tab for instructions on how to cite various kinds of sources.
      (The presentation assignment has specific instructions on documenting your work.)

    • Citing conversations. If you got an idea while talking with, say, a friend or relative, that is fine! But you must still cite it. It's easy: just give the name of the person and the date and say, "personal communication." Example: (Joan Anderson, personal communication, 5/23/2005). If you use their words, put them in quotation marks and cite.

    • Citing AI: 
      • Before using AI in this course, read my policy on it.
      • Whenever you use something from an AI in your work, cite it in parentheses with the name of the AI, like (Bing) or (ChatGPT). If you use the AI's words you must put them in quotation marks and cite.

Punctuate your citations properly:

Citing without quoting: We would not want to live our lives in an experience machine because, in it, we would never actually do things (Nozick, 43).

(The period goes after the citation.)
Citing when quoting: Although Williams grants that "Utilitarianism is in more than one way an important subject," he rejects it and predicts that "The day cannot be too far off in which we hear no more of it" (Williams, 9).

(The citation goes outside the quotation marks and the period goes after the citation.)
Also:
 When you mention the author's name in the sentence, you don't need to include it in the citation (though including it is never wrong). For example:

Although Williams grants that "Utilitarianism is in more than one way an important subject," he rejects it and predicts that "The day cannot be too far off in which we hear no more of it" (9).



Paper format
  • 1100-1500 words (3-5 pages). I grade content, not length. I will not reduce your grade just because your paper is a little below or a little over the suggested word count.
    • Instead, think of it this way: if you write your paper and end up well below the suggested length, you probably need to say more. If you end up well over the suggested length, you probably need to omit some material or be more concise.

  • Word (.docx or .doc), Rich Text Format (.rtf), or shared Google doc only. No PDF's or Pages files, please.

  • Double spaced, 1" margins, numbered pages, 12-point Times New Roman or similar font.

  • Your name and an accurate word count (for the text of the paper, not counting any footnotes, endnotes, or works cited) at the top.

  • Free of errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc. Do not trust your college GPA to auto-correct or to grammar- or spell-checkers; proofread your paper yourself. Read it aloud!


Style

Strive for simplicity. Use simple, clear language as much as possible. Don't use big words or complicated sentences when simpler words and sentences will do. For more specific advice:
    • Here are quick, helpful guides to
      • writing concisely,
      • rephrasing to eliminate extra words, and 
      • avoiding some common kinds of wordiness.
    • Here is a method for simplifying your writing.
    • Here is a guide to how NOT to write.
Avoid clichés. Please don't tell me how long humanity has pondered your topic ("For centuries, humanity has sought the basis of morality..."), or give me Webster’s definition of anything.

You may write in the first person in this course if you prefer.
 
Revise and rewrite. Complete a draft and then let it sit for at least a few hours. Then come back and revise to make it even clearer and more logical, and to proofread it. I also recommend you get assistance from DePauw's Writing center. Of course all this means you should get started well before the deadline.



Advice for writing a good paper

I am not saying this is the only way, but this works. If you have different ideas about how to do a paper in this course, it would be good to meet with me and discuss them.

First, give a readers a clear preview of what you will do in the paper in a brief (no more than half a page) introduction which includes a thesis and map.
  • Two reasons for including a thesis and map in your intro: first, it's a courtesy to readers. Second, writing it gets you to reflect on, and refine, what you're going to say in the paper.
  • Your thesis is the overall conclusion that you are going to argue for; the main thing you want to persuade readers of.
    • Examples: 
      • "Although Thrasymachus' claim that 'injustice is always stronger than justice' is open to some counterexamples, it is nevertheless more plausible than Socrates thinks," or 
      • "Benedict exaggerates how deeply our values are 'moulded' by culture; Rachels shows how we can identify some universally-valid moral rules."
  • After the thesis, give a map -- a short preview of the main steps you will take to argue for your thesis. For example, you could concisely summarize some objection(s) you will consider, and how you intend to rebut it/them (if you intend to).
  • Resist the urge to do much more than this in your intro.
Then, explain an author's view, using the text, in a page or two.
  • Pick something substantial from one of our texts which you want to support or criticize.
  • State the strongest reasons your chosen author provides for it, not all of them. Whether you agree with it or not, present it charitably: what is the most persuasive way the author's view can be stated?
  • Do not just write out your notes from class. Go to the text and find the argument there. You may find that it differs from the impression you got in class.
    • A good way to do this is quote-explain-quote-explain: to reconstruct an author's view, quote the author and explain what they mean, quote some more, explain some more, and so on.
  • Give yourself a page or two to do this well.
Then, critically evaluate that view.
  • One way to critically evaluate a view is to challenge it with objections. You could provide objections of your own or borrow from texts, class discussion, or outside research (cite all sources, of course, and choose sources wisely if you do research outside course materials). Given the disagreements between our authors, you will often be able to bring up what one author says to rebut another.
  • It's more effective to give one or two strong points against the author's view, and state them well, than to raise many brief objections.
  • Again, give yourself a page or two to do this well.
In the end, draw a reasonable conclusion from the evidence you're provided.
  • You may support the author's view or reject it, in whole or in part. But whatever you conclude needs to be clearly supported by the evidence you've given.
  • Do not just give your personal opinion. Instead, explain why any reasonable reader should agree with your conclusion, giving clear reasons why. Philosophy is less about personal expression than it is about rational persuasion.
  • Evaluating the author's view doesn't have to mean showing it is bad. Maybe it turns out the view is good -- or at least that some objection(s) fail to show it's bad. There are lots of ways a paper can go. Here are some examples:
    • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Here's why the objection shows the author is wrong. The end.
    • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. The objection, though, fails to show the author is wrong. So, the author's view is immune to that objection.
    • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Here's why this objection fails. But wait, here's another objection, and it succeeds.
    • Here's something this author says. Here's an objection. Oh, and here's another. But here's why they both fail to show the argument is bad.
    • Here's this author's view. Here's why it's partly right, and here's why it's partly wrong.
    • ...and so on.
Rewrite your paper at least once, and check your work.
Clean up any misspellings, bad grammar, awkward phrasing, and so on.
Make sure you have met the requirements for integrity, format, and style.
