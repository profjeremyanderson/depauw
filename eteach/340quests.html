Evaluative criteria

The syllabus says that “Various terms, ideas, arguments, approaches, etc. will come up in readings, lectures, presentations, and discussion. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate that you understand and have thought about that material.” So your essays should demonstrate that you have understood the relevant reading well, paid close attention in class, thought about it all carefully. Per the syllabus, the exam is worth 10% of the grade for the course.

All of the prompts ask you for exposition, and some also ask for opinion. Your expositions should be as thorough, well-organized, accurate, and clear as you can make them in the time available. Whenever you give an opinion, provide reasons for whatever stand you take and strive to make them persuasive. That is, try to show not why you think as you do but why any reasonable person should think as you do.


  1. Obedience. In Crito, Socrates says that “you must either persuade [your government] or else do whatever it commands; that you must mind your behavior and undergo whatever treatment it prescribes for you, whether a beating or imprisonment; that if it leads you to war to be wounded or killed, that’s what you must do, and that’s what is just” (Plato, 69; at 51b). First explain, in as much detail as you can, Socrates’ argument in the Crito for this claim. Second, to what extent to you agree with Socrates here--partially, fully, not at all? Give the best reasons you can for why reasonable people should agree with your appraisal.

  2. Shutting Thrasymachus up. Thrasymachus defends the view that injustice is better than justice (Plato, 82-6; 338b-344c), concluding with the claim that "injustice if it is on a large enough scale, is stronger, freer, and more masterful than justice" (Plato, 86; at 344c). Socrates responds with several rebuttals (Plato, 86-93; 344d-354a), to which Thrasymachus eventually feels forced to agree by the end of Republic Book I. But how good are Socrates' rebuttals, really? There are too many to discuss all of them, so pick one and, first, explain it in as much detail as you can, including how it is supposed to rebut what Thrasymachus said. Then, critically evaluate Socrates' argument, giving the best reasons you can for why reasonable people should agree with your appraisal.
  3. Lying for a good cause. In Book III of Republic, Socrates explains his “noble lie” (Plato, 128-9; at 414c – 416b). First explain, in as much detail as you can, the lie and how it is intended to support the ideal city. Then explain why this sort of practice should be approved, or not, showing why reasonable people should agree with you. Resist the temptation to think this is easy. You might perhaps consider similar practices in your own upbringing, such as how you were taught to be patriotic; you should consider the goals of the lie and whether they are worthy, and how they might be attained.

  4. The good (?) of Socratic justice. Socrates declares in Republic Book IV that “each person must practice one of the pursuits in the city, the one for which he is naturally best suited” and goes on to say this is among the things that “will most contribute to making our city good” (Plato, 139; at 433a, 433d). On the other hand, when people try to perform tasks they are not naturally suited for, he says “these exchanges and meddling destroy the city” (Plato 140; at 434b). First  explain, in as much detail as you can, the arrangement that Socrates has in mind here (the three-class system of the city, and the requisite virtues). Then, critically evaluate this arrangement. There are any number of ways you might do that well; one would be to consider some strong objection to it and argue for how well (or not) Socrates' arrangement can withstand it. (You might use any of the objections we discussed in class--which themselves came from reading responses for our February 14 class; because of time constraints I suggest only considering one objection, or at most two.)
