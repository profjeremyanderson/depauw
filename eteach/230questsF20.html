Exam questions about Ethical Theory (our first day).

1. In class we listed many reasons why you should, or should not, hook up with a stranger when your significant other is away. These reasons fell into three main groups. First, identify the groups. (Hint: remember what the reasons in each group had in common.) Which group was biggest? Which was smallest? For each group, identify one reason for or against hooking up in that group, and explain why that reason fits in that group. Finally, name the type of ethical theory which corresponds to each group.

Exam questions about the basis of moral rules.

2. Cephalus believes that justice is being truthful and giving back what you have taken from another (Plato, 7). Why does Socrates reject this definition?

3. In class we saw that when Socrates, Cephalus, and Polemarchus try to define justice (Plato, 2-8) they make some important assumptions about it. What were their assumptions about justice?

4. Explain what Thrasymachus means when he says that “the just is nothing else than the advantage of the stronger” (Plato, 15; Phil 230, 2020-09-02). How does this challenge the others' assumptions about justice?

5. Thrasymachus defends his moral skepticism by arguing that being just does not benefit you--rather, it is bad for you--and that "justice" is really just a sort of scam used to exploit the weak. How does he argue for this? In other words, what reasons does he give for his view (Plato, 16, 21-2)?

6. Define these terms as they are used in this course: “moral skepticism,” “moral subjectivism,” “cultural relativism,” and “ethical objectivism” (Phil 230, 2020-09-04).

7. Benedict describes two main kinds of cultural differences. One kind is where people we have labeled "abnormal" and unfit--such as those who are cataleptic or homosexual--have a respected (though not necessarily central) place in other societies (Benedict, 1-2). Explain one of Benedict's examples of that kind of difference.

8. Benedict says, "The most spectacular illustrations of the extent to which normality may be culturally defined are those cultures where an abnormality of our culture is the cornerstone of their social structure" (3). First, explain what this sentence means. Then, pick one of the cultures she uses to illustrate this point, and explain how that culture's practices are supposed to provide evidence for Benedict's claim.

9. How does Benedict use her examples of differences to support cultural relativism? In class, this was summarized in "Benedict's argument," so you could explain that argument.

10. How does Rachels criticize the "Cultural Differences Argument" (Rachels, 3-4)? What problems for Rachels were discussed in class? (There were two.)

11. How does Rachels argue that there is less disagreement about values between cultures than it seems (Rachels, 5-6)? Explain both the cows and Eskimos examples.

12. Rachels says, “There are some moral rules that all societies must have in common, because those rules are necessary for society to exist” (7). How does he support this claim?

13. According to Rachels, there is a culture-neutral standard for telling whether moral rules are justified (Rachels, 9). What is it? How does he argue for this?

14. King lists negative experiences suffered by Black people (King, 14) and says they are unjust--that they are objectively wrong (16). What would a moral skeptic say about whether they are wrong? What would a moral subjectivist say? A cultural relativist?

15. King says we should obey just laws but disobey unjust laws (15). How, according to King, can we objectively distinguish just laws from unjust laws (King, 15-20 but especially 16)?

16. Benedict observes individual cultures and finds a range of human traits and moral values. How does Aquinas differ? (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video will be helpful here.)

17. Aquinas argues for ethical objectivism by saying that human nature--specifically, our natural inclinations--provide an objective basis for judging human laws. What are the inclinations Aquinas identifies (Summa, 4-5)? What precepts of natural law does Aquinas derive from these inclinations?

18. What does it mean to say that the precepts of natural law are vague, and that they are authoritative? What are the implications of them being vague, and being authoritative? (Class notes and the Aquinas explainer video will be helpful here.)

Situation: Azim and Beth disagree. Azim is a conservative Saudi Muslim. Azim criticizes Beth for dressing immodestly because Beth does not cover her face or legs in public. Beth is an American liberal atheist. Beth criticizes Azim for wanting to restrict women’s freedom by dictating how they dress.
19. Explain how a moral subjectivist would “resolve” the dispute between Azim and Beth. Then explain how a cultural relativist would. In other words, how would a subjectivist think about their disagreement? How would a cultural relativist? What prospect is there for resolving this dispute? How come?

20. Explain how Aquinas might resolve the dispute between Azim and Beth. In other words, given what Aquinas says in the assigned reading, is there any prospect of resolving their dispute? What might it be? (What’s most important here is to give a sense of how Aquinas would approach this matter. The more thoroughly you can answer the other Aquinas questions, the better you will be able to explain that.)

Note: Any exam questions with highlighted numbers (like this: 20. ) will be worth more points on exams because they call for more extensive, thoughtful answers. Some might also ask you to present and support your own opinion.

21. Glaucon and Socrates agree there are three ways something can be good (Plato, 35-6). Explain what each is, and give two examples of each. (You may use examples from the book or class discussion, but you will get more points if you come up with your own unique examples.) Which kind of good is justice, according to Socrates?

Note: material helpful for answering the following Glaucon questions can be found in the Glaucon's Challenge slides PDF, which is stored with class notes on our Google drive.

22. Glaucon describes "what justice is and where it came from" (Plato, 36-7). What four claims about human nature does he make there (or, rather, what four claims does he think "the many" believe)?

23. Suppose Glaucon's four claims about human nature are true. What, then, does Glaucon think we should do (Plato, 37)? What, then, is Glaucon implying about us & society?

24. In class we discussed what people would do if they had a ring that would enable them to do as they please. What were the results--would people generally behave justly or not? How does that relate to Glaucon's claims about human nature?

Exam questions about Consequentialist Ethics.

25. What four things do all consequentialist (aka teleological) ethical theories do? Why, then, can there be many different consequentialist theories? In your answer, be sure to identify the different possible kinds of accounts of the good, and at least three possible scopes.

26. State the Principle of Utility either from class or from Bentham's or Mill’s text (Bentham, 1; Mill calls it the “Greatest Happiness Principle,” 1). What steps would you need to take to apply the PU properly? (Bentham's description in section V on pp. 3-4 may be helpful here.)

27. Explain why utilitarianism has the "accounting problem" we discussed in class. How does Bentham respond to the problem? (Bentham, section VI on p. 4.)

Note: #27 used to ask, "How do Bentham and Mill respond to the accounting problem?" Mill's answer is pretty elaborate, so there is now a separate question about it (#31), and #27 only asks you for Bentham's response.

28. Why does utilitarianism need some way to rank pleasures?

29. How does Bentham think we should rank pleasures? Take two very different activities (maybe, eating Cheetos or watching a dramatic film like Parasite) and explain how Bentham's scheme might rank them, using the parameters he mentions (121). (Well, you don't have to use all--let's say, use four of them). Which of your two activities would be better, according to Bentham's scheme, and why?

30. What is the "swine problem" (Mill, 1)? Mill responds to the swine problem in two ways; explain both of them. In doing so, be sure to answer the following questions. What pleasures does Mill say that only humans can enjoy (Mill, 1)? How does he argue that we value them much more highly (Mill, 2)? What does Mill say are the causes of unhappiness in those who are well off, and those who are not (Mill, 4)? How does Mill think a life of self-development and service alleviates unhappiness for all (Mill, 4-5)?

31. Mill has two responses to the "accounting problem" (Mill, 5-6). Explain each one. In doing so, be sure to answer the following questions. How does Mill argue that some people have more responsibility than others (Mill, 5)? (In class this was compared to the Spider-Man Rule.) How does Mill argue that, really, we do not need to stop and calculate the effects of our actions before we can act (Mill, 6)?

32. Nozick gives three reasons why we would not plug in to the Experience Machine (Nozick, 43-4). What are they?

33. What is the Experience Machine Argument? (See the Experience Machine explainer video and class notes.) Be sure to make clear how the argument is supposed to show a problem with utilitarianism.

34. Explain why utilitarianism would imply that, in Le Guin’s imaginary city of Omelas, it is morally right for the citizens to persecute the child. Then, explain why this might make utilitarianism objectionable.

35. What is the "moral conservative" argument against consequentialism (Nielsen, 219; class notes)? What is Nielsen's strategy for countering this objection?

36. Moral conservatives have used magistrate-type cases to argue that consequentialism should be rejected because it requires deliberately harming the innocent. How does Nielsen argue that moral conservatives need not reject consequentialism for how it deals with the magistrate case (223-5)? (Be sure to discuss what he says could happen if word gets out, 224.) How satisfactory is Nielsen's argument here? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.

37. Nielsen says consequentialism may clearly require harming the innocent sometimes--for example, in the case of the fat man stuck in the cave mouth (Nielsen,  222). How does he argue that this is the right thing to do here (228-30)? (Your answer will be better if you include how Nielsen responds to concerns about callousness, using the fat man as a means, and inhumanity, 228-30.) How satisfactory is his argument regarding this case? Give the strongest reasons you can to agree with you.


Advice: some exam questions ask you to give your opinion and your reasons for it (like #36 and 37). The best strategy is to--first, before giving your opinion--explain what the author says in as much detail as you can. That will give you things to think critically about: you might agree with part of it, and disagree with another part, for example. Second, when giving your opinion, it's better to give independent reasons; don't just echo the author's own reasons. For example, saying "I agree with Nielsen in the fat man case because blowing the fat man up is the lesser evil" does not show much thought on your part. If that's all you can say, you should answer a different question.

43. Singer identifies two principles: a "strong" one and a "moderate" one (Singer, 231). What is the "strong" principle? How is it more demanding (requires more sacrifice) than the "moderate" one?

45. Among Harwood’s eleven objections to utilitarianism are that it fails to take promises seriously enough (#4; Harwood, 6-7); overstates our duties to animals (#8; Harwood, 11); and that it panders to bigots and sadists (#9; Harwood, 11-2). Pick one of these objections and explain it. That is, put the objection in your own words and make clear how it is supposed to give us a reason to reject utilitarianism.
 
46. Harrison and Harwood (in Harwood, 9-10) object to rule utilitarianism (RU) on several grounds. In class, they were combined into a dilemma: either (a) RU says to do the same thing as act utilitarianism in every situation, or (b) at least sometimes RU does not--but that RU is objectionable either way. Exam questions about Deontological Ethics.

47. Lecture discussed three types of deontological ethical theories, which were distinguished by how we are supposed to know what is right and wrong. Identify and define these three types. Of these three, which type is Kant's theory?

Kant says this about the nature of moral laws and the proper sort of justification for them:
That there must be [a “pure” moral] philosophy is evident from the common idea of duty and of moral laws. Everyone must admit that if a law is to be morally valid, i.e., is to be valid as a ground of obligation, then it must carry with it absolute necessity. He must admit that the command, “Thou shalt not lie,” does not hold only for men, as if other rational beings had no need to abide by it, and so with all the other moral laws properly so called. And he must concede that the ground of obligation here must therefore be sought not in the world in which man is placed, but must be sought...solely in the concepts of pure reason.... (Kant, 219).

48. Without using Kant's words, explain how Kant argues here that the justification of moral laws must come from pure reason.
 
49. Given Kant's commitment to justifying moral laws by pure reason, explain why Kant would reject some other views we have looked at in the course. Pick any two of the following: (a) conventionalism; (b) Aquinas' natural law theory, (c) Pojman's objectivism; (d) Glaucon's social contract; or (e) Bentham & Mill's classical utilitarianism. Identify specific things about those two that would lead Kant, based on the argument given in the quote above, to reject it.

50. Lecture discussed why our motives are important in ethics. Give an example of some action, and explain how having different motives could make that act morally right or morally wrong. (You could use an example from class such as your boss giving you a gift, or make up some other example.)

51. According to Kant, what are "inclinations"? (This was explained in class.) Why does Kant say that there may never have been a truly moral action (7)? (To answer this, it will help to explain how inclinations are (a) unreliable (Kant, 1) and (b) sneaky (that is, unseen by us; 7).) Finally, what does Kant think a truly moral action would be? In other words, what's a "good will"?

52. What is a maxim? (Feldman discusses maxims on pp. 2-4, but the discussion in class may be more accessible, and the notes include a definition.)  Give 2 examples NOT used in class or the text.

53. State Kant’s categorical imperative, in the formula of universal law (FUL) (Kant, 10). Then identify the steps used to apply it (i.e, the "4-step test").

54. Lecture discussed the maxim, "When I need money I shall rob a bank to get it" (assignment 22). Explain how to test this maxim using the 4-step test. Why does it fail?

55. Kant gives an example of someone making a false promise (Kant, 11, #2, from "Another man in need..."). What is the maxim in this case? Why does it fail the 4-step test?

56. Lecture talked about Kant's example of refusing to give to the needy (Kant, 11, #4, from "A fourth man..."). What is the maxim in this case? Why does it fail? How is the failure here different from the failure in the bank-robbing case?

Situation (from Williams, 3): George’s lack of a job is causing trouble at home. He has the option of taking a research job at a chemical and biological weapons lab. Taking the job would be against his conscience; he opposes such weapons. But if George does not take the job, Zed will take it and enthusiastically pursue the research, creating very icky ways to kill people.
57. Using the four-step test for applying Kant’s formula of universal law, determine whether George may take the job. To do this well you will have to present and explain each step carefully, especially steps 3 and (if you get to it) 4.
 
58. Feldman criticizes Kant’s formula of universal law for permitting some actions which really are wrong (Feldman, 14-5). Give an example of this--one of Feldman’s, or one from class, or your own--and explain how it demonstrates this problem.

59. Feldman argues that Kant’s formula of universal law is faulty because it forbids some actions which really should be allowed (Feldman, 15). Give an example of this, and explain how it demonstrates this problem. (The example may be one of Feldman’s, or one from class, or your own.)

60. Using the example of choosing whether to keep a lunch date or give aid to an accident victim (Ross, 2), explain what Ross thinks is wrong with Kantianism.

61. Using the example of choosing whether to keep a lunch date or aid an accident victim (Ross, 2), explain what Ross thinks is wrong with consequentialism. Be sure to explain how Ross thinks that utilitarianism oversimplifies our relationships with others, and how Ross' view is different (2-3).

62. Given the problems he identifies with Kantianism and consequentialism, what features does Ross think a proper ethical theory should have?

63. According to Ross, what does it mean to say our duties are prima facie (Ross, 3, 5)? Identify six of our prima facie duties, according to Ross (3-4).

64. How does Ross say we should decide what to do (3)? (In class, we identified some steps to take.) When we tried applying Ross's procedure in class, what problems did we find?

Situation: See the situation described above for question 57 (George's job offer).
65. Suppose you are George. Adopt Ross’ ethical theory. Explain whether you would take the job or not, and how you would arrive at that decision. To do so, first identify at least three relationships that are salient in this situation, and at least three prima facie duties arising from those relationships which apply in this situation. (By "at least three" I mean that is the minimum for a passable answer.) Given those duties to those people, and considering the situation, which duty (or duties) is overriding in this case? Why?
