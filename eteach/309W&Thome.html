Course description and goals
We will look at activities that, despite our protestations of dislike for them, we humans engage in rather a lot: war and terrorism. Specifically, we will critically examine (a) realism, i.e., the view that morality is irrelevant to international relations, (b) pacifism, (c) traditional ideas concerning the morality of war and some recent innovations, (d) the nature of terrorism and (e) responses to it. Our goals will be to:
  • Understand and come to a reasoned evaluation of major schools of thought regarding (a)-(e)
  • Enable and encourage you to critically reflect on your position regarding these matters
  • Appreciate the complexity of the issues but nevertheless be able to take reasoned stands on them
  • Understand and intelligently take part in current and ongoing debates regarding the use of force
  • Further develop your skills at research, writing, and speaking
  • Demonstrate the above in graded assignments and classroom participation.

Assignments & grading
Various terms, ideas, arguments, approaches, etc. will come up in readings, lectures, presentations, and discussion. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate that you understand and have thought about that material. (Ways of demonstrating you’ve thought about it include critically appraising it, connecting it to other ideas presented inside or outside the course, etc.) Where independent research is required, your grade will also depend on the quality of that research and demonstrating that you understand and have thought carefully about that material. This is a 300-level course so my expectations for the quality of your work will be significantly higher than in 100- or 200-level courses. The course grade will be derived as follows: 
 
10%    Early exam (around 5th week)
 10%    Reading responses
 15%     Two short essays @ ~1200-1500 words each
 15%     Small-group presentation
 20%    One research paper @ ~3000-4000 words
  5%     Class participation
  5%     C0-leading discussion
 20%    Comprehensive final exam

Reading responses: at least once each week, starting with our next meeting, you will need to write a response of at least 250 words to at least one of the assigned readings and post it to that week's reading responses forum in Moodle. In each response you need to do two things:
1. Very briefly summarize the reading--that is, tell me what you take to be the two or three main points the author tries to make. Your goal here is to demonstrate you have done the reading carefully. 
2. In a separate paragraph (or more), give your reaction—positive, negative, neutral, tangential, whatever—to the reading(s). Your reaction may be short or long, but should be detailed enough for me to understand why you react to the reading(s) the way you do.
Responses are due the night before the class meeting where that reading will be discussed.  (Just to be clear, "the night before" means "any time before sunrise on the day we discuss that reading"; the time of sunrise shall be determined by wunderground.com; the time of submission is determined by the time stamp on your reading response.) Late responses, no matter what the cause or how slightly they are late, receive no credit.
      Responses will be scored on a simple scale: excellent (4), good (3), or deficient (1). An average of one good response per week will yield a grade of B- for this portion of the course grade. More and/or better responses will yield a higher grade. Earning a total of 53 points will get you full credit for this part of the course as long as you turn in at least one response per week. That many points can be earned by submitting one good response per week plus an additional good response four times during the semester. Alternatively, submitting one excellent response per week would earn full credit.     
      Qualities that tend to raise a reading response from good to excellent include: clarity of writing; thoughtfulness (e.g., offering careful critical appraisal of the reading); responding to more than one reading at a time (when more than one is assigned); and relating the assigned material to other assignments, other class work, outside research, etc. 
            Suggestion: since Moodle can log you out if you take too long (and this could wipe out your work), try typing your response using a word processor first, then log in to Moodle and copy your response into the forum.

Two short essays of about 1200 to 1500 words each will be required. Each will ask you to take a position regarding material we have studied and defend it against some objection(s). I can suggest topics for you or you may choose your own. Your grade for this portion of the course will be the average of the two individual papers’ grades. More details are available on the Papers web page.

Presentations will entail you forming small groups of 2 or 3 or perhaps working alone. More details will be posted on the Presentation Assignment web page, but here’s a quick description. Each individual/group will take responsibility for researching a topic within the area of war and/or terrorism and presenting it, or some portion of it, to the class. Your presentations are part of the course (their contents will be fair game for the final exam, for example), so they must be done well. I will suggest presentation topics, or you may choose your own with my approval. Using audio/visual aids is recommended, but it is your responsibility to bring or reserve any equipment you may need if it is not already in the room.

Your research paper will require you to take a stand on some problem in war and/or terrorism and support it effectively. It must be based on substantial research. Its topic may be related to that of your presentation as long as you’re not seeking credit twice for the very same work. You will need to clear your topic with me in advance and meet various stringent requirements which are posted to the Papers web page. 

Class participation consists of demonstrating you’re thinking about the course in some way besides the required reading responses. The best form is participating in class discussions, but it could also consist of discussing course-related stuff with me outside of class in person or by e-mail, etc. Mere attendance, looking interested, taking notes, etc. do not count. Participating once per week, by whatever means, will earn you a C for participation. Occasional short informal writing assignments, if given, will also count towards general participation.

 Co-leading class discussion will require you to (a) master an assigned reading, (b) create discussion questions concerning the most important points in that reading, and (c) co-lead class discussion with me on that assignment. You'll need to do this at least once.

How to succeed in this course

Remember you are now in an upper-division seminar. I expect a lot of you here. You should participate in all class meetings if possible and demonstrate careful preparation in doing so. The quality of all your written work should be strong. I will generally expect not to have to explain the readings to you very much. Rather, it will be mostly your job to figure them out for yourself and be ready to discuss them in class. My expectations for your presentations will also be high.

Read, and read carefully.

Reading is an act of contemplation, perhaps the only act in which we allow ourselves to merge with the consciousness of another human being. We possess the books we read, animating the waiting stillness of their language, but they possess us also, filling us with thoughts and observations, asking us to make them part of ourselves.... In order for this to work, however, we need a certain type of silence, an ability to filter out the noise.
David L. Ulin, “The Lost Art of Reading,” Los Angeles Times, 8/9/2009

Make time, in a quiet space, to read. Not just once, not quickly like it’s a news article, but deeply. Turn off your TV, phone, radio, MP3. When reading online, close all other windows, set Skype to offline, turn off chats, and release yourself from Facebook etc. Philosophical texts reward focused, careful, and repeated reading. Besides, you can expect me to call on you with questions about it.
     Reading assignments for this course typically consist of a selection written by a philosopher and some introductory material written by the book’s editor, Andrew Bailey. Bailey suggests you read the selection first, and then go back and read the introductory material, and then read the selection again (Preface, p. vii). I recommend following this advice. (The few exceptions are noted on the Reading Assignments page.)

Come to class, on time, prepared. Bring the current reading and your notes with you. Bring your responses to the current reading questions with you. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, at the beginning of the semester, to share notes.

Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take a moment after class to fill them out.

Participate a lot. Some students assume their questions aren't worth asking or their comments not worth making, but often others wanted to say the same thing. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you are not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.

Give yourself time to write good papers, including revising/rewriting them well before the deadline.

 Get enough sleep. If that means you cut back on extracurricular activities, so be it, because:
Studies have shown that sleep quantity and sleep quality equal or outrank such popular campus concerns as alcohol and drug use in predicting student grades and a student’s chances of graduating.
Jane E. Brody, "An Underappreciated Key to College Success: Sleep," New York Times, 8/13/2018

Seek help when you need it, or when you think you might need it, or just because. You do not need an appointment to drop in, though we can make one if you like. I do not bite, I promise.
