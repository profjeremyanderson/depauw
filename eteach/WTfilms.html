General information
Below are some films that would be appropriate for presentations, along with some of the philosophical issues they raise. Those discussed in our textbook may be most convenient since a substantial reading assignment is right there waiting for you. However, there are a lot of great films not discussed in our textbook, too.

I have listed some below. But you do not have to choose a film listed on this page. You may select almost any film (besides porn) if (a) you can persuade me that it has substantial philosophical content which you can articulate and discuss with the class, and (b) can find appropriate readings to assign for it.

There are a few other limits on films in the course. Alas:
  • Only one part of multi-part films may be shown (i.e., Spider-Man 1 or 2 but not both, Kill Bill volume 1 or 2 but not both, etc.).
  • No more than two films by any one director may be shown in the course. This will not be a Tarantino festival.
  • I reserve the right to limit the number of films with the same theme (for example, The Matrix, Total Recall, Dark City, Inception, and The Truman Show all feature radical deception; we won't watch many of them).
  • Unless there is very strong reason to show an extended version of a film, we will show the shorter version (for example, Pulp Fiction has an extended "special edition" that is 168 minutes long, but it's not philosophically much better than the shorter version; the "director's cut" of Blade Runner, on the other hand, is).
Many of the films on this list are available in Roy O. West Library’s extensive media collection. I have listed call numbers for them. Some others--marked PCPL--may be had via the Putnam County Public Library, which is a block north of campus, across the street north of Marvin's. (Click here for a map to PCPL.) Other appropriate films may be available via rental outlets such as Family Video or Netflix.
