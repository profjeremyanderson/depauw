The answers to some exam questions are relatively easy and straightforward.
Even for easy questions, there are better and worse answers.

Example: one exam question might be, "Cephalus believes that 'justice...is simply to speak the truth and to pay back any debt one may have contracted' (Plato, 3). Why does Socrates reject this definition?"

Here is one possible answer:
Socrates says it wouldn't be just to give weapons back to a man when he was insane.

The answer above isn't wrong, but it's very sparse. This next one gives a more thorough explanation of Socrates' reasoning:
Socrates gives an example where following Cephalus' definition of justice would lead us to do something we don't think is just. Suppose a man has loaned you his weapons while he was sane, but then wants them back when he is insane: to give the weapons back to him, or to be completely truthful with him (maybe by telling him truthfully where they are), would result in harm. Socrates says that would be unjust.

Per the syllabus, your grade depends mostly on how well you demonstrate that you understand and have thought about the stuff presented in the course. The second answer above does a much better job at that than the first. It would get full credit while the first answer would not.


Suppose you are asked to define terms, like "moral skepticism" and "subjectivism." Once again, it's possible to write an accurate but superficial answer:
Moral skepticism: there are no moral truths.
Subjectivism: morality is a personal decision.

Nothing in this answer is false, but it's still not very good. It contains less information than is in the book (Pojman, 19), and much less than was on the board and discussed in class. It shows very little understanding.

This answer is far better:

Moral skepticism and subjectivism are two different views about the basis of moral rules--that is, about how moral rules get their validity, if any.

Moral skepticism admits that there are moral rules, but it holds they have no basis, or none we can know for sure. So, skepticism does not consider any moral principles to be binding (or "valid" as the book says). Moral skepticism implies that ethics is a kind of sham to the extent that it tries to convince us that rules are justified.

Unlike moral skepticism, subjectivism holds that moral rules can be justified. But subjectivism says their justification depends entirely on one's personal decision or belief. In other words, subjectivism holds that for an act is right only if you think it is; otherwise it is not.

This shows a clear understanding that both moral skepticism and subjectivism are views about whether and how moral principles can be justified, and clearly states each view's position on the matter.


Answering some questions is more challenging.

Here's a challenging question: "Suppose you've already explained why Hobbes thinks there would be no natural winners in the state of nature. (In other words, you don't need to explain that; just assume it's so.) Explain why Hobbes thinks this competition would result in severe misery (Hobbes, 71). In other words, how does Hobbes argue that we would probably not cooperate with each other but instead end up attacking each other (Hobbes, 70-1)?"

This question asks you to explain a fairly complicated part of Hobbes.

Here is one possible answer:
Hobbes says we would have the right of nature. He also says there are glory seekers, and that pure defense loses in the long run, and that we are vain, and that we are translucent. So there would be a war of all against all, and our lives would be miserable.

Ahem. Everything this answer says is correct, so it would get a few points. But it just lists a bunch of stuff copied off the chart from class. It does not show any understanding of what the words mean, or how Hobbes thinks those things go together to make us miserable in a state of nature. So this is a bad answer.

This one is much better:
Hobbes points out that we are not all the same. Some of us "would be glad to be at ease within modest bounds" (70), that is, they would only use the resources they need and thereby minimize conflicts with others. Let's call those people "nice." But Hobbes says that some people enjoy conquering others and taking more than they need (70). In class we called them "glory seekers."

So, what happens when nice people meet glory seekers? In the state of nature, there are no laws nor police to enforce them. So Hobbes says there we have the "right of nature," that is, the right to act however we please (72). So in any situation, it's up to each person to decide how to respond. So when nice people meet glory seekers, the glory seekers will attack, because that's what they like to do and there's nothing to stop them.

If the nice people don't defend themselves, they're conquered. If they defend themselves, but ONLY defend themselves and never get aggressive or invade others, Hobbes says they will be defeated in the long run (70). So--if we assume, as Hobbes does, that even nice people want to survive--the nice people cannot just defend themselves but will also have to attack and invade.

To make things worse, Hobbes points out (outside the assigned reading) that we are "translucent." That is, you can't tell who is a glory seeker and who is nice just by looking at them. So you always have to be suspicious of everyone: you might be a nice person who's willing to cooperate with other nice people, but it's hard to trust anyone else.

In other words, the presence of glory seekers, and our translucence, forces even nice people to behave like glory seekers: they are motivated to attack others both because they will be conquered if they don't, and because they cannot tell who their enemies are. Hobbes says that in this situation the best way to respond is "anticipation," that is, to pre-emptively attack others.

So here we already have a situation where people are attacking each other: glory seekers attack nice people and other glory seekers because they want to, and nice people attack glory seekers and other nice people because they have to.

But there's more. Hobbes points out that we have a hard time getting along with others because we are vain. Each of us feels entitled to a certain level of respect from others, and when we don't get it, we're motivated to take vengeance on those who (we feel) disrespect us. Again, because of the right of nature, there's nothing preventing us from being as vengeful as we please, so Hobbes thinks we may go as far as killing each other (70).

Hobbes says this vanity arises everywhere there's no government to enforce laws (70), so we all have it, not just the glory seekers. So even nice people have a hard time getting along with each other, even when they know they are among nice people.

Given all these things (glory seekers, the failure of pure defense, the right of nature, translucence, vanity, and our interest in survival) Hobbes thinks we would pretty much all be fighting each other rather than cooperating.

Which is ironic: we would all be pursuing our self interest, but doing it without restraint leads to us all being miserable.

This answer explains how all of those factors on the chart combine to prevent us from cooperating with each other in a state of nature.

Of course I wouldn't expect you to give this exact answer. Nor would I expect you to quote Hobbes or give page numbers! I put them here to help you find the material in the text if you want.
