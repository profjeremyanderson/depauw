 		War
Instigator: Jeremy
 		Thucydides, “The Melian Dialogue” from The Peloponnesian War (e-reserve).

Machiavelli, The Prince. The whole book is short and a great read. We will focus most on what Machiavelli has to say about war and morality--esp. why and when it's OK to be immoral--in chapters 3, 7, 8, and 14-25.

Peck, “Peck’s Postulates” (online--and very short!).
02 Monday 2/3 War
Instigator: Mitchael 		Walzer, Just and Unjust Wars, Part I (chs. 1-3, pp. 3-47). We will mainly focus on how Walzer tries to refute realism and show that morality applies to war. Much of this is in ch. 1, and it's elaborated in chs. 2-3. If you don't have your book yet, here is a copy of the assigned text.

Kilner, "Military Leaders' Obligation to Justify Killing in War." The whole piece is accessible and interesting. For our purposes the most relevant portion is the last section, pp. 29-31; you can just skim the rest of the article.

Optional: Thucydides, "The Mytilene Episode" from The Peloponnesian War (e-reserve). Walzer contrasts the Melian dialogue with the Mytilene episode (pp. 9-12); this is the text he is talking about.

Instigator: Mitchael CJ
 Sam Harris, "In Defense of Torture" (PDF). Harris presents a version of the "ticking bomb" argument, which is by far the most popular argument for torture; original posted here.

Catherine McDonald, "Defusing the ‘ticking bomb’: why the argument for torture fails" (PDF), Online Opinion, April 12, 2007; original posted here.

Read just the opening summary, and browse the photos, in "Abu Ghraib torture and prisoner abuse" (Wikipedia)

 Read the Table of Contents (and, if you have time, pp. 1-28) of "ICRC Report on the Treatment of Fourteen 'High-Value Detainees' in CIA Custody," International Committee of the Red Cross, February 14, 2007. This report details the torture of people held by the CIA in its "black sites."

Optional: more background on torture, especially as practiced by the US, is here.
