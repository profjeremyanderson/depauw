Questions for the Early Exam

The first exam will consist of three of the four questions I will post below. Of those three, you will choose two and answer them as best you can in class without texts, notes, or other aids.

Treat your answers as a chance to show off what you've learned in this course and how sensitive you are to the issues. Each question gives you the opportunity to demonstrate a lot of learning if you connect it to course materials as much as you can.

Once the questions are posted, I recommend the following to prepare. Review the readings and your notes from class. As you consider each question, try to identify everything (cases, legal doctrines, moral theories, articles, arguments, etc.) that might possibly be relevant to answering the question. Then, decide which of those considerations are most relevant to the question, and helpful for making a decision. Of these, which (if any) are decisive, and why? Which considerations might seem decisive but turn out to be mistaken? (It would be good to rebut these in your answers.)

Questions will ask you to draw some conclusion and justify it. In philosophy it is never good merely to state why you happen to think something is so. Nor is it good to present tricky arguments, attempting to persuade your reader in the sort of way, for example, that politicians often try to persuade voters. You must always strive to give reasons that should persuade any rational reader, without being tricky. Your job is to demonstrate as best you honestly can what the truth of the matter is. To do this well, keep in mind that some smart people will disagree with you, and you must strive to persuade them. Thinking well about the questions may require you to change your conclusions, and change your mind. That is an important kind of growth, and I intend for it to happen as part of the process of preparing for the exam.
