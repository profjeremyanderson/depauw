Questions for the Final Exam (Thursday May 14, 8:30 - 11:30am in our regular classroom)

The final exam will consist of four of the five questions below. Of those four, you will choose three and answer them as best you can in class without texts, notes, devices, or other aids.

Per the syllabus, answers will be evaluated by how well you demonstrate you understand and have thought about the material presented in readings and in class. As with the first exam, treat your answers as a chance to show off how much you’ve learned in this course and how sensitive you are to the issues.

Advice:
  • Review any texts related each question to refresh your memory of the issues to consider and relevant arguments you might discuss.
  • Questions will ask you to draw some conclusion and justify it. In philosophy it is never good merely to state why you happen to think something is so. Nor is it good to present tricky arguments, attempting to persuade your reader in the sort of way, for example, that politicians often try to persuade voters. You must always strive to give reasons that should persuade any rational reader, without being tricky. Your job is to demonstrate as best you honestly can what the truth of the matter is. To do this well, keep in mind that some smart people will disagree with you, and you must strive to persuade them. Thinking well about the questions may require you to change your conclusions, and change your mind. That is an important kind of growth, and I intend for it to happen as part of the process of preparing for the exam.

1. Liability, harm, and religious belief. Jadine Russell, 55, was a devout Jehovah’s Witness killed in a car accident. The Witnesses believe their relationship to God is threatened by the infusion of “foreign” blood in the operating room. They have the right to refuse even life-saving treatment, as both medical ethics and the law support all patients’ freedom to refuse unwanted treatment. But Russell’s case has a complication. She was in an accident on the open road, 25 miles east of Los Angeles. Then Keith Cook, driving with twice the legal limit of alcohol in his bloodstream—while on probation for drunk driving—sped over a hill and smashed into Russell’s car. But, as one of his attorneys told the jury: “People are free to have their religious choice and freedom. But when it has consequences for someone else, that is where the line is drawn.” Cook’s attorney said Russell killed herself by refusing the transfusion. Her daughter confirmed that Russell, in the life-flight helicopter, refused a transfusion. By the time they reached the hospital, she had lost half the blood in her body. She told an intern, “If it’s my turn to go, it’s my turn to go.” Defendant Cook’s mother told the press her son wasn’t guilty of murder, saying, “He just wishes the lady had gotten all the medical help she could get.” If convicted of manslaughter or second-degree murder, Cook could get 10 to 25 years in prison. If convicted only of drunken driving—a charge he doesn’t dispute—he could get four to eight years.
    Imagine you are on the jury. Using relevant concepts and readings from the course, explain what the jury should decide. What details would be most important? Who was most to blame for Jadine Russell’s death, and why?


2. Statutory interpretation. Randolph Lee received a traffic citation which he falsely signed in the name of his deceased brother, Edward Watson. A few weeks later, Lee was again stopped for various traffic infractions. Lee told officers that he was Edward Watson, and argued that, since he had received the previous citation in that name, “he had to be Mr. Watson.” When his subterfuge was discovered, Lee was charged with violating a section of the California penal code that made it an offense to “falsely impersonate another either in his private or official capacity.” Lee insisted upon his innocence, arguing that a deceased person “has no private or official capacity” and therefore it could not be a crime to impersonate a dead person. Lee claimed that the obvious purpose of the statute was to prohibit forms of impersonation that could result in actual harm to another existing individual. The prosecution countered that the legislature must have intended to make impersonation of the dead punishable, as in the case of protecting the interests of the deceased during the period between her death and the settling of her estate.
    Using relevant concepts and readings from this course, explain how this case should be decided, and why. You might consider questions such as: Is it clear what the statute means? Is the legislative intent behind this law clear?


3. Free expression. Lawrence Horn arranged for James Perry to murder Horn’s ex-wife and disabled son, and the son’s nurse, so that Horn could collect the son’s trust money. One aspect of the case raised unusual freedom-of-expression claims concerning what terrorism experts call “mayhem manuals”—books and magazine available from small, independent publishers (not to mention online) with “how to” instructions on making bombs, silencers, sniper weapons, and other devices. In Horn’s apartment police discovered a 130-page mail-order guidebook for murder: Hit Man: A Technical Manual for Independent Contractors. In the book, the pseudonymous author announces that “the professional hit man fills a need in society and is, at times, the only alternative for personal justice.” The killer need feel “no twinge of guilt,” because “the hit man is merely the executioner, an enforcer who carries out the sentence.” Prosecutors found that Horn and Perry had followed twenty-two of the book’s steps in committing their crime. The killers used an AR-7 rifle, recommended by Hit Man because it “breaks down easily.” The book advises that the killer fire “at least three shots to ensure quick and easy death. Aim for the head,” the book suggests, “preferably the eye sockets. Close kills enable you to determine right away whether you have successfully fulfilled your part in the contract.” The victims died from shots to the face. The victims’ family sued the publisher of Hit Man, Paladin Press. Paladin argued that it could not be held liable for abetting the murders because it had no knowledge of Horn’s intentions, and also claimed that mystery writers and other law-abiding citizens buy Hit Man. A number of major publishers sided with Paladin for fear that a decision against them would be a vote for censorship.
    Using relevant concepts and readings from this course, decide: should the First Amendment shield the publication of such material? How come? To answer these questions, you might consider others, such as: Did Paladin Press, by virtue of publishing Hit Man, incite criminal acts? Do such publications pose a “clear and present danger” of imminent lawlessness? Certainly harm was done here (a triple murder), but to what extent can that harm be attributed to Paladin Press?


4. That Billy Budd guy. The court martial of Billy Budd presents the ship’s officers with a daunting array of considerations: their duties as officers, the oaths they have taken, their feelings of humanity and their sense of justice, the risks they face if they hang Billy, the risks they face if they don’t. These—surprise, surprise—echo some of the themes arising in this course: utilitarian vs. deontological considerations, excuses, the role of intent, the justification of punishment, the purposes of law, and the difficulty of finding satisfactory solutions to significant problems.
    Imagine you are in the First Lieutenant’s position as senior member of the tribunal. You must decide what to do with Billy. It is your duty as senior member to explain your ruling in a report to the Admiralty. Do so, referring to relevant concepts and readings from the course, and being careful to take into account as many of the case’s circumstances as you can. Since there will be objections to whatever you decide, be sure to address the most significant objections. 


4. Presentations. Choose one student presentation besides your group’s.
    First, identify the most important controversy arising in the presentation. Explain why it is the most important. You will need to be clear about your criteria here.
    Then, using relevant concepts and readings from the course, argue for how that controversy ought to be resolved. To do this well, you will need to demonstrate your grasp of the material provided in the presentation and elsewhere in the course, and use it to construct a persuasive argument for whatever conclusion you reach. The same criteria for the rest of the exam apply here: use your answer as an opportunity to show off how much you have learned from course materials (by referring to them where appropriate) and how sensitive you are to the issues.

1. Attempts. Victor is unhappily married to Esmeralda. In fact, Victor so despises his wife that he has on more than one occasion seriously thought of killing her. Victor was raised on a small island in the Caribbean and, as a young boy, was initiated into the black-magic cults of the native peoples. Victor still retains deep beliefs in the power of voodoo magic. One day, when he feels he can stand his wife no longer, Victor retires to his secret workshop, where he has over the years meticulously collected the accoutrements of the black arts. Carefully he prepares a tiny, doll-like replica of the despised Esmeralda. When at last the doll is finished, Victor takes a deep breath and, with nervous fingers and a look of hatred, viciously and repeatedly stabs the doll with “sacred” needles. Exhausted by his deed, Victor collapses. When he awakens, he is overcome with remorse and disgust at what he has done. He promptly leaves his workshop, marches to the local police station, and turns himself in, believing with all sincerity that he has murdered his wife.
     Use relevant concepts and readings from this course to explain why Victor might be guilty of attempted murder, and why he might not. Then explain which view (that he is, or is not guilty) should be preferred, giving the strongest reasons you can for why others should agree with you. (It will be helpful to review the excerpt from the Model Penal Code on p. 490 [4th edition: 405], but in your answer you should not merely try to apply the letter of the law.) 


2. Excuses. Consider the following excerpt from an 1897 criminal case in Canada:

     It appeared from the evidence that the prisoner was a member of a tribe of pagan Indians who believed in the existence of an evil spirit clothed in human flesh, or in human form called a Wendigo which would eat a human being.
     That it was reported that a Wendigo had been seen and it was supposed was in the neighbourhood of their camp desiring to do them harm. That among other precautions to protect themselves, guards and sentries, the prisoner being one, were placed out in pairs armed with firearms (the prisoner having a rifle); that the prisoner saw what appeared to be a tall human being running in the distance, which he supposed was the Wendigo; that he and another Indian gave chase, and after challenging three times and receiving no answer fired and shot the object, when it was discovered to be his own foster father, who died soon afterward.

The defense argued that the defendant lacked the “intention even to harm a human being, much less to kill.” The court rejected the defendant’s argument.
     Was the court correct? Use relevant concepts and readings from this course to explain why it may have been, and why it might not have been. Then, argue for which view is the right one, giving the strongest reasons you can for why others should agree with you.


3. Justification, excuse, or neither? Defendant Haynes, an inmate in a federal prison, suddenly attacked another prisoner by pouring scalding oil on the other inmate’s head as he played dominoes. In defense, Haynes claims that the victim had threatened more than once to sexually assault Haynes. The day before the attack, the victim had thrown Haynes to the ground within sight of a guard, who did nothing. On the day of the attack, the victim had threatened to “finish what he started.” The court held that Haynes could not argue self-defense in the form of a pre-emptive strike: “Under the law of the jungle, a good offense may be the best defense,” the court wrote, “but although prisons are nasty places, they are not jungles.” Because Haynes “was not faced with an imminent use of force” by the victim, he should have used other, lawful alternatives.
     Was the court correct to rule Haynes could not claim it was self-defense? Use relevant concepts and readings from this course to explain why it may have been, and why it might not have been. Then, argue for which view is the right one, giving the strongest reasons you can for why others should agree with you. Should Haynes’ act be considered justified? Excused? Neither? How come?


4. Possible punishments. The Women’s Coalition of Pasadena, California proposed in 1997 to seek legislation requiring that permanent, lifetime cuffs be affixed to sexual offenders after they leave prison. The cuffs would contain an anti-tampering device to prevent removal. Children could then be taught to recognize the bracelet and avoid those who wear it.
     Use relevant concepts and readings from this course to argue for whether or not such a measure would be justified, assuming that the cuffs could be shown to reduce the rate of child molestation. (Resist the temptation to think there is an easy answer here. Whether you consider this from a retributive, utilitarian, mixed, etc. point of view, be sure to apply your chosen principle(s) carefully to the situation, taking into account what would likely happen as a result of following this proposal.)
