1. Disclaimer
 
As the title says, this guide is about writing papers for me. Other instructors--including other philosophy instructors--may have different expectations, and they are entitled to grade your work based on their expectations. If you are writing a paper for someone besides me, confer with that instructor before, or instead of, relying on this guide.


2. Two kinds of papers

Most papers I will assign will be either expository or argumentative.

In an expository paper, your job is to explain something. For example, I may give you a piece of text and ask you to explain what it means. I might also ask you to critique the text some, but the main task is to explain it, making its meaning as clear as possible.

In an argumentative paper, your main task is to critique something. For example, you may be asked to critically evaluate a philosophical argument from the book or from class. Your main job then is to present your own case for why that argument is good (that is, sound or plausible) if you think it is good, or why it is bad (that is, unsound or implausible) if you think it is bad. In the course of your critique you may need to explain (i.e., do some exposition of) what you are criticizing, but the exposition is secondary to the critique. So, if the assignment is to write an argumentative paper but your paper is mostly exposition, it is probably not a good paper.
 
 
3. Parts of the paper
 
A good undergraduate philosophical paper will usually have at least two main parts. It will have an introduction and a body; often but not necessarily always, it will also have a conclusion.

This three-part structure can be summarized as, “Tell them what you’re going to say; then say it; then tell them what you just said.” This may seem ridiculous: why say it three times? The reason is that each part of the paper serves a different purpose, requiring you to communicate with readers in a different way. Doing this is actually a good way to convey scholarly ideas in writing. It works.


4. The introduction
 
Let me tell you what an introduction's purpose is, give some tips on how to get started, and give an example of writing and revising one.

A. The introduction's purpose.
The purpose of your introduction is to allow a reader to quickly determine whether your paper is of interest. In academia, scholars come across hundreds of papers, far more than we have time to read. So it is important to enable readers to discern what your paper is about without having to read all of it.

Your introduction should let readers know two things: what your topic is and what you are going to say about it. Therefore, it should have two main parts: a brief discussion of your topic, and your thesis statement. That is all. 

Your topic will generally be something you want to explain or clarify (if your paper is expository), or some philosophical problem or issue you will try to solve (if your paper is argumentative). You don’t want to go on and on about it; that would defeat the introduction’s purpose. This part of your introduction should be as brief and to the point as you can make it, especially in a short paper.

Your thesis statement is a summary of what you are going to say about your topic. It tells your reader what the main point of your paper is: if your paper is expository, it states what you think is the most important thing to know about what you will be explaining; or, in an argumentative paper, it states your solution to the problem or your position on the issue. Whether your paper is argumentative or expository, your thesis statement also gives a brief summary of how you intend to support your thesis: the main points you are going to make to argue for it. Think of this summary as giving your reader a road map to the rest of your paper. In keeping with the purpose of an introduction, keep your introduction succinct. (If you don’t know what “succinct” means, look it up now.)

Since the introduction is supposed to tell a reader what you're going to do and how you will do it, it is a sort of promise to your reader. You must keep this promise. If the rest of your paper does not match what your introduction says, you must revise the introduction, or the rest of the paper, or both, until they match.

B. Tips on getting started.
Suppose the assignment is to write a 3-page/~1100-word paper evaluating Descartes’ first argument for God's existence in his Meditations on First Philosophy.

This prompt asks you to write an argumentative paper about a particular argument in the text. This suggests certain questions you need to ask yourself. You should ask these sorts of questions with regard to any argumentative paper assignment:

1. Do you know which argument the prompt is referring to? Can you find exactly where it is in the book? If not, ask!
2. Do you understand that argument? If not, re-read it. Try to figure it out using the text, and your notes if it was discussed in class. If you are still mystified, seek help! Set up an appointment to see me, and soon.
3. Once you understand the argument well, do you agree with it? In other words, does Descartes come to the correct conclusion, and do his reasons provide solid support for that conclusion (i.e., is his argument sound)?
4. Your answer should be be either "Yes" or "No." If it is "Maybe," consider the argument more carefully, since any argument either is sound or is not. If you can't decide, come see me. Regardless of what your answer is, ask: Why? Imagine your friend disagrees with you. What would you say to convince her of your answer?

Your answer to #4 is the starting point of your paper. In the process of writing your paper you will have to think carefully about it, and this will most likely lead you to change your mind a little or a lot about it. (At the very least you will have to refine how you present your ideas.) That is fine. It's called "learning," and it is supposed to happen. When it does, you will need to revise your paper accordingly. But that's later; this is a place to start.

You may find it helpful to start by writing out your answers to #4 in some detail. When you do this, you are sketching the body of the paper. That is fine: it's often good to write the body first and make sure it is clear and logical, and then write the introduction and conclusion afterwards.

C. Here is an example of how you might write an introduction for this paper.

Let's suppose I believe Descartes' argument is bad. Here is a first draft of an introduction:

Like many of us, Descartes is dissatisfied with the quality of knowledge he had learned previously. He is filled with doubts. But rather than living as a skeptic, he wants to find a firm and lasting foundation for knowledge (Descartes 147). He begins by finding a few powerful reasons to doubt all his previous beliefs. He hopes that, by overcoming all of these doubts, he will establish such a foundation (145, 150). Among those doubts is what later scholars have dubbed “defective nature doubt”: the possibility that God—an all-powerful, all-knowing being—is deceiving him or, alternatively, that there is no God and he, Descartes, is the product of mere chance and thus is naturally prone to error (149). However unlikely these possibilities may be, even the slightest possibility of them suffices to cast doubt on all of his beliefs: for any belief he has, it may be that God is tricking him into believing it even though it is false, or perhaps he believes it only because he is naturally defective. To dispel defective nature doubt, then, Descartes decides he must establish both that God exists and that God is not a deceiver (154-5). For if God exists, then Descartes cannot be the product of mere chance; and if God is no deceiver, then God would not trick him. Descartes has several arguments for God’s existence in the Meditations; in this paper I will focus on the first argument in the Third Meditation (156-8). I will argue that it is unsound.

Okay: using what I said above about what an introduction should do, can you tell me what’s good and what’s bad about this one without peeking at what I say about it? Try.

Here is what’s good about it. Most importantly, it contains the necessary parts of an introduction: a discussion of the topic (the whole paragraph except for the last sentence) and a thesis statement (the last sentence). Also, the thesis statement identifies the main thing I will do in the paper. Moreover, the discussion of the topic is accurate, reflecting a good understanding of relevant readings (in this case Descartes' Synopsis and his first three Meditations). And although it is not vital to do so, this introduction gives context, showing how the particular topic of this paper (Descartes' argument for God) is related to Descartes' general project in the Meditations: it explains that his argument for God's existence is a step towards establishing a firm foundation for all knowledge. That's not a bad thing to do.

So the first draft is not horrible. But it’s not very good, either. What needs improvement? Although the discussion of the topic is thorough, the thesis statement does not do enough. It articulates the main point I will make in the paper—that Descartes’ argument is unsound—but doesn’t say anything about how I will support this point; there is no road map. Also, the first draft is too long for such a short paper. Wouldn’t it be better to (a) provide a more succinct discussion of the topic and (b) give a more detailed thesis? Sure, even though it may mean sacrificing some of the other nice things about the first draft.

Here is my second draft:

Descartes finds powerful reasons to doubt his beliefs. He hopes that, by overcoming all of these doubts, he will establish a firm foundation for knowledge (Descartes 145, 150). The most comprehensive of these doubts is what later scholars have dubbed “defective nature doubt.” This doubt has two parts: on one hand, (a) if God exists and is able to do anything, then it is possible for God to trick Descartes into believing something is true when it is not. On the other hand, (b) if God does not exist, then Descartes’ creator was imperfect and perhaps Descartes is then naturally prone to be mistaken (149). However unlikely these may be, even the slightest possibility of them suffices to cast doubt on all of his beliefs. To dispel defective nature doubt, then, Descartes decides he must establish both that God exists and that God is not a deceiver (154-5). For if God exists, then Descartes’ creator is not imperfect; and if God is no deceiver, then God would not trick him. Descartes gives several arguments for God’s existence in the Meditations. In this paper I will focus on the first argument in the Third Meditation (156-8). I will argue that it is unsound. After a brief exposition of Descartes’ argument, I will show that we have reason to doubt two of its premises and that the argument is invalid because it is circular.

Overall this is a bit shorter, the discussion of the topic is more concise, and the thesis is more detailed. This draft, then, is a better introduction because it does a better job at what an intro needs to do.

But there is still room for improvement. It is still longer than it needs to be. That is because it discusses material that is not actually relevant to the assignment. Notice that the assignment is to critique Descartes’ argument that God exists. The second draft explains not only why Descartes needs to show that God exists, but also why he needs to show that God is no deceiver. The assignment does not call for that, so we can cut some of that out, focusing more on the issue of God’s existence.

Here is my third draft:

Descartes hopes that by overcoming all reasons for doubt he will establish a firm foundation for knowledge (Descartes 145-150). His most comprehensive doubt, which has come to be called “defective nature doubt,” has two parts: on one hand, (a) if God exists and is able to do anything, then it is possible for God to trick Descartes into believing something is true when it is not. On the other hand, (b) if God does not exist, then Descartes’ creator was imperfect and perhaps Descartes is then naturally prone to be mistaken (149). To overcome defective nature doubt, then, Descartes needs two arguments: that God exists (to dispel b) and that God is no deceiver (to dispel a)(154-5). In this paper I will criticize the first of his arguments that God exists, arguing that his first cosmological argument in M3 (156-8) is unsound. After a brief exposition of Descartes’ argument, I will show that we have reason to doubt two of its premises and that the argument is invalid because it is circular.

This is fine, though it could be still shorter; we could take out some more of the background material. Still, it does the job of an introduction and takes up no more than half a page of our three-page paper.

There are other ways to write a succinct introduction for this paper. For example, if I am short of space for the rest of the paper I might cut most of the background information out and focus even more narrowly on the topic given in the prompt, like this:

In the Third Meditation, Descartes argues that God must exist because there is no other way to explain why he, Descartes, has the idea of God in his mind (Descartes 156-8). In this paper I will show that this argument is unsound. After a brief exposition of it, I will show that we have reason to doubt two of its premises and that the argument is invalid because it is circular.

This is fine. It is also about as brief as you can get and still do what an introduction needs to do.

 
 
5. The body

Let me tell you the purpose of the body, give you some tips on how you might write it, and sketch some examples.

A. The body's purpose.
Your introduction promised your reader that you would do certain things. The body's purpose is to do those things. If your paper is expository, this is where you show your reader how to understand some difficult text or idea(s). If your paper is argumentative, this is where you persuade your reader that you are correct. Obviously it should be by far the longest part of your paper.

 B. Tips on writing it.
Since there are many, many different ways of writing a good expository or argumentative paper, I cannot cover them all here. Instead I will give you some ideas about how to do your task well.

Tips for expository papers, or for whatever exposition you need to do in an argumentative paper. Let's suppose I gave you a piece of text and asked you to explain its meaning.
  • First, make sure you understand the assigned text. You cannot explain what you do not know. Get help if you need it, and soon.
  • Identify which parts of the text are hardest to understand. You will need to devote extra effort to them.
  • Make sure that, in your exposition, you define any technical terms used in the text.
  • If there is some background knowledge needed to understand the text, provide it.
  • Try restating the text in different words--simpler words. Do this several times, discovering different ways to restate it.
  • Think of ways to illustrate what the text says. Can you give a real-life example? Can you make an analogy? Can you compare what the text says to something that will be familiar to your reader?
  • If the text is open to multiple interpretations, consider saying so. You might mention some of them, and then briefly argue for which interpretation you think is best. Consider which interpretation best fits not only the given text but also everything else the author says.
  • You might find it helpful to explain things in a different order than the author did.
Tips for argumentative papers.
  • First, since the paper is argumentative, you should know some things about logic and arguments. At least read this.
  • Second, follow the steps given above on how to get started.
    • In following those steps (especially 3 and 4), review all the information you have: the text, your notes, any class discussion of the argument you will critique, etc. Carefully think about the argument and everything that was said about it. What things were said in support of it? What criticisms were offered? What considerations for and against it can you think of? If this is a research paper, do your research: what have others said about this argument?
    • Based on all of this evidence, decide what evaluation the argument most deserves.
    • Be aware that I do not care what position you argue for; it is the quality of your argument that matters. You get no more credit for agreeing with me than for disagreeing. So follow the best evidence. Period.
  • For an argument to be good, its premises must be true, and they must support the conclusion at least well enough to make it plausible (if the argument is inductive) or to guarantee that the conclusion is true (if the argument is deductive). This suggests several possible strategies you might use to criticize it. What follows is just some ideas, not an exhaustive list:
    • Show that the argument is bad because some key premise (or more than one) is false, or at least doubtful. 
      • Note: merely showing that a premise is false or doubtful is not enough for a good paper. You need to explain persuasively why its falsity or doubtfulness gives good reason to be significantly less confident in the conclusion.
    • Show that even if the premises are true we have reason to doubt the conclusion. There are many ways to do this.
      • You might give a counterargument--that is, construct an argument using similar logic but which reaches a conclusion we know is false or at least dubious, and use that to argue that the argument in the text may be similarly unreliable.
      • You might argue that the conclusion is in fact false, and hence that there could be no valid argument for it. That may be hard; instead you might try arguing that the conclusion is likely to be false, and hence that we'd better not believe it.
      • If the argument is deductive you might find a way to show that even if the premises are all true the conclusion might not be true.
      • Etc. etc.
    • Of course, even though most arguments have some flaws, you don't have to devote yourself to saying an argument is bad. You could show that an argument is, in fact, good. Often what this involves is showing that some criticism(s) of the argument do not actually work: that the argument is better than some critic appreciates. (Note that it needs to be a good critic. Defending an argument against a stupid criticism won't get you much credit.)
      • In class, or in an article you find in your research, someone might argue that a premise is false and that this makes the argument's conclusion much less plausible. You might disagree, by saying either that the premise is not false (or is not nearly as doubtful as the critic thinks), and/or that even if it is false (or doubtful) that doesn't make the conclusion so doubtful. 
      • Again, some critic might have argued that the argument is invalid. You might show that the critic fails to show this; perhaps the critic has misunderstood the argument. Or you might grant that the critic has a point, but show that the argument can be rescued in some way. 
In a longer paper you might combine a number of these strategies. For example, you might (a) argue that some key premise in an author's argument is false and that this badly damages the argument, but that nevertheless (b) there are good independent reasons for believing the conclusion is true. The longer your paper is, the more important it is that you anticipate objections to your arguments and deal with those objections. In a very short paper (2-3 pages) you may have no space for this, but in a longer paper it is good to show you have reflected critically on your own arguments.

These are general strategies; it is up to you to decide how to implement them (or whatever strategy you employ). Beyond that, I have little specific advice for the body of your paper except this "commandment" from Robert N. Johnson:

4.   THOU SHALT PLAN. At some point in the process of writing, list the points you want to make in favor of your thesis. Each paragraph should make one of these points. At the end of your paper, you should have a collection of paragraphs making a set of points that, taken together, support your thesis.

In other words, develop an outline of your paper, consisting of the main points you will make in support of your thesis. Do this even for a short paper. The outline should make an argument for your thesis: a series of premises, in a logical order, which should lead a rational reader to believe your thesis is true. Then, for each premise, write a paragraph explaining and arguing for it. That series of paragraphs then makes the body of your paper. (And those premises then make a thorough thesis statement for your introduction.)

C. Examples.
I will not supply sample papers here, but rather sketch ideas that show how you might apply the strategies listed above.

Let's suppose I gave you the assignment discussed above:  write a paper evaluating Descartes’ first argument for God's existence in his Meditations on First Philosophy. Your task, then, is to argue either that his argument is good (that is, sound, plausible) or bad (unsound, implausible).

For the examples below, it will be helpful for you to know that Descartes essentially argues as follows:
a. He has an idea of God, that is, an idea portraying an infinite, perfect being.
b. So this idea is, in a way, infinite.
c. Because this idea is infinite, it could not have been created by a finite being such as Descartes himself.
d. So, only an infinite being--namely, God--could have created this idea.
e. Therefore, God must exist.
There are more details in the argument that I would expect you to know if you were writing a paper about it, but this summary will do for our purposes here.

To show the argument is bad, you might...
  • Cast doubt on a premise. A sample thesis might be, "I will argue that Descartes' argument fails because a finite human mind can, in fact, construct an infinite idea of God." The thesis here is that premise (c) above is false. This is a significant premise, because without it Descartes cannot establish (d) or (e) and his argument fails. You might, alternatively, argue that he doesn't really have an idea of God, or that his idea cannot portray an infinite being--effectively denying premise (a). Consider all the premises of the argument and ask yourself whether they are true.
  • Show that even if the premises are true we have reason to doubt the conclusion...
    • By counterargument. For example, you might try to show that "If we follow the logic of Descartes' argument we can "prove" the existence of any number of beings, even those we are confident do not actually exist. This shows that Descartes' reasoning is faulty.
    • By showing the conclusion is false: "The notion of a supremely perfect being, such as Descartes' God, is logically incoherent and therefore cannot exist. So Descartes' argument for this God cannot be sound."
    • By showing the conclusion is, if not false, at least doubtful: "Although it is logically possible that God exists as Descartes describes, the amount of evil, suffering, and defect in the world make it hard to believe."  Or: "Descartes argues that God must exist because there is no other way to explain why his idea of God is infinite. I will show, using Descartes' own premises, that even if he did not create it, such an idea need not have been created by God." This would show that Descartes' inference from (c) to (d) is invalid. 
    • Etc. etc. Be aware that not all strategies will work against a given argument. So think about different ways you might critcize an argument, and settle on the best one: the one you believe makes the most persuasive case about the argument. Also, be open to the idea that you have chosen the wrong side. When you write for me, the "right" side is the one you can make the best case for. If you find yourself having trouble showing that an argument is unsound, you might well decide that instead of criticizing it you should defend it.
To show the argument is good, you might...
    • Consider an objection to some premise and show that the objection fails. For example, "Against Descartes it might be objected that he simply made up his idea of God or was taught it in church and that, therefore, we need not suppose God made it. However, I will show that nothing short of an infinite being could have created this idea."
    • Take some attempt to show the argument is invalid, and argue that the attempt fails. For example, "It has been objected that if Descartes' argument is valid, we could 'prove' that unicorns or other mythical creatures exist. However, I will show that this is not the case."
    • Again, think about different strategies and choose the best one(s) to use in your paper, and remain open to the possibility that you have chosen the wrong side. 


 
6. The conclusion

The purpose of the conclusion is to briefly summarize what you have said. The body of the paper, especially in a long paper, may be so complicated that it is hard for a reader to keep track of everything and identify what the main point(s) were. Now, you should revise and re-revise the body to make it as clear as possible. But even when you have done all you can it may still be challenging to keep track of what you have said. The conclusion allows you to remedy that problem, reminding the reader of your main point(s).

In addition, in a long paper the conclusion also may be used to suggest some further implications of what you have said, things which may fall outside the topic of your paper but are interesting to mention.

In a short paper, the conclusion must be brief: no more than a few sentences. It may be omitted in a very short paper--say, 2-3 pages--because there is no need to recap what you have said; the paper is short enough for readers to retain it already. A conclusion may also be omitted from a short paper if you really need the available space for the body. But you should only do this if you have done all you can to make the body clear yet succinct.


7. Formatting and style
 
A. Format. All papers turned in to me must...
  • Be typed in 12-point Times New Roman or similar font.
  • Have identifying information at the beginning: your name, the course number and semester.
  • Have an accurate word count of the main text of the paper (not counting notes, bibliography, etc.) just under the identifying information.
  • Have the main text double spaced. Other material--your identifying information and word count, block quotes, footnotes, bibliography, etc., if any--should be single spaced.
  • Have margins set to 1". Check to make sure yours are set at 1" and not 1.25" etc.
  • Have numbered pages: bottom center or top right.
  • Be free of mechanical errors--that is, errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, and so on. Your computer's grammar- and spell-checking features are not adequate. You will have to proofread your paper yourself.
  • When papers are submitted electronically:
    • They should be in Microsoft Word (.doc or .docx) format. If you cannot put it in MS Word format you may use Portable Document Format (.pdf).
    • Name the file this way: CourseNumber_AssignmentNumber_YourLastNameYourFirstName.doc (or .docx etc.). For example: Phil101_Paper1_AndersonJeremy.doc.
  • When citing assigned readings, give the author's name and the page number from the assigned text--for example (Descartes 145). When you cite the same text multiple times in the same paragraph and it is clear that you are doing so, you may drop the author's name from citations after the first. To see what this looks like, see the sample introductions above.
  • When using sources outside of assigned readings, cite them using MLA, APA, Turabian or Chicago format. (See the Academic Integrity section below for important notes about this.)
If any of these requirements is a problem for you, discuss it with me well before the paper deadline.

B. Style.
When you write for me, the key to good style is simplicity. Philosophical ideas are hard already. Don't make it harder to grasp your ideas than it has to be. Make your language as clear, plain, and simple as possible. Identify the point and stick to it. No fluff. No filler. No drama. No bullshit. Avoid doing the catchy, flashy sorts of things you may have been advised to do in English classes back in the day. For example:
  • Do not embellish your introduction. Don't say how long your topic has been around (“Since the beginning of time, humankind has wondered...” or any such). Don't begin with a famous quote or give a dictionary definition. Don't open with a heartstring-tugging or exciting anecdote to catch your reader’s attention. All of these waste space and your reader's attention span.
  • However tempted you may find yourself, no matter the heights of brilliant poetic eloquence you are capable of, do not employ excessively lengthy words, nor a gratuitously great number of them, in hopes of conveying to your reader the impression that you are highly intelligent and witty, or for any other reason. Translation: Do not use big words to sound smart, nor use more than you need. Fewer, simpler words, used well, work better. (See what I mean?)
And do not turn in your first draft, no matter how great a writer you are. Complete the paper and then let it sit for at least a few hours. Then come back and revise the whole thing to make it even clearer and more complete. Then repeat that process. Of course this means it is a bad idea to start the paper the day before it is due.

For some reason, philosophical papers generally use the present tense even when talking about people from long ago. We'll say, "Descartes says..." or "Chuang-tzu argues that..." even though those folks are long dead. This is not required; but whatever tense you use, be consistent about it.


8. Using sources

The first and most important rule about using sources is to cite them whenever you use them. For example, look at the sample introductions above: every time I made a claim about what a source said, I cited it.

If you use any sources outside of assigned texts, you must be judicious about them. This means three things:
  1. First, don't limit yourself to just one kind of source.
    • For example, some students simply do a search on JSTOR and use any relevant-sounding articles near the top of the search results. That is not the worst way to go, but it is lazy and is unlikely to find you the really good stuff. You should also search the Library catalog for relevant books, search other article databases (EBSCO, the Philosopher's Index), consult a reference librarian, etc.
  2. Second, be careful about the quality of your sources.
    • Peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields are best. This does not mean they are necessarily correct, but they are generally the best sort of material you can get; they are often the most thoughtful, sophisticated, and well informed.
    • Among the worst are those of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by people with unknown qualifications. Reliance on these can seriously hurt your paper.
      • The internet is rife with this stuff. Many web sites fail to tell you who wrote them, so you cannot assess the authors' qualifications. Many others present information as if it were objective or scientific when actually it is biased towards a particular point of view. Others may present information that is plagiarized, perhaps from multiple sources of unknown quality. Note that Wikipedia articles often contain some or all of these faults! So do blogs, sites sponsored by advocacy groups (e.g., a site on stem cell research sponsored by a Catholic organization), think tanks, and so on. This does not mean there is no good information to be had from these sources, but you must be extra cautious.
    • In the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, etc. These are nice places to start your research--even Wikipedia can be a good place to start, especially when it gives you leads to other and better sources--but they had better not be where it ends. In other words, encyclopedia-type sources may be used to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources in the paper you turn in will count against it.
  3. Third, you may cite lecture/discussion only for material that is not in any text. For anything that is in a text (i.e., any time you make a claim about what an author says), you must cite the text.
When in doubt about the appropriateness of a given source, consult with me.

9. Academic integrity--without which you are doomed

Academic integrity has a number of different forms: not disrupting class, not cheating on tests, etc. But for writing papers, the key to academic integrity is this: never, ever make someone else's work appear as if it were yours, even by accident.

The first and most important rule for maintaining academic integrity, then, is the same as the first rule for using sources: cite them whenever you use them. Specifically: if you use an idea you got from somewhere besides yourself, cite it. If you express that idea using someone else's words or a close paraphrase, put it in quotation marks and cite it.

DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to all my paper assignments (and, indeed, to the entire course). For any claim that is not common knowledge or your own idea, I require that you cite any and all of the sources you rely on. This includes ideas gleaned from assigned texts, reading responses, class discussion, your roommate, somebody's blog, the news, your mom, everything, whether you quote it, paraphrase it, or express the idea using your own words. Every violation of this policy will hurt the paper's grade. What's worse, any violation of it could constitute plagiarism and be prosecuted. The penalty for plagiarism is worse than turning in nothing at all, and could include failing the course, suspension, or expulsion from the University.

You may cite your sources using MLA, APA, Turabian, or Chicago format. You must give the information I need to (a) see where and how you used someone else’s ideas or information and (b) track down the source if I want to. I often do check sources. Specifically, your list of sources (whether an alphabetized bibliography or in footnotes or endnotes) must include:
  • Full bibliographic information for any print source, including assigned texts.
  • Full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.whitehouse.gov" or "http://www.jstor.org" but the full URL, so that I can paste it into my browser and find the exact material you relied upon. For articles in the library databases, use the "persistent" or "permanent" URL.
  • If citing a journal article accessed electronically, give both the bibliographic data plus the full URL.
For more information about citation, quoting, paraphrasing, etc. consult Charles Lipson's book Doing Honest Work in College, which you received when you came to DePauw. Even more information about plagiarism and how to avoid it is available through my Research Links page. If you want a format suggestion, I happen to like the "author-date" system of the Chicago Style (or the very similar "parenthetical citations/reference list" Turabian style), with one exception: the Chicago manual says that some sources may be omitted from your reference list or bibliography, but in papers for me you must include EVERY source in your list of references.


10. Paper rubric

In this guide, I have given a number of recommendations and requirements. It seems only fair, then, that your papers be graded based on how well you follow the recommendations and meet the requirements. To summarize them (and thereby provide a conclusion to this guide), here are the questions you should ask yourself about your work before you turn it in. It is also the list of questions I will ask when I read it, and the answers will determine the paper's grade.

About the paper as a whole:
How well does it demonstrate that you understand what you're talking about?
How well does it demonstrate that you've been thinking about the material?
How well do you clarify the material (when called for) and argue for your side (when called for?)
About the parts of the paper:
Does the paper have at least an introduction and a body? If it is more than three pages, does it also have a conclusion?
Does the introduction do its job? I.e., does it succinctly provide a discussion of your topic and a thesis statement, including a road map to your paper?
About the body of the paper:
How well organized is it?
How well does it explain the material where necessary?
How well does it make a case for your side, when called for?
Are your arguments cogent? Logical? Fair? Clear?
Does it match what the introduction promised?
About the formatting and style:
Is the formatting correct?
Is the writing clear, and not overcomplicated?

About academic integrity:
If sources are used, are they all properly cited?
Do the sources contain all the information I need to track them down?
