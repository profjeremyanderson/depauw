For this course you'll be doing a presentation (either solo or in pairs) based on independent research on a course-related topic that interests you. Here's how to do it.

Here's what the syllabus says:
Presentations will entail you forming small groups of 2 or 3 or perhaps working alone. ...Each individual/group will take responsibility for researching a topic within the philosophy of law and presenting it, or some portion of it, to the class. Your presentations are part of the course (their contents will be fair game for the final exam, for example), so they must be done well. I will suggest presentation topics, or you may choose your own with my approval. Using audio/visual aids is recommended, but it is your responsibility to bring or reserve any equipment you may need if it is not already in the room.

Here's the basic idea:
During the course I help guide you through a bunch of readings which discuss topics in war and terrorism: realism, pacifism, just-war theory, Iraq, humanitarian intervention, shooting kids, double effect, blah, blah, blah. None of these has been exhausted in class, however, and you might want to understand one of those issues in more depth. Or you might want to explore something very different. You will need to pick something of interest to you, find a fellow student or two with similar interests and form your own groups of 2-3 people (though I might accept other sizes), get me to approve your topic (which is easy), research your topic, and present what you have learned to the class.

If you don't already have a topic in mind, there are many places to seek inspiration. You may draw from our texts (look at what we're reading and what we're skipping), your or others' reading responses, class discussion, current events, etc. Also, look at the topics listed at the bottom of the Papers page.

The class will need to do some prep work for presentations as well. Each group of presenters needs to provide at least one reading relevant to the presentation topic, which the rest of the class will study prior to the presentation. This preparatory reading could be some news article(s), a scholarly article, book chapter, or something the presenters create. It should help us all to become familiar with the topic and some of its key issues, and thereby enhance discussion. Since these will be reading assignments, responses to them will be required--and count--just like all other reading responses in the course. (And yes, when you're presenting you're still required to submit reading responses.)
The deadline for choosing a topic and forming a group is 6pm Monday after Spring Break (April 1): you will need to e-mail me to let me know who is in your group, and give me your proposed topic and at least one alternate topic in case your first choice is already taken.

By Wednesday April 3, at the beginning of class, we will assign dates for you to present. The sooner you clear your topic and group with me, the better the choice of dates you'll have (not to mention having more time to prep), so I encourage you to form groups and notify me of topics sooner. Once a date is set, it will be subject to the late policy stated in the course syllabus. Use the class open forum to find partners and air out ideas.

Sources
This is a research project. I will not dictate a number of sources you must have. So, for example, if you read a couple of books that could count as well as reading a large number of articles. But as with all research-based work, the more, and more credible, sources you have the better. Larger groups will have to use more—otherwise, there won’t be enough work involved for each person to receive much credit. Follow the advice on the Papers page to be judicious about your sources.

How to begin? Here are suggestions. First, see me. Also, identify the names of the people and/or the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic.

You will also find military/war-related links on my philosophy links page.


Format
Expect to take at least 20 minutes, though presentations usually go longer. Make sure you include something that involves the class in your presentation. Visual aids (writing on the board, creating posters, doing PowerPoint presentations, etc.) are encouraged. Handouts which summarize the main points or arguments are strongly encouraged. As it says on the syllabus, if you need any equipment that isn't already in the room it is your responsibility to bring it. (Media Services has equipment available for checkout.)

Note to Macintosh users: if you prepare a PowerPoint using your Mac it might not look right when played using a PC (such as the computers in our tech classrooms). So you should either prepare PowerPoints using a PC or make sure you have the video adapter necessary to hook up your Mac laptop to a PC video cable.

You might...
  • Have each person present a different perspective on the issue, or
  • Stage a debate, or
  • Lecture the way I sometimes do, or
  • Make a film, or include clips from movies on your topic, or
  • Do something completely different.
Each group will be expected to
  • Provide the preparatory reading to me and the rest of the class by the previous class meeting at the very latest; two meetings prior is much preferred. (One good way is to supply me with either links to readings or PDF scans which I can post. I can help with this if given advance notice.)
  • Demonstrate strong understanding of the topic. (I expect this of every member of every group, not just of groups as a whole.)
  • Present more than one side of an issue.
  • Be fair to all sides. By "fair" I do not mean making them all look equally good; I mean not presenting any view or argument as less (or more) good than it really is, and demonstrating an appreciation of the strengths as well as the weaknesses even of views you oppose. Philosophy is, after all, a search for truth, not merely an attempt to win a point. This doesn't mean you can't take a side; it just means you can't defend your side by misrepresenting anything.
  • Do something to involve the class in some way: some exercise, activity, discussion questions, etc.

Documentation & Academic Integrity
READ THIS CAREFULLY:

You will be graded individually for your work; there are no group grades. You (each individual presenter) must submit written evidence of what you contributed and what sources you relied on, including:
1. A single e-mail or Word document attachment (like this sample here--or if you want more explanation, see this one with annotations) containing:
  • A brief statement of the work you individually did, whatever it was.
  • A bibliography of all of the sources you individually consulted, divided into two parts: those sources used in the presentation and those consulted but not used. Use a standard bibliographic format, and give the URL for any online sources. (When available, use the "stable URL or "persistent URL" or "Permalink.")
    • A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page.
  • This evidence is due via e-mail by 6:00pm the calendar day before the presentation is given.
2. Any audio/visual aids you use (PowerPoint, handouts, etc.) with any sources used in them documented in them. Endnotes are one good way to document sources in PowerPoints: put a bibliography on the last slide(s) and number each item on it. Then, for each item in the PowerPoint that comes from some source--image, claim, quote, whatever--put the number of the source by it.
  • This is due by 6pm on the day of your presentation. (You can turn it in earlier, but presenters are sometimes revising their slides right up to the presentation.)
Without your documentation, the assignment is incomplete (and hence violates the late policy) and you could be prosecuted for plagiarism.

DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to this assignment. The documentation you submit must include all the sources you relied on in any way whether you quote them or not. The policy also applies to any audio/visual aids you use: for example, PowerPoint presentations must cite (e.g., using endnotes) all sources used for quotes, ideas, graphs, images--everything.

Presenting work done outside this course (for example, work already submitted for credit in this or another course, or done for some extracurricular activity) is not acceptable. Having a portion of your presentation consist of work done elsewhere might be acceptable; consult with me beforehand. Any work done outside this course, if you are allowed to use it at all, must also be documented.

Failing to document any source will automatically harm your grade and may be prosecuted as plagiarism, even if it is accidental.


How to do well at this assignment
 
I will expect to see evidence of substantial, new work by every person in each group. Just downloading a web page and reading it to the class or copying it onto a PowerPoint slide, for example, is not acceptable. You need to show that you've done significantly more with the information than merely acquiring it.
