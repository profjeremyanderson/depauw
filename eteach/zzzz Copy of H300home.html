Course description and goals
We will look at activities that, despite our protestations of dislike for them, we humans engage in rather a lot: war and terrorism. Specifically, we will critically examine (a) realism, i.e., the view that morality is irrelevant to international relations, (b) pacifism, (c) traditional ideas concerning the morality of war and some recent innovations, (d) the nature of terrorism and (e) responses to it. Our goals will be to:
  • Understand and come to a reasoned evaluation of major schools of thought regarding (a)-(e)
  • Enable and encourage you to critically reflect on your position regarding these matters
  • Appreciate the complexity of the issues but nevertheless be able to take reasoned stands on them
  • Understand and intelligently take part in current and ongoing debates regarding the use of force
  • Further develop your skills at research, writing, and speaking
  • Demonstrate the above in graded assignments and classroom participation.

Texts
The following texts are required:

Assignments & grading
    Various terms, ideas, arguments, approaches, etc. will come up in readings, lectures, presentations, and discussion. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate that you understand and have thought about that material. (Ways of demonstrating you’ve thought about it include critically appraising it, connecting it to other ideas presented inside or outside the course, etc.). Where independent research is required, your grade will also depend on the quality of that research and demonstrating that you understand and have thought carefully about that material. The course grade will be derived as follows: 
10%    Early exam (around 4th-5th week)
10%    Reading responses
10%    Two 2-3 page (~740-1110 word) position papers
15%    Individual or small-group presentation
20%    One research paper of at least 10 pages (~3700 words)
15%    Participation, including leading discussion
20%    Comprehensive final exam
    Reading responses: at least once each week, starting with our next meeting, you will need to write a response to at least one of the assigned readings and post it to a discussion board on our Moodle site. In each response you need to do two things:
1. Very briefly summarize the reading--that is, tell me what you take to be the two or three main points the author(s) try to make. A sentence or two will suffice. Then
2. Give your reaction—positive, negative, neutral, tangential, whatever—to the reading(s). Your reaction may be short or long, but should be detailed enough for me to understand why you react to the reading(s) the way you do.
You are encouraged to submit more than the once-per-week minimum. Your responses will be due the night before the class meeting where that reading will be discussed.
     Responses will be scored on a simple scale: Excellent (4 points), Good (3), or Deficient (2); an average of one Good response per week will yield a grade of B- for this portion of the course grade. More and/or better responses will yield a higher grade. Qualities that make a reading response better include: clarity of writing; thoughtfulness (e.g., offering careful criticism of the reading); responding to more than one reading at a time (when more than one is assigned); and relating the assigned material to other assignments, other class work, outside research, etc. Reading responses will be considered separately from your participation. Earning a total of 53 points will get you full credit for this part of the course, as long as you turn in at least one response per week. 53 points can be earned by submitting one Good response per week plus an additional Good response four times during the semester. Alternatively, submitting one Excellent response per week would earn full credit.
    Two position papers, each 2-3 pages long, will be required. Each will ask you to take a position regarding the material we have studied and defend it against some objection(s). Topics will be given to you or you may negotiate a topic with me. Your grade for this portion of the course will be the average of the two individual papers’ grades. More details are available on the Papers section of the course web site.
    Presentations will entail you working alone or forming small groups of 2 or 3 at the most. More details will be posted to the presentations web page, but here’s a quick description. Each individual/group will take responsibility for summarizing and critiquing some contrasting readings related to some topic within the area of this course in front of the class. The presentations are part of the course, so they must be done well and their content will be fair game for the final exam. I will suggest presentation topics, or you may choose your own with my approval. Using audio/visual aids is recommended, but it is your responsibility to bring or reserve any equipment you may need.
    Your research paper will require you to take a stand on some issue related to our course topics and defend it against serious opposition. It must be based on substantial research. Its topic may be related to that of your presentation as long as you’re not seeking credit twice for the same work. You will need to clear your paper topic with me in advance and meet various stringent requirements which will be posted to the papers web page.
    Participation will take two forms: general, and leading class discussion of an assigned text.
  • General participation consists of demonstrating you’re thinking about the course in some way besides the required reading responses. The best form is participating in class discussions, but it could also consist of discussing course-related stuff with me outside of class in person or by e-mail, etc. Mere attendance, looking interested, taking notes, etc. do not count, though I reserve the right to lower grades or fail students for excessive unexcused absences. Participating once per week, by whatever means, will earn you a C for participation. Occasional short informal writing assignments, if given, will also count towards general participation.
  • Leading class discussion will require you to (a) master an assigned reading well enough to identify its main points for the class, (b) create a few discussion questions concerning that reading, and (c) lead class discussion on that assignment for around 20 minutes.

How to succeed in this course
  • Remember you are now in an upper-division seminar. I expect a lot of you here: you will need to participate in most if not all class meetings and demonstrate careful preparation in doing so. I will generally expect not to have to explain the readings to you very much. Rather, it will be mostly your job to figure them out for yourself and be ready to discuss them in class. My expectations for your writing and presentations will also be high. So:
  • READ, AND READ CAREFULLY. Not just once, and not quickly like it’s a newspaper article. Philosophical texts reward careful and repeated reading, and invite critical reflection. Besides, you can expect me to call on you with questions about it.
  • Come to class, and bring the current reading and your notes with you. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. Do not expect me to share my notes.
  • Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take a moment after class to fill them out.
  • PARTICIPATE. Do NOT assume that your question isn’t worth asking or your comment not worth making. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you’re not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.
  • Cell phones, PDA’s, pagers, etc. should have ringers turned OFF.
