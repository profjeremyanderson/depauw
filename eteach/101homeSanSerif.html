 Course goals

The goals of this course are to acquaint you with philosophy’s content and techniques, and sharpen your writing and thinking. Through reading, lecture, discussion, and  writing you’ll get to know some of philosophy’s major divisions, including epistemology, metaphysics, and ethics, and some of the puzzles that philosophers concern themselves with. Is the outside world as it appears to be? Does God exist? Are we free? How should we lead our lives? You will learn about and evaluate major thinkers' answers to such questions. You will also enhance your critical thinking skills so that your beliefs can be better grounded in logic, deepened by an understanding of the issues at stake, and tempered with an appreciation of opposing views.

Participation means demonstrating you are thinking about the course. It is not hard, but mere attendance, looking thoughtful, taking notes, etc. do not count. The best way is to participate in class discussions, but you can also get credit for discussing course-related things with me outside of class in person or by e-mail or posting to the online open forum. You can earn up to one point per class meeting (i.e., three points per normal week). Earning one point per week, by whatever means, will earn you a C (75%) for participation. Participating less often earns a lower grade; more often earns a higher grade. Nineteen points total, with at least one point earned per week, will get you an A+ (100%).

 Reading responses: There are some reading questions with each reading assignment. At least once per week, starting in the first full week of class, respond to at least one (or more!) of them in writing, before class, in the forums I'll set up on Moodle. (If Moodle is down, e-mail it to me by the deadline.)
      Each reading response will be graded credit/no credit. To receive credit, each reading response must:
  1. Be on time. Your response must be time-stamped by 9:30pm the day before the class meeting where that assignment will be discussed. Late responses never receive credit.
  2. Be at least 200 words. I mean, your answer(s) must total at least 200 words. Putting in your name, typing in the question, etc. do not count. Answering more than one reading question for a given assignment counts as one response.
  3. Respond thoughtfully and completely to one or more assigned reading questions, demonstrating you have read the assigned text(s) carefully.
  4. Meet the academic integrity requirements for writing in this course (they're really not hard, but they're important) and have no, or almost no, errors in grammar, spelling, or punctuation.
As with participation, submitting one passing reading response per week will earn a C (75%). If you want to earn an A+ (100%), then submit at least one response each week (passing or not), with a total of nineteen passing responses during the semester.
      Suggestion: if you take too long Moodle can log you out, which could wipe out your work. So try typing your response using a word processor first, then copy and paste your response into the forum before the deadline.

How to succeed in this course.
Read, and read carefully. 

Reading is an act of contemplation, perhaps the only act in which we allow ourselves to merge with the consciousness of another human being. We possess the books we read, animating the waiting stillness of their language, but they possess us also, filling us with thoughts and observations, asking us to make them part of ourselves.... In order for this to work, however, we need a certain type of silence, an ability to filter out the noise.
David L. Ulin, “The Lost Art of Reading,” Los Angeles Times, 8/9/2009

Make time, in a quiet space, to read. Not just once, not quickly like it’s a news article, but deeply. Turn off your TV, phone, radio, MP3. When reading online, close all other windows, set Skype to offline, turn off chats, and release yourself from Facebook etc. Philosophical texts reward focused, careful, and repeated reading. Besides, you can expect me to call on you with questions about it.
     Reading assignments for this course typically consist of a selection written by a philosopher and some introductory material written by the book’s editor, Andrew Bailey. Bailey suggests you read the selection first, and then go back and read the introductory material, and then read the selection again (Preface, p. vii). I recommend following this advice. (The few exceptions are noted on the Reading Assignments page.)

Come to class, on time, prepared. Bring the current reading and your notes with you. Bring your responses to the current reading questions with you. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, at the beginning of the semester, to share notes.

Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take a moment after class to fill them out.

Participate a lot. Some students assume their questions aren't worth asking or their comments not worth making, but often others wanted to say the same thing. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you are not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.

Keep up with the study guide every week. When new items are added to the study guide, sit down with your books and notes and classmates (if you like--studying in groups is helpful) and write out the clearest, most complete and thoughtful answers you can. If you have trouble formulating answers, come see me for help. Study your answers regularly to prepare for class discussion and for exams. This will pay off.

Give yourself time to write good papers, including revising/rewriting them well before the deadline.

Seek help when you need it, or when you think you might need it, or just because. You do not need an appointment to drop in, though we can make one if you like. I do not bite, I promise.

Get enough sleep. If that means you cut back on extracurricular activities, so be it:
Studies have shown that sleep quantity and sleep quality equal or outrank such popular campus concerns as alcohol and drug use in predicting student grades and a student’s chances of graduating.
Jane E. Brody, "An Underappreciated Key to College Success: Sleep," New York Times, 8/13/2018

Remember you are now in a very selective university. That means you're smart, but it also means that the sort of effort that got you A’s in high school might not get you an A here.
