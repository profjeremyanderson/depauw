1. General instructions for all papers in this course
  • Your paper should be primarily argumentative. That is, it must defend a conclusion which other smart people might well disagree with, and this must be your paper's major focus.

  • To take a stand effectively, your paper must be critical but fair to your opposition, and your papers' conclusions should not depend on mere expressions of personal opinion or armchair arguments. It is not enough to explain why you think something is so; you must strive to convince a reasonable reader that any right-thinking person should agree with you.

  • Submit your paper as a Word doc or Rich Text Format (.rtf) file, or share a Google doc. Papers should be double spaced, have one-inch margins and numbered pages, use 12-point Times New Roman or similar font, and be free of mechanical errors (grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc.). Your computer's grammar- and spell-checking features are not adequate; you will have to proofread your paper yourself. Include a word count for the body of the paper (that is, not including your name, end- or footnotes, bibliography, etc.) at the top with your identifying information.
     
  • Papers should be clear and logically organized. I am much more impressed by writers who express ideas clearly using simple language than by those who try to impress readers with big words and long sentences.

  • DePauw's Academic Integrity policy applies to all assignments in the course.

    • Whenever you use ideas that are not your own, cite them. Whenever you use another's words, put them in quotation marks and cite them. It does not matter whether the material came from assigned texts, class discussion, a journal article, a book, a conversation with a friend or parent, a web page, your mom, space aliens, or whatever: if the idea isn’t yours, cite it; if the wording isn't yours, put it in quotes and cite it.

    • Failure to cite as required will automatically lower the assignment's grade, potentially down to failing. It may also be prosecuted as plagiarism per DePauw’s academic integrity policy. If it is prosecuted successfully, the minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all. So it is vital you keep track of whose words and ideas you are using. If you have any sources to cite outside our textbook or class discussion, list them on a “Works Cited” page in addition to citing them in the text.

      • This is not hard to do. As you work on the paper, make a note of what you read and who you talk to about it, keeping track of what ideas and words came from each source. Then, if you decide to use any ideas or words from these notes, cite the sources.

    • Cite assigned texts like this:
      • If there are margin numbers (like with Plato) or paragraph numbers (like with Locke) use those; for example, (Plato, 45a) or (Locke, §123) or (Locke, par. 123).
      • Otherwise, give the page number, like (Aquinas, 461).
      • No bibliographic information is needed for assigned readings.

    • Cite sources outside of assigned texts using a standard format. I happen to like Turabian or Chicago. A quick guide to Turabian format may be found here on the Library web page; click the tab labeled "Turabian (P/RL)" for instructions on how to cite various kinds of sources.

      • Your list of sources should include:
        • Full bibliographic information for any print source aside from assigned texts.
        • Full URL for any Internet source. For example, not just "http://www.whitehouse.gov" but the full URL, so that I can paste it into my browser and find the exact material you used.
        • For a journal article accessed electronically, give both the bibliographic data plus the full URL. Use the "stable" URL.

  • Be judicious about your sources if you are going to do any research beyond assigned texts (as is required for the long research essay and optional for the short essays). This means three things:

    1. First, don't limit yourself to just one kind of source.
      • For example, some students simply do a search on JSTOR and use any relevant-sounding articles near the top of the search results. That is not the worst way to go (the worst is to do that with Google), but it is lazy and, worse, is unlikely to find you the really good stuff. You should also search the Library catalog for relevant books, search other article databases (EBSCO, the Philosopher's Index, Google Scholar), consult a reference librarian, etc. 
2. Second, mind the quality of your sources. The internet, for example, is much like a city dump: there are great things out there, but to reach them you have to dig through a lot of garbage.
      • Peer-reviewed articles and books by professionals in relevant fields are best. If you are unclear about how to tell which periodicals are scholarly, consult a librarian or me.

      • Among the worst are websites of unknown origin, those with obvious bias, or by authors with unknown qualifications. Reliance on these can seriously hurt your project.

        • Handy rule: if you cannot tell who the author is, and that the author is qualified to write about your topic, you probably should not use that source. When in doubt, either ask me or do not use the source.

      • Around the middle are encyclopedias such as the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Encyclopedia Britannica, Wikipedia, etc. These are nice places to start your research, but they had better not be where it ends (especially Wikipedia). In other words, encyclopedia-type sources may be used to orient yourself to your subject and to find leads to other and better sources of information, but much reliance on such sources for your final paper will count against it.
Some good and interesting sources are available on my Philosophy Research Links page.

3. Third, avoid citing lecture, discussion, reading responses, forum posts, etc. for anything that came from a text. Cite the text instead. If you cite material from class discussion, do so like this: (Phil 490 [date]).

 When in doubt about the appropriateness of a given source, consult with me.

Here is a suggestion for getting started in your research. First, identify the names of the people and the topics you are writing about. Then, look for general information about them from reference sources such as encyclopedias. Using the general sources (including the bibliographies they provide), look for more key terms and names related to your topic. Then, use the Library catalog, reference librarians, and article databases to find scholarly material directly on your topic. The Library has kindly posted helpful research tips. 
  • Although not required, it would be a good idea to seek advice from DePauw's Writing Center prior to each paper's due date.

2. Tips for better papers (i.e., things students can often improve at):
  • Proofread! Do not trust your college G.P.A. to your computer's spelling & grammar checkers. I recommend reading your paper aloud to spot typos, grammatical errors, awkward word choices, etc.

  • Avoid fluff, filler, bull, and gratuitous drama. Please don't tell me how long humanity has pondered your topic or give me Webster's definition of anything.

  • State your thesis early in the paper, give a map (preview) of the main points of the paper, and then do what the map says you'll do.

  • Take objections seriously. Present them charitably, as opposed to, say, whacking straw men or presenting opposition so briefly it doesn't get a fair hearing. Athletes say there's no glory in defeating an opponent who is unfairly disadvantaged; you should take the same approach to your papers and grant the side you oppose the credit it deserves.

  • Use better sources of information when research is employed (e.g., scholarly articles as opposed to encyclopedias, pundits, some dude's blog, etc.).

  • Give relevant empirical data where appropriate, as opposed to armchair arguments.

  • Arrive at conclusions that are clearly implied by the sum total of your arguments and information, as opposed to stating a conclusion that goes beyond the evidence provided, or going off on a tangent.


3. Short paper instructions

In this course you'll write three short papers on issues raised in our readings. By “short” I mean 1200-1700 words, about 4-5 pages.

Choose your own topics. One place to look for potential topics is in your reading responses: what portion of the readings has most intrigued you? Is there a particular argument or issue you want to write about? Feel free to discuss possible topics with me.

As with all papers in the course, your short papers should be argumentative: take a side on some controversy.

To do this well in a short space, limit the scope of your essay. Take on a small question rather than a large one. For example, do not try to show that Plato's conception of a just society is correct, or that Hobbes's social contract theory is a load of bunk. (These would be tough to do even in a long paper.) Instead, take on some smaller but significant part, such as the "noble lie" or Hobbes's reasons for insisting a sovereign must be above the law. It often works well to find a specific argument given in a text and critically evaluate it: is its conclusion correct? How good is the reasoning for it?

The general requirements for your short essays are above. No outlines or drafts are required, though at least discussing your paper with me well before it is due would be a good idea, as would discussing your paper with folks at the Writing Center.

No research is required for a short paper, though of course it's fine to use some. Also, your argument regarding one assigned text may be helped by points made by the author of another text. In such cases, by all means bring in the other author's arguments.

Short-paper deadlines will be posted on our class schedule Google doc and on Moodle.

 

4. Research paper instructions
As it says in the syllabus, you will write a a substantial research paper: roughly 10-14 pages: 3000-4000 words not counting footnotes, endnotes or bibliography. The point of this assignment is for you to pursue in depth some controversial course-related topic that is important to you.

This paper is linked to the "S" requirements for this course in the following ways:
  • You will prepare an informal explanation of your work, to be given later in the semester.

  • You will prepare a formal presentation of your nearly-finished paper, to be given in the last weeks of class. As part of this presentation you will receive formal comments from a classmate. Part of the preparation for this presentation is that you will supply a nearly-finished draft of your paper to the instructor and to your assigned commentator at least one week before your presentation.

    (Exception for spring 2021: the first two presenters' drafts will be due at least six days beforehand--6pm Saturday May 1 at the latest. This is because a full week would make them due Friday April 30, and the faculty have decreed that no papers etc. may be due then because it comes after the spring break day of April 29. If the May 1 deadline is a problem for you, please see me.)

    • Failure to supply a complete draft on time will result in your final paper grade being reduced by a full letter grade. If it is so late as to interfere with your commenter's ability to comment effectively, the paper may fail.

  • You will also serve as the commentator for a classmate's paper. You'll turn in a copy of your comments on Moodle by the night before you give your comments in class.

  • Dates for all these, and the deadline for the finished paper, will be found on the Assignments web page.
Your paper should show clear evidence of substantial research on your subject. A good way to do this is to include at least six items in your bibliography which are not assigned readings and which, taken together, give balanced and reasonably thorough coverage of your chosen topic. You must demonstrate understanding of those sources in your paper. This is a suggested minimum; to write a really good paper you will likely need more. You should also heed all instructions above regarding academic integrity and judiciousness about your sources.

Pick a topic that matters to you and explore it by researching and writing about it. Please feel free to discuss your ideas with me concerning what to write about. Appropriate topics include anything within the scope of this course, that is, within political philosophy. I urge you to think back over what we've covered and ask yourself which topic, problem, puzzle, reading, argument, etc. most excited you, inspired you, or annoyed you. Build a topic based on that.

If you are casting about for possible ways to handle your favorite topic, here are some suggestions.
    • There's always the obvious: Here's position X. Here's an argument for X. Here are some objections to X. Here's why those objections don't defeat the arguments for X. So, X.

      Or, you could get fancier:

    • Show that position X seems plausible but really suffers from significant objection O. (For example: "So-and -so believes that threatening to use nuclear weapons is never justified, and gives appealing reasons for this position. But I'll show that (at least under certain circumstances) it is nevertheless justified.")
    • Show that position X seems to suffer from objection O, but in fact it does not.
    • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that X may be correct but the justification is faulty. (For example, you might like Libertarianism but dislike a particular argument for it, so show that although that argument may be respected/popular/influential it's really not good.)
    • Discuss so-and-so's justification for position X, showing that although the justification may seem bad it's really not.
    • Show that P is a vexing problem, for which S is a more promising solution than some alternative(s). (For example: "Here are some proposed responses to al Qaeda-type terrorism; of them, S is superior.")
    • Show that P is a vexing problem for which S seems like a promising solution, but really S is bad (and perhaps that we should do T instead).


 5. Research paper topic ideas
Here are a few ideas I came up with; many more are possible. Before latching onto something I mention below, I urge you to think back over what we've covered and ask yourself which topic, reading, argument, etc. most excited you, inspired you, or annoyed you. Build a topic based on that. Or go through the Table of Contents of our textbook, including what we have not covered, and see what sparks your interest.

Social Contract theory
  • Criticize one of the particular contract theories we examine in class, presenting a problem we have not considered in class
  • Conversely, you might defend one of our social contract theories (Plato's, Hobbes's, Locke's, Rawls's) from some serious criticism
  • Rather than criticizing some particular social contract theory, you could present criticisms (say, from communitarians or libertarians or anarchists or Filmer) of social contract theory as a whole
  • Present a social contract theory that's different from ones we've looked at in class (say, from Rousseau, Harsanyi, etc.)
Equality
  • (How) are we supposedly equal? In what way(s) is our equality important? Is equality compatible with authority, i.e., with government?
Freedom
  • Is freedom just a "western"/"liberal" value, or is it universal? How come?
Natural Law
  • Alternative theories of natural law
  • Criticism of natural law, and/or of alternatives to it
  • Natural law's relation to civil law: Hobbes says they are tightly connected, such that the civil law and natural law "contain each other." This raises a variety of questions: What might Hobbes mean? What is the real relationship, if any, between civil and natural law? When, for example, might one override the demands of the other?
Obedience, or not
  • To what extent should obedience require sacrifice of one's personal interests? You might consider some controversial case of obedience or disobedience.
  • When, if ever, is violent (as opposed to nonviolent) defiance of government justified? Again, you might consider some real-life controversial case. FYI, there is a literature on the relative efficacy of violent vs nonviolent action.
  • More specifically, when, if ever, might terrorism be justifiable? (Bear in mind terrorism takes various forms.)
  • Thrasymachus argues that obedience to government does not serve the interests of the governed, but rather serves the interests of the rulers. To what extent can his position be defended?
  • Thrasymachus implies that when they obey the rules, the "weak" (citizens) are the victim of a conspiracy perpetrated by the "strong" (rulers). In The Genealogy of Morals, Friedrich Nietzsche argues that the opposite has been the case in Western history: that obedience to (moral) rules is a way the weak have managed to subdue the strong. You might critically evaluate either or both of these stances.
Rulers and Laws
  • Hobbes, for example, claims that the interests of a monarch coincide with the interests of citizens. To what extent is this correct?
  • There's a constant tension between the need to protect people against harms (security) and freedom. How can we determine the proper balance? (You might want to consider some controversial case of expression, privacy, etc.)
  • To what extent should the state be devoted to protecting people from harm vs. fostering our flourishing (i.e., protecting so-called negative rights vs. enforcing positive rights)?
  • Several of our authors say that although rulers ought to be good to those they rule, they may treat others --whether foreign governments or individuals--less kindly. (Some examples: Plato's dog that is kind to friends but fierce to outsiders; Hobbes on princes as gladiators, and his remarks on conquest; Locke on enslavement; etc.) You could pick one of these and critically evaluate it, or state and defend your own position on government's obligations to those outside its jurisdiction.
  • Other authors seem to allow even wider latitude for rulers to behave badly. You might, for example, critically compare Machiavelli and Locke.
  • Many jobs that were once done by government are instead being given to private companies. (Some examples: prison management; highway construction and maintenance; various military functions including housing and feeding soldiers, bodyguarding, intelligence gathering; etc.) You might choose one such area and critically assess whether its privatization is appropriate and/or beneficial.
Forms of government
  • Later in Republic, Socrates describes various forms of government and argues that some are preferable to others (notoriously he holds that democracy is among the worst). You might pick some portion of his argument and either critique it or defend it.
  • Rather than focusing on some text(s) we read, you might write about the relative merits/justifiability of different forms of government.
