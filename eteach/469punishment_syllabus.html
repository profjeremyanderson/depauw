About the course
The belief that crimes should be punished is so deeply ingrained that to question it may seem nutty. But it is ripe for philosophical examination. One of the starkest exercises of governments’ power is the threat and practice of legal punishment. Thus, insofar as government itself requires justification, legal punishment requires it all the more. Further, if it is true (as some political theorists hold) that effective government requires the power to punish, then the basis for government itself might be called into question if punishment cannot be justified. Punishment’s justification is important, then. But it is also problematic. When we punish we do things that, under other circumstances, are morally wrong and illegal: lock people up against their will, take away their property, deprive them of various rights such as the right to vote or free association, or we kill them. The purpose of this course is to critically examine justifications offered for legal punishment and alternatives to it. We will delve into the long-standing and complex debate over whether and how punishment may be justified, and consider relevant empirical data.

Assignments & grading
Various terms, ideas, arguments, theories, etc. will come up in readings, lectures, presentations, and discussion. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate you understand and have thought about that material. Ways of demonstrating you’ve thought about it include applying it to cases, critically appraising it, connecting it to other ideas presented inside or outside the course, etc. When independent research is required, your grade will also depend on the quality of that research and demonstrating that you understand and have thought carefully about that material.

The course grade will be derived as follows (subject to change):
 
10%    Reading responses
 30%   Three short essays @ ~1200-1500 words each
 15%    Small-group presentation
 25%    One long research essay @ ~3000-5000 words (counts towards Writing In the Major)
  5%    Class participation
  5%    Co-leading discussion
10%    Take-home exam

Reading responses: at least once each week, starting with our second meeting, you will need to write a response of at least 300 words to at least one of the assigned readings and post it to our forum in Moodle. In each response you need to do two things:
1. Briefly summarize the reading(s)--that is, tell me what you take to be the two or three main points the author tries to make. Your goal here is to demonstrate you have done the reading carefully. 
2. In a separate paragraph or more, give your reaction—positive, negative, neutral, tangential, whatever—to the reading(s). Your reaction may be short or long, but should be detailed enough for me to understand why you react to the reading(s) the way you do.
Responses are due the night before the class meeting where that reading will be discussed.  (Just to be clear enough for a lawyer, "the night before" means "any time before sunrise on the day we discuss that reading"; the time of sunrise shall be determined by wunderground.com; the time of submission is determined by the time stamp on your reading response.) Late responses never receive credit.
      Responses will be scored on a simple scale: excellent (4), good (3), or deficient (1). An average of one good response per week will yield a grade of C (75%) for this portion of the course grade. More and/or better responses will yield a higher grade. Earning a total of 56 points will get you full credit (100%/A+) for this assignment as long as you turn in at least one response per week. That many points can be earned by submitting 19 good responses  with at least one per week (i.e., one per week plus an additional good response five times during the semester). Alternatively, submitting one excellent response per week would earn full credit.     
      Qualities that tend to raise a reading response from good to excellent include: clarity of writing; thoughtfulness (e.g., offering careful critical appraisal of the reading); responding to more than one reading at a time (when more than one is assigned); and relating the assigned material to other assignments, other class work, outside research, etc. 
      Suggestion: since Moodle can log you out if you take too long (and this could wipe out your work), try typing your response using a word processor first, then log in to Moodle and copy your response into the forum.

Papers. You'll write three short essays of about 1200 to 1500 words each and a longer essay, based on research, of 3000-5000 words. Each will ask you to take a position regarding material we have studied and defend it against some objection(s). I can suggest topics for you or you may choose your own. More details are available on the Papers section of the course web site.

Presentations will entail you forming small groups of 2 or 3 or perhaps working alone. More details will be posted to the presentations web page, but here’s a quick description. Each individual/group will take responsibility for researching a topic within the subject of this course and presenting it to the class. Your presentations are part of the course, so they must be done well. I will suggest presentation topics, or you may choose your own with my approval. 

Class participation consists of demonstrating you’re thinking about the course in some way besides the required reading responses: participating in class discussions, discussing course-related stuff on the class open forum on Moodle or with me outside of class in person or by e-mail, etc. Mere attendance, looking interested, taking notes, etc. do not count. Participating once per week, by whatever means, will earn you a C for participation. Occasional short informal writing assignments, if given, will also count towards participation.

 Co-leading class discussion will require you to (a) master an assigned reading, (b) create discussion questions concerning the most important points in that reading, and (c) co-lead class discussion with me on that assignment. You'll need to do this at least twice. Signups begin in the first week of class.

How to succeed in this course

Read, and read carefully. Not just once, and not quickly like it’s a news article. Philosophical texts reward careful and repeated reading, and invite critical reflection. You can expect me to call on you with questions about it, and grasping the readings is crucial to performing well on exams and writing good papers.

Come to class on time, prepared. Bring the current reading and your notes with you. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, early in the semester, to share notes.

 Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take time after class to fill them out.

Participate a lot. Assume your question is worth asking and your comment is worth making. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you’re not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.

 Give yourself time to write good papers. Haste never makes for better writing. Allow time to seek help at the W center and from me well before your papers are due.
