Assignment 7: Justifying punishment, cont.
  • Main readings:
    • Richard B. Brandt, The Utilitarian Theory of Criminal Punishment, pp. 553-7 [not in 4th edition; find it on e-reserves: access it via the Library Course Reserves link on our main  Moodle page]
      • Optional: Jeremy Bentham, A Utilitarian Theory of Punishment [4th edition pp.  452-6; not in 5th edition; find it on e-reserves: access it via the Library Course Reserves link on our main  Moodle page] (When discussing Bentham, Brandt alludes to this piece.)
    • Michael Moore, The Argument for Retributivism, pp. 558-62 [4th ed.: 456-61]
    • Kant, excerpts from The Metaphysics of Morals.
      • Moore argues for retributivism largely by rejecting the consequentialist rationale for punishment. He refrains from arguing much for retributivism, and doesn't say much about the content of retributivism. Kant gives more substance regarding his particular "eye for an eye" version of retributivism.
  • Supplemental readings: portions of several previous readings are relevant to the justification of punishment one way or another, and so are worth another look.
    • Review the portions of "C. Punishment and Responsibility," pp. 528-31 [4th ed.: 442-6] that discuss the justification of punishment. 
    • Review the portions of Kadish ("The Criminal Law and the Luck of the Draw") which discuss different kinds of retributivism--specifically, the last five paragraphs of the long section titled, "The Rational Indefensibility of the Harm Doctrine." This text gives you a sense of how some different forms of retributivism work, though from a critic's perspective. 
    • Review the portion of Rosen ("The Battered Woman's Defense") which discusses "forfeiture theory"--the four paragraphs from "Essentially, Robinson's theory is a forfeiture theory" onward. Rosen discusses forfeiture theory specifically regarding self-defense, but forfeiture's basic idea is that by committing a crime you "forfeit" some rights, and that idea is very common in people's thinking about punishment.

  • Main reading: Nora Zamichow, The Fractured Life of Jeremy (PDF), Los Angeles Times, July 19, 1998.
