The VPAA has announced the following changes to DePauw policies:
 
With approval from appropriate faculty committees, DePauw is putting three policies in place to provide students with more flexibility:

1. Expanded Pass/Fail Options (see instructions here)
For the spring 2020 term, each student will be able to request to designate one or more courses to be taken Pass/Fail through Friday, May 1, 2020.  Requests may be made by students from any class year and all requests received by the deadline will be honored by the faculty Petitions Committee.  During spring 2020, courses taken pass/fail (assuming they are not failed) will count toward all University requirements including major requirements, minor requirements and general education requirements. Courses taken pass/fail during the spring of 2020 will not count toward the usual limit on the number of pass/fail enrollments each student is permitted during their DePauw career.  Students should consult with their advisors before requesting that a course be converted to pass/fail.  Additionally, a set of frequently asked questions will be made available to students and advisors before April 1st, 2020.

DePauw's pass/fail system is actually a pass/D/fail system meaning the instructors can assign grades of "Pass", "D", or "Fail".  Course taken under this system to do not impact a student's GPA. However, students earn regular course credit for grades of "Pass" or "D".  Additional information will be distributed before April 1st regarding the advantages and disadvantages of electing the pass/fail option.  In addition, we will provide instructions for electing this option should you wish to do so.  Since the deadline for selecting the pass/fail option is May 1st, you have plenty of time to see how your courses are going before you make decisions.

2. Deadline for Withdrawing from a Course
The deadline for Withdrawing from a course has been extended from Friday, March 20th to Friday, April 10th.  This will allow students to experience two weeks of remote learning before finalizing decisions about course withdrawals.

3. Incomplete Grades
For spring 2020, there will be more flexibility in allowing Incomplete grades, with support from a faculty member, for students who have difficulty completing course work for reasons including technology barriers.  With the faculty member's permission, students will be able to finish these Incomplete grades in the fall.

Course description and goals
In this course we will examine three topics. First is the extent to which moral constraints apply to war. Do they apply at all? If so, is war still allowable? The second is love, specifically erotic love (eros) as discussed in Plato’s Symposium. Partly we will try to understand what love is, and partly try to understand the peculiar but transcendent role Plato thinks love can play for us. The third is compatibilism, the view that we can be morally responsible even if our actions are predetermined. Within each of these topics we will consult a mix of old and recent texts. Although ethics forms a component of each of these topics, we will also venture into history of philosophy, political philosophy, political science, metaphysics, epistemology, philosophy of religion, philosophy of mind, and possibly others.
      This course will be unusual and maybe unique in your DePauw experience because it consists only of senior philosophy majors. We can consider the material with greater sophistication. It also implies that more independent work and original thought will be expected of you. This course is a challenging opportunity, and I encourage you to make the most of it. Your effort should yield important benefits, including broadening the range of your philosophical knowledge and taking your existing skills of critical evaluation, productive discussion, research, and argumentative writing and improving them even further.

  • Niccolo Machiavelli, The Prince. Translated by David Wootton. Indianapolis: Hackett, 1995. (ISBN: 0872203166.) The text is also easily available for free online, but Wootton's is a recent and lively translation.

Assignments & grading
1. Writing requirements.
a. Papers. For each of the three course topics you will write a short paper (1200-1700 words). There will also be a substantial research paper (3000-4000 words). Details of these assignments are posted on the Papers web page.
 
b. Reading Responses. To prepare for class, at least once each week write a reading response and post it to the appropriate forum on Moodle by the night before we discuss that reading. Instructions for these are posted in the Reading Response forums on Moodle.
 
2. “S” requirements
This is an “S” course. You will engage in each of the following speaking/listening activities. More detailed instructions are on the "S" Work page:
 
A. Discussion instigation. We will take turns instigating discussion. During a given class meeting you will do your best to instigate a stimulating and productive discussion of the assigned reading for that day by giving a brief (5-10 minute) talk about some aspect of the reading at the start of class. (Hint: if you wish to develop this presentation into one of your short papers, that’s fine.)
 
B. Informal paper topic explanation. You will give a brief (roughly 5 minute) presentation in which you explain the topic of your research paper and the main issue(s), question(s), puzzle(s), etc. that you plan to discuss in it. Your presentation should be pitched as if to an audience of non-philosophers who are skeptical about the worth of doing philosophy. Accordingly, you should not only explain your topic in a clear and accessible way, but you should also say something about why your topic is worth thinking about in the first place. After your presentation, you will field questions from your classmates and professor for ten minutes or so. April 6th and 8th are set aside for these presentations. You will need to have selected and started thinking about your paper topic by this time, so you must start thinking about your research paper topic early.
 
C. Formal paper presentation. Near semester’s end you will give a 15-20 minute talk in which you explain and defend the central thesis of your research paper. You may not read from your paper. Following your talk, a classmate will give a brief (roughly 5 minute) response to your talk. This will be followed by a brief question-and-answer period with the whole class. You must provide your commentator and professor with a complete draft of your research paper no later than one week prior to your presentation.
 
D. Paper comments. You will also serve as a commentator for a research paper presented by one of your classmates. You will give a brief (about 5 minute) talk in which you offer some critical comments on the paper in question. Your comments will immediately follow your classmate’s formal paper presentation. You must submit a copy of your comments Moodle by 6pm the night before you comment.
 
E. Participation during class discussion and question and answer periods. I expect you to contribute a lot to class discussion, to listen to what your classmates have to say, and ask thoughtful questions during the various question and answer periods.
 
To earn the “S” for this course you must perform each of these five speaking/listening activities passably. Earning a passing grade in the course does not guarantee that you will receive your “S,” nor does earning the “S” guarantee you will pass the course.
 
Don’t forget the Writing and Speaking & Listening Centers. This course has substantial writing and speaking & listening components. I strongly encourage you to get help from the “W” and “S” centers. Everyone could be a better writer. Everyone could be a better speaker and listener. The “W” and “S” centers do not perform a remedial function; going there doesn’t make you “special”; their function is to improve your writing, speaking, and listening skills. Don’t ignore these valuable resources!

Course Policies
If any of these policies might be a problem for you, please let me know.

Attendance: You should be in class on time and prepared to discuss the day's assignment unless you have strong reason not to be, such as a serious medical problem, University-recognized event, religious obligation, family emergency, etc. Your course grade may be penalized, potentially down to failing, for excessive unexcused tardiness or absences, and you may have to drop the course if you miss 2 weeks of class (6 meetings) regardless of why you are absent.

Here are some special situations:
  • Academically approved (by the VPAA) extracurricular activities, such as athletics: inform me of possible conflicts at the beginning of the semester or as early as possible. I'm willing to work with you , but be aware your extracurriculars do not automatically let you miss class. You must get faculty permission, and we can say "No." Read the policy here.
  • Religious obligations: I support and follow DePauw's policy on religious holy days. If your religious obligations conflict with class meetings or deadlines in this course, let me know at least one week in advance. Then (a) you are excused from attending those meetings and (b) I will work with you to make up any work you miss.
Suggestion: since everyone misses class now and then, make a deal with two classmates, at the beginning of the semester, to share notes in case of absences.

Minimize distractions in class. Cell phones, smart watches, PDA’s, pagers, etc. must be set to vibrate only, and put away, unless you clear it with me beforehand. No laptops (or the equivalent) except for writing your exams. Please visit the restroom before class rather than during. And so on: be here physically and mentally.

 Late policy. Make-up exams will not be allowed nor late papers accepted for full credit except in cases where there is a compelling excuse such as a serious medical problem, University-recognized event, religious obligation, etc. (See Attendance policy, above.) As noted above, late reading responses never receive credit. Notify me in advance by phone (765-658-4679), e-mail, or in person if you can. I may require documentation.

Academic integrity. I take academic integrity very seriously and I expect you to. Often, cheating is unintended, but even then it is a serious offense and I may prosecute you. The minimum penalty is worse than turning in no work at all, and penalties can include failing the course, suspension, or expulsion. Much plagiarism is due to ignorance of the standards and requirements for documenting sources, so I urge you to:
  • Get to know DePauw's policy and your obligations and rights, which are stated in the Academic Handbook,
  • Follow the academic integrity instructions in the paper assignments, and
  • Use these resources to better understand academic integrity at DePauw. 
You can avoid many problems if you follow these simple rules: if you use an idea you got from someone or somewhere else, cite it; if you use their words, put them in quotes and cite.
