About the course
Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679) is both one of the most influential and reviled philosophers in the Western canon. This course is designed for you to come to your own informed judgment about him. He's best known for his picture of a chaotic, miserable “state of nature,” which has become one of the most famous ideas in political theory and international relations. But Hobbes was a comprehensive and systematic thinker whose work encompassed not only politics but mathematics, physics, psychology, ethics, and religion. In this course we’ll explore his system of thought, mainly by studying his best-known work, Leviathan, but also some lesser-known work and secondary literature.

Assignments & grading
Various terms, ideas, arguments, theories, etc. will come up in readings and discussion. Your grade will depend on how well you demonstrate you understand and have thought about that material. Ways of demonstrating you’ve thought about it include applying it to cases, critically appraising it, connecting it to other ideas presented inside or outside the course, etc. When independent research is required, your grade will also depend on the quality of that research and demonstrating that you understand and have thought carefully about that material.

Because this is a 400-level course my expectations for the quality of your work will be high. The course grade will be derived as follows:

 

            10%     Class participation

            10%     Reading responses (see description just below)

            40%     Four short (3-5 page) papers @ 10% each

            30%     Long (10-13 page) research essay

            10%     Mini-conference (reading and critiquing drafts of each others' long papers)


Reading responses: before each class meeting, write a response of at least 250 words to at least one of the assigned readings and post it to our forum in Moodle. As the name "reading response" suggests, write about your reactions—positive, negative, neutral, tangential, whatever—to the reading(s). Your reactions may be short or long, but should be detailed enough for me to understand why you react to the reading(s) the way you do. For example, you might point to some part(s) of the text, give your interpretation of it, and then elaborate on that. Your responses serve several purposes: they help start the day's discussion in advance, they give me some idea of your interests so that I can better prepare to lead class, and they also may provide inspiration for your papers in this course.
       Responses are due the night before the class meeting where that reading will be discussed.  (Just to be clear enough for a lawyer, "the night before" means "any time before sunrise on the day we discuss that reading"; the time of sunrise shall be determined by wunderground.com; the time of submission is determined by the time stamp on your reading response.)
       Responses will be scored on a simple scale: excellent (4), good (3), deficient (1), or missing (0). A "good" response meets the requirements above. Submitting a "good" response for every class meeting will earn an "A-" for this portion of the course grade. Missing responses, or those that are too brief or unclear (i.e., "deficient"), will lower the grade.
       You can make up for some missed responses, or improve your grade above A-, by writing "excellent" responses. Qualities that can raise a reading response from good to excellent include: clarity of writing; substantially more extensive responses; thoughtfulness (e.g., offering careful critical appraisal of the reading, making especially interesting points); responding to more than one reading at a time (when more than one is assigned); and relating the assigned material to other assignments, other class work, outside research, etc.
       Suggestion: since Moodle can log you out if you take too long (and this could wipe out your work), try typing your response using a word processor first, then log in to Moodle and copy your response into the forum.

Your short papers may be interpretive or argumentative. That is, you may explain how we could understand some challenging passage in Hobbes (interpretive), or argue that some controversial claim of Hobbes’ or one of our secondary texts’ is correct or incorrect (argumentative). I can suggest topics for you but I prefer you choose your own. The long research essay will, as the name implies, be an argumentative paper based on independent research. It may be an expanded, research-augmented version of one of your short papers if you like, as long as it is mostly new work.

Class participation consists of demonstrating you’re thinking about the course in some way besides the required reading responses. The best form is participating in class discussions, but it could also consist of discussing course-related stuff with me outside of class in person or by e-mail, etc. Mere attendance, looking interested, taking notes, etc. do not count.

How to succeed in this course

Read, and read carefully. Not just once, and not quickly like it’s a news article. Philosophical texts reward careful and repeated reading, and invite critical reflection. You can expect me to call on you with questions about it, and grasping the readings is crucial to performing well on exams and writing good papers.

Come to class on time, prepared. Bring the current reading and your notes with you. If you don’t come, make arrangements with others to share notes with you. I suggest agreeing with at least two other people in the class, early in the semester, to share notes.

 Take detailed notes. Just copying what’s on the board is not sufficient. If the notes you take during class are sketchy, take time after class to fill them out.

Participate a lot. Assume your question is worth asking and your comment is worth making. Don’t allow yourself to stay confused or lost. If you’re not comfortable speaking in class, see me outside it.

 Give yourself time to write good papers. Haste never makes for better writing. Allow time to seek help at the W center and from me well before your papers are due.
